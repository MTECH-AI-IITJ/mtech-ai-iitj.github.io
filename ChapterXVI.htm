Chapter XVI: Working of Patents, Compulsory Licences and Revocation

Main Idea: A patent isn't just a certificate; it's a deal. The inventor gets a monopoly, and in return, they must work the invention in India (make it available to the public). If they don't, the government can step in through Compulsory Licences or even Revoke the patent.

Key Definitions (Sec. 82)

  • Patented Article: Any article or product that has a patent granted in India.
  • Patentee: Not just the owner! It includes:
    • The person for the time being entered in the register as the patent owner.
    • The exclusive licensee (if applicable).

MCQ: Who is considered a 'patentee' under the Act?
a) Only the original inventor
b) Only the person currently registered as the owner
c) The registered owner and the exclusive licensee
d) Any licensee of the patent
Answer: c) The registered owner and the exclusive licensee.

General Principle (Sec. 83)

Patents are granted to encourage invention and to ensure that the inventions are worked in India on a commercial scale. They are not granted merely to enable patentees to enjoy a monopoly by importing the patented article.

MCQ: The primary purpose of granting a patent in India is to:
a) Reward the inventor with a monopoly forever.
b) Ensure the invention is commercially worked within India.
c) Prevent foreign companies from selling products in India.
d) Generate revenue for the government through fees.
Answer: b) Ensure the invention is commercially worked within India.

Compulsory Licence (CL) (Sec. 84)

This is a licence granted by the Controller (the patent boss) to a third party to make, use, or sell a patented invention without the patent owner's consent.

When can someone apply for a CL? (After 3 years from the date of patent grant)

  1. Reasonable Requirements of the Public have not been satisfied.
  2. The patented invention is not available to the public at a reasonably affordable price.
  3. The patented invention is not worked in the territory of India.

Use Case: Pharma Company A holds a patent for a life-saving drug in India but sells it at ₹10,000 per dose, making it unaffordable for most people. Company B can apply for a compulsory licence to manufacture a generic version to sell at a lower price.

MCQ: An application for a compulsory licence can be made after:
a) 1 year of patent grant
b) 2 years of patent grant
c) 3 years of patent grant
d) 5 years of patent grant
Answer: c) 3 years of patent grant

Revocation for Non-Working (Sec. 85)

If, even after granting a compulsory licence, the patent is still not worked properly in India, the Controller can revoke (cancel) the patent. This is the last resort.

Special Types of Compulsory Licences

  • Sec. 92 (Special CL): The Central Government can notify a specific patent area in circumstances of national emergency (e.g., a pandemic), extreme urgency, or public non-commercial use. This makes the process for getting a CL much faster.
  • Sec. 92A (Pharma Export CL): This allows a company to get a compulsory licence primarily to manufacture and export patented pharmaceutical products to a country with insufficient manufacturing capacity. This is for addressing public health issues in other developing countries.

MCQ: Which section allows for a compulsory licence specifically for exporting medicines to a country facing a health crisis?
a) Section 84
b) Section 85
c) Section 92
d) Section 92A
Answer: d) Section 92A

Controller's Powers & Process (Sec. 87, 88, 89)

  • The Controller has wide powers to decide on CL applications.
  • The main purpose for granting a CL is to make the patented invention available to the public in sufficient quantities, at an affordable price.
  • The Controller will consider:
    • The nature of the invention.
    • The ability of the applicant to work the invention.
    • The time elapsed since the patent was granted.

Terms of the Licence (Sec. 90)

A compulsory licence is not free. The licensee must pay royalties to the patentee. The terms are set by the Controller to be fair and reasonable.

MCQ: A compulsory licence granted under Section 84 requires the licensee to:
a) Pay no royalties as it is compulsory.
b) Pay a nominal, token royalty to the government.
c) Pay adequate royalties to the patentee as determined by the Controller.
d) Share profits equally with the patentee.
Answer: c) Pay adequate royalties to the patentee as determined by the Controller.

Related Patents & "License of Right" (Sec. 91, 94)

  • Sec. 91: If Patent B cannot be worked without infringing Patent A, the Controller can grant a licence for Patent A to the owner of Patent B.
  • Sec. 94: A compulsory licence can be terminated by the Controller if the circumstances that led to its grant have changed and it is no longer justified.

Final Summary & Big Picture Use Case

Scenario: "MedPharma" holds a patent for a critical cancer drug in India. They import it in small quantities and sell it at a very high price (₹1 lakh per month's dose). Three years after the patent was granted, the drug is inaccessible to 99% of the population.

What happens?

  1. GenericCo applies to the Patent Controller for a Compulsory Licence (Sec. 84).
  2. They argue the public's requirements are not met, the price is not affordable, and the invention is not "worked" (manufactured) in India.
  3. The Controller hears both sides (Sec. 87).
  4. The Controller grants the licence to GenericCo, setting terms like how much they can produce and the royalties they must pay to MedPharma (Sec. 88, 90).
  5. GenericCo starts manufacturing the drug locally and sells it at ₹5,000 per dose.
  6. If MedPharma still doesn't start local manufacturing or improve supply, the Controller could eventually Revoke the patent entirely (Sec. 85).

MCQ: The ultimate consequence for a patentee who consistently fails to work their patent in India, even after a compulsory licence is granted, is:
a) A heavy fine
b) Imprisonment for the company directors
c) Revocation of the patent
d) Automatic transfer of the patent to the government
Answer: c) Revocation of the patent

 

 

Working of Patents, Compulsory Licences, and Revocation

This chapter of the Patents Act deals with how patents are used, the conditions under which the government can force a patent holder to license their invention to others, and when a patent can be cancelled. The main goal is to ensure that patented inventions are not just held for profit but are actually made available and used for the public's benefit in India.

Definitions (Sections 82)

  • Patented Article: This is the product that is protected by a patent.
  • Patentee: The person or company who holds the patent. They have the exclusive right to make, use, and sell the invention.

General Principles (Section 83)

The law says that patents aren't just for the patentee's gain. The government should:

  • Encourage Invention: Promote new inventions.
  • Ensure Commercial Use: Make sure the invention is actually being used in India.
  • Prevent Monopoly: Stop the patentee from misusing their exclusive rights to unfairly price or limit the supply of the product.
  • Promote Public Interest: Ensure the patented invention is available to the public at a reasonable price.

Compulsory Licences (Sections 84, 88-92A)

A compulsory licence is when the government forces a patent holder to allow another company to produce their patented invention. This happens when the patent holder isn't making the invention available to the public in a reasonable way.

When can a compulsory licence be granted?

After three years from the date the patent was granted, any interested person can apply for a compulsory licence if one of the following conditions is met:

  1. Public Needs Not Met: The public's reasonable needs for the patented product aren't being met. This could be because the product isn't available or the supply is too limited.
  2. High Price: The patented product is not available at an affordable price.
  3. No Working in India: The patent isn't being "worked" (produced or used) in India to its full potential.

What happens after an application?

  • The Controller (an official from the patent office) reviews the application.
  • They consider if the applicant tried to get a voluntary licence from the patentee on reasonable terms and failed.
  • The Controller can grant the licence and set the terms, including the royalty (payment) the new company must pay to the patentee.

Special Circumstances for Compulsory Licences

  • Public Health Crisis (Section 92): The government can issue a compulsory licence during a national emergency or extreme urgency, like a pandemic.
  • Export of Drugs (Section 92A): A compulsory licence can be granted to allow a company in India to produce and export a patented drug to a country with a public health problem that doesn't have the manufacturing capacity to make the drug itself.

Use Case for Practice:

A pharmaceutical company, PharmaCorp, holds a patent for a life-saving cancer drug. They are only manufacturing a small quantity of the drug in India, and the price is extremely high, making it unaffordable for most patients. A local non-profit organization, HealthCare India, applies for a compulsory licence to produce a generic version of the drug at a much lower price.

  1. Which section of the Patents Act would HealthCare India use to apply for the licence? (Hint: Look at the conditions for a compulsory licence).
  2. What information would HealthCare India need to provide to the Controller?
  3. If the licence is granted, what might the Controller require HealthCare India to do? (Hint: What does the new company have to pay the patentee?)

Revocation of Patents (Sections 85)

The Controller can revoke (cancel) a patent if, after two years from the date a compulsory licence was granted, the reasons for granting the licence still exist. This means if the patentee still isn't meeting the public's needs, their patent can be taken away completely. The Controller can also cancel a patent if the compulsory licence was not effective in making the product available.

Use Case for Practice:

A patent for a new type of water purifier was granted to PureH2O Inc. They were not manufacturing the purifier in India, so a compulsory licence was granted to AquaClean. Two years later, despite AquaClean's efforts, PureH2O Inc. still hasn't started manufacturing their own purifiers in India, and the overall supply in the country is still insufficient to meet public demand.

  1. Can the Controller revoke PureH2O's patent?
  2. Which section of the Patents Act would allow for this revocation?

Miscellaneous Provisions (Sections 86, 87, 89, 90, 91, 93, 94)

  • Procedure (Section 87): This section lays out the detailed process for how the Controller handles applications for compulsory licences or revocation.
  • Terms and Conditions (Section 90): The law specifies what the Controller must consider when setting the terms of a compulsory licence, such as the royalty rate, the scope of the licence, and how long it will last.
  • Related Patents (Section 91): If a patented invention can only be used with another patented invention, the Controller can grant a compulsory licence for both.
  • Termination (Section 94): The Controller can end a compulsory licence if the conditions that led to its grant are no longer present.

Exam Notes: Chapter XVI - Working of Patents, Compulsory Licences and Revocation (Sections 8294)

Key Concepts in Simple Language

  1. Section 82: Definitions
    • Patented Articles: Products made using a patented invention.
    • Patentee: The person or entity granted the patent (or their legal successor).
  2. Section 83: General Principles for Working Patents
    • Patents must be worked in India (used/manufactured locally) to serve public interest.
    • Patents cannot be used to:
      • Restrict trade or technology.
      • Charge unreasonably high prices.
      • Prevent importation of affordable goods.
  3. Section 84: Compulsory Licences
    • A licence granted without the patentees permission by the Controller.
    • Grounds for grant (after 3 years of patent grant):
      • Public needs are unmet.
      • Invention is unaffordable.
      • Invention is not worked in India.
    • Applicant must first try to negotiate with the patentee (waived in emergencies).
  4. Section 85: Revocation for Non-Working
    • Patent can be cancelled if:
      • After 2 years of a compulsory licence order, the invention is still not worked adequately.
      • Public needs remain unmet.
  5. Section 86: Adjournment of Applications
    • Controller may delay compulsory licence hearings if the patentee is taking steps to work the invention or make it affordable.
  6. Section 87: Procedure for Applications
    • Steps:
      • Applicant notifies patentee.
      • Controller investigates (e.g., checks efforts to work invention, affordability).
      • Hearing held if needed.
  7. Section 88: Controllers Powers in Granting Licences
    • Controller can:
      • Limit the licence to specific purposes/areas.
      • Set royalties.
      • Ban unauthorised assignment of the licence.
  8. Section 89: Purposes of Compulsory Licences
    • To ensure:
      • Invention is worked in India.
      • Public gets affordable access.
      • No abuse of patent rights.
  9. Section 90: Terms & Conditions of Licences
    • Includes:
      • Royalty amount.
      • Licence duration.
      • Territory/purpose of use.
      • Conditions to protect patentees interests.
  10. Section 91: Licensing of Related Patents
    • Compulsory licence granted if:
      • One patent cannot be used without another.
      • The second patent is a significant technical improvement.
    • Both patentees get cross-licences.
  11. Section 92: Compulsory Licences in National Emergencies
    • Central Government can notify Controller to grant licences immediately (no 3-year wait) during:
      • National emergencies.
      • Extreme urgency.
      • Public non-commercial use.
  12. Section 92A: Export of Pharmaceuticals
    • Compulsory licence allowed to export medicines to countries with:
      • No manufacturing capacity.
      • A compulsory licence/notification for import.
  13. Section 93: Licence as a Deed
    • Controllers order acts as a legal contract between patentee and licensee.
  14. Section 94: Termination of Compulsory Licence
    • Licence can be ended if:
      • Grounds for grant no longer exist.
      • Licensee violates terms.
      • Invention is not used properly.

Use Cases for Practice

Use Case 1: Compulsory Licence for Non-Working

  • Scenario: A patent for a life-saving drug was granted 4 years ago. The patentee (a foreign company) only imports small quantities at high prices. An Indian company wants to manufacture it locally but failed to negotiate a licence.
  • Question: Can the Indian company apply for a compulsory licence? If yes, under which section and on what grounds?
  • Answer: Yes, under Section 84. Grounds: (1) Invention not worked in India, (2) Unaffordable pricing, (3) Public needs unmet.

Use Case 2: Revocation of Patent

  • Scenario: A compulsory licence was granted 3 years ago. The licensee produces the invention but still charges high prices. Public demand remains unmet.
  • Question: Can the patent be revoked? Under which section?
  • Answer: Yes, under Section 85. Reason: After 2 years of the licence order, the invention is still not meeting public needs.

Use Case 3: Export of Pharmaceuticals (Section 92A)

  • Scenario: Country X faces a malaria outbreak but lacks drug manufacturing capacity. It issues a compulsory licence for import. An Indian company holds a patent for the drug.
  • Question: Can the Indian company get a compulsory licence to export to Country X?
  • Answer: Yes, under Section 92A. Conditions: (1) Country X has no manufacturing capacity, (2) Country X allows import, (3) Export quantities meet Country Xs needs.

Use Case 4: Termination of Compulsory Licence

  • Scenario: A compulsory licence was granted because the patentee wasnt working the invention. Now, the patentee manufactures it locally at affordable prices.
  • Question: Can the patentee terminate the compulsory licence?
  • Answer: Yes, under Section 94. Grounds: Original reason (non-working) no longer exists.

  • Scenario: During a pandemic, a patented vaccine is in short supply. The government notifies a national emergency.
  • Question: Can the Controller grant a compulsory licence immediately?
  • Answer: Yes, under Section 92. The 3-year wait and negotiation requirements are waived due to the emergency.

Summary for Quick Revision

  • Compulsory Licence: Forced by Controller if patent is underworked/expensive (Sections 84, 92, 92A).

  • Key Principles: Patents must serve public interest, not just profit (Section 83).
  • Export Licence: Allowed for medicines to countries in need (Section 92A).
  • Termination: Licence ends if original issues are resolved (Section 94).

Tip: Focus on public interest (Sections 83, 84, 89), timeframes (3 years for licence, 2 years for revocation), and emergency exceptions (Sections 92, 92A).
