Chapter VI: GENERAL (Sections 24-31) - Practice Module

A. Tricky & Comprehensive MCQs

1. Section 24: Fees

●      Question: 'Aesthetica Ltd.' files an application for design registration but pays only 80% of the prescribed fee due to a clerical error in their accounts department. The Controller processes the application and issues a filing receipt. Three months later, the shortfall is discovered. What is the most accurate legal position regarding the application?
a) The application is valid as the Controller has already taken it on record.
b) The application is deemed never to have been filed, as the proceeding is of no effect from inception.
c) The application is valid, but Aesthetica Ltd. must pay the balance with a penalty.
d) The application is temporarily suspended until the full fee is paid.
Answer: (b) The application is of no effect. S. 24(2) is a non-obstante provision stating a proceeding is "of no effect unless the fee has been paid." This implies validity is contingent on full payment.

2. Section 25: Notice of Trust

●      Question: Mr. X holds the registered design for a novel chair as a beneficial owner for a trust created for his minor children. The trustees apply to the Controller to enter a notice of this trust in the register. What will be the Controller's action?
a) Register the notice with details of the beneficiaries.
b) Refuse to enter the notice, as the Act prohibits entry of any trust.
c) Register the trustees as proprietors instead of Mr. X.
d) Ask for a court order before making any entry.
Answer: (b) S. 25 is explicit: "Notice of any trust... shall not be entered." The register only reflects the legal proprietor, not equitable interests.

3. Section 26: Inspection of Registers

●      Question: An investigator wishes to inspect the register of designs to gather intelligence on a competitor's portfolio. The register is maintained digitally. How is the inspection to be carried out as per the Act?
a) Direct access to the live database under Controller's supervision.
b) By inspecting a certified computer printout of the relevant entries.
c) Only by obtaining certified copies on payment of fee; no direct inspection.
d) By filing a formal application under the RTI Act, 2005.
Answer: (b) As per the proviso to S. 26, when the register is on computer, inspection is made by viewing the computer printout.

4. Section 27 & 28: Privilege & Prohibition

●      Question: Which of the following documents is NOT prohibited from public inspection under Chapter VI of the Designs Act?
a) A report prepared by the Controller on a novelty search for a contested design.
b) Drawings and specimens for an application abandoned due to non-compliance.
c) The report of the Controller referred to in Section 45 (Report to be laid before Parliament).
d) Correspondence between the Controller and an applicant regarding a refused design.
Answer: (c) S. 27 carves out an exception for the report under S. 45. All other reports are privileged. S. 28 covers abandoned/refused applications.

5. Section 29: Correction of Clerical Errors

●      Question: 'Kreative Designs' successfully registers a pattern. Later, they realize the visual representation omits a key ornamental feature shown in their original drawings. They apply to the Controller under S. 29 for correction. What is the likely outcome?
a) The Controller will allow the correction as it's a clerical error in the representation.
b) The Controller will refuse, as this alters the substantive appearance of the design.
c) The Controller will treat it as a request for amendment requiring re-examination.
d) The Controller will cancel the registration and ask for a fresh application.
Answer: (b) S. 29 allows correction of "clerical errors," not substantive changes that alter the identity or scope of the design itself. Adding a feature is a substantive change.

6. Section 30: Assignment & Transmission

●      Question: Design 'D' is assigned from Company A to Company B via a written agreement executed on Jan 1, 2023. The application for registration of this title is filed with the Controller on August 15, 2023 (7.5 months later) without any prior extension. What is the validity of this assignment against a third party who acquires the design from Company A in good faith in July 2023?
a) It is valid; the 6-month period is directory, not mandatory.
b) It is invalid against the third party, as registration was not filed within 6 months.
c) It is valid because the assignment was in writing.
d) It will be valid only if the Controller condones the delay now.
Answer: (b) S. 30(3) states the assignment "shall not be valid unless..." the application is filed within 6 months (extendable by 6 more on application). Failure to do so invalidates it against subsequent parties. The proviso on retrospective effect (from date of execution) applies only upon valid entry.

7. Section 30(5): Evidence

●      Question: In a suit for infringement, the plaintiff claims ownership through an unregistered assignment deed. The defendant challenges the title. How will the court treat the deed as evidence?
a) It is conclusive proof of title.
b) It is admissible as primary evidence without any conditions.
c) It shall not be admitted in evidence, unless the court directs otherwise for recorded reasons.
d) It is admissible only if a certified copy is produced.
Answer: (c) S. 30(5) creates a strict embargo. An unregistered instrument (except for S. 31 applications) is not admissible as proof of title unless the court uses its discretion and records reasons.

8. Section 31: Rectification of Register

●      Question: The Controller's power to rectify the register under S. 31 does NOT extend to:
a) Expunging an entry made without sufficient cause.
b) Cancelling the registration of a design on the ground of prior publication (which is a ground under S. 19).
c) Correcting an error in the address of the proprietor.
d) Adding an entry of a license omitted from the register.
Answer: (b) S. 31(5) expressly bars the Controller from making an order cancelling registration on grounds provided in S. 19. That power resides with the High Court in revocation proceedings.

C. Practical Use Cases

  1. Due Diligence in M&A: A company acquiring another must inspect the Designs Register (S.26) to verify the target's ownership. They must check for registered assignments/licenses (S.30) and not rely on mere deeds. An unrecorded license would not be valid against the acquirer.
  2. Litigation Strategy: An infringer may search the register for abandoned/refused applications of the plaintiff (confidential under S.28) to find prior art, but cannot access them directly. They may have to rely on other sources or seek discovery from the court.
  3. Portfolio Management: A design proprietor must ensure any license or assignment is registered within 6+6 months (S.30) to maintain its validity against the world and its admissibility in court.
  4. Correcting Public Record: A proprietor noticing a typo in their name on the certificate can use S.29. If the error is in the ownership entry itself (e.g., wrong person registered), they must seek rectification under S.31.

D. Case Study: "The Curious Case of the Unregistered Assignment"

Background:

●      Jan 2022: "Studio Elegance" creates and registers Design No. 300000 for an ornamental lighting fixture.

●      Mar 1, 2023: Studio Elegance enters a written Assignment Deed, selling the design copyright to "Lumina Corp." for a lump sum. The deed is executed but no application is filed with the Controller.

●      June 2023: Studio Elegance (acting fraudulently) enters into a License Agreement with "HomeStyles Ltd.", granting them an exclusive license to manufacture lights based on the design. HomeStyles, after a quick register check (which still showed Studio Elegance as proprietor), pays a hefty premium and has the license registered on July 1, 2023.

●      Sep 2023: Lumina Corp. discovers the license to HomeStyles. They file an application for registration of their assignment title on Sep 15, 2023, with a request to condone the 2.5-month delay beyond 6 months.

●      Oct 2023: Lumina Corp. sues HomeStyles for infringement and seeks a declaration of title.

Legal Issues & Questions for Analysis:

  1. As of July 1, 2023, who had a better right in the design – Lumina Corp. (with an unregistered deed) or HomeStyles Ltd. (with a registered license)? Apply S.30(3) & its proviso.
  2. Can the Controller condone the delay by Lumina Corp. and register their assignment? What is the total maximum extendable period under S.30(3)?
  3. In the infringement suit, can Lumina Corp. rely on the Assignment Deed of Mar 1, 2023, as evidence of their title, given it was unregistered at the time of filing the suit? Refer to S.30(5).
  4. What remedy is available to HomeStyles Ltd., assuming they acted in good faith? Can they seek rectification of the register under S.31? On what grounds?
  5. If HomeStyles had discovered the earlier assignment deed during due diligence, what clause should they have insisted on in their license agreement with Studio Elegance?

Suggested Analysis Directions:

●      HomeStyles likely has a superior right as a registered interest holder against an unregistered assignment (S.30(3)). The proviso's retrospective effect works only after entry.

●      The maximum extension is "such further period not exceeding six months in the aggregate." The initial 6 months ended Aug 31, 2023. Filing on Sep 15 is within the total 12-month window (provided Controller allows on application). It might be possible.

●      S.30(5) would bar the admissibility of the deed as proof of title. Lumina would have to get the register rectified first (S.31) or persuade the court under the "unless" clause.

●      HomeStyles, as an "aggrieved person," can apply under S.31 for rectification to remove Lumina's later-registered title, arguing it was entered "without sufficient cause" as their license was earlier and registered.

●      This highlights the critical importance of the "Warranty of Title" clause and potentially an "Indemnity" clause in all IP transactions.

III. Use Cases

Use Case A: The Forgotten Assignment (Section 30)

Scenario: Company A buys a design from an independent designer. They sign a contract and pay the money, but they forget to file an application with the Controller to register the title. A year later, Company B infringes the design.

●      Legal Standing: Under Section 30(5), Company A cannot produce the assignment deed as evidence of their title in court unless the court specifically directs otherwise. Their lawsuit for infringement may fail because the "paper trail" in the official register is incomplete.

Use Case B: The Data Privacy of Abandoned Ideas (Section 28)

Scenario: A tech firm applies for a design for a revolutionary smartphone case but decides to abandon the application because they couldn't find a manufacturer. A competitor tries to use the "Right to Information" (RTI) to see the abandoned blueprints.

●      Outcome: The Controller must refuse. Under Section 28, abandoned or refused applications remain confidential forever to protect the creator's trade secrets.

IV. Case Study: Rectification vs. Cancellation

The Situation: "Solar-X Corp" registered a design for a solar panel. A competitor, "Eco-Flow," realizes that the design was actually created by an employee of Eco-Flow who left and took the files. Eco-Flow applies to the Controller to have Solar-X’s name removed and their own name inserted as the proprietor.

Legal Analysis (Applying Section 31):

  1. Person Aggrieved: Eco-Flow is an "aggrieved person" because their legal rights are affected by the incorrect entry.
  2. Controller's Power: Under Section 31, the Controller can "vary" or "expunge" the entry.
  3. The Overlap Rule: Section 31(5) clarifies that the Controller cannot use "Rectification" as a shortcut to "Cancel" a design under Section 19 (grounds like lack of novelty). Rectification is for title disputes, errors, or omissions, not for challenging the design's inherent validity.
  4. Evidence: The court or Controller will look at the "Instrument" (contract) as per Section 30 to see who the rightful owner is.

Conclusion: If Eco-Flow proves the theft of IP, the Controller will rectify the register. However, if Eco-Flow tried to argue the design shouldn't exist at all because it wasn't "new," they would have to file for Cancellation under Section 19 instead.

40. A person aggrieved by wrong entry seeks cancellation of design registration. Should he use Section 31? a) Yes, Section 31 covers all rectifications b) No, Section 31 excludes Section 19 cancellations c) Yes, with Controller's permission d) Yes, but must approach High Court

Answer: b) No, Section 31 excludes Section 19 cancellations (Section 31(5))

USE CASES

Use Case 1: Assignment Registration Timeline

Scenario: ABC Ltd. executed an assignment deed for Design No. 12345 on March 1, 2024. They filed the application for registration on September 15, 2024.

Issues:

●      Is the assignment valid?

●      What is the effect on third parties?

Analysis:

●      Initial 6-month period: March 1 to September 1, 2024

●      Application filed after 6 months (September 15)

●      Invalid unless extension was sought and granted

●      Section 30(3) requires filing within 6 months or further period up to 6 months aggregate

●      If no extension sought: assignment invalid, cannot be registered

●      Third parties protected as instrument not properly registered

Resolution: ABC Ltd. should have applied for extension before September 1, 2024.

Use Case 2: Conflicting Register Entries

Scenario: The register shows:

●      Design proprietor: X Ltd.

●      Also shows mortgage in favor of Y Bank

●      Also shows license to Z Corp.

W claims he purchased the design from X Ltd. via unregistered deed.

Issues:

●      What are W's rights?

●      Can W enforce his claim?

Analysis:

●      Section 30(5): unregistered document not admitted in evidence

●      Unless court directs otherwise for reasons in writing

●      Register shows X Ltd. as proprietor

●      Y Bank's mortgage interest registered

●      Z Corp's license registered

●      W's purchase unregistered: weak position

●      X Ltd. may have dealt with registered design subject to registered encumbrances

Resolution: W should immediately apply under Section 30 to register his title with proof, may face challenges from Y and Z.

Use Case 3: Abandoned Application Misuse

Scenario: P Ltd. applied for design registration. Application was refused. Competitor Q Ltd. somehow obtained copies of the drawings and representations and wants to use them for market research.

Issues:

●      Can Q Ltd. use these documents?

●      What are P Ltd.'s rights?

Analysis:

●      Section 28: abandoned/refused applications not open to public inspection

●      Not to be published by Controller

●      Protects confidentiality of failed applications

●      Q Ltd. has no legal right to these documents

●      If Q obtained improperly: breach, possible legal action

●      P Ltd. can seek injunction, damages

Resolution: P Ltd. should report to Controller and may pursue legal remedies against Q Ltd.

Use Case 4: Clerical vs. Substantive Error

Scenario: Register entry shows:

●      Proprietor name: "Acme Designs Pvt. Lmt." (should be "Ltd.")

●      Design No.: 9876 (should be 9867)

●      Registration date: Wrong year entered

Issues:

●      Which section applies?

●      What is the procedure?

Analysis:

●      Name spelling ("Lmt." vs "Ltd."): Clerical error - Section 29

●      Design number transposition: Could be clerical (typo) - Section 29

●      Wrong year: May affect rights - possibly Section 31 if substantive

●      Section 29: Written request with fee for clerical errors

●      Section 31: Rectification for more substantive issues

●      If in doubt, Section 31 is broader and safer

Resolution: Apply under Section 29 for clear clerical errors; Section 31 for substantive/doubtful ones.

Use Case 5: Trust Arrangement Challenge

Scenario: A creates a design and assigns it to B to hold in trust for C (minor child). B gets registered as proprietor but wants to note the trust in the register.

Issues:

●      Can trust be noted?

●      What are C's rights?

Analysis:

●      Section 25: Express prohibition on trust notices

●      Controller cannot receive or enter any trust notice

●      B registered as proprietor: has full powers under Section 30(4)

●      C's rights: Equitable interest enforceable as movable property

●      Trust arrangement valid between parties but not recordable

●      Third parties dealing with B protected

Resolution: Trust cannot be noted. C must rely on trust law remedies against B, separate from register.

CASE STUDY

Case Study: MultiTech Industries vs. NeoDesign Corp.

Background: MultiTech Industries (MTI) registered a design for an innovative furniture joint mechanism (Design No. 45678) on January 15, 2023. On June 1, 2023, MTI executed a detailed assignment agreement transferring the design to NeoDesign Corp. (NDC), including all terms and conditions, consideration details, and warranties.

Timeline of Events:

June 1, 2023: Assignment executed June 15, 2023: NDC's legal team reviews the document December 10, 2023: NDC files application for registration of assignment December 20, 2023: Controller rejects application as beyond 6-month period January 5, 2024: NDC applies for extension February 1, 2024: Meanwhile, FlexiFurn Ltd. (FFL) takes a license from MTI (still showing as registered proprietor) and registers it February 15, 2024: NDC discovers FFL's license registration March 1, 2024: Controller grants NDC's extension request

Parallel Development: The register also contained an error showing the design registration date as "15/02/2023" instead of "15/01/2023". Additionally, a report was prepared by the Controller regarding the examination of this design, which a competitor sought access to under RTI.

Questions:

  1. Was NDC's application for assignment registration timely?
  2. What is the validity of FFL's license registered while MTI was still shown as proprietor?
  3. Can NDC challenge FFL's license registration?
  4. What remedy exists for the date error in the register?
  5. Can the competitor access the Controller's examination report?
  6. If NDC's assignment is registered, from what date does it take effect?
  7. What would happen if NDC tried to sue for infringement during the period when assignment was not registered?

Analysis & Solutions:

  1. Timeliness of NDC's Application:

○      Initial period: June 1 to December 1, 2023 (6 months)

○      Application filed December 10: Beyond 6 months

○      Extension application January 5: After rejection

○      Issue: Should have applied for extension BEFORE December 1

○      Section 30(3): Extension must be sought within allowable timeframe

○      Controller's discretion to grant extension on March 1

○      If granted: Application deemed timely

  1. Validity of FFL's License:

○      Registered when MTI shown as proprietor

○      Third party acting on faith of register

○      Register is public record

○      FFL's license prima facie valid

○      Section 30(4): Registered proprietor has power to deal with design

○      FFL protected as bona fide licensee

  1. NDC's Challenge to FFL License:

○      Can apply under Section 31 for rectification

○      Ground: FFL's license entry wrongly made as MTI not true proprietor

○      Must prove: MTI had no right to license at that time

○      Counter-argument: Register showed MTI as proprietor

○      Likely outcome: Courts may protect FFL if acted in good faith

○      NDC may have remedy against MTI for breach of assignment

  1. Remedy for Date Error:

○      Error in registration date: "15/02/2023" vs "15/01/2023"

○      Appears to be clerical/typographical error

○      Section 29: Controller can correct on written request with fee

○      Alternative: Section 31 if deemed more substantive

○      Section 29 is simpler and faster for clear typos

  1. Access to Examination Report:

○      Section 27: Reports to/from Controller not published or open to inspection

○      Exception: Section 45 reports

○      Examination report: Covered by Section 27

○      Competitor cannot access

○      RTI Act may be overridden by specific statutory prohibition

○      Controller must refuse access

  1. Effective Date of Assignment:

○      Section 30(3) proviso: Instrument has effect from date of execution

○      Once registered: Effect from June 1, 2023

○      Retrospective operation

○      But registration is mandatory for validity

  1. Infringement Action with Unregistered Assignment:

○      Section 30(5): Unregistered document not admitted in evidence

○      Unless court directs otherwise

○      NDC would face difficulty proving title

○      Court may refuse to admit assignment document

○      Must show exceptional circumstances for court to allow

○      Strong incentive to register promptly

Key Learnings:

●      Strict compliance with timeframes is essential

●      Extension must be sought proactively

●      Register entries create legal presumptions

●      Third parties are generally protected when acting on register

●      Procedural requirements (writing, fees, forms) are mandatory

●      Different remedies exist for different types of errors

●      Confidentiality of certain documents is absolute
