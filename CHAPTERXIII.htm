Chapter XIII: Register of Patents - Exam Notes

Core Concept: Think of the Register of Patents as the "birth certificate and property deed" for every patent in India. It's an official, public record maintained by the Patent Office that lists who owns what patent and any changes to that ownership.

Section 67: Register of Patents and Particulars

  • What it says: This section mandates the creation of an official register. It lists the specific details that must be recorded for each patent.
  • In Simple Words: This is the rulebook for what information goes into the patent's official file.
  • Key Details to be Entered:
    • Names and addresses of the true owners (proprietors) of the patent.
    • Notifications of assignments (sales) and transmissions (inheritance) of the patent.
    • Details of any licenses granted to others to use the patent.
    • Details of any mortgages (using the patent as security for a loan).
    • Renewal fees paid, and the expiration date of the patent.
  • Why it matters: It creates a single source of truth. Anyone can check this register to see who legally owns a patent and what rights are attached to it.

Use Case Practice:

  • Scenario: TechCorp wants to buy a patent from an inventor, Dr. Sharma. Before paying, what should they do?
  • Action: TechCorp must check the Register of Patents to confirm that Dr. Sharma is indeed the registered proprietor listed there. This protects them from buying a patent from someone who doesn't actually own it.

MCQ for Practice:
1. The Register of Patents, under Section 67, does NOT contain information about:
a) The names and addresses of the patent owners.
b) The complete technical specification of the invention.
c) Notifications of assignments and transmissions of the patent.
d) particulars of licenses granted under the patent.

Answer: b) The complete technical specification of the invention. (The register is for ownership details, not the technical details which are in the patent document itself).

Section 68: Assignments, etc., not valid unless in writing

  • What it says: Any legal transaction for transferring patent rights (like selling it or mortgaging it) is legally void unless it is done through a proper written document and signed by all involved parties.
  • In Simple Words: You can't just do a handshake deal to sell a patent. It must be on paper, like a contract.
  • Why it matters: It prevents fraudulent or disputed verbal agreements and ensures there is a clear legal record of the transaction.

Use Case Practice:

  • Scenario: An inventor verbally agrees to sell his patent to his friend for ₹10 lakhs. He takes the money and gives a verbal okay. Is this legally binding?
  • Action: No. Under Section 68, this agreement is not valid. It must be a written and signed contract to be enforceable in court.

MCQ for Practice:
2. According to Section 68, an assignment of a patent is valid only if:
a) It is registered with the Patent Office.
b) It is made in writing and duly executed.
c) It is notified to the Central Government.
d) It is published in the Patent Office journal.

Answer: b) It is made in writing and duly executed. (Registration comes later; first, the agreement itself must be a valid written contract).

Section 69: Registration of Assignments, Transmissions, etc.

  • What it says: After you have a valid written agreement (as per S.68), you must apply to the Patent Office to record this change in the Register.
  • In Simple Words: Just having a written contract isn't enough for the world to know. You must officially file that contract with the patent office to update the public record.
  • Key Point: If you don't register the transaction, it cannot be used as evidence in court to prove your ownership rights. The registered owner's name in the register will still be considered the true owner.

Use Case Practice:

  • Scenario: Company A buys a patent from Company B with a perfect written contract, but forgets to register it with the Patent Office. Later, Company B fraudulently sells the same patent to Company C, who immediately registers it. Who likely has the stronger legal claim?
  • Action: Company C. Because they registered the transaction first, the register will show them as the owner. Company A would have a very difficult time in court proving their ownership despite having the first contract.

MCQ for Practice:
3. What is the primary legal consequence of failing to register an assignment under Section 69?
a) The assignment becomes automatically illegal.
b) The document shall not be admitted as evidence of title in any court.
c) The patent is immediately deemed revoked.
d) The assignee is liable to pay a heavy penalty.

Answer: b) The document shall not be admitted as evidence of title in any court.

Section 70: Power of Registered Owner to Deal with Patent

  • What it says: Any legal dealing (sale, license, mortgage) by the person whose name is registered as the owner is valid, even if there was a prior unregistered transaction.
  • In Simple Words: The law protects someone who deals in good faith with the person whose name is officially on the register.
  • Why it matters: It gives certainty to commerce. You can trust the public register. If you buy from the registered owner, your purchase is safe, even if it later turns out there was an earlier, unregistered owner.

Use Case Practice:

  • Scenario: (Continuing from above) Company C bought the patent from Company B, who was still the registered owner. Company C did not know about the prior unregistered sale to Company A.
  • Action: Company C's purchase is protected under Section 70. They are now the valid owner because they dealt with the registered proprietor in good faith.

MCQ for Practice:

a) Must investigate the complete history of all previous unregistered agreements.
b) Gets a valid title, even if it conflicts with an earlier unregistered transaction.
c) Is liable if any previous unregistered owner comes forward.
d) Must get a No-Objection Certificate from all previous owners.

Answer: b) Gets a valid title, even if it conflicts with an earlier unregistered transaction.

Section 71: Rectification of Register by High Court

  • What it says: If someone's name is wrongly entered, omitted, or there is an error in the register, any "aggrieved person" can apply to the High Court to fix it.
  • In Simple Words: This is the "correction mechanism." If there's a mistake in the official record, you can ask a high court to order the patent office to correct it.
  • Who can apply? Any person who is affected by the error (the aggrieved person).

Use Case Practice:

  • Scenario: Mr. X proves in court that a patent was wrongfully registered in Mr. Y's name due to fraud. The court rules in Mr. X's favor.
  • Action: Mr. X (the aggrieved person) can now apply to the High Court under Section 71 for an order of "rectification" – to have the register corrected to show his name as the true owner.

MCQ for Practice:

a) Controller of Patents
b) District Court
c) High Court
d) Supreme Court

Answer: c) High Court

Section 72: Register to be Open for Inspection

  • What it says: The Register of Patents must be open for any member of the public to look at during all working hours of the Patent Office. You can also get a certified copy of any entry upon payment of a fee.
  • In Simple Words: The register is not a secret document. Anyone can inspect it, just like a public property record.
  • Why it matters: It ensures transparency. It allows potential buyers, competitors, researchers, or licensees to verify ownership and the legal status of a patent.

Use Case Practice:

  • Scenario: A journalist is writing a story on a large corporation's patent portfolio and wants to verify if they truly own all the patents they claim to.
  • Action: The journalist can walk into the Patent Office (or likely check online) and inspect the register for those patent numbers to see the recorded proprietorship.

Exam Notes: Chapter XIII - Register of Patents

67. Register of Patents and Particulars

This section establishes a Register of Patents . It's a formal record kept by the Indian Patent Office that contains all the essential details about every granted patent. Think of it as the official birth certificate for a patent.

Key things entered in the register:

  • Names and addresses of the patentee (the person who owns the patent).
  • Patent number and the date of filing and grant.
  • Subject matter of the patent (what the invention is).
  • Title of the invention.
  • Any amendments or changes made to the patent.
  • Details of any assignments or licenses related to the patent.

68. Assignments, etc., Must be in Writing

This is a crucial point: any agreement that transfers a patent or an interest in it (like an assignment or a license) is only legally valid if it is in writing and duly executed. This means it must be properly signed by all parties involved. A verbal agreement is not legally enforceable under this Act. This rule prevents disputes and provides a clear record of ownership.

69. Registration of Assignments, etc.

This section requires that any document transferring ownership or an interest in a patent (like an assignment, mortgage, or license) must be registered with the Controller of Patents. If you buy a patent, you have to formally notify the Patent Office and get the change of ownership recorded in the Register. This is a very important step because it legally establishes your claim and helps protect your rights against future disputes.

70. Power of Registered Proprietor

Once a person's name is entered in the Register of Patents as the proprietor (owner), they have the full legal right to deal with that patent. They can sell it, license it to others, or mortgage it. A person whose name isn't in the register cannot legally enforce their rights as the owner. This section reinforces the importance of registration.

71. Rectification of Register by High Court

The High Court has the power to order the rectification (correction) of the Register of Patents. This happens if there is an error or an omission in the entries. For example, if a patentee's name is spelled incorrectly or an assignment was wrongly entered, a person can apply to the High Court to have the record corrected.

72. Register to be Open for Inspection

This section makes the Register of Patents a public record. Any person can inspect it and get a certified copy of an entry. This ensures transparency and allows the public to know who owns which patents and what their status is. It also helps potential licensees or investors verify the ownership details before entering into an agreement.

🔹 Why register?
To make the change publicly known and legally enforceable against third parties.

⚠️ Note:
Failure to register doesn’t cancel the agreement, but you can’t sue someone for infringement unless you're registered.

✅ Section 70: Power of Registered Grantee or Proprietor to Deal with Patent

Simple explanation:
Once someone is the registered patent owner, they can:

  • Sell the patent
  • License it (give others rights to use)
  • Mortgage it (use as security)
  • Transfer it by will or inheritance

👉 They have full legal power to deal with the patent as if they are the true owner — even if there’s a dispute later.

✅ Section 71: Rectification of Register by High Court

Meaning:
If there’s a mistake or wrong entry in the Register (e.g., wrong name of owner), the High Court can order it to be corrected.

  • Any person aggrieved (affected) can apply.
  • The court may direct the Controller to add, remove, or change entries.

Example:
If A’s name is wrongly entered as owner instead of B, B can go to High Court to fix it.

✅ Section 72: Register to be Open for Inspection

What it says:
The Register of Patents is open to the public.

  • Anyone can inspect it (view records)
  • Can get copies of entries
  • Helps in checking ownership, licenses, etc.

👉 Promotes transparency and helps businesses or inventors make informed decisions.

✅ Chapter XIII: Register of Patents – Summary in Simple Language

Section 67 – Register of Patents

  • A Register of Patents is maintained at the Patent Office.
  • It contains:
    • Names and addresses of patent holders.
    • Details of assignments, extensions, revocations.
    • Other important information affecting ownership or validity.
  • No trust details (express or implied) can be recorded.
  • The register can be kept electronically.
  • Certified copies from the register are legal evidence.

Section 68 – Assignments, etc., not valid unless in writing

  • Any assignment, license, or interest in a patent must:
    • Be in writing.
    • Be signed by both parties.
  • Oral agreements are not valid.

Section 69 – Registration of assignments, transmissions, etc.

  • Any change in ownership (assignment, license, transmission) must be registered with the Controller.
  • Until registration, the new owner cannot claim legal rights against third parties.

Section 70 – Power of registered grantee or proprietor

  • The registered owner can:
    • Assign the patent.
    • Grant licenses.
    • Create mortgages or other interests.
  • These rights are subject to the Act and any existing agreements.

Section 71 – Rectification of register by High Court

  • If there is an error or dispute in the register:
    • The High Court can order correction.
  • Example: Wrong name entered as patent owner.

Section 72 – Register to be open for inspection

  • The register is open to the public for inspection.
  • Copies can be obtained on payment of a fee.
