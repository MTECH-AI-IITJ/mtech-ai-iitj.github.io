Exam Notes on Chapter VIII: Grant of Patents and Rights Conferred Thereby

Section 43: Grant of patents

  • Patent is granted by the Controller after the application meets all requirements
  • Grant is published in the Official Journal
  • Patent document includes the specification and drawings
  • Grant signifies the invention meets all patentability criteria (novelty, inventive step, industrial applicability)
  • After grant, patentee gets exclusive rights for the patent term

Section 44: Amendment of patent granted to deceased applicant

  • If applicant dies before patent grant, patent can be granted to their legal representative
  • Legal representative must provide proof of their right to represent the deceased applicant
  • Protects rights of deceased inventor and transfers rights to legal heirs
  • Legal representative assumes all rights and obligations of the deceased applicant

Section 45: Date of patent

  • Date of patent is the date of publication of the application (if eventually granted)
  • This date is crucial for determining patent term
  • Important for establishing priority and determining infringement
  • Earlier date of patent gives priority over later applications for same invention

Section 46: Form, extent and effect of patent

  • Patent document follows specific form prescribed by Patent Rules
  • Patent has effect throughout India
  • Includes specification and drawings
  • Serves as evidence of validity and rights of patentee
  • Functions as public record of the invention and conferred rights

Section 47: Grant of patents to be subject to certain conditions

  • Patents granted subject to conditions protecting public interest
  • Patent cannot be used in ways prejudicial to State interests
  • Government has right to use invention for its purposes
  • Patent subject to all provisions of Patents Act and related rules
  • Balances patent rights with public interest

Section 48: Rights of patentees

  • Exclusive right to prevent others from making, using, selling, or importing patented invention without permission
  • Right to assign patent or grant licenses
  • Right to take legal action against infringers
  • Right to surrender patent if no longer wished to be maintained
  • Rights are territorial (apply only within India)

Section 49: Patent rights not infringed when used on foreign vessels etc., temporarily or accidentally in India

  • Exception to patent infringement for foreign vessels temporarily in Indian waters
  • Applies to patented inventions on foreign vessels, aircraft, or land vehicles
  • Use must be solely for needs of the vessel/vehicle and not for commercial purposes
  • Aligns with international maritime law principles

Section 50: Rights of co-owners of patents

  • Each co-owner entitled to equal share unless otherwise agreed
  • Co-owner can exploit invention for own benefit but must account to other co-owners for profits
  • Co-owner cannot assign or license patent without consent of all co-owners
  • Disputes can be resolved by Controller under Section 51
  • Co-ownership can arise from joint invention or through assignment

Section 51: Power of Controller to give directions to co-owners

  • Controller can give directions to co-owners regarding exploitation if they cannot agree
  • Controller can determine shares of co-owners in patent
  • Can direct co-owner to grant license to another co-owner or third party
  • Controller's directions are binding on all co-owners
  • Ensures invention is exploited for benefit of all co-owners and public

Section 52: Grant of patent to true and first inventor where it has been obtained by another in fraud of him

  • True inventor can apply for patent if someone else obtained it fraudulently
  • Must provide evidence of being true inventor and fraud by original patentee
  • Controller can revoke fraudulent patent and grant it to true inventor
  • Protects rights of true inventor against fraudulent claims
  • Must apply within specified time period

Section 53: Term of patent

  • Term is 20 years from date of filing application
  • Patent must be renewed annually by paying renewal fees
  • Patent lapses if renewal fees not paid
  • Term cannot be extended beyond 20 years
  • After expiration, invention enters public domain

Tricky Use Cases with Answers

Use Case 1: Co-ownership Dispute

Situation: A and B are co-owners of a patent. A wants to license the patent to a third party, but B refuses to consent. A licenses the patent without B's consent and earns substantial profits. B sues A for unauthorized licensing.

Answer: Under Section 50, a co-owner cannot assign or license the patent without consent of all co-owners. A's action is unauthorized. B can seek legal remedies, and the Controller may intervene under Section 51 to give directions regarding exploitation. A must account to B for profits earned from unauthorized licensing.

Use Case 2: Patent Obtained by Fraud

Situation: X files a patent application for an invention actually invented by Y, X's employee. X conceals this fact and obtains the patent. Y discovers this after 3 years and wants the patent granted in his name.

Answer: Under Section 52, if a patent was obtained by fraud of the true inventor, the true inventor can apply for the patent to be granted to them. Y must provide evidence that he is the true inventor and that X obtained the patent fraudulently. The Controller can revoke X's patent and grant it to Y. However, the 3-year delay might be a bar unless Y can justify it.

Use Case 3: Foreign Vessel Exception

Situation: A foreign cruise ship temporarily docks in Mumbai for repairs. During its stay, the ship uses a patented water purification system patented in India. The patentee sues the ship owner for infringement.

Answer: Under Section 49, patent rights are not infringed when used on foreign vessels temporarily in India. The use of the patented system on the foreign ship would not constitute infringement. The patentee's lawsuit would fail under this exception.

Use Case 4: Patent Term and Renewal

Situation: A patent was granted on January 1, 2010. The patentee forgot to pay the renewal fee for 2020. After notification, the patentee paid only on December 31, 2020. The patentee claims the patent is still valid.

Answer: Under Section 53, the patent would have lapsed due to non-payment of renewal fees. Payment after due date doesn't automatically restore the patent. The patentee must apply for restoration within prescribed time and pay renewal fees plus any additional fees as prescribed.

Use Case 5: Government Use of Patented Invention

Situation: A pharmaceutical company holds a patent for a life-saving drug. The government wants to use it for a public health emergency but objects to the high royalty. The government uses the drug without permission.

Answer: Under Section 47, the government has the right to use the invention for its purposes. The government's use for a public health emergency would be covered, but the government must pay adequate remuneration to the patentee. The company cannot prevent government use but is entitled to reasonable compensation.

Tricky Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1: Joint Invention and Co-ownership

Situation: Two researchers, A and B, jointly invent a machine and file a joint patent application. After grant, A manufactures and sells the machine without informing B or sharing profits. B demands a share, but A refuses, claiming greater contribution. B approaches the Controller.

Answer: Under Section 50, each co-owner is entitled to an equal share unless otherwise agreed. A cannot exclude B from profits. Under Section 51, the Controller can direct A to account to B for profits and determine their shares, which would be equal unless they had a different agreement.

Scenario 2: Death of Applicant Before Grant

Situation: An inventor files a patent application and dies before grant. The inventor's son, as legal representative, wants the patent granted in his name. However, the inventor's daughter claims she should also be named as co-owner.

Answer: Under Section 44, the patent can be granted to the legal representative. The son can apply for grant in his name, but the daughter's claim depends on inheritance laws. If she's also a legal heir, she may have a right to a share. The Controller must determine who the legal representatives are and their entitlement proportions.

Scenario 3: Patent Infringement by Government Contractor

Situation: A company holds a patent for specialized construction equipment. The government awards a contract to another company for a public project, and this company uses the patented equipment without permission. The patentee sues for infringement.

Answer: Under Section 47, the government has the right to use inventions for its purposes. The contractor's use for a public project would be considered government use. The lawsuit would fail, but the patentee is entitled to remuneration from the government, with the amount determined by the Controller if necessary.

Scenario 4: Patent Term Calculation

Situation: An inventor files a patent application on January 1, 2010. The application is published on January 1, 2011. The patent is granted on January 1, 2012. The patentee believes the term is 20 years from grant (until 2032), but a competitor claims it expires in 2030.

Answer: Under Section 53, the term is 20 years from filing date (January 1, 2010), so the patent expires on January 1, 2030. The competitor is correct. The date of grant is relevant for determining the patent date under Section 45, but not for calculating the term under Section 53.

Scenario 5: True and First Inventor Claim

Situation: A company files a patent application and obtains a patent. After 5 years, an individual claims he is the true inventor and that the company obtained the patent by fraud. He provides evidence he disclosed the invention to the company's employee in confidence, and the company filed without his knowledge.

Answer: Under Section 52, the individual can apply for the patent to be granted to him if he proves he's the true inventor and that the company obtained the patent fraudulently. The evidence of confidential disclosure supports his claim. The Controller can revoke the company's patent and grant it to the individual, but the 5-year delay might be a bar unless justified.

 

 

 

 

Exam Notes: Chapter VIII - Grant of Patents and Rights Conferred Thereby

This chapter is crucial as it defines the moment a patent is born, its legal form, the rights it grants, and their limitations. Understanding the nuances and exceptions is key to tackling tricky questions.

Section 43: Grant of Patents

  • Concept: The formal act of granting the patent by the Controller.
  • When: After the application has been found to be in order for grant (i.e., all objections overcome, fees paid).
  • Procedure: The patent is granted to the applicant(s), the names are published in the Patent Office Journal, and the patent deed is issued.
  • Key Point: Grant is the culmination of the examination process. Rights commence from the date of grant, but are retrospectively enforceable from the date of publication (u/s 11A).

Section 44: Amendment of patent granted to deceased applicant

  • Concept: A procedural section to correct an error if a patent is granted to a deceased applicant.
  • Action: The Controller can amend the patent by substituting the name of the legal representative of the deceased applicant.
  • Why it's tested: It highlights that a patent is a property that can devolve to legal heirs.

Section 45: Date of Patent

  • Concept: This is one of the most important dates.
  • Rule: The date of patent is the date of filing the complete specification (for a Ordinary/PCT National Phase application). It is not the date of grant.
  • Significance:
    • The term of the patent (20 years) is calculated from this date.
    • It is the priority date for the invention against prior art.

Section 46: Form, Extent and Effect of Patent

  • Form: A patent is granted in the form of a "Patent Deed".
  • Extent: The grant of a patent is effective throughout India.
  • Effect: The patentee gets the exclusive rights to prevent third parties from the acts mentioned in Section 48.

Section 47: Grant of Patents to be Subject to Certain Conditions

  • Concept: These are inherent limitations on the patent rights from the moment of grant. They define what the patentee cannot stop others from doing.
  • Conditions:
    1. Government Use: Any machine, apparatus, or process may be used by or for the government for its own use.
    2. Pharmacy Exception: Any drug/medicine may be imported by the government for its own use or for distribution in its dispensaries/hospitals.
    3. Research Exception: Any person may make or use the invention merely for experiments or research, including instruction of pupils.
    4. Pharmacy Preparation: A pharmacist can make a medicine according to a prescription for an individual. This is a "pharmacy compounding" exception.
  • Exam Focus: This is a hotspot for MCQs and scenarios. You must be able to distinguish between infringing acts and acts permitted under S.47.

Section 48: Rights of Patentees

  • Concept: This defines the positive rights of the patentee – what they can exclude others from doing.
  • For a Product Patent: The right to prevent others from making, using, offering for sale, selling, or importing the patented product in India.
  • For a Process Patent: The right to prevent others from using that process, and also from using, offering for sale, selling, or importing the product obtained directly by that process in India.
  • Key Nuance: The right is a "right to prevent others" (a negative right), not a positive right to use the invention (which may be subject to other laws, e.g., drug regulations).

Section 49: Patent Rights Not Infringed when Used on Foreign Vessels, etc.

  • Concept: An exception to infringement based on principles of international comity and temporary presence.
  • Applies to: Foreign vessels, aircraft, land vehicles, or parts thereof that temporarily or accidentally enter India (including territorial waters/airspace).
  • Condition: The invention must be used exclusively for the needs of the vessel/vehicle, not for manufacturing or commercial purposes within India.

Section 50: Rights of Co-owners of Patents

  • Default Rule (unless agreed otherwise):
    • Each co-owner is entitled to an equal undivided share in the patent.
    • No licensing/assignment: A co-owner cannot license or assign their share without the consent of the other co-owners.
    • Can work themselves: A co-owner can make, use, or sell the patented invention for their own benefit without consent of the others, but cannot grant a license to a third party to do so.
  • Exam Focus: The distinction between what a co-owner can do alone (practice the invention) vs. what requires consent (deal with the legal right itself - license/assign).

Section 51: Power of Controller to Give Directions to Co-owners

  • Concept: If co-owners cannot agree on a matter (e.g., manufacture, sale, licensing), any co-owner can apply to the Controller.
  • Power: The Controller can give directions as they see fit to resolve the dispute.

Section 52: Grant of Patent to True and First Inventor where it has been Obtained by Another in Fraud of Him

  • Concept: A remedy for someone who has been defrauded of their invention.
  • If the Patent Office finds that a patent was obtained by a person who was not the true and first inventor (e.g., by stealing the idea), the Controller can direct:
    1. The patent be revoked.
    2. OR that the application be amended by substituting the name of the true inventor, who becomes the patentee.

Section 53: Term of Patent

  • Rule: The term of every patent is 20 years from the date of filing of the application (i.e., the international filing date for PCT apps, or the complete specification filing date for ordinary applications).
  • + Rule: The patentee must pay renewal fees annually to keep the patent in force. The first renewal fee is due from the 3rd year and must be paid before the expiration of the 2nd year.
  • + Important: For patents granted under the 1911 Act (i.e., "old" patents), the term is 16 years from the date of filing or 7 years from the date of grant, whichever is longer. (This is a common trick).

Tricky Use Cases & Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1: The Research Lab
Dr. Sharma works at a university lab. He reverse-engineers a patented diagnostic device to understand how it works and publishes a paper on his findings. He then builds a copy of the device to use in his future research projects on a different disease.

  • Q: Has Dr. Sharma infringed the patent?
  • A: Partially.
    • Reverse-engineering for research/experiment: This is likely covered under the S.47(3) research exception. Merely understanding it is not infringement.
    • Building a copy for future research: This is a grey area. The exception is for "merely for experiments". If the act of making the device is itself the experiment, it's allowed. If he is making it to have a tool for future, unrelated research, this may cross the line into an unauthorized "making" which is infringement. The purpose and scale are key factors.

Scenario 2: The Co-Owner's Dilemma
Amit and Bimal are co-owners of a patent for a new type of solar panel. Without telling Bimal, Amit starts a company and begins mass manufacturing and selling the patented solar panels. He also grants a license to his friend to manufacture the panels in another state in return for a royalty.

  • Q: Which of Amit's actions are permissible and which are not under the Patents Act?
  • A:
    • Manufacturing and selling himself: This is PERMISSIBLE under S.50. A co-owner can work the invention for their own benefit.
    • Granting a license to his friend: This is NOT PERMISSIBLE under S.50. A co-owner cannot grant a license without the consent of the other co-owner(s). This action infringes Bimal's rights.

Scenario 3: The Government Hospital
The Government of India imports a large batch of a patented anti-cancer drug from Canada. It uses half the batch in its armed forces hospitals and distributes the other half to state-run civil hospitals across the country.

  • Q: Is this importation and distribution an infringement of the patent?
  • A:
    • Import for armed forces: This is NOT INFRINGEMENT under S.47(1) & (2) (government use).
    • Distribution to state-run civil hospitals: This is a trickier, but still NOT INFRINGEMENT. S.47(2) explicitly allows the import of medicine "for distribution in any dispensary, hospital or other medical institution maintained by or on behalf of the government". State-run hospitals would likely fall under this definition.

Scenario 4: The Accidental Tourist
A Japanese tourist visits India on a vacation. She uses her personal, patented medical device (patented in India) daily to manage a chronic health condition.

  • Q: Is her use of the device in India an infringement?
  • A: Yes, it is infringement. S.49 applies to foreign vessels, aircraft, or land vehicles and their parts. It does not apply to personal items brought by individuals. She is "using" a patented product in India without the patentee's authority, which is infringing under S.48. The "temporarily or accidentally" exception is for transport vessels, not people.

 

 

 

 

 

Exam Notes on The Patents Act, 1970, Chapter VIII

Section 43. Grant of patents

  • The Controller of Patents, if satisfied that the application and specification are in order for grant, will seal and grant the patent to the applicant or the true and first inventor.
  • The grant is a formal step after the application process is complete, including examination and opposition proceedings.

Section 44. Amendment of patent granted to deceased applicant

  • If an applicant dies before the patent is granted, the Controller has the power to amend the application and grant the patent to the deceased person's legal representative.

Section 45. Date of patent

  • The date of the patent is the date on which the complete specification was filed.
  • This is a crucial concept as it is the date from which the patent term and rights are calculated, not the date of grant.

Section 46. Form, extent and effect of patent

  • A patent is granted in the prescribed form and confers upon the patentee the exclusive right to make, use, sell, or import the patented invention in India.

Section 47. Grant of patents to be subject to certain conditions

  • This section places limitations on the exclusive rights of the patentee. For instance, the government can import the patented product for its own use.
  • The use of the invention for the purposes of research or instruction is not considered an infringement.

Section 48. Rights of patentees

  • This is a fundamental section outlining the exclusive rights conferred by a patent.
  • For a product patent, the patentee has the exclusive right to prevent others from making, using, offering for sale, selling, or importing that product in India.
  • For a process patent, the patentee has the exclusive right to prevent others from using that process, or using, selling, or importing a product obtained directly by that process.

Section 49. Patent rights not infringed on foreign vessels

  • A patent is not infringed by the use of the patented invention on a foreign vessel, aircraft, or land vehicle that enters India temporarily or accidentally. This is an exception to the infringement rules.

Section 50. Rights of co-owners of patents

  • Unless there is an agreement to the contrary, each co-owner of a patent has an equal, undivided share in the patent.
  • Each co-owner can work the invention for their own benefit and can grant licenses, but cannot assign their share without the consent of the other co-owners.

Section 51. Power of Controller to give directions to co-owners

  • If co-owners cannot agree on how to exercise their rights, they can apply to the Controller for directions.
  • The Controller can make orders regarding the sale of the patent, the grant of licenses, or the division of the proceeds from working the invention.

Section 52. Grant of patent to true and first inventor

  • This section deals with the situation where a patent is obtained by someone who is not the true and first inventor.
  • If a patent is granted in fraud of the true and first inventor, the Controller can revoke the patent and grant a new one to the rightful inventor.

Section 53. Term of patent

  • The term of a patent granted under the Act is 20 years from the date of filing of the complete specification.
  • This term is not extendable.

Tricky Use Cases and Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1: Rights of Co-owners

  • Use Case: A, B, and C jointly invent a new type of solar panel and are granted a patent. A decides to start manufacturing and selling the solar panels without B and C's consent. B and C are upset and want A to share the profits.
  • Analysis: Under Section 50, A, B, and C are co-owners. While a co-owner can work the invention for their own benefit, they cannot assign their share or grant a license without the consent of the others. In this case, A is working the invention, which is within their rights. B and C are not entitled to a share of A's profits unless they had a prior agreement.
  • Resolution: B and C can't force A to share profits, but they can apply to the Controller under Section 51 for directions, especially if A's actions are detrimental to the patent or their rights. They could seek an order to regulate how the invention is worked, or even force a sale of the patent.

Scenario 2: Date of Patent

  • Use Case: An inventor, Mr. Sharma, files a provisional application on January 1, 2024. He files the complete specification on December 31, 2024. The patent is finally granted on July 15, 2026. What is the patent's term, and when does it expire?
  • Analysis: According to Section 45, the date of the patent is the date the complete specification was filed, which is December 31, 2024. As per Section 53, the term of a patent is 20 years from this date.
  • Resolution: The patent will expire on December 31, 2044. The date of grant (July 15, 2026) is irrelevant to the calculation of the patent term.

Scenario 3: Rights of the Patentee and Government Use

  • Use Case: A pharmaceutical company holds a patent for a new life-saving drug. Due to a national health emergency, the government needs a large supply of this drug but the company's price is prohibitively high.
  • Analysis: Section 47(1) states that the grant of a patent is subject to the condition that the government can import the patented article for its own use. This power is independent of any compulsory license provisions. The government does not need the company's permission to import the drug for this purpose.
  • Resolution: The government can proceed with importing the drug for its use in the health emergency without infringing the company's patent rights in India, as this is a specific condition of the patent grant.

  • Process Patent: Right to use process and products directly obtained

  • Use must be for needs of vessel/aircraft/vehicle

  2. Accidental: Or accidental entry

  • Calculated from priority date

Expiry:

  • Automatic expiry after 20 years

- If an applicant dies before grant, the patent may be granted to their legal representative.

- Requires proof of title/relationship.

- The date of filing of the application is deemed the date of the patent.
- This is crucial for determining priority and term (20 years from filing).
- Applies regardless of actual grant date.

- Patent includes: specification, drawings, abstract.
- Extent: covers what is claimed in the claims.
- Effect: confers exclusive rights under Section 48.

- Patent may be granted subject to conditions:
a) Use for public service (e.g., national emergency)
b) Prevent abuse of monopoly
c) Protection of public health or nutrition
d) National security concerns
- These lay foundation for compulsory licensing (Section 84).

Section 48 – Rights of Patentees

- Exclusive right to make, use, sell, offer for sale, import the patented invention.
- Applies only in India.
- For process patents: extends to product directly obtained by that process.

Section 49 – Use on Foreign Vessels, Aircraft, etc.

- No infringement if used temporarily or accidentally in India by foreign vessels, aircraft, land vehicles.
- Must be for their own needs.
- Reciprocity required: country of vessel must give reciprocal protection to Indian patents.

Section 50 – Rights of Co-owners

- Joint applicants become co-owners.
- Each co-owner can exploit the patent without consent of others.
- Cannot license or assign without consent of all co-owners.
- Profit-sharing not automatic unless agreed.

Section 51 – Power of Controller to give directions to co-owners

- Controller may intervene in disputes between co-owners.
- Can direct how proceeds from exploitation should be shared.
- Applies only if there's dispute and request is made.

Section 52 – Grant to true and first inventor defrauded

- If patent obtained by fraud, true inventor can apply to Controller.
- Controller may revoke the patent and re-grant it to the true inventor.
- Requires proof of fraud and prior invention.

Section 53 – Term of Patent

- Term: 20 years from date of filing (provisional or complete).
- Not extendable.
- Applies uniformly to all patents (product/process), including PCT national phase entries.

Exam notes: The Patents Act, 1970 - Chapter VIII 

Section 43: Grant of patents 

  • Conditions for grant: The Controller will grant a patent as expeditiously as possible if the application is in order and has not been refused or found to be in contravention of the Act.
  • Grant formalities: The patent is issued under the seal of the Patent Office and the date of grant is entered into the register.
  • Publication: The Controller publishes the grant in the Official Journal, and the application becomes open for public inspection. 

Section 44: Amendment of patent granted to deceased applicant 

  • Provision: The Controller can amend a granted patent if the grantee died or ceased to exist before the grant, substituting the original grantee's name with the proper person, and the patent is deemed to have always been in effect with the correct name. 

Section 45: Date of patent 

  • Rule: A patent is dated from the application filing date and this is entered into the register.
  • Infringement limitation: Infringement suits are not permitted for acts occurring before the date of the application's publication. 

Section 46: Form, extent, and effect of patent 

  • Form and extent: Patents are in a prescribed form and effective throughout India.
  • Unity of invention: A patent should be for a single invention, though this cannot be a ground for objection in a legal proceeding. 

Section 47: Grant of patents subject to certain conditions 

  • The grant of a patent is subject to conditions related to government use, such as the government importing medicines for its own use or authorizing use of the invention for public purposes. 

Section 48: Rights of patentees 

  • Exclusive rights: Patentees have exclusive rights, subject to other Act provisions, to prevent others from making, using, selling, or importing the patented product or using the patented process (and products derived directly from it) without consent. 

Section 49: Patent rights not infringed when used on foreign vessels, etc. 

  • Using a patented invention on foreign vessels, aircraft, or land vehicles temporarily or accidentally in India is not infringement, provided the home country offers similar reciprocal rights for Indian vehicles. 

Section 50: Rights of co-owners of patents 

  • Co-owners have equal undivided shares unless agreed otherwise. Each co-owner can work the patent independently without accounting to others, but cannot grant a license or assign a share without co-owner consent. 

Section 51: Power of Controller to give directions to co-owners 

  • The Controller can issue directions to resolve disputes between co-owners regarding dealing with the patent, and can authorize a person to act if a co-owner fails to comply, though existing agreements are not overridden. 

Section 52: Grant of patent to true and first inventor 

  • If a patent obtained fraudulently is revoked, the court or appellate board can grant a new patent to the true inventor with the same date and number as the revoked patent. Infringement suits are limited to acts after the new patent is granted. 

Section 53: Term of patent 

  • The term of a patent is twenty years from the application filing date. The patent ceases if renewal fees are not paid, and the subject matter loses protection upon expiry or cessation. 

Tricky MCQs

1. Based on Section 45, which of the following statements is most accurate?
A. The date of the patent is the date of its grant by the Controller.
B. A patent is effective from the date of its publication.
C. A suit for infringement can only be initiated after the date of patent grant.
D. A patent is dated from the date of application filing, but infringement suits cannot target acts before publication. 

Answer: D. A patent is dated as of the date of filing (Section 45(1)), but infringement proceedings are barred for acts committed before the date of publication (Section 45(3)). 

2. A patent for a product is granted jointly to two inventors, A and B, with no agreement to the contrary. B manufactures and sells the patented product for his own benefit. Which of the following is true?
A. B is not allowed to sell the product without A's consent.
B. B must account for his profits to A, as they are co-owners.
C. B can manufacture and sell the product without needing A's consent or sharing profits.
D. B cannot assign his share of the patent without A's consent. 

Answer: C. As per Section 50(2), a co-owner can work the patent for his own benefit without accounting to the other(s) unless there is an agreement to the contrary. However, a license or assignment requires joint consent. 

3. Which of the following best describes the extent of a patent's effect under the Patents Act, 1970?
A. It has effect in the state where the application was filed.
B. It has effect only in India, excluding its territorial waters.
C. It has effect throughout India, including its territorial waters.
D. It has effect in all signatory countries of the Paris Convention. 

Answer: C. Section 46(1) states that every patent "shall have effect throughout India". Section 49(1) implicitly confirms this by clarifying the non-infringement status of foreign vessels "in India (including the territorial waters thereof)". 

4. A patent is granted to Mr. Sharma. Before the grant, however, he had passed away. What is the correct procedure?
A. The patent grant becomes null and void.
B. The Controller can amend the patent by substituting the name of Mr. Sharma's legal representative.
C. The legal representative must file a new patent application.
D. The patent is automatically transferred to Mr. Sharma's next of kin. 

Answer: B. Section 44 allows the Controller to amend the patent by substituting the name of the rightful person to whom the patent ought to have been granted, and it is deemed to have always had that effect.

Tricky use cases with answers

1. Use case: The Foreign Airline 

  • Scenario: A foreign airline lands in India with a component infringing an Indian patent. The airline's home country does not have a reciprocal agreement under Section 49. The patentee sues for infringement.
  • Question: Is the foreign airline liable?
  • Analysis: Section 49 exempts foreign vehicles if their home country offers reciprocity. Lack of reciprocity removes this exemption.
  • Answer: Yes, the airline is likely liable for infringement because the Section 49 exemption does not apply due to the lack of reciprocity. 

2. Use case: The Cooperative Inventors 

  • Scenario: Joint patentees A, B, and C own a patent. B wants to grant a license, but A refuses, while C is indifferent.
  • Question: Can B license the patent without A's consent? What can B do if they disagree?
  • Analysis: A license requires the consent of all co-owners (Section 50(3)). Disputes can be resolved by applying to the Controller (Section 51).
  • Answer: No, B needs A's consent to grant a license. B can apply to the Controller under Section 51(1) for a direction. 

3. Use case: The Forgotten Renewal 

  • Scenario: Mr. Patel's patent, granted in 2010, lapses in 2015 due to non-payment of renewal fees. In 2016, a competitor infringes the patent. Mr. Patel pays fees in 2017 to restore the patent.
  • Question: Can Mr. Patel sue the competitor for infringement in 2016?
  • Analysis: A patent ceases effect if renewal fees are unpaid (Section 53(2)). Even if restored, there is no protection during the lapsed period (Section 53(4)).
  • Answer: No. Mr. Patel cannot sue for infringement that occurred in 2016 because the patent had lapsed and provided no rights during that period. 

Tricky scenarios with answers

1. Scenario: The Pre-Grant Infringement

  • Scenario: A patent application is filed on Jan 1, 2025, published on July 1, 2025, and granted on Dec 1, 2025. A third party, with knowledge of the invention, exploits it from Sept 2025.
  • Question: Can the patentee sue for infringement in Sept 2025? What about before publication?
  • Analysis: Infringement suits are barred before publication (Section 45(3)). However, rights conferred by the grant can be exercised for acts between publication and grant.
  • Answer: The patentee can sue for infringement from July 1, 2025, onwards (after publication) but not for acts before that date. 

2. Scenario: The Wrongful Inventor

  • Scenario: Research assistant A steals an invention from supervisor B, obtains a patent, which is later revoked for fraud. B is then granted a new patent for the same invention. A third party was licensed by A while A's patent was in force.
  • Question: What happens to the third-party licensee?
  • Analysis: B can get a new patent with the same date as the original, but the law protects those who dealt with the previous patentee in good faith (Section 52). Infringement suits by B are limited to acts after the grant of B's new patent (Section 52(3)).
  • Answer: The good-faith licensee is protected and cannot be sued by B for actions taken under A's license. B's rights with the new patent are not retrospective against such parties. 

 

 

 

Exam Notes on Chapter VIII: Grant of Patents and Rights Conferred Thereby (The Patents Act, 1970)

This chapter deals with the process of granting patents, the rights granted to patentees, and related provisions for co-owners, fraud, and term. Below is a section-wise summary with key points, implications, and cross-references for exam preparation. Notes are based on the Patents Act, 1970 (as amended, e.g., by the 2005 amendment for term extension). Focus on procedural aspects, rights, limitations, and interplay with other chapters (e.g., opposition under Chapter V, compulsory licensing under Chapter XVI).

Section 43: Grant of Patents

  • Key Provisions:
    • (1) Upon acceptance of a complete specification (post-examination and opposition resolution under Sections 25-27), the Controller grants the patent if no valid opposition remains or if decided in the applicant's favor.
    • (2) Grant occurs "as expeditiously as possible" after acceptance.
    • (3) The patent is sealed in prescribed form, entered in the register, and notified in the Official Gazette.
  • Implications: Grant is not automatic; it's conditional on compliance. No appeal against grant itself, but revocation possible under Section 64.
  • Cross-References: Links to Section 25 (opposition), Rule 55 (opposition procedure). Note: Pre-grant opposition can delay grant.
  • Exam Tip: Emphasize "acceptance" vs. "grant" – acceptance is under Section 21, grant follows.

Section 44: Amendment of Patent Granted to Deceased Applicant

  • Key Provisions: If the applicant dies before grant, the Controller may amend the patent to substitute the legal representative (heir or executor) upon request.
  • Implications: Ensures continuity; patent rights vest in successors. Request must be made before grant.
  • Cross-References: Ties to Section 20 (substitution of applicants), Rule 60 (procedure for amendment).
  • Exam Tip: Rare but tricky – distinguish from assignment (Section 68) or transmission.

Section 45: Date of Patent

  • Key Provisions:
    • (1) The patent date is the filing date of the application (or priority date for convention/PCT applications).
    • (2) Rights accrue from publication date (Section 11A), but infringement suits only post-grant.
  • Implications: Backdates protection but limits enforcement. For divisional applications, date is parent's filing date.
  • Cross-References: Section 53 (term calculation), Section 11A (publication).
  • Exam Tip: Key for term calculation; remember provisional specs don't confer rights until complete spec filed.

Section 46: Form, Extent, and Effect of Patent

  • Key Provisions:
    • (1) Patent form as per Schedule; grants exclusive rights per Section 48.
    • (2) Territorial to whole of India; no extraterritorial effect.
  • Implications: Patent is a negative right (prevents others); not a positive right to exploit (may need regulatory approvals).
  • Cross-References: Section 48 (rights), Rule 13 (spec form).
  • Exam Tip: Extent limited by claims; effect subject to conditions (e.g., Section 47).

Section 47: Grant of Patents to Be Subject to Certain Conditions

  • Key Provisions: Grant subject to:
    • Government use without royalty (for defense, etc.).
    • Importation by government for public distribution (e.g., medicines).
    • Experimentation or teaching not infringing.
  • Implications: Balances private rights with public interest; precursors to compulsory licensing.
  • Cross-References: Section 100 (government use), Section 107A (Bolar provision for experiments).
  • Exam Tip: Conditions are mandatory; often tested with infringement scenarios.

Section 48: Rights of Patentees

  • Key Provisions:
    • Exclusive right to prevent third parties from making, using, offering for sale, selling, or importing the patented product/process.
    • For products: Direct rights; for processes: Rights over products obtained directly.
    • Subject to other Act provisions (e.g., exhaustion not explicit but implied).
  • Implications: Infringement if unauthorized acts; burden on patentee to prove.
  • Cross-References: Section 107 (defenses), Section 140 (limitations on licenses).
  • Exam Tip: Rights are post-grant; pre-grant publication gives provisional protection (damages from publication date if granted).

Section 49: Patent Rights Not Infringed When Used on Foreign Vessels, etc., Temporarily or Accidentally in India

  • Key Provisions: No infringement if patented invention used on foreign vessels/aircraft/vehicles temporarily/accidentally in India, provided not for commercial import/sale.
  • Implications: Aligns with international conventions (e.g., Paris Convention); transit exception.
  • Cross-References: Section 48 (rights scope).
  • Exam Tip: "Temporarily" key – not for permanent use; often in MCQs with border scenarios.

Section 50: Rights of Co-Owners of Patents

  • Key Provisions:
    • (1) Each co-owner has equal undivided share; can exploit without accounting to others.
    • (2) Assignment/license needs all co-owners' consent.
    • (3) Exclusive license to one co-owner doesn't affect others' rights.
  • Implications: Prevents deadlock but requires consensus for transfers.
  • Cross-References: Section 51 (Controller's intervention), Section 68 (assignment).
  • Exam Tip: Distinguish from joint applicants (Section 7); co-owners can sue independently for infringement.

Section 51: Power of Controller to Give Directions to Co-Owners

  • Key Provisions:
    • (1) If co-owner hinders exploitation, Controller can direct sale/license on terms.
    • (2) Application by any co-owner; hearing required.
  • Implications: Resolves disputes; equitable relief.
  • Cross-References: Section 50 (rights), Rule 78 (procedure).
  • Exam Tip: Conditions for directions: benefit to public/one co-owner; often in dispute scenarios.

Section 52: Grant of Patent to True and First Inventor Where Obtained by Another in Fraud

  • Key Provisions:
    • (1) True inventor can petition Controller to revoke fraudulent patent and grant to them.
    • (2) Time limit: 1 year from grant.
    • (3) Fraudulent grantee loses rights.
  • Implications: Protects true inventors; fraud includes theft of invention.
  • Cross-References: Section 64 (revocation grounds), Section 6 (who may apply).
  • Exam Tip: "Fraud" broadly interpreted; petition like opposition.

Section 53: Term of Patent

  • Key Provisions:
    • (1) 20 years from filing date (or international filing for PCT).
    • (2) Annuities from 3rd year; lapse if not paid.
    • (4) Term extension not allowed (post-TRIPS).
  • Implications: Uniform term; restoration possible under Section 60.
  • Cross-References: Section 45 (date), Rule 80 (renewal fees).
  • Exam Tip: Calculation: For pre-2005 pharma/food patents, was 14 years – but now 20 for all.

  4. Use Case: True inventor discovers fraud 18 months after grant; can they petition? Answer: No, time-barred (1 year under Section 52(2)). Alternative: Revocation under Section 64 on fraud grounds, no time limit.
  5. Use Case: Government imports patented drugs for hospital distribution without notice. Answer: Allowed without royalty (Section 47); not infringement. Patentee cannot sue.

Tricky Scenarios with Answers

These scenarios test edge cases; answers provide reasoning and outcomes.

  1. Scenario: Applicant files on Day 1, publishes on Day 18 months, grants on Day 36 months. Infringement occurs on Day 20 months. Can patentee claim damages from Day 18 months? Answer: Yes, provisional protection from publication (Section 11A(7)), but suit only post-grant (Section 45(2)). Damages retrospective to publication if grant occurs.
  2. Scenario: Deceased applicant's heir requests amendment post-grant, claiming ignorance. Answer: Denied; Section 44 applies pre-grant. Post-grant, use register rectification (Section 69) or transmission, proving entitlement.
  3. Scenario: Patentee sues for process infringement; defendant proves product made by different process abroad. Answer: No infringement if not "directly obtained" (Section 48(b)). Burden shifts if patentee shows identical product (Section 104A reverse burden).
  4. Scenario: Co-owner exploits patent commercially; other demands share of profits. Answer: No accounting required (Section 50(1)); each has independent rights. But if exclusive license granted with consent, terms apply.
  1. Scenario: Patent lapses due to non-payment of annuity in year 19; invention enters public domain. Answer: Term ends at 20 years anyway (Section 53), but lapse accelerates (Section 53(2)). Restoration possible within 18 months (Section 60), but third-party rights protected.
