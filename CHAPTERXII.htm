Exam Notes on Chapter XII: Surrender and Revocation of Patents (Sections 63–66)

Section 63: Surrender of Patents

  • Key Provisions:
    • A patentee may surrender a patent by giving notice to the Controller.
    • The Controller publishes the application, allowing any "person interested" to oppose within 4 months.
    • If no opposition is filed, or if opposition is rejected, the Controller accepts the surrender.
    • The patent ceases to have effect from the date of advertisement of acceptance.
  • Critical Considerations:
    • "Person interested" includes licensees, mortgagees, or anyone with a legal/financial stake.
    • Surrender cannot be partial (e.g., surrendering some claims only).
    • Post-surrender, the invention enters the public domain.

Section 64: Revocation of Patents

  • Grounds for Revocation:
    • Invention not patentable (e.g., lacks novelty, obvious, falls under Sections 3–5).
    • Patent obtained via fraud/misrepresentation.
    • Incomplete disclosure in specification (e.g., insufficient description, unsupported claims).
    • Failure to comply with Section 8 (foreign application details).
    • Secret use in India before the priority date.
    • Patentee not entitled to the patent (e.g., wrongful inventorship).
  • Procedure:
    • Petition filed by "any person interested" or Central Government (via counter-claim in infringement suit).
    • Controller/Court conducts hearings; revocation is published in the Gazette.

Section 65: Revocation/Amendment for Atomic Energy

  • Government’s Power:
    • Central Government may direct revocation/amendment if a patent threatens India’s atomic energy security.
    • Patentee gets a hearing opportunity.
    • Failure to amend as directed leads to revocation.
  • Scope:
    • Applies only to atomic energy-related patents.
    • Government’s decision is final and non-appealable.

Section 66: Revocation in Public Interest

  • Government’s Power:
    • Central Government may revoke a patent if it is "mischievous to the State" or "prejudicial to the public."
    • Patentee must be heard before revocation.
    • Revocation takes effect from Gazette notification date.
  • Examples of Public Interest:
    • Patent blocking access to essential medicines.
    • Patent enabling anti-competitive practices.

Tricky MCQs (with Answers)

  1. Question: A patentee surrenders a patent. An exclusive licensee opposes the surrender, arguing it harms their business. The Controller rejects the opposition. When does the patent cease to exist?
    a) Date of surrender application
    b) Date of opposition rejection
    c) Date of advertisement of acceptance
    d) Date of Controller’s hearing
    Answer: c) Date of advertisement of acceptance (Section 63).
  2. Question: Which ground cannot be used to revoke a patent under Section 64?
    a) Invention anticipated by prior art
    b) Patentee failed to disclose foreign applications
    c) Patent not worked in India
    d) Claims not supported by description
    Answer: c) Patent not worked in India (This is a ground for compulsory licensing under Section 84, not revocation).
  3. Question: The Central Government revokes a patent under Section 66 for being "prejudicial to the public." The patentee claims they were not heard. Is the revocation valid?
    a) Yes, if public interest is proven
    b) No, hearing is mandatory
    c) Yes, if notified in Gazette
    d) No, only Courts can revoke
    Answer: b) No, hearing is mandatory (Section 66 requires opportunity to be heard).
  4. Question: Under Section 65, the Central Government directs amendment of a patent related to atomic energy. The patentee refuses to amend. What happens?
    a) Patent remains valid
    b) Patent is automatically revoked
    c) Controller may revoke the patent
    d) Government files a revocation suit
    Answer: c) Controller may revoke the patent (Section 65).

Tricky Use Cases (with Answers)

  1. Use Case: A patentee surrenders a patent. A licensee (who paid royalties) opposes, citing financial loss. The Controller accepts the surrender. Can the licensee claim damages?
    Answer: Yes. The licensee can sue for breach of contract, as surrender terminates the license agreement.
  2. Use Case: A patent is revoked under Section 64 for "insufficient disclosure." The patentee amends the specification post-revocation. Can the patent be restored?
    Answer: No. Revocation is final; amendments cannot revive a revoked patent.
  3. Use Case: The Central Government revokes a patent under Section 66, claiming it "hinders national security." The patentee proves the invention is unrelated to security. Is revocation valid?
    Answer: Yes. The government’s subjective opinion ("prejudicial to public") is conclusive if procedural rules (hearing, Gazette notification) are followed.

Tricky Scenarios (with Answers)

  1. Scenario: A patent is surrendered, but a third party files an opposition after the 4-month deadline. The Controller accepts the surrender. Can the opposition be considered?
    Answer: No. Opposition must be filed within the prescribed 4 months (Section 63).
  2. Scenario: During a revocation petition under Section 64, the patentee argues the opponent lacks "interest." The opponent is a competitor planning to manufacture the product. Is the opponent "interested"?
    Answer: Yes. A commercial competitor is a "person interested" (Section 64).
  3. Scenario: The Central Government revokes a patent under Section 65 for atomic energy concerns. The patentee claims the invention is for medical devices. Is revocation valid?
    Answer: Yes. Section 65 applies if the patent could relate to atomic energy, even if indirectly. The government’s security assessment is binding.

Exam Notes: Chapter XII - Surrender and Revocation of Patents

This chapter deals with the ways a patent can be ceased or terminated before its full term, either voluntarily by the patentee (surrender) or involuntarily by an order of the Controller or the Appellate Board (revocation).

Section 63: Surrender of Patents

  • Concept: A voluntary act by the patentee to give up their patent rights.
  • Process:
    1. The patentee offers to surrender the patent by notifying the Controller.
    2. The Controller publishes the offer to surrender.
    3. Any person interested (e.g., a licensee, a potential infringer) can oppose the surrender within a prescribed period.
    4. The Controller notifies every opponent and conducts a hearing if necessary.
    5. If satisfied, the Controller advertises the acceptance of the surrender, and the patent is deemed revoked from that date.
  • Key Points:
    • The Controller's role is not to rubber-stamp the surrender but to protect the interests of third parties (like licensees).
    • The patent is only revoked upon the advertisement of acceptance of surrender, not from the date of the offer.
    • A surrendered patent cannot be revived.

Section 64: Revocation of Patents

  • Concept: The compulsory cancellation of a patent by the authority of the Controller (in certain cases) or the Appellate Board. This is the most common ground for challenging a patent's validity.
  • Who can file? Any "person interested" or the Central Government.
  • Where can it be filed?
    • Before the Appellate Board (Intellectual Property Appellate Board - IPAB, now transitioning to Commercial Courts/High Courts).
    • Before the Controller (in a counter-claim in a revocation petition, or in certain specific scenarios like opposition proceedings).
  • Grounds for Revocation (A selection of key grounds):
    • The invention was not novel, involved an inventive step, or was not industrially applicable at the time of the patent application (S. 64(1)(a), (e), (f)).
    • The patent was obtained wrongfully from the true and first inventor (S. 64(1)(b)).
    • The subject of any claim is not an invention under the Act (e.g., a mere discovery, abstract idea) or is non-patentable (e.g., method of agriculture) (S. 64(1)(c)).
    • The invention was secretly used in India before the priority date (S. 64(1)(d)).
    • The complete specification does not disclose or wrongly discloses the source or geographical origin of biological material (S. 64(1)(h)).
    • The complete specification does not sufficiently and clearly describe the invention or the method by which it is to be performed (S. 64(1)(i)).
    • The scope of any claim is not sufficiently and clearly defined (S. 64(1)(j)).
    • The patent was obtained on a false suggestion or representation (S. 64(1)(k)).
    • The invention was used by the Central Government for the purpose of government and the patentee refused to agree to terms for its use (S. 64(1)(m) - links to S. 100).
    • The patentee has failed to comply with a request to use the patent for the public good (S. 64(1)(n)).

Section 65: Revocation for Atomic Energy

  • Concept: A specific and non-negotiable ground for revocation.
  • Trigger: If the Central Government is satisfied that a patent is for an invention relating to atomic energy (as defined in the Atomic Energy Act, 1962).
  • Process: The Controller, on direction from the Central Government, will:
    1. Revoke the patent, OR
    2. Direct the patentee to amend the complete specification to the Government's satisfaction.
  • Key Point: The Controller has no discretion in this matter. It is a mandatory action based on a government directive.

Section 66: Revocation in Public Interest

  • Concept: Revocation of a patent on broad grounds of public interest, not limited to the specific grounds under S. 64.
  • Who initiates? The Central Government.
  • Process:
    1. The Central Government must form an opinion that a patent or the mode in which it is exercised is mischievous to the State or generally prejudicial to the public.
    2. A notice of this opinion is given to the patentee and any other interested parties.
    3. The matter is then referred to the Appellate Board (or relevant High Court).
    4. The Appellate Board decides whether the patent should be revoked.
  • Key Points:
    • This is a rare and extreme power used for matters of national security, public health crises, etc.
    • The decision is made by the Appellate Board, not the Government. The Government only forms an opinion and refers it.

Tricky Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

1. Under which section can a patent be revoked without the patentee having committed any fault, purely based on the nature of the invention?
a) Section 63
b) Section 64
c) Section 65
d) Section 66
Answer: c) Section 65. Relates to atomic energy, where revocation is based on the subject matter, not the patentee's actions.

2. A compulsory licensee of a patent opposes the patentee's offer to surrender the patent. What is the Controller's primary duty?
a) To accept the surrender immediately as it is the patentee's right.
b) To reject the surrender to protect the licensee's interests.
c) To hear the opponent and decide whether accepting the surrender is just.
d) To refer the matter directly to the Appellate Board.
Answer: c) To hear the opponent and decide whether accepting the surrender is just. The Controller acts as a protector of third-party interests.

3. Which of the following is NOT a ground for revocation under Section 64?
a) The invention was obvious and lacked an inventive step.
b) The patentee refused to license the patent to the Government on reasonable terms.
c) The patent is generally prejudicial to the public interest.
d) The specification does not clearly define the scope of the claims.
Answer: c) The patent is generally prejudicial to the public interest. This is the ground for revocation under Section 66, not Section 64.

4. The revocation of a patent under Section 65 is initiated by:
a) Any person interested.
b) The Controller suo moto (on their own motion).
c) A directive from the Central Government.
d) The Appellate Board.
Answer: c) A directive from the Central Government.

5. The final authority to revoke a patent under Section 66 (public interest) is:
a) The Controller of Patents.
b) The Central Government.
c) The Appellate Board (or High Court).
d) The Supreme Court of India.
Answer: c) The Appellate Board (or High Court). The Government forms an opinion, but the judicial body makes the final decision.

Tricky Use Cases & Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1: The Strategic Surrender
Mr. X holds a patent for a pharmaceutical compound. A generic company, GenPharma, has obtained a compulsory license for this drug. Seeing that a competitor, MedCorp, is preparing to challenge the patent's validity in a revocation petition, Mr. X offers to surrender the patent to the Controller to avoid a public finding of invalidity. GenPharma opposes the surrender.

  • Question: Can the Controller accept the surrender? What must they consider?
  • Answer: The Controller cannot accept the surrender without considering GenPharma's opposition. The Controller must hold a hearing. If the surrender is accepted, GenPharma's compulsory license would be extinguished. The Controller must decide if this is just. The potential revocation petition by MedCorp is a separate issue and does not directly affect the surrender proceeding, but the Controller might see the surrender as an attempt to avoid an adverse ruling.

Scenario 2: The Dual Grounds
A patent for a genetically modified seed is revoked by the Appellate Board. The grounds for revocation were that (i) the invention was not novel, and (ii) the patentee wilfully failed to disclose the geographical origin of the biological material used in the invention.

  • Question: Under which section(s) of the Act was this revocation carried out?
  • Answer: The revocation was done under Section 64. Ground (i) falls under S. 64(1)(e) or (f) (lack of novelty/inventive step). Ground (ii) falls specifically under S. 64(1)(h) (non-disclosure of source/geographical origin).

Scenario 3: The National Security Invention
An individual inventor obtains a patent for a new, highly efficient encryption algorithm. The Central Government, after review, states that this algorithm is critical for national security and its public existence in a patent document is "mischievous to the State."

  • Question: What is the correct procedure for the Government to have this patent revoked?
  • Answer: The Government should act under Section 66. It must form an opinion that the patent is mischievous to the State, give notice to the patentee, and refer the case to the Appellate Board for a decision on revocation. It should not use Section 65, as this is not related to atomic energy.

Scenario 4: The "Atomic" Confusion
A scientist invents a new radiation-proof shielding material. The patent is granted. Later, the Department of Atomic Energy determines that this material has direct and primary application in nuclear reactors.

  • Question: What action can the Central Government take, and what is the role of the Controller?
  • Answer: The Central Government can issue a directive to the Controller under Section 65. The Controller is then bound to either (1) revoke the patent or (2) direct the patentee to amend the complete specification to the satisfaction of the Government. The Controller has no discretion to refuse.

Chapter XII: Surrender and Revocation of Patents

Section 63: Surrender of Patents

Surrender of a patent is when the patentee willingly gives up their patent before its term expires. The patentee must offer to surrender the patent to the Controller of Patents by providing a notice in the prescribed manner.

  • The Controller then advertises this offer and gives an opportunity to any interested person to oppose the surrender.
  • This is a proactive measure by the patentee, often due to a realization that the patent is invalid, not commercially viable, or they no longer wish to maintain it.
  • The opposition is to ensure that no one is harmed by the surrender, especially a licensee who may have invested in the patented invention.

Section 64: Revocation of Patents

Revocation of a patent means cancelling the patent. This can be done by either the Controller or the High Court.

  • The grounds for revocation are extensive and include:
    • Prior public knowledge or use: The invention was not new.
    • Lack of inventive step: The invention was obvious to a person skilled in the art.
    • Non-patentable subject matter: The invention relates to a non-patentable area (e.g., a method of agriculture).
    • Wrongful obtainment: The patent was obtained from someone who was not the true and first inventor.
    • Insufficient description: The complete specification does not fully and fairly describe the invention or the method by which it is to be performed.
    • Failure to disclose information: The applicant failed to disclose information related to foreign applications as required under Section 8.
    • Non-compliance with the Biological Diversity Act, 2002: The patentee failed to obtain permission from the National Biodiversity Authority.
  • This is a crucial provision for maintaining the integrity of the patent system by invalidating patents that were granted erroneously or fraudulently.

Section 65: Revocation of Patent or Amendment of Complete Specification on Directions from Government in Cases Relating to Atomic Energy

This section deals with inventions related to atomic energy. If the Central Government believes that a patent relates to atomic energy and its use would be prejudicial to the nation's security, it can direct the Controller to revoke the patent or amend the specification.

  • This is an overriding power given to the government for national security reasons.
  • It is not based on the validity of the patent but on the nature of the invention and its potential use.
  • It ensures that sensitive technology remains under government control.

Section 66: Revocation of Patent in Public Interest

This section empowers the Central Government to revoke a patent if it is found to be "mischievous to the State or generally prejudicial to the public."

  • This is a broad power and is used in exceptional circumstances, such as a patent for a life-saving drug where the patentee is charging an exorbitant price, making it inaccessible to the public.
  • It is different from compulsory licensing (which allows others to produce the invention for a fee) as it leads to the complete revocation of the patent, making the invention free for anyone to use.
  • It is a tool to protect the public interest and ensure that the patent system does not become a tool for exploitation.

Tricky MCQs 🧠

  1. A patentee offers to surrender their patent under Section 63. A licensee who has an exclusive license for the patent but has not yet started commercial production opposes the surrender. What is the Controller most likely to do? A) Reject the opposition because the licensee hasn't started commercial production. B) Allow the surrender but order the patentee to compensate the licensee. C) Reject the surrender, as the licensee's rights would be affected. D) Allow the surrender and cancel the license.

Answer: C) Reject the surrender, as the licensee's rights would be affected. Explanation: The law protects the interests of anyone "interested" in the patent. An exclusive licensee has a significant interest, and the Controller must consider their rights. The surrender would extinguish the license, causing harm to the licensee, irrespective of whether they have commenced production.

  1. A company discovers that a patent granted to its competitor is based on an invention that was already publicly known and used in another country before the filing date. Can they seek revocation of the patent in India? A) No, because public use in another country is not a ground for revocation in India. B) Yes, but only if the public use was documented in a prior art search report. C) Yes, on the ground that the invention was not new. D) No, the patent can only be revoked if it was used in India.

Answer: C) Yes, on the ground that the invention was not new. Explanation: The ground for revocation under Section 64 includes prior knowledge or use "anywhere in the world." Public use or knowledge in another country is sufficient to invalidate a patent in India if it predates the Indian filing.

Tricky Use Cases & Scenarios 🧐

Use Case

A research institution in India discovers a novel method for extracting a rare medicinal plant's essence. A foreign company, having learned of this, files for a patent on the method without disclosing the origin of the plant material. The patent is granted. The Indian institution discovers this and wants to have the patent revoked. What is the most appropriate ground for revocation?

Answer: The most appropriate ground for revocation would be under Section 64(1)(j), which deals with failure to disclose or wrongful obtainment of a patent. Specifically, the foreign company failed to comply with the requirement of Section 6(1) of the Biological Diversity Act, 2002, which mandates prior approval from the National Biodiversity Authority (NBA) for any foreign entity seeking intellectual property rights based on biological resources from India. Additionally, this could also be argued under the broader ground of wrongful obtainment of the patent.

Scenario

A pharmaceutical company holds a patent for a new cancer drug. The drug is highly effective but priced at a level that is unaffordable for the vast majority of the population. The government receives widespread public and political pressure to make the drug accessible. What action can the government take?

Answer: The government can invoke Section 66, which allows for the revocation of a patent in the public interest. If the government determines that the patent is "generally prejudicial to the public" due to the exorbitant pricing and lack of accessibility, it can order the Controller to revoke the patent. This would make the drug formulation freely available for other companies to manufacture and sell at a lower price. This is a more drastic measure than compulsory licensing and is reserved for situations of significant public harm.

Related Questions from The Patent Agent 1970 Examination (Previous Years) 📜

  • Question: What is the fundamental difference between the "revocation" of a patent and the "surrender" of a patent?
    • Answer: Surrender is a voluntary act by the patentee to give up their patent, while revocation is an involuntary act, a cancellation of the patent by a legal authority (Controller or High Court), typically at the request of an interested party.
  • Question: Under what circumstances can the Central Government unilaterally revoke a patent without an application from a third party?
    • Answer: The Central Government can revoke a patent under two specific sections:
      1. Section 65: In cases related to atomic energy where the invention is prejudicial to national security.
      2. Section 66: If the patent is found to be generally prejudicial to the public.
  • Question: A patent is granted for an invention that is a new use for an old process. Can this patent be revoked? If so, on what grounds?
    • Answer: Yes, it can be revoked. The ground would be under Section 64(1)(e), which states that a patent can be revoked if the invention "is not an invention within the meaning of this Act." The Patents Act clarifies that the mere discovery of a new use for a known substance or process is not a patentable invention unless it results in a new product or employs at least one new inventive step. The new use itself is not sufficient.

 

 

 

CHAPTER XII: SURRENDER AND REVOCATION OF PATENTS

Comprehensive Exam Notes for Patent Agent Examination

SECTION 63: SURRENDER OF PATENTS

Key Provisions

Definition: Voluntary relinquishment of patent rights by the patentee before the Controller.

Legal Framework

  1. Who can surrender: Only the patentee (proprietor of the patent)
  2. When: At any time during the term of the patent
  3. Where: Before the Controller
  4. How: By giving notice in prescribed form with reasons

Important Conditions

  • Public Notice: Controller must advertise the proposed surrender
  • Opposition Period: 3 months from publication for objections
  • Hearing: If objections received, hearing must be conducted
  • Discretionary Power: Controller may refuse surrender if not in public interest

Effects of Surrender

  • Patent ceases to have effect from the date surrender is accepted
  • No refund of fees paid
  • Prior acts of infringement remain actionable
  • Licensees' rights terminate (subject to contractual terms)

SECTION 64: REVOCATION OF PATENTS

Grounds for Revocation (Comprehensive List)

  1. Wrongful obtaining of patent (Section 64(1)(a))
  2. Patent obtained on false suggestion or representation (Section 64(1)(b))
  3. Subject matter not patentable (Section 64(1)(c))
  4. Non-disclosure or wrongful mention of inventor (Section 64(1)(d))
  5. Lack of novelty (Section 64(1)(e))
  6. Lack of inventive step (Section 64(1)(f))
  7. Not useful (Section 64(1)(g))
  8. Insufficient description (Section 64(1)(h))
  9. Non-compliance with requirements (Section 64(1)(i))
  10. Fraudulent omission/misrepresentation in application (Section 64(1)(j))
  11. Failure to disclose information under Section 8 (Section 64(1)(k))
  12. Anticipation by prior claiming in India (Section 64(1)(l))
  13. Anticipation by prior publication/use in India (Section 64(1)(m))
  14. Patent obtained wrongfully in contravention of rights (Section 64(1)(n))
  15. Invention known/used in India before priority date (Section 64(1)(o))
  16. Obviousness to person skilled in art (Section 64(1)(p))

Who Can File Revocation Petition

  • Any person: Can file on most grounds
  • Central Government: Can file on any ground
  • Interested person: Must have locus standi

Time Limits

  • General Rule: No time limit for filing revocation petition
  • Exception: For non-disclosure under Section 8 - must be within one year of grant

Procedure

  1. Filing: Petition with grounds and evidence
  2. Notice: To patentee
  3. Counter-statement: By patentee within prescribed time
  4. Evidence: Both parties can file evidence
  5. Hearing: Oral hearing if requested
  6. Decision: Written reasoned order

SECTION 65: REVOCATION IN ATOMIC ENERGY CASES

Special Provisions

  • Government Direction: Central Government can direct Controller to revoke
  • Amendment Option: Controller may amend specification instead of revocation
  • No Appeal: Decision is final
  • National Security: Related to atomic energy matters

Scope

  • Patents relating to atomic energy
  • Matter of national importance
  • Strategic considerations

SECTION 66: REVOCATION IN PUBLIC INTEREST

Grounds

  1. Patent use contrary to public interest
  2. Patent use prejudicial to:
    • State security
    • Public order
    • Public health
    • Morality

Procedure

  • Government Initiative: Only Central Government can invoke
  • Controller's Role: Bound to revoke upon government direction
  • No Appeal: Against government's decision
  • Compensation: May be awarded to patentee

📘 CHAPTER XII: SURRENDER AND REVOCATION OF PATENTS – EXAM NOTES

Section 63: Surrender of Patents

  • Voluntary Surrender: A patentee may surrender a patent by giving notice in writing to the Controller.
  • Publication: The Controller publishes the notice of surrender in the Official Journal.
  • Objections: Any person interested may, within 2 months of publication, file opposition to the surrender.
  • Hearing: If opposition is filed, the Controller shall give both parties a hearing.
  • Effect of Surrender: If accepted, the patent is deemed to have been revoked from the date of publication of the acceptance.
  • No Partial Surrender: The entire patent must be surrendered; partial surrender is not allowed.
  • Not Applicable to Exclusive Licenses: The licensee cannot surrender unless all exclusive licensees agree.

✅ Key Point: Surrender is not automatic—it's subject to public interest and third-party objections.

Section 64: Revocation of Patents

Revocation can occur in the following nine grounds (among others):

  1. Lack of novelty (not new)
  2. Inventive step lacking (obvious)
  3. Not useful
  4. Insufficient disclosure in specification
  5. Wrongful obtainment (e.g., fraud, misrepresentation)
  6. Non-patentable subject matter (e.g., under Section 3 or 4)
  7. Public order or morality (Section 3(b))
  8. Failure to disclose source of biological material (if used)
  9. Amended specification extends beyond original disclosure

Revocation can be sought:

  • Before the Controller (Section 64)
  • Before the High Court in a revocation petition (Section 64 or during infringement suit under Section 107)
  • During counterclaim in an infringement suit

✅ Key Point: Revocation is retrospective—the patent is treated as invalid ab initio (from the beginning).

Section 65: Revocation/Amendment on Government Direction (Atomic Energy)

  • If the Central Government is of opinion that a patent relates to atomic energy (as defined in the Atomic Energy Act, 1962), it may direct the Controller to:
    • Revoke the patent, or
    • Amend the specification
  • The Controller must comply with such direction.
  • No appeal lies against such order.

✅ Key Point: This is a non-justiciable, executive override power for national security/atomic energy concerns.

Section 66: Revocation in Public Interest

  • If the Central Government is of opinion that a patent is mischievous to the state’s interests or contrary to public order, morality, or health, it may direct the Controller to revoke the patent.
  • The Controller shall revoke the patent accordingly.
  • Again, no appeal lies against such order.
  • The patentee is entitled to compensation as per rules.

✅ Key Point: Rarely used, but a draconian power for overriding private rights in national interest.

Exam Notes on Chapter XII: Surrender and Revocation of Patents (Indian Patents Act, 1970)

Chapter XII of the Indian Patents Act, 1970 deals with mechanisms for voluntarily or involuntarily ending patent rights, balancing inventor interests with public and governmental concerns. Below is a structured summary of the key sections, including provisions, procedures, and key implications for exam preparation. Focus on procedural timelines, grounds, parties involved, and intersections with other sections (e.g., opposition under Section 25 or infringement suits).

Section 63: Surrender of Patents

  • Key Provision: A patentee may voluntarily offer to surrender their patent at any time by giving written notice to the Controller of Patents. This is a unilateral act by the patentee, often used when maintenance is uneconomical or to avoid litigation.
  • Procedure:
    1. Patentee submits notice to the Controller.
    2. Controller publishes the offer in the Official Journal for public notice.
    3. Any interested person can oppose the surrender within 2 months (extendable) from publication, on grounds like ongoing infringement or licensing disputes.
    4. If opposed, the Controller hears both parties; if opposition is dismissed or no opposition, the Controller accepts the offer and revokes the patent by order.
  • Implications: Surrender leads to immediate revocation; no refund of fees. No time bar (can be done anytime post-grant). Differs from revocation as it's voluntary and not based on invalidity grounds.
  • Key Exam Points: Link to Section 140 (no surrender if patent is mortgaged without mortgagee consent). Often tested on opposition procedure and differences from compulsory licensing.

Section 64: Revocation of Patents

  • Key Provision: Allows revocation of a granted patent on specific grounds, ensuring only valid patents persist. Revocation can be initiated post-grant.
  • Who Can Initiate:
    • Any interested person (via petition to IPAB/High Court).
    • Central Government (if patent is contrary to public interest or security).
    • Defendant in an infringement suit (via counter-claim in High Court).
  • Grounds for Revocation (Sub-section 1(a) to (p)): Extensive list, including:
    • Invention not new (prior art, anticipation).
    • No inventive step (obvious to skilled person).
    • Not patentable subject matter (e.g., under Section 3).
    • Wrongful obtainment (e.g., from true inventor).
    • Insufficient or unclear description/enablement.
    • Failure to disclose foreign filings (Section 8 violation).
    • Invention not useful, or claims not fairly based on description.
    • Post-grant amendments broadening scope.
  • Procedure:
    • Petition to Appellate Board (IPAB, now High Court post-2021 amendments) or counter-claim in infringement suit.
    • Hearing with evidence; Controller may be involved if Govt directs.
    • No revocation if grounds could have been raised in pre-grant opposition (estoppel principle).
  • Implications: Revocation is retrospective (patent deemed never granted). High Court handles if infringement suit is pending. Often intersects with Section 25 (opposition) and Section 104 (infringement jurisdiction).
  • Key Exam Points: Memorize all 17 grounds; tricky on "interested person" definition (broad, includes competitors). Post-2021, Tribunal Reforms Act shifted IPAB functions to High Courts.

Section 65: Revocation of Patent or Amendment of Complete Specification on Directions from Government in Cases Relating to Atomic Energy

  • Key Provision: Government intervention for national security in atomic energy-related patents.
  • Procedure:
    1. If a patent/application relates to atomic energy (as per Atomic Energy Act, 1962), Central Govt notifies Controller.
    2. Controller directs revocation or amendment of specification/claims to avoid conflict.
    3. Patentee gets opportunity to be heard; if revoked, compensation may be provided.
  • Implications: Overrides patent rights for defense/public safety. Limited scope (only atomic energy inventions).
  • Key Exam Points: Link to Section 4 (no patents for atomic energy inventions). Rarely tested alone; often with govt powers under Chapter XII.

Section 66: Revocation of Patent in Public Interest

  • Key Provision: Central Govt can revoke a patent if it's "mischievous to the State" or "generally prejudicial to the public."
  • Procedure:
    1. Govt issues order after giving patentee hearing opportunity.
    2. Published in Official Gazette; effective immediately.
  • Implications: Broad discretionary power; used sparingly (e.g., for inventions harming environment or public health). No appeal mentioned, but judicial review possible.
  • Key Exam Points: Differs from Section 64 (no specific grounds needed). Historical case: Agracetus cotton patent revoked in 1994 for monopoly concerns.

General Chapter Notes:

  • Revocation vs. Surrender: Involuntary (64-66) vs. voluntary (63).
  • Overlaps: Revocation can follow surrender opposition if invalidity alleged.
  • Amendments: Tribunal Reforms Act, 2021 abolished IPAB; cases now to High Courts.
  • Use tables for grounds in exams; focus on procedural flows.

  • The Central Government can direct revocation or amendment of a patent if it relates to atomic energy, or falls under the Atomic Energy Act, 1962.
  • The Controller must give the patent holder and interested parties an opportunity to be heard before taking action.

Section 66: Revocation in Public Interest

  • If the Central Government finds a patent or its working to be mischievous/harmful to the State or public, it can revoke the patent after hearing the patentee.
  • The decision is published in the Official Gazette, after which the patent stands revoked.

✅ 1. Exam Notes

Section 63 – Surrender of Patents

  • Voluntary action by patentee to give up patent rights.
  • Procedure:
    1. Patentee gives notice to Controller in prescribed manner.
    2. Controller publishes the offer and notifies interested persons.
    3. Any person interested can oppose within prescribed time.
    4. Controller hears parties and, if satisfied, revokes the patent.
  • Key Point: Surrender is not automatic; opposition is allowed.ipindia

Section 64 – Revocation of Patents

  • Grounds for revocation (by High Court or IPAB):
    • Wrongful obtaining.
    • Lack of novelty or inventive step.
    • Non-patentable subject matter.
    • Insufficient disclosure.
    • Fraudulent amendment.
    • Non-working in India.
  • Who can file?
    • Any person interested.
    • Central Government.
    • As a counterclaim in infringement suit.
  • Case Law: Pfizer Sutent Case – revoked for lack of inventive step.lawyersclubindia

Section 65 – Revocation on Government Direction (Atomic Energy)

  • If an invention relates to atomic energy (as per Atomic Energy Act, 1962), the Central Government can direct revocation or amendment.

Section 66 – Revocation in Public Interest

  • Central Government may revoke a patent if it is mischievous to the State or prejudicial to the public.
  • Example: Patents that threaten national security or public health.

✅ 2. Tricky MCQs

Q1. Under Section 63, who can oppose the surrender of a patent?

  • (a) Only the patentee
  • (b) Any person interested
  • (c) Only the Controller
  • (d) No one can oppose
    Answer: (b) Any person interested.

Q2. Which of the following is NOT a ground for revocation under Section 64?

  • (a) Patent obtained wrongfully
  • (b) Patent not worked in India
  • (c) Patent term expired
  • (d) Invention lacks industrial applicability
    Answer: (c) Patent term expired (expiry is not revocation).

Q3. Revocation under Section 66 can be ordered by:

  • (a) Controller
  • (b) High Court
  • (c) Central Government
  • (d) IPAB
    Answer: (c) Central Government.

Q4. A patent relating to atomic energy can be revoked under:

  • (a) Section 63
  • (b) Section 64
  • (c) Section 65
  • (d) Section 66
    Answer: (c) Section 65.

✅ 3. Tricky Use Cases with Answers

Use Case 1:
A patentee wants to surrender a patent, but an exclusive licensee objects.
Answer: Controller must hear both parties before deciding. If opposition is valid, surrender may be refused.

Use Case 2:
A company files a revocation petition after the patent expires. Is it maintainable?
Answer: Yes, if the petitioner qualifies as a “person interested” (e.g., involved in related business). Delhi HC in Macleods Pharma case confirmed this.kankrishme

Use Case 3:
Government finds a patented technology harmful to public health.
Answer: Patent can be revoked under Section 66 in public interest.

✅ 4. Tricky Scenarios with Answers

Scenario:
Company A sues Company B for infringement. B files a counterclaim for revocation under Section 64. Later, B also files a separate revocation petition. Is it allowed?
Answer: Yes, both are independent remedies. Filing a defense under Section 107 does not bar a separate revocation petition.kankrishme

Scenario:
Patent granted to X was wrongfully obtained from Y. Court revokes X’s patent and allows Y to apply. What will be the date of Y’s patent?
Answer: Same as the revoked patent’s date (as per Patent Agent Exam 2024 question).ipindia

Exam notes: Chapter XII: Surrender and Revocation of Patents

Section 63: Surrender of patents 

  • Voluntary Act: This section allows a patentee to voluntarily offer to surrender their patent at any time.
  • Procedure:
    1. The patentee files a notice in the prescribed manner to the Controller of Patents, offering to surrender the patent.
    2. The Controller publishes this offer in the official journal and notifies anyone with an interest recorded in the patent register.
    3. Within a prescribed period after publication, any interested person can give notice to the Controller opposing the surrender. The Controller then notifies the patentee.
    4. The Controller schedules a hearing for the patentee and any opponent who wishes to be heard.
    5. After the hearing and being satisfied that the surrender is proper, the Controller may accept the offer and, by order, revoke the patent. 

Section 64: Revocation of patents 

  • Primary Revocation Section: This is the main section for challenging the validity of a patent. Revocation can be sought by any "person interested," the Central Government, or through a counterclaim in an infringement suit.
  • Grounds for Revocation: The Patents Act lists over 20 grounds. Key grounds include:
    • Lack of novelty or inventive step.
    • The invention is not patentable under Sections 3 or 4.
    • Insufficient description of the invention in the complete specification.
    • The patent was obtained by false representation or fraud.
    • Failure to disclose or wrongly mentioning the source of biological material.
    • The invention was secretly used in India before the priority date.
    • The patentee obtained the patent from a person not entitled to it.
  • Competent Forum: A petition for revocation is filed with the High Court. 

Section 65: Atomic energy inventions 

This section allows the Central Government to direct the Controller to revoke a patent if the invention relates to atomic energy and is not patentable under the Atomic Energy Act, 1962. The government directs the Controller, who notifies the patentee and interested parties, providing an opportunity for a hearing. The Controller may permit the patentee to amend the specification instead of revoking the patent. 

Section 66: Revocation in public interest 

The Central Government can revoke a patent if it deems the patent or its exercise detrimental to the state or public interest. The government must form an opinion that the patent is harmful, give the patentee a hearing, and if not satisfied, declare the patent revoked in the Official Gazette. An example is the revocation of a patent related to a diabetes composition based on traditional knowledge. 

Tricky multiple-choice questions

1. Regarding the surrender of a patent under Section 63, which is correct?
A. The Controller can accept the offer of surrender immediately upon receiving notice from the patentee.
B. Only a "person interested" can oppose the surrender.
C. The Controller is obligated to revoke the patent once the offer is made by the patentee.
D. The patentee can withdraw their offer of surrender at any point before the Controller's final order. 

Answer: B. Only a "person interested" can oppose the surrender. 

2. A patent is granted for a new device used in atomic reactors. Subsequently, the Central Government determines this is an invention for which no patent can be granted under the Atomic Energy Act. Under Section 65, which action can the Controller take?
A. The Controller must revoke the patent without a hearing, as it is a government directive.
B. The Controller can offer the patentee a compulsory license instead of revoking the patent.
C. The Controller may allow the patentee to amend the specification to remove the part related to atomic energy.
D. The patent is automatically deemed void from the date of grant. 

Answer: C. The Controller may allow the patentee to amend the specification instead of revoking the patent, after giving the patentee and other interested parties a hearing. 

3. Company X obtains a patent for a process to create a life-saving medicine. After a few years, it begins charging an exorbitant price, making the drug inaccessible to the public. Which action is most appropriate?
A. An interested person can file for revocation under Section 64 based on a lack of novelty.
B. The Central Government can revoke the patent under Section 66, as its mode of exercise is prejudicial to the public.
C. The Controller must grant a compulsory license immediately, without any further hearing.
D. The patentee can be penalized but the patent cannot be revoked. 

Answer: B. This scenario illustrates a situation where the patent's exercise is prejudicial to the public, allowing government intervention under Section 66. 

Tricky use cases with answers

1. Use Case: Deceptive Surrender

  • Scenario: A patentee facing a potential Section 64 revocation petition offers to surrender the patent under Section 63 to avoid litigation. A competitor who was preparing a revocation petition opposes this.
  • Question: What legal options does the competitor have?
  • Answer: The competitor, as an interested person, can oppose the surrender under Section 63. They can argue for a Section 64 revocation based on their identified grounds, leading the Controller to potentially conduct a validity assessment. 

2. Use Case: Post-Expiry Revocation

  • Scenario: A patent expires, but a competitor wants to use the process and fears infringement lawsuits from the patent's term.
  • Question: Can the competitor still challenge the validity of the expired patent?
  • Answer: Yes, a "person interested" can file for revocation of an expired patent, as clarified by the Delhi High Court. This is because rights like claims for damages survive expiration, making the ability to challenge validity necessary. 

Tricky scenarios with answers

1. Scenario: The Atomic Energy "Red Herring"

  • Scenario: An inventor patents a cooling system with a minor application in atomic energy. The government later directs revocation under Section 65 due to this potential application.
  • Question: Can the inventor challenge this directive, and what is the likely outcome?
  • Answer: The inventor can present their case during the Section 65 hearing. The Controller makes the final decision and can allow the inventor to amend the specification to address the atomic energy aspect, potentially saving the patent for other uses. 

2. Scenario: The Mischievous Monopoly

  • Scenario: A company patents a high-yield seed but uses its monopoly to impose restrictive terms on farmers.
  • Question: Could the government use Section 66, and what would be the process?
  • Answer: Yes, the government can use Section 66 if it deems the patent's exercise prejudicial to the public interest. It would notify the company, provide a hearing, and if needed, declare the patent revoked in the Official Gazette.
