Chapter VIII: Evidence, etc. - Practice Module

Part 1: Tricky & Comprehensive MCQs

Q1. Under Section 37, in proceedings before the Controller, the default mode of giving evidence is:
a) Oral evidence (viva voce)
b) Evidence by way of an affidavit
c) Documentary evidence only
d) As directed by the High Court
Correct Answer: (b). Section 37 states evidence shall be given by affidavit "in the absence of directions by the Controller to the contrary." The Controller has discretion to call for viva voce evidence.

Q2. Section 38 states that a certificate under the hand of the Controller is:
a) Conclusive proof of the facts stated therein.
b) Prima facie evidence of the entry/matter.
c) Not admissible in a court of law.
d) Only valid if countersigned by the Central Government.
Correct Answer: (b). The section uses the term "prima facie evidence," meaning it is acceptable as initial evidence unless proven otherwise.

Q3. As per Section 39, a printed copy of a register entry, certified by the Controller and sealed, is:
a) Admissible in evidence only in the Controller's proceedings.
b) Admissible in all courts and proceedings without production of the original.
c) Admissible only if the original is lost.
d) Not admissible if the opposing party objects.
Correct Answer: (b). The section explicitly states such certified copies "shall be admitted in evidence in all courts in India, and in all proceedings, without further proof or production of the originals."

Q4. Under Section 41, if a person is incapable due to lunacy of making a statement required by the Act, who can act on their behalf?
a) Only the spouse of the person.
b) Only a person appointed by the Controller of Designs.
c) The lawful guardian, committee, manager, or a person appointed by a competent court.
d) No one; the proceeding must wait until the person is capable.
Correct Answer: (c). Section 41(1) provides a hierarchy: first the lawful guardian/committee/manager, and if none, a person appointed by a court with jurisdiction over the person's property.

Q5. Section 42 aims to avoid restrictive conditions in contracts concerning registered designs. Which of the following conditions would likely be VOID under this section?
a) A condition requiring the licensee to use only the spare parts supplied by the licensor for the registered article to keep it in repair.
b) A condition in a licence agreement prohibiting the licensee from manufacturing any article that is an obvious imitation of the registered design.
c) A condition in a sale contract forcing the buyer to also purchase an unrelated product (e.g., raw materials) exclusively from the seller as a precondition for buying the article with the registered design.
d) A condition in a contract prohibiting the lessee from sub-leasing the article bearing the registered design.
Correct Answer: (c). This falls under Section 42(1)(a) - it ties the sale of the registered design article to the acquisition of an unrelated "article other than the article in respect of which a design is registered." Options (a) and (d) are protected under exceptions in Section 42(4). Option (b) relates to protecting the design itself, not a tie-in arrangement.

Q6. According to Section 42(3), in an infringement suit (contravention of S.22), which of the following is a valid defence for the defendant?
a) Proving they were not aware the design was registered.
b) Proving the registered design was not new or original.
c) Proving there was a contract in force containing a condition declared unlawful by Section 42.
d) Proving the plaintiff had impliedly consented to the use.
Correct Answer: (c). This is a specific and often counter-intuitive defence provided in Section 42(3). The existence of an unlawful restrictive condition in a contract can be a defence against an infringement action by the other party to that contract.

Q7. The proviso to Section 42(3) states that the defence under that sub-section will NOT apply if:
a) The defendant is a minor.
b) The plaintiff was not a party to the contract and proves the restrictive condition was inserted without his knowledge and consent.
c) The contract was entered into before the commencement of the Designs Act, 2000.
d) The condition relates to the packaging of the article.
Correct Answer: (b). This proviso protects a plaintiff (e.g., a licensor) who was not involved in a sub-license or subsequent sale contract that contained an unlawful condition inserted without their agreement.

Q8. Section 40 states that any application or notice may be sent by post. This implies:
a) Service is deemed effective only upon acknowledged receipt.
b) Service is deemed effective at the time of posting, subject to provisions of the General Clauses Act, 1897.
c) It is the only permitted mode of service.
d) It is invalid for urgent communications.
Correct Answer: (b). While not explicitly stated in Section 40, the interpretation follows standard legal principles and relevant rules. The General Clauses Act governs service by post unless specified otherwise.

Part 2: Previous Years' Patent Agent Examination Questions (Designs Act Context)

(Note: The Patent Agent Exam focuses on Patents, but principles of evidence and procedure are common. These are illustrative questions based on the "Evidence, etc." chapter's legal concepts.)

  1. In proceedings before the Controller under the Patents Act, 1970, what is the primary mode of giving evidence, and who has the authority to direct otherwise? (Concept from S.37, Designs Act)

○      Ans: The primary mode is by affidavit. The Controller has the authority to direct that evidence be given viva voce (orally), either in lieu of or in addition to affidavit evidence.

  1. A certified copy of an entry in the Register of Patents, sealed by the Patent Office, is produced as evidence in a court. What is its evidentiary value? (Concept from S.38 & S.39, Designs Act)

○      Ans: It is prima facie evidence (S.38 concept) of the entry and is admissible in all courts without the need to produce the original register (S.39 concept). However, the court may require the original if it doubts its authenticity.

  1. A patent license agreement contains a clause that the licensee must purchase all raw materials exclusively from the licensor. Discuss the validity of such a clause under the Patents Act. (Concept parallel to S.42, Designs Act)

○      Ans: While the Patents Act has specific provisions (S.140) against such "tie-in" arrangements, the concept is identical to S.42 of the Designs Act. Such a clause tying the licensed patent to the purchase of an unrelated product (raw materials) is likely to be void as an unlawful restrictive condition.

Part 3: Use Cases

Use Case 1: Evidence in Opposition Proceedings (S.37)

●      Scenario: "A" files an application to cancel the registered design of "B" on grounds of prior publication. "B" contests this.

●      Application: Both parties will submit their primary evidence via affidavits (statements of experts, dated photographs, catalogs). The Controller, finding discrepancies in the affidavits regarding a key exhibit, can invoke his power under S.37 to call the deponents for viva voce cross-examination to test their credibility and the authenticity of the exhibit.

Use Case 2: Proving Registration in Infringement Suit (S.38 & S.39)

●      Scenario: Design holder "X" sues "Y" for infringement in the District Court.

●      Application: To prove ownership and validity of registration, "X's" lawyer does not need to summon the Controller or produce the original Register. They can simply submit a certificate of registration (S.38) and certified extracts from the Designs Register (S.39) issued by the Designs Office. These documents are prima facie evidence, shifting the burden to "Y" to rebut them.

Use Case 3: Dealing with a Licensor's Overreach (S.42)

●      Scenario: Company "Licensor" licenses its registered design for water bottles to Company "Licensee". The license agreement states: "Licensee shall purchase all its plastic granule raw material only from Licensor's sister company, Alpha Polymers."

●      Application: This is a classic tying arrangement. Under S.42(1)(a), this condition is void. Licensee can refuse to comply with this clause. If Licensor sues for breach of contract for buying granule from elsewhere, Licensee can defend by citing S.42. Furthermore, if Licensor sues for design infringement (say, for non-payment of royalty), Licensee can use the existence of this void condition as a defence under S.42(3), provided they were party to this contract.

Use Case 4: Protecting a Vulnerable Rights Holder (S.41)

●      Scenario: Mr. Sharma, a successful designer, becomes mentally incapacitated due to an accident. A rival files for cancellation of his valuable registered design. A response must be filed with the Designs Office.

●      Application: Mr. Sharma's wife can petition the appropriate civil court (having jurisdiction over his property) to be formally appointed to act on his behalf for this specific proceeding. Once appointed by the court order, she can file the necessary statements and affidavits in the cancellation proceeding in the name and on behalf of Mr. Sharma, as permitted by S.41.

Part 4: Case Study

M/s. Elegant Furniture vs. M/s. QuickCopy Interiors & Anr.

Facts:

  1. M/s. Elegant Furniture ("Elegant") owns a registered design for an ornate wooden chair leg (Reg. No. 123456).
  2. Elegant entered into a License Agreement with M/s. QuickCopy Interiors ("QuickCopy") to manufacture chairs using this design.
  3. Clause 7 of the Agreement: "The Licensee (QuickCopy) agrees that for the duration of this license, it shall not purchase upholstery fabric for any of its furniture products from any supplier other than M/s. Premium Fabrics (a nominee of Elegant)."
  4. QuickCopy, finding Premium Fabrics' prices high, started sourcing fabric from another vendor, M/s. Budget Fabrics.
  5. Elegant terminated the license and filed a lawsuit against QuickCopy for:

○      a) Infringement of its registered design (under S.22) for continued manufacture after termination.

○      b) Breach of contract for violating Clause 7.

  1. During the lawsuit, Elegant submitted a certified copy of its Design Register entry and a certificate from the Controller stating the design was valid and in force.

Legal Issues & Questions for Analysis:

  1. What is the evidentiary value of the documents submitted by Elegant to prove its design registration? (Apply S.38/S.39)

○      Analysis: The certified copy of the Register and the Controller's certificate are prima facie evidence of Elegant's ownership and the validity of the registration. QuickCopy now has the burden to prove otherwise, for example, by proving prior publication.

  1. Is Clause 7 of the License Agreement valid? Analyze with reference to Section 42.

○      Analysis: Clause 7 is void under S.42(1)(a). It ties the license for the registered design article (chair) to the acquisition of an unrelated article (upholstery fabric). It restricts QuickCopy's right to acquire fabric from any other person. Therefore, Elegant's claim for breach of contract based on this clause is not maintainable.

  1. Can QuickCopy use Clause 7 as a defence against the infringement claim (part a of the lawsuit)? Apply Section 42(3).

○      Analysis: Yes. S.42(3) provides a specific defence. QuickCopy can argue that at the time of the alleged infringement (contravention of S.22), there was a contract in force (the License Agreement) which contained a condition (Clause 7) declared unlawful by S.42. This would be a valid defence against Elegant's infringement claim.

  1. Would the defence under Issue 3 hold if Elegant proves it never knew about Clause 7? (Apply Proviso to S.42(3))

○      Analysis: This is a nuanced scenario. The proviso states the S.42(3) defence does not apply if the plaintiff (Elegant) is not a party to the contract. Here, Elegant is a party. The proviso typically protects a superior licensor if a sub-licensee inserts such a clause without the head licensor's knowledge. Since Elegant itself drafted the agreement, this proviso is unlikely to save them. The defence would likely stand.

  1. What if the agreement had a clause stating "Licensor reserves the right to supply replacement chair legs to the Licensee"? Would that be valid?

○      Analysis: Yes, likely valid. This falls under the exception in S.42(4)(c), which allows a condition where the licensor reserves the right to supply new parts of the article in respect of which the design is registered (here, the chair leg itself) as may be required to put or keep it in repair. This is not an unrelated product tie-in.

Conclusion:
This case study illustrates how Chapter VIII operates. Sections 38 & 39 facilitate proof of registration. More importantly, Section 42 acts as a shield against anti-competitive practices by design holders. QuickCopy has strong grounds to defeat both claims: the contract claim because Clause 7 is void, and the infringement claim by invoking the statutory defence under S.42(3), turning the unlawful condition in the contract against the party who inserted it.

2. Previous Years Patent Agent Exam Style Questions

Subjective/Descriptive Questions (Based on Chapter VIII):

  1. Question: "The Certificate of the Controller is considered prima facie evidence." Explain the significance of this under Section 38 of the Designs Act, 2000.

○      Model Answer Snippet: A certificate signed by the Controller regarding any entry or matter he is authorized to do serves as initial evidence. This shifts the "burden of proof" to the party challenging the entry. They must prove the certificate is wrong; otherwise, the court accepts the certificate as fact.

  1. Question: Discuss the "Statutory Defence" available to an infringer under Section 42(3).

○      Model Answer Snippet: If a design owner sues for infringement, the defendant can win by proving that the owner has a contract in force (with anyone) that contains unlawful restrictive conditions (like "tie-in" clauses). This acts as a complete defense unless the plaintiff can prove they weren't a party to that contract and didn't know about the clause.

3. Use Cases & Scenarios

Use Case A: The "Tie-in" Trap (Section 42)

Scenario: "Company A" registers a design for a unique coffee machine. They license the design to "Company B" but include a clause stating: "Company B must only purchase coffee beans from Company A's subsidiary."

●      Application: Under Section 42(1)(a), this is a "tie-in" arrangement. The coffee beans are "other than the article in respect of which a design is registered."

●      Result: This clause is void. Furthermore, if Company A tries to sue a third party for infringing their coffee machine design, that third party can use this illegal contract as a defense to escape liability!

Use Case B: The Minor Inventor (Section 41)

Scenario: A 16-year-old child (infant) creates a novel ergonomic chair design.

●      Application: The minor cannot legally sign the declarations required by the Patent Office.

●      Result: Their lawful guardian can sign the application and make statements "in the name and on behalf of" the minor.

4. Case Study: The "Anti-Competitive" Shield

Case: Design-Corp vs. Innovate-Tech

Facts: Design-Corp owns a registered design for a specialized industrial valve. They have a distribution agreement with a third party, "Distro-X." In that agreement, Design-Corp inserted a clause: "Distro-X shall not sell any valves manufactured by Design-Corp’s competitors."

Design-Corp later sues Innovate-Tech for copying their valve design. Innovate-Tech discovers the "Distro-X" agreement and presents it to the court.

Legal Issue: Is Innovate-Tech liable for infringement?

Analysis (per Section 42):

  1. Section 42(1)(b) prohibits conditions that restrict a person from using or selling articles other than the registered design.
  2. However, Section 42(4)(a) provides an exception: It does not affect a condition where a person is prohibited from selling goods other than those of a particular person.
  3. Since the clause was about "not selling competitors' valves" (an exclusive distributorship), it falls under the exception of 42(4)(a).

Conclusion: The clause is lawful. Innovate-Tech cannot use the "Section 42 defense" and will remain liable for infringement.

●      D) Neither A nor B

●      A) Only registration entries

●      B) Only matters done

●      C) Entry, matter, or thing authorized under the Act

●      C) Weak evidence requiring corroboration

USE CASES

Use Case 1: Evidence Procedure (Section 37)

Scenario: Acme Designs opposes Beta Corp's design application, claiming prior use. The Controller schedules a hearing.

Application:

●      Acme submits evidence by affidavit (default mode)

●      Controller finds contradictions and directs viva voce examination

●      Acme's representative testifies orally

●      Controller allows Beta Corp to cross-examine on specific affidavit points

●      Decision based on combined affidavit and oral evidence

Legal Basis: Section 37 - Controller's discretion to modify evidence procedures

Use Case 2: Certified Documents (Sections 38-39)

Scenario: In litigation, the plaintiff needs to prove the date of design registration.

Application:

●      Plaintiff obtains certified copy of register entry from patent office

●      Copy is certified by Controller and sealed with patent office seal

●      Court admits the copy without requiring original register

●      Defendant challenges authenticity

●      Court exercises discretion under proviso to require additional verification

Legal Basis: Sections 38-39 - Prima facie evidence and admissibility of certified copies

Use Case 3: Minor Designer (Section 41)

Scenario: A 14-year-old student creates an innovative furniture design but lacks legal capacity to file application.

Application:

●      Student's natural guardian (parent) files application on behalf of the minor

●      Guardian makes declarations as nearly corresponding to what the student would make

●      Application proceeds in the minor's name with guardian acting on behalf

●      If no guardian exists, court appoints a representative on petition

Legal Basis: Section 41 - Declarations by persons under disability

Use Case 4: Tie-in Licensing (Section 42)

Scenario: Designer licenses his registered bottle design to Beverage Co. with condition that they must buy bottling equipment only from Designer's equipment company.

Application:

●      The tie-in condition (buying unrelated equipment) is void under Section 42(1)(a)

●      If Beverage Co. breaches by using other equipment, Designer sues for infringement

●      Beverage Co. defends under Section 42(3) - unlawful condition was in force

●      Defence succeeds unless Designer proves no knowledge of condition's insertion

Legal Basis: Section 42 - Prohibition of restrictive conditions

Use Case 5: Spare Parts Clause (Section 42)

Scenario: Car manufacturer leases design-registered car body panels with a clause reserving right to supply replacement panels for repairs.

Application:

●      This spare parts clause appears restrictive

●      However, Section 42(4)(c) specifically exempts such conditions

●      Manufacturer can legally reserve right to supply new parts and maintain articles

●      This promotes quality control and safety

Legal Basis: Section 42(4)(c) - Exception for spare parts supply

CASE STUDIES

Case Study 1: The Cross-Examination Dilemma

Facts:

●      Innovate Ltd. applies for design registration for a new smartphone case design

●      Competitor Corp. opposes, submitting affidavit claiming prior public disclosure

●      Affidavit contains vague dates and contradictory statements

●      Innovate Ltd. requests cross-examination of opponent's deponent

Issues:

  1. Can the Controller allow cross-examination on affidavit contents?
  2. Is the Controller bound to allow it on request?
  3. What factors should Controller consider?

Analysis:

●      Section 37 Application: Default is affidavit evidence, but Controller has discretion

●      Controller's Power: Can allow cross-examination when "he thinks it right so to do"

●      Discretionary Nature: Not automatic on request; Controller assesses necessity

●      Factors: Contradictions, vagueness, credibility issues justify cross-examination

Outcome: Controller allows cross-examination due to contradictions. During cross-examination, deponent admits uncertainty about dates. Opposition weakens, and application proceeds to registration.

Learning Points:

●      Affidavit is default but not absolute

●      Controller has broad discretion over evidence procedures

●      Quality of affidavit evidence affects Controller's decision

●      Cross-examination is a tool for testing credibility

Case Study 2: The Disputed Certificate

Facts:

●      In infringement litigation, plaintiff produces Controller's certificate showing design registration on January 15, 2020

●      Defendant claims design was abandoned earlier, challenges certificate

●      Certificate is under Controller's hand per Section 38

●      Defendant presents no contrary evidence initially

Issues:

  1. What is the evidentiary value of the Controller's certificate?
  2. Can defendant challenge it?
  3. What must defendant prove to rebut?

Analysis:

●      Section 38 Application: Certificate is prima facie evidence

●      Rebuttable Presumption: Defendant can challenge with contrary evidence

●      Burden Shift: Initially on defendant to rebut prima facie evidence

●      Standard: Defendant must present credible contrary evidence

Procedural Development:

●      Court initially accepts certificate as prima facie proof

●      Defendant files additional evidence of abandonment proceedings

●      Court requires Controller to produce original records

●      Original records confirm registration validity

Outcome: Certificate upheld. Defendant's challenge fails for lack of credible contrary evidence.

Learning Points:

●      Prima facie evidence starts with presumption of truth

●      Can be rebutted but requires substantive contrary evidence

●      Controller's certificates carry significant weight

●      Challenging party bears burden of proof

Case Study 3: The Orphaned Design

Facts:

●      15-year-old orphan creates innovative textile design

●      No appointed guardian exists

●      Design has commercial potential

●      Orphan lives with uncle who wants to file application

●      Uncle has no legal guardianship

Issues:

  1. Can the uncle file directly?
  2. What procedure should be followed?
  3. Who has standing to petition for appointment?

Analysis:

●      Section 41(1) Application: Minor cannot file independently

●      No Guardian: Uncle is not automatically qualified without legal guardianship

●      Court Appointment Required: Section 41(1) - "if there be none, any person appointed by any court"

●      Section 41(2) Petition: Uncle can petition as person acting on behalf of orphan

Procedure:

  1. Uncle files petition in court with jurisdiction over orphan's property
  2. Court assesses best interests of minor
  3. Court appoints uncle as representative for design application purposes
  4. Uncle files application in minor's name as appointed representative
  5. Application states it's made on behalf of minor per Section 41

Outcome: Court appoints uncle. Design registered in minor's name. Uncle manages until minor attains majority.

Learning Points:

●      Legal guardianship required for acting on behalf of minors

●      Court appointment necessary when no guardian exists

●      Any interested person can petition

●      Statement must correspond as nearly as circumstances permit

Case Study 4: The Tied License

Facts:

●      Designer holds registered design for industrial valve

●      Licenses manufacturing to ValveCo

●      License agreement includes:

○      Clause A: ValveCo must buy raw steel only from Designer's steel company

○      Clause B: ValveCo prohibited from selling valves of other manufacturers

○      Clause C: Designer reserves right to supply replacement parts

Issues:

  1. Which clauses are void under Section 42?
  2. Which clauses are permissible?
  3. What are the consequences if ValveCo buys steel elsewhere?

Analysis:

Clause A (Tie-in for raw materials):

●      Section 42(1)(a) Application: Requires acquiring articles from licensor's nominees

●      Status: VOID - classic tie-in arrangement

●      Rationale: Steel is unrelated to design licence; anti-competitive

Clause B (Exclusive dealing):

●      Section 42(4)(a) Application: Prohibiting sale of goods other than particular person's

●      Status: VALID - explicitly exempted

●      Rationale: Protects brand integrity and exclusive dealing relationships

Clause C (Spare parts):

●      Section 42(4)(c) Application: Right to supply new parts and maintain

●      Status: VALID - explicitly exempted

●      Rationale: Quality control and safety considerations

Scenario Development:

●      ValveCo buys steel from third party (violating Clause A)

●      Designer sues for breach and design infringement (unauthorized manufacture claim)

●      ValveCo defends under Section 42(3) - unlawful condition was in force

Court Analysis:

  1. Identifies Clause A as void restrictive condition
  2. ValveCo's defence under Section 42(3) succeeds
  3. However, Clauses B and C remain valid
  4. Designer's infringement claim fails due to void condition

Outcome:

●      ValveCo can purchase steel from any source

●      Must still comply with Clauses B and C (valid provisions)

●      Designer cannot enforce Clause A or use it for infringement action

●      Contract continues minus the void provision

Learning Points:

●      Not all restrictive conditions are prohibited

●      Section 42(4) provides important exceptions

●      Void conditions provide defence in infringement

●      Severability - void conditions don't void entire contract

●      Anti-competitive tie-ins distinguished from legitimate business restrictions

Case Study 5: The Pre-Commencement Contract

Facts:

●      Design registered in 1999 under old Act

●      Licensing agreement from 1999 contains tie-in condition

●      Designs Act, 2000 commences on May 11, 2001

●      Licensee continues under old agreement with void condition

●      Dispute arises in 2003 about the condition's validity

Issues:

  1. Does Section 42 apply to pre-commencement contracts?
  2. From what date does it apply?
  3. What was the grace period?

Analysis:

●      Section 42(5) Application: Applies to contracts made before commencement

●      Condition: "If restrictive conditions continue in force"

●      Grace Period: One year from commencement

●      Application Date: May 11, 2002 (one year after commencement)

Timeline:

●      1999: Contract with restrictive condition signed

●      May 11, 2001: Act commences

●      May 11, 2001 - May 10, 2002: Grace period

●      May 11, 2002: Restrictive condition becomes void

●      2003: Dispute arises - condition is void

Outcome: Court holds that condition became void on May 11, 2002. Licensee cannot be bound by it in 2003 dispute. Defence under Section 42(3) available.

Learning Points:

●      Transitional provisions apply to old contracts

●      One-year grace period provided for adaptation

●      After grace period, conditions become void

●      Parties should have amended contracts during grace period

●      Section 42 has retrospective application with grace period

PRACTICAL TIPS FOR EXAMINATION

  1. Section 37: Remember the discretionary power of Controller - not bound by affidavits
  2. Section 38: "Prima facie" ≠ "conclusive" - can be rebutted
  3. Section 39: Two requirements for certified copies - certified AND sealed
  4. Section 40: Simple provision - don't over-complicate
  5. Section 41: Priority order: guardian → committee → manager → court appointee
  6. Section 42: Know the three exceptions in Section 42(4) - frequently tested
  7. Section 42(5): One-year grace period for old contracts
  8. Integration: These sections often interact in complex scenarios
