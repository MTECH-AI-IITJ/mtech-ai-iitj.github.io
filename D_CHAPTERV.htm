Chapter V: Legal Proceedings - The Designs Act, 2000

Core Concepts:

●      Section 22: Defines the act of "Piracy of a Registered Design" and prescribes the penalties (recovery of damages, injunction) and exceptions.

●      Section 23: Applies specific provisions of The Patents Act, 1970 (related to legal proceedings, evidence, etc.) to designs, creating procedural harmony.

Section 1: Tricky Comprehensive MCQs

Instructions: Choose the most accurate answer. Assume all designs mentioned are registered unless stated otherwise.

1. "Piracy" under Section 22 of the Designs Act involves:
a) Unauthorized making or applying a registered design to any article.
b) Unauthorized selling or importing for sale an article bearing a registered design.
c) Doing anything described in (a) or (b) with intent to fraudulently use the design.
d) All of the above, but only if done for a class of article in which the design is registered.
Correct Answer: (d). This is the trick. The scope of infringement is limited to the class of articles for which the design is registered. Selling a spoon with a design registered for "utensils" infringes; selling a car with the same design likely does not if "automobiles" is a different class.

2. A key defense against a piracy accusation under S.22(2) is:
a) The defendant was not aware the design was registered.
b) The defendant had obtained a license from a previous owner.
c) The alleged pirated article is fundamentally different in its core function from the registered article.
d) The defendant proves the article was made by a process of "industrial application" not contemplated by the proprietor.
Correct Answer: (c). S.22(2) states it is not piracy if the alleged pirated article is substantially different from the registered design based on the judgment of an informed eye. Option (a) is irrelevant; (b) is a license defense but not under S.22(2); (d) is not a statutory defense.

3. Which provision of the Patents Act is NOT applied to designs via Section 23 of the Designs Act?
a) Provisions regarding powers of the Controller (S.77 of Patents Act).
b) Provisions regarding appeals to the Appellate Board (S.117A of Patents Act).
c) Provisions regarding working of patents and compulsory licenses (Chapter XVI of Patents Act).
d) Provisions regarding evidence before the Controller (S.79 of Patents Act).
Correct Answer: (c). Section 23 applies provisions related to "proceedings in respect of" offences like falsification, secrecy, etc., and powers of the Controller, but not the substantive commercial provisions like working and compulsory licenses.

4. "Mr. X imports table lamps from China that bear a design identical to a registered design for "lighting fixtures" owned by "Designtech Ltd." Mr. X claims he didn't know it was registered. Which of the following is TRUE?
a) Mr. X is not liable as there was no fraudulent intent.
b) Mr. X is liable only if Designtech can prove fraudulent intent.
c) Mr. X is liable for piracy irrespective of intent, as importation for sale is a direct infringing act.
d) Mr. X must apply to the Controller for a compulsory license before being held liable.
Correct Answer: (c). S.22(1) lists acts like importing for sale as piracy. The "fraudulent intent" clause applies to acts like exposing for sale with knowledge, but importation itself is a primary infringing act. Ignorance is not a defense for primary infringement.

5. The limitation period for filing a suit for piracy of a registered design is:
a) 3 years from the date of infringement, as per the Designs Act.
b) 3 years from the date the design ceased to have effect.
c) Governed by the Limitation Act, 1963, typically 3 years from the date of infringement.
d) 5 years from the date of registration, as it is a statutory right.
Correct Answer: (c). The Designs Act does not specify a limitation period. Therefore, the general law, i.e., the Limitation Act, 1963, applies. For recovery of damages for infringement, it is 3 years from the date of infringement (Article 88 of the Limitation Act).

Section 2: Previous Years' Patent Agent Examination Questions (Analyzed)

1. (Previous Year Question - Often repeated in concept)
"Which of the following acts amount to piracy of a registered design?"
(i) Applying a registered design to an article for sale.
(ii) Publishing a registered design in a journal for educational purposes.
(iii) Importing an article bearing a registered design for private use.
(iv) Making an article bearing a registered design for the purpose of trade.

Correct Choices: (i) and (iv).

●      Analysis: (i) and (iv) are directly from S.22(1)(a) & (b). (ii) is not an act for "sale or use in trade," so it's not piracy. (iii) "Importing for private use" is tricky; S.22(1)(c) criminalizes "importing for sale," not for private use. Private use may not constitute piracy under the Act.

2. (From a previous paper)
"The provision of the Patents Act, 1970 relating to ________ are applicable to designs under the Designs Act, 2000."
a) Invention
b) Anticipation
c) Secrecy directions
d) Register of Patents

Correct Answer: (c) Secrecy directions.

●      Analysis: Section 23 of the Designs Act explicitly states that provisions of the Patents Act relating to "secrecy directions," "falsification of entries in the register," "powers of Controller," "evidence before Controller," etc., apply to designs. "Invention" and "Anticipation" are patent-specific concepts.

Section 3: Use Cases

Use Case 1: The E-commerce Seller

●      Scenario: An Amazon seller "StyleHome" sources cushion covers from a local manufacturer. The covers have a unique geometric pattern. "FabDesigns," a registered proprietor of that design for "textile articles, not of clothing," sends a legal notice alleging piracy.

●      Legal Points & Strategy for StyleHome:

1.    First Check: Verify FabDesigns' registration certificate and the "Class" (Locarno Classification). Is it Class 5 (Textiles) or a specific subclass?

2.    Defense Investigation: Is the pattern on the cushion cover substantially different when viewed as a whole by an informed customer? Can prior art (e.g., traditional patterns) be cited?

3.    Procedural Check: Is the suit filed within the limitation period? Did FabDesigns send a cease & desist earlier?

4.    Action: If prima facie infringement exists, negotiate a settlement or license. If a strong defense exists, file a countersuit for groundless threat (concepts from Patents Act applied via S.23).

Use Case 2: The Component Manufacturer

●      Scenario: "AutoParts Ltd." manufactures alloy wheels with a distinctive 5-spoke design for the aftermarket. "LuxuryCars Inc." has a registered design for identical wheels for "motor vehicles and parts thereof."

●      Legal Points:

○      This is a classic case of piracy. The class of articles ("parts of motor vehicles") matches exactly.

○      Exception? S.22(3) provides an exception for parts used for "repair" to restore the whole article to its original appearance. Is AutoParts Ltd. selling to repair a LuxuryCar, or for aesthetic customization? The former may be a defense (Mustang v. Rikhab), the latter is clear infringement.

○      Remedy for LuxuryCars: Can seek damages (calculated on lost profits or royalty) and a permanent injunction.

Section 4: Case Study Analysis

Case Title: Micolube India Ltd. v. Rakesh Kumar Trading as Saurabh Industries & Ors. (Delhi High Court, 2013)

Facts:

  1. Plaintiff (Micolube): Registered proprietor of a design for the shape and configuration of a "Lubricant Can."
  2. Defendant (Saurabh Industries): Allegedly manufacturing and selling lubricant cans deceptively similar to Micolube's registered design.
  3. Defense Raised: The defendant argued that the design was:

○      Not new or original.

○      Functional in nature (the shape was dictated by the function of holding and pouring lubricant).

○      Prior published in various countries.

Legal Issues & Judgment:

●      Issue 1: Validity of Registration (Often a counter-claim in piracy suits): The court held that in a suit for injunction for piracy, the presumption of validity under S.22(3) of the Designs Act applies. The defendant's challenge on grounds of novelty/functionality was a triable issue requiring evidence, but for an interim injunction, the prima facie validity of the registration was upheld.

●      Issue 2: Test for Piracy/Infringement: The court applied the "eye of an instructed observer" test. It compared the registered design and the defendant's can, considering the overall visual appeal, shape, and configuration. It concluded they were substantially similar, leading to a high likelihood of deception.

●      Issue 3: Defense of Functionality: The court distinguished between a design that is solely functional (not protectable) and one that has an aesthetic appeal beyond function. It held that Micolube's can had distinct visual features (proportions, contours, ribbing) that were not purely functional and hence protectable.

●      Decision: The Delhi High Court granted an interim injunction in favor of Micolube, restraining the defendants from manufacturing/selling the infringing cans. It emphasized that piracy undermines the statutory monopoly and causes irreparable harm.

Key Takeaways for Exam:

●      In a piracy suit, the burden initially shifts to the defendant to challenge the design's validity due to the presumption under S.22(3).

●      The "instructed eye" test is the cornerstone for determining substantial similarity.

●      A design with functional elements can still be registered if it has visual appeal that is not solely dictated by function. The "solely functional" argument is a high threshold.

●      Interim relief (injunction) is commonly granted in clear cases of piracy to prevent irreparable damage during the pendency of the suit.

Chapter V: Legal Proceedings (Sections 22 & 23)

Section 22: Piracy of Registered Design

This section defines what constitutes infringement (piracy) and the remedies available to the registered proprietor.

Key Components:

●      Prohibited Acts: During the existence of copyright, it is unlawful to apply the design (or an imitation) to any article in the class of goods in which the design is registered, for the purpose of sale, without consent.

●      Liability: Any person who contravenes this section is liable to pay a sum not exceeding ₹25,000 for every contravention as a contract debt (subject to a maximum of ₹50,000 for one design).

●      Alternative Remedy: The proprietor can elect to bring a suit for the recovery of damages and for an injunction.

●      Defense: The defendant can plead any ground on which the registration of the design may be cancelled under Section 19.

Section 23: Application of Patent Provisions

This section acts as a "bridge," stating that certain provisions of the Patents Act (specifically regarding certificates of validity and the remedy for groundless threats of legal proceedings) apply to designs as well.

Practical Use Cases

Use Case 1: The "Fraudulent Imitation" Scenario

Scenario: A company creates a perfume bottle that is not a literal copy of a registered design but changes 5% of the curve at the base. Legal Application: Under Section 22, "obvious or fraudulent imitation" is prohibited. If the "essential features" and the "eye appeal" remain the same to a consumer, it is piracy.

Use Case 2: The "Export Only" Defense

Scenario: An Indian manufacturer produces goods using a registered design but only exports them to a country where the design is not registered. Legal Application: Section 22 prohibits "applying or causing to be applied" the design within India for the purpose of sale. Even if the sale happens abroad, the act of applying it in India constitutes piracy.

Case Study: Crocs Inc. USA vs. Liberty Shoes Ltd. & Others

Background: Crocs Inc. filed a suit against several Indian manufacturers for infringing their registered design of "clogs" (footwear).

The Legal Twist (Section 22 Defense): The defendants used the defense provided under Section 22(3), arguing that the registration was invalid under Section 19. They proved that the design was published on the Crocs website and was in the public domain before the priority date of the Indian registration.

The Outcome: The Court held that since the design was "published" previously, the registration was not new or original. The suit for infringement failed because the underlying design registration was found to be vulnerable to cancellation.

Lesson for Patent Agents: Always verify the "Prior Publication" status before advising a client to initiate an infringement suit under Section 22, as the defendant will almost always challenge the validity of the design.

📚 TRICKY COMPREHENSIVE MCQs

Question 1: A registered design proprietor discovers that X is selling articles with a design that differs from the registered design only in immaterial details. Which statement is MOST accurate?

A) This is not piracy as the designs are different
 B) This constitutes piracy if the differences don't significantly affect the identity of the design
 C) The proprietor must first prove commercial damage before claiming piracy
 D) X can continue if they developed the design independently

Answer: B
 Explanation: Section 22 covers designs that differ only in immaterial details or features commonly traded. Independent development is not a defense in design piracy.

Question 2: A design is registered in 2020. In 2023, the registration is cancelled due to prior publication. Can the proprietor claim damages for alleged piracy that occurred in 2022?

A) Yes, because the acts occurred when registration was valid
 B) No, because cancellation has retrospective effect from the date of registration
 C) Yes, but only for the period before the cancellation order
 D) No, unless the proprietor can prove they were unaware of the prior publication

Answer: B
 Explanation: When a design registration is cancelled, it is treated as if it never existed. The proprietor cannot claim piracy for acts during the period when registration was wrongfully maintained.

Question 3: P holds a registered design for a chair. Q manufactures chairs with P's design but only for export to countries where P has no design protection. Under Indian law:

A) Q is liable for piracy under Section 22
 B) Q is not liable as the chairs are not sold in India
 C) Q is liable only if the exported goods re-enter India
 D) Q is liable only for manufacturing, not exporting

Answer: A
 Explanation: Section 22 prohibits applying a registered design to articles in India without consent. Manufacturing in India, even for export, constitutes piracy.

Question 4: Which of the following is NOT a recognized defense against piracy under Section 22?

A) The design was not validly registered
 B) The defendant independently created an identical design
 C) The alleged piracy occurred before the design was registered
 D) The registration has expired

Answer: B
 Explanation: Independent creation is not a defense in design law, unlike copyright. Design rights are monopolistic, not based on copying.

Question 5: A retailer unknowingly sells articles bearing a pirated design. The retailer purchased these articles from a wholesaler in good faith. Is the retailer liable for piracy?

A) No, because they were an innocent infringer
 B) Yes, but damages may be limited or denied
 C) No, only the manufacturer is liable
 D) Yes, and must pay full damages regardless of knowledge

Answer: B
 Explanation: While selling pirated designs constitutes piracy, courts may refuse or limit damages against innocent infringers who had no reasonable grounds to suspect piracy.

Question 6: P's design registration for a decorative tile pattern is valid. Q creates tiles where the pattern is reversed (mirror image). This is:

A) Not piracy as it's a different design
 B) Likely piracy if it doesn't alter the overall impression
 C) Permitted as a variation
 D) Only piracy if Q deliberately copied P's design

Answer: B
 Explanation: Mere reversal or rotation typically constitutes an immaterial detail that doesn't prevent a finding of piracy if the overall design identity is maintained.

Question 7: Under Section 22, the burden of proof regarding fraudulent imitation lies on:

A) The registered proprietor at all times
 B) The defendant once the proprietor establishes prima facie similarity
 C) The registered proprietor throughout the proceedings
 D) The court to determine independently

Answer: C
 Explanation: The proprietor must prove that the defendant's design is a fraudulent or obvious imitation. However, once registered, the design is presumed valid unless challenged.

Question 8: A design registered for "textile fabrics" is applied by an unauthorized person to wallpaper. This is:

A) Not piracy as wallpaper is a different class of article
 B) Piracy if wallpaper falls within the registered class
 C) Not piracy unless specifically registered for wallpaper
 D) Piracy regardless of the class of article

Answer: B
 Explanation: The class of article matters. If the registration covers the class to which wallpaper belongs, it's piracy. Design protection is article-specific.

Question 9: Which remedy is NOT available under Section 22 for piracy of a registered design?

A) Injunction to prevent further piracy
 B) Damages or account of profits
 C) Criminal prosecution of the infringer
 D) Delivery up or destruction of infringing articles

Answer: C
 Explanation: Design piracy under Section 22 is a civil wrong, not a criminal offense. Remedies are civil in nature.

Question 10: P discovers Q's piracy in January 2023 but files suit in January 2026. The design was registered in 2020. What is the likely outcome regarding limitation?

A) The suit is barred by limitation
 B) The suit is timely filed
 C) Limitation runs from registration date, so the suit is barred
 D) There is no limitation period for design piracy

Answer: A
 Explanation: The Limitation Act applies. Generally, suits for infringement must be filed within 3 years from when the cause of action arose (discovery of piracy).

Question 11: An article bearing a pirated design is imported into India by Z, who is unaware of the design registration. Z is:

A) Not liable as importation is not covered under Section 22
 B) Liable for piracy as importation is an infringing act
 C) Liable only if Z subsequently sells the articles in India
 D) Not liable due to lack of knowledge

Answer: B
 Explanation: Importing articles bearing a pirated design into India without consent constitutes piracy, regardless of knowledge.

Question 12: In a piracy suit, the defendant proves that the registered design lacks novelty. The most likely outcome is:

A) The suit continues but damages are reduced
 B) The registration is vulnerable to cancellation, affecting the suit
 C) The piracy claim is unaffected as validity is a separate issue
 D) The defendant must file a separate cancellation petition

Answer: B
 Explanation: While validity and infringement are theoretically separate, proving lack of novelty can lead to cancellation proceedings, which retrospectively invalidates the registration.

Question 13: Two parties independently create identical designs. Party A registers first. Party B, unaware of A's registration, continues manufacturing. Party B is:

A) Not liable due to independent creation
 B) Liable for piracy despite independent creation
 C) Liable only from the date they became aware of A's registration
 D) Not liable if they can prove prior use

Answer: B
 Explanation: Design rights are absolute. Independent creation is not a defense, though prior use might be grounds for challenging validity.

Question 14: A registered design proprietor licenses the design to L with territorial restrictions. L violates these restrictions. This is:

A) Piracy under Section 22
 B) A contractual breach but not piracy
 C) Piracy only if L exceeds the permitted quantity
 D) Not actionable under the Designs Act

Answer: B
 Explanation: Exceeding license terms is a breach of contract, not piracy under Section 22, as L has authorized use within certain bounds.

Question 15: For "fraudulent imitation" under Section 22, what must be proven?

A) Actual copying from the registered design
 B) Intent to deceive purchasers
 C) Obvious imitation that would likely deceive
 D) All of the above

Answer: C
 Explanation: "Fraudulent" in this context means obvious imitation likely to deceive, not necessarily requiring proof of dishonest intent or actual copying.

 B) Compulsory licensing
 C) Powers of the court
 D) Relief in infringement suits

 Explanation: Section 23 applies specific provisions like suit procedures and reliefs, but compulsory licensing is not extended to designs.

Question 17: Under Section 23, which Patents Act provision regarding jurisdiction applies to design piracy suits?

 B) Suits can be filed where the defendant resides or works for gain
 C) Suits must be filed in Delhi High Court only

 Explanation: Section 104 of the Patents Act (applied via Section 23) allows suits where the defendant resides, carries on business, or the cause of action arose.

Question 18: The Patents Act provision on "relief in suits for infringement" is applied to designs. Which is a recognized form of relief?

A) Mandatory licensing to competitors
 B) Account of profits or damages
 C) Cancellation of the defendant's business license
 D) Criminal imprisonment

Answer: B
 Explanation: Civil remedies like damages, account of profits, injunctions, and delivery up are available, similar to patent infringement.

Question 19: When Section 23 applies Patents Act provisions on "certificate of contested validity," it means:

A) A design's validity is automatically certified after registration
 B) If validity is successfully defended in court, a certificate may be issued
 C) The Controller must certify validity before suit can be filed
 D) Certificates have no practical significance

Answer: B
 Explanation: Similar to patents, if a design's validity is contested and upheld, a certificate may issue, potentially affecting costs in future litigation.

Question 20: Under the applied Patents Act provisions, can a district court hear design piracy suits?

A) Yes, concurrent jurisdiction with High Courts
 B) No, only High Courts have jurisdiction
 C) Yes, but only for damages below a specified amount
 D) Yes, if both parties consent

Answer: B
 Explanation: Design piracy suits, like patent suits, are within the exclusive jurisdiction of High Courts, not district courts.

Question 21: The Patents Act provision on "relief for infringement of partially valid registration" applies to designs. This means:

A) If part of a design is invalid, no relief can be granted
 B) Relief may still be granted for the valid part if severable
 C) The entire registration must be cancelled
 D) The proprietor must amend before seeking relief

Answer: B
 Explanation: If a design registration is partially invalid but the valid portion is distinct and was independently registrable, relief can still be granted for infringement of the valid part.

Question 22: Which Patents Act provision regarding limitation periods is applied to designs?

A) 1 year from registration
 B) 3 years from infringement
 C) No limitation period
 D) The Limitation Act, 1963 applies as per general law

Answer: D
 Explanation: The Designs Act doesn't specify limitation periods, so the general Limitation Act applies (typically 3 years from when the cause of action arose).

Question 23: Under applied Patents Act provisions, an alleged pirate can challenge design validity:

A) Only through a separate cancellation petition
 B) As a defense in an infringement suit
 C) Only before responding to the suit
 D) Not at all; validity is conclusive upon registration

Answer: B
 Explanation: Like in patent litigation, validity can be challenged as a defense in infringement proceedings, though separate cancellation petitions are also possible.

Question 24: The Patents Act provision on "restriction on recovery of damages for infringement" applies when:

A) The defendant was an innocent infringer
 B) The proprietor failed to mark articles with the registration number
 C) The design was not novel
 D) The infringement was minor

Answer: A
 Explanation: Damages may be refused or limited for innocent infringers who had no reasonable means of knowing about the registration.

Question 25: Under Section 23, the power to grant an injunction in design piracy cases:

A) Is mandatory upon proof of infringement
 B) Is discretionary with the court
 C) Requires proof of irreparable harm
 D) Is only available as an interlocutory measure

Answer: B
 Explanation: Like patents, injunctive relief is discretionary, though proof of infringement creates a strong case for it. Courts consider balance of convenience and other factors.

Question 26: Can a party appeal against a decision in a design piracy suit?

A) No, High Court decisions are final
 B) Yes, to the Supreme Court
 C) Yes, but only on questions of law
 D) Yes, to the Controller

Answer: B
 Explanation: Appeals from High Court decisions in design matters lie to the Supreme Court, subject to standard appellate procedures.

Question 27: The Patents Act provision on "power of court to make orders as to inspection" when applied to designs means:

A) Courts cannot order inspection of manufacturing processes
 B) Courts can order defendants to allow inspection of allegedly infringing articles
 C) Only the Controller can order inspections
 D) Inspection orders violate trade secret rights

Answer: B
 Explanation: Courts have discretion to order inspection of articles, documents, or processes to determine whether piracy has occurred.

Question 28: Under applied provisions, when can a court refuse damages despite finding piracy?

 B) When the infringement was innocent and the defendant had no reasonable grounds for knowledge
 C) When the design is more than 5 years old
 D) Never; damages are mandatory upon finding piracy

Answer: B
 Explanation: Courts may deny damages to innocent infringers but typically grant injunctions to prevent future infringement.

Question 29: Section 23 applies Patents Act provisions on "costs." Generally, in design litigation:

 B) Each party bears their own costs
 C) Costs are in the court's discretion based on conduct and outcome
 D) Costs are capped at 10% of damages awarded

 Explanation: Courts have discretion on costs, considering factors like conduct of parties, whether validity was contested successfully, and reasonableness of the suit.

Question 30: Under Section 23, can a design proprietor claim both damages and account of profits?

 B) No, the proprietor must elect one remedy

🎯 PREVIOUS YEARS' PATENT AGENT EXAMINATION QUESTIONS (Adapted for Designs)

Question 31 (2019 - Adapted):

A design is registered for "bottles" in Class 09-01. The defendant manufactures jars with the same design. Is this piracy?

Model Answer:
 It depends on whether jars fall within the same class or are considered sufficiently similar articles. Design protection is article-specific. If jars are in a different class and considered distinct articles, this may not be piracy unless the registration was broad enough. The test is whether the articles belong to the same "set or class."

Question 32 (2020 - Adapted):

Explain "fraudulent imitation" under Section 22 and distinguish it from mere similarity.

Model Answer:
 "Fraudulent imitation" under Section 22 means an obvious imitation of a registered design, even if not an exact copy, such that it would likely deceive observers into believing it's the original design. It doesn't require proof of dishonest intent but requires more than coincidental similarity. Mere similarity in functional features or differences in immaterial details doesn't negate fraudulent imitation if the overall design identity is maintained.

Question 33 (2021 - Adapted):

What are the remedies available to a registered design proprietor under the Designs Act for piracy?

Model Answer:
 Under Sections 22 and 23 (applying Patents Act provisions), remedies include:

  1. Injunction - temporary or permanent
  2. Damages - compensation for loss suffered
  3. Account of profits - defendant's gains (alternative to damages)
  4. Delivery up or destruction of infringing articles
  5. Declaration of validity and infringement
  6. Costs as per court's discretion

Criminal remedies are not available for design piracy.

Question 34 (2018 - Adapted):

Can a defendant in a piracy suit challenge the validity of the design registration? Under which provision?

Model Answer:
 Yes. Under Section 23, which applies provisions of the Patents Act, a defendant can challenge the validity of a design registration as a defense in an infringement suit. Grounds include lack of novelty, prior publication, or that the design isn't registrable. The defendant may also file a separate cancellation petition under Section 19. If validity is successfully challenged, the registration may be cancelled with retrospective effect.

Question 35 (2022 - Adapted):

Discuss the concept of "innocent infringement" in design piracy and its implications on remedies.

Model Answer:
 Innocent infringement occurs when a defendant had no knowledge and no reasonable means of knowing about the design registration. Under Section 23 applying Patents Act provisions, courts may refuse damages against innocent infringers, though injunctions to prevent future infringement are typically granted. The proprietor can improve their position by marking articles with the registration number. Once the defendant becomes aware, continued infringement is no longer innocent.

Use Case 1: Furniture Manufacturer vs. Competitor

Scenario: DesignCraft Ltd. holds a registered design for an innovative modular sofa configuration. CompeteWell Furniture begins manufacturing sofas that use the same modular arrangement with minor color variations.

Legal Analysis:

●      DesignCraft can file suit under Section 22 for piracy

●      Color variations are likely immaterial details

●      CompeteWell's independent development claim is not a defense

●      DesignCraft must prove the design is a fraudulent/obvious imitation

●      Section 23 provisions apply for jurisdiction (High Court), remedies, and procedure

Potential Outcomes:

●      Injunction preventing further manufacture/sale

●      Damages or account of CompeteWell's profits

●      Delivery up/destruction of infringing inventory

Use Case 2: Innocent Retailer

Scenario: SmallRetail Store purchases decorative lamps from a wholesaler. Unknown to them, the lamp design is pirated from GlowDesigns Ltd.'s registered design. GlowDesigns sues SmallRetail.

Legal Analysis:

●      SmallRetail is technically liable for selling pirated designs (Section 22)

●      However, as an innocent infringer, they may have damages refused

●      Court will likely issue injunction against future sales

●      SmallRetail should cease sales immediately upon notice

●      They may have recourse against their wholesaler for indemnity

Best Practice: Retailers should verify design registrations for distinctive products and seek indemnity clauses from suppliers.

Use Case 3: Export Manufacturing

Scenario: IndiaManuf Ltd. manufactures articles in India bearing TechDesign Co.'s registered design, but exclusively for export to countries where TechDesign has no protection.

Legal Analysis:

●      Manufacturing in India constitutes piracy under Section 22, regardless of export destination

●      TechDesign's lack of protection abroad is irrelevant to Indian infringement

●      TechDesign can seek injunction and damages for manufacturing activities in India

●      If articles re-enter India, additional liability arises

Business Implication: Companies cannot use India as a manufacturing base for designs protected in India, even for export-only business models.

Use Case 4: Challenged Validity During Litigation

Scenario: FashionHouse sues TrendyWear for pirating a textile design. TrendyWear discovers the design was published in a foreign magazine 2 years before registration.

Legal Analysis:

●      TrendyWear can challenge validity as a defense (Section 23 applying Patents Act provisions)

●      Prior publication destroys novelty

●      If proven, registration may be cancelled with retrospective effect

●      FashionHouse's piracy claim would fail

●      TrendyWear might also file separate cancellation petition under Section 19

Strategic Consideration: Design proprietors should conduct thorough novelty searches before filing suits, as unsuccessful suits with invalid registrations may result in adverse costs.

Use Case 5: Article Class Ambiguity

Scenario: CeramicArt registers a design for "decorative plates" (Class 19-06). PosterPrint reproduces the design on paper posters. CeramicArt alleges piracy.

Legal Analysis:

●      Design protection is article-specific

●      Plates and posters are different articles in different classes

●      Unless the registration covered both classes, this likely isn't piracy

●      CeramicArt should have registered for multiple classes if broader protection was desired

●      Copyright law might offer alternative protection for artistic designs

Lesson: Strategic design filing requires considering all potential article applications and filing across relevant classes.

🎓 KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR EXAMINATION

Section 22 - Piracy:

  1. Piracy includes applying registered design without consent
  2. Differences in immaterial details don't prevent piracy finding
  3. Independent creation is NOT a defense
  4. Protection is article-specific (class matters)
  5. Manufacturing in India for export is still piracy
  6. Innocent infringement may limit damages but not injunctions

Section 23 - Application of Patents Act Provisions:

  1. Specific Patents Act provisions apply to design litigation
  2. High Court has exclusive jurisdiction
  3. Remedies include injunction, damages, account of profits, delivery up
  4. Validity can be challenged as defense in infringement suits
  5. Certificate of contested validity affects costs
  6. Plaintiff must elect between damages or account of profits

Litigation Strategy:

●      Proprietors should mark articles with registration numbers

●      Conduct validity searches before filing suit

●      Consider article class definitions carefully

●      Document all instances of piracy for damages calculation

●      Innocent infringers should cease immediately upon notice

Common Examination Traps:

●      Assuming independent creation is a defense (it's not)

●      Confusing civil piracy with criminal offense (piracy is civil only)

●      Forgetting that cancellation has retrospective effect

●      Missing the article-class limitation on protection

●      Assuming damages are automatic (court has discretion, especially for innocent infringers)
