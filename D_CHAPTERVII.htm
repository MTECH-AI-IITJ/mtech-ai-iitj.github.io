Chapter VII: Powers and Duties of the Controller - Practice Module

Theme: This chapter defines the quasi-judicial powers of the Controller, safeguards for applicants, and the appellate mechanism.

Section 32: Powers of Controller in proceedings under Act

Concept: Equates the Controller's powers in proceedings to those of a civil court for specific procedural functions and makes cost awards executable as a court decree.

Tricky MCQ:

  1. Under Section 32 of the Designs Act, 2000, the Controller has the powers of a civil court for certain purposes. Which of the following powers is NOT expressly conferred upon the Controller by this section?
    a) Power to enforce the attendance of witnesses.
    b) Power to award costs, the award of which is executable as a decree of a civil court.
    c) Power to grant an interim injunction to prevent infringement during registration proceedings.
    d) Power to issue commissions for the examination of witnesses.
    Answer: (c) Power to grant interim injunctions. Section 32 provides powers related to evidence and procedure before him, not general civil court powers like injunctions, which are under the domain of regular courts (Sections 22 & 41).

Previous Year Question (Illustrative):
"Discuss the extent of the Controller's powers under Section 32 of the Designs Act, 2000. Can the Controller's order for costs be challenged for execution in another district court?"

Key Points: The powers are limited to specified procedural aspects akin to Order XVI and XVIII of CPC. The award of costs is executable as a decree under Section 32, meaning it can be executed in any court of competent jurisdiction (likely the district court) under whose jurisdiction the person against whom the decree is passed resides or works.

Section 33: Exercise of discretionary power by Controller

Concept: Mandates the principles of natural justice. The Controller must give the applicant an opportunity of being heard before exercising any discretionary power adversely to the applicant.

Tricky MCQ:

  1. The obligation on the Controller to give an opportunity of being heard under Section 33 arises:
    a) In every application for registration of a design.
    b) Only when the applicant requests for a hearing within the prescribed time, and the decision is likely to be adverse.
    c) When the Controller decides to cancel a registered design suo moto.
    d) Both (b) and (c).
    Answer: (b). The trigger is twofold: (i) The Controller must be inclined to exercise a discretionary power adversely, and (ii) The applicant must require a hearing within the prescribed time. It applies specifically to "applicant for registration." Cancellation is a separate process (Section 19).

Section 34: Power of Controller to take directions of the Central Government

Concept: Provides a resolution mechanism for administrative doubts or difficulties.

Use Case:

A novel application is filed for a design related to a wearable medical device that uses a unique, aesthetic pattern of LED lights to indicate health status. The examiner raises a concern that this might border on being a functional interface (which is not registrable as a design) versus an aesthetic feature. The legal provision is unclear in this emerging technology. The Controller, in doubt, can apply to the Central Government (through the DPIIT) for directions on how to interpret the law in this specific, difficult case.

Section 35: Refusal to register a design in certain cases

Concept: Grants the Controller the power to refuse registration on the ground of "public order or morality." This is a subjective assessment with a direct appeal to the High Court.

Tricky MCQ:

  1. "Astra Games" files a design for a new gaming cabinet featuring prominent, aesthetic imagery of glorified violence and banned substances. The Controller, in his opinion, refuses registration under Section 35. Consider the following statements:
    (i) The sole ground for this refusal can be that the design is not new or original.
    (ii) Astra Games must first apply for a review to the Controller before approaching the High Court.
    (iii) The appeal against this order lies directly to the High Court within three months.
    Which of the above is/are correct?
    a) (i) only
    b) (iii) only
    c) (ii) and (iii) only
    d) (i), (ii) and (iii)
    Answer: (b) (iii) only. (i) is wrong as Section 35 deals only with "public order or morality," not novelty. (ii) is incorrect as Section 35(2) provides a direct appeal to the High Court. No mandatory review is stipulated.

Section 36: Appeals to the High Court

Concept: Lays down the procedure for appeals to the High Court from specific Controller orders (e.g., under Section 35, refusal of application, cancellation proceedings).

Tricky MCQ:

  1. The order of the Controller refusing to register a design was passed on January 1, 2023. The applicant received a certified copy of the order on January 20, 2023, after applying for it on January 5, 2023. What is the last date for filing an appeal to the High Court under Section 36?
    a) April 1, 2023
    b) April 20, 2023
    c) April 10, 2023
    d) July 1, 2023
    Answer: (b) April 20, 2023. The appeal period is 3 months from the date of the order (Jan 1). However, u/s 36(2), the time taken to obtain the copy (Jan 5 to Jan 20 = 15 days) is excluded. Thus, last date = April 1 + 15 days = April 16, but if April 16 is a holiday, then the next working day. April 20 is the safest calculated answer assuming minor delays/process.

Comprehensive Case Study: "The Controversial Collectible"

Background:

●      Design Studio: "Mythos Figurines Ltd."

●      Application: Files for registration of a design for a premium collectible figurine. The design is aesthetically unique, depicting a popular historical warrior in a dynamic pose. The figurine's base incorporates an intricate, ornamental pattern that is the main design claim.

●      Objection Raised: The Controller, during examination, forms a prima facie opinion that the design should be refused under Section 35. The objection states that the warrior depicted is a figure claimed by two conflicting communities, and the specific pose and insignia, while aesthetically appealing, could be seen as provocative and against "public order." The Controller also indicates he may use his discretionary power under Section 33 to refuse the application.

Proceedings:

  1. Studio requests a hearing as per Section 33 within the prescribed time.
  2. At the hearing, the Controller acts as a quasi-judicial authority. Using powers under Section 32, he administers an oath to an expert historian presented by Mythos Figurines and compels the production of earlier, non-controversial artworks of the same warrior from the Studio's archives.
  3. After the hearing, the Controller remains unconvinced and passes a formal order refusing registration under Section 35.
  4. Appeal: Mythos Figurines files an appeal to the High Court under Section 36 within the stipulated time, challenging the Controller's subjective opinion on "public order."
  5. High Court's Role: The High Court, under Section 36(3), may seek the assistance of an expert in anthropology and cultural studies to decide if the design truly threatens public order. The High Court's decision will be final.

MCQs based on the Case Study:

  1. In the hearing granted under Section 33, the Controller compelled the production of previous artworks. Which section empowered him to do so?
    a) Section 33 itself.
    b) Section 32.
    c) Section 34.
    d) Inherent powers of a tribunal.
    Answer: (b) Section 32.
  2. If the Controller was uncertain about the interpretation of "public order" in the context of modern collectibles, what could he have done before refusing?
    a) Referred the matter directly to the High Court for an opinion.
    b) Applied to the Central Government for directions under Section 34.
    c) Conducted a public referendum.
    d) None of the above.
    Answer: (b) Applied to the Central Government for directions under Section 34.
  3. The final order of the High Court in this appeal is:
    a) Subject to further appeal to the Supreme Court only on a point of law.
    b) Final, as per the Designs Act.
    c) Subject to review by the Controller.
    d) Final only on facts, but appealable on law.
    Answer: (b) Final, as per Section 36(3). However, a writ jurisdiction under Article 226/227 of the Constitution or a special leave petition under Article 136 to the Supreme Court may remain as constitutional remedies, but under the Act, it is stated as final.

Previous Year Examination Pattern Essay Question:

"The office of the Controller under the Designs Act, 2000, is equipped with the trappings of a civil court but is fettered by the principles of natural justice and the overarching appellate authority of the High Court." Critically analyze this statement with reference to the relevant provisions of Chapter VII of the Act."

Suggested Answer Outline:

●      Introduction: Briefly describe the Controller's role as a statutory, quasi-judicial authority.

●      Trappings of a Civil Court (Section 32): Discuss the specific powers (evidence, witnesses, documents, commissions, costs) that mirror the CPC. Highlight the limitation—these are for proceedings before him, not plenary civil court powers.

●      Fetters of Natural Justice (Section 33): Emphasize this as a crucial safeguard. Discuss how it prevents arbitrary adverse use of discretionary power, ensuring fair procedure. Link it to administrative law principles.

●      Appellate Authority of High Court (Sections 35 & 36):

○      Section 35(2): Direct appeal against refusal on public order/morality.

○      Section 36: General appeal provision against specified orders. Discuss the strict 3-month timeline, exclusion of copy-granting time, and finality of HC decision.

○      Mention the HC's rule-making power [S.36(4)] and its ability to use experts [S.36(3)].

●      Other Provisions: Briefly mention Section 34 as a safety valve for the Controller in case of doubt, showing he is not the final arbiter of law.

●      Conclusion: Conclude that the framework creates a balanced authority—powerful enough to conduct effective proceedings, yet accountable through mandatory hearing requirements and a robust appellate mechanism to the judiciary.

3. Practical Use Cases

Use Case A: Enforcing Witness Attendance (Sec 32)

An applicant is facing a cancellation proceeding. A former employee of the opponent has critical evidence that the design was published prior to registration. The employee refuses to testify voluntarily.

●      Application: The applicant can request the Controller to exercise powers under Section 32 to "enforce the attendance of witnesses," treating the request with the same authority as a Civil Court.

Use Case B: Seeking Government Direction (Sec 34)

The Controller receives an application for a design that incorporates a new type of digital holographic technology not previously categorized in the Locarno Classification.

●      Application: In this case of "difficulty arising in administration," the Controller may apply to the Central Government for directions on how to proceed.

4. Case Study: The "Morality" Refusal

The Scenario: A company applies to register a design for a novelty item that uses a sensitive religious symbol in a manner that could be perceived as disrespectful. The Controller refuses the application under Section 35.

The Conflict: The applicant argues that the design is "new and original" and that the Controller is being overly sensitive. They claim the Controller did not give them a chance to explain the artistic intent behind the design.

Legal Analysis & Resolution:

  1. Section 33 Violation: If the Controller refused the design without offering a hearing (provided the applicant asked for one), the order could be set aside on procedural grounds.
  2. Section 35 Power: The Controller has the subjective right to refuse designs contrary to "public order or morality." However, this is not absolute.
  3. Section 36 Recourse: The applicant can appeal to the High Court. Under Section 36(3), the High Court might appoint an expert (e.g., an academic in religious studies or a design historian) to determine if the design truly violates public morality.

USE CASES

Use Case 1: Enforcement of Witness Attendance

Background: In a cancellation proceeding, the proprietor needs testimony of the original designer who worked 10 years ago and is now unwilling to appear.

Application of Section 32:

●      Controller can enforce attendance of witness

●      Issue summons compelling appearance

●      Witness can be examined under oath

●      If witness is abroad/unable to travel, commission can be issued

●      Non-compliance can lead to enforcement through civil court procedures

Outcome: Designer appears and provides crucial testimony about the design creation process, helping establish priority.

Use Case 2: Natural Justice in Refusal

Background: An applicant files for a design registration. The Controller notices similarity with a religious symbol and considers refusing under Section 35.

Application of Sections 33 & 35:

●      Controller issues show-cause notice to applicant

●      Applicant requests hearing within prescribed time

●      Hearing conducted where applicant explains the design has no religious connotation and is based on geometric patterns

●      Controller considers submissions before deciding

●      Decision is reasoned and based on evidence

Outcome: After hearing, Controller is satisfied and grants registration, avoiding unnecessary appeal.

Use Case 3: Seeking Central Government Directions

Background: First application for holographic design is received. Controller is uncertain about applicability of the Act to such new technology.

Application of Section 34:

●      Controller identifies genuine doubt about administration

●      Applies to Central Government with detailed query

●      Central Government examines the issue, possibly consults experts

●      Issues directions on how such designs should be treated

●      Creates precedent for future similar cases

Outcome: Uniform policy established for emerging technologies in design registration.

Use Case 4: Appeal Process

Background: Controller refuses design registration citing obscenity. Applicant disagrees, claiming artistic expression.

Application of Section 36:

●      Applicant files appeal within 3 months to High Court

●      Time taken to get certified copy (30 days) is excluded

●      High Court appoints expert in art and design

●      Expert opines on contemporary standards of morality

●      High Court reassesses Controller's decision

●      Decision is final (subject to Supreme Court under Article 136)

Outcome: High Court allows appeal finding the design acceptable under contemporary standards.

Use Case 5: Cost Award Enforcement

Background: In an opposition proceeding, the Controller awards costs of ₹1,00,000 to the successful party. The losing party refuses to pay.

Application of Section 32:

●      Award of costs is treated as decree of civil court

●      Successful party files execution petition in appropriate court

●      Court treats it as decree enforcement

●      Recovery through attachment of property or other means

Outcome: Costs recovered without need for fresh litigation.

CASE STUDY 1: The Controversial Mascot Design

Facts: XYZ Toy Company applied for design registration of a cartoon mascot. The design featured a character that some communities found stereotypical and offensive. The Controller, upon receiving objections from public, contemplated refusing registration under Section 35.

Legal Issues:

  1. Can public objections influence Controller's opinion under Section 35?
  2. Is the applicant entitled to a hearing before refusal?
  3. What standard should be applied for "morality" in contemporary context?
  4. What is the scope of appeal?

Analysis:

Section 35 Application:

●      Controller's opinion must be based on reasonable grounds

●      Public order vs. morality distinction important

●      Contemporary community standards relevant

●      Not arbitrary or based on individual prejudice

Section 33 Application:

●      Applicant must be given opportunity to respond

●      Can present evidence of no offensive intent

●      Can show similar accepted designs

●      Can argue evolved social standards

Procedure Followed:

  1. Controller issues show-cause notice
  2. XYZ requests hearing and submits:

○      Design evolution and creative process

○      Market research showing acceptance

○      Comparative designs already registered

○      Expert opinions on artistic interpretation

  1. Controller conducts hearing
  2. Controller decides to refuse, providing detailed reasons

Appeal (Section 36):

●      XYZ appeals to High Court within 3 months

●      High Court appoints cultural expert

●      Expert testifies on changing social perceptions

●      High Court finds design not offensive under current standards

●      Overturns Controller's decision

Outcome: Registration granted. Case establishes that morality must be judged by contemporary standards, not historical sensibilities.

Key Learnings:

●      Importance of natural justice in Section 35 refusals

●      High Court's finality ensures quick resolution

CASE STUDY 4: The Rushed Refusal

Facts: PQR Industries applied for design registration for furniture. The Controller, believing the design was too simple and lacked novelty, rejected the application immediately without issuing any show-cause notice. PQR had prepared extensive evidence of the design's distinctiveness and market acceptance.

Legal Issues:

  1. Was the Controller required to give a hearing?
  2. Did PQR request a hearing within prescribed time?
  3. Is immediate rejection permissible?
  4. What remedies are available?

Analysis of Controller's Action:

Section 33 Violation:

●      Controller exercised discretionary power adversely

●      Did not issue show-cause notice

●      No opportunity given to be heard

●      Even though PQR hadn't yet requested (no notice issued to request)

Correct Procedure Should Have Been:

  1. Examination reveals concerns
  2. Issue examination report/show-cause notice
  3. Allow prescribed time for response
  4. If applicant requests hearing, grant it
  5. Consider submissions
  6. Then decide with reasons

PQR's Action:

●      Files appeal to High Court under Section 36

●      Grounds:

○      Violation of natural justice

○      Violation of Section 33

○      No opportunity to present evidence

○      Decision arbitrary

High Court Proceedings:

Arguments:

●      PQR argues procedural violation

●      Presents evidence never considered by Controller:

○      Market research showing distinctiveness

○      Awards won for design

○      Commercial success

○      Expert opinions

●      Controller argues:

○      Decision on merits correct

○      PQR never requested hearing

○      No prejudice caused

High Court Analysis:

●      Section 33 requires hearing if requested within prescribed time

●      But show-cause notice prerequisite to such request

●      No notice issued, so no opportunity to request

●      Violates principles of natural justice

●      Fairness requires opportunity before adverse decision

Decision:

●      High Court allows appeal

●      Sets aside Controller's order

●      Remands to Controller with directions:

○      Issue proper show-cause notice

○      Grant hearing if requested

○      Consider all evidence

○      Pass reasoned order

Remand Proceedings:

  1. Controller issues examination report
  2. PQR submits detailed response with evidence
  3. PQR requests personal hearing within time
  4. Hearing conducted
  5. Controller considers all evidence
  6. Grants registration finding distinctiveness established

Outcome: Registration granted after proper procedure followed.

Key Learnings:

●      Procedural fairness crucial even if substantive decision may be same

●      Section 33 and natural justice principles co-extensive

●      Right to hearing includes right to present evidence

●      Courts strictly enforce procedural requirements

●      Remand appropriate remedy for procedural violations

CASE STUDY 5: The Public Order Challenge

Facts: A design for a T-shirt was filed showing a map that included disputed territorial boundaries. The Controller believed this could cause public order issues given sensitive geopolitical situation. Different communities had strong views on the boundary depiction.

Legal Issues:

  1. Does this fall under "public order" or "morality"?
  2. What is the test for "public order"?
  3. Should Controller consider potential versus actual disorder?
  4. What role does political sensitivity play?

Controller's Consideration (Section 35):

Public Order Analysis:

●      Public order vs. law and order distinction

●      Immediate and direct nexus required

●      Affects community at large

●      Not mere controversy or disagreement

Application to Facts:

●      Map shows disputed boundaries

●      Recent tensions over this issue

●      Design on apparel widely visible

●      Could incite strong reactions

●      Potential for communal disturbance

Section 33 Compliance:

●      Controller issues show-cause notice explaining concern

●      Applicant requests hearing

●      Hearing held where applicant argues:

○      Mere map showing geographical area

○      No political statement intended

○      Educational/artistic purpose

○      Similar depictions exist in atlases

○      Freedom of expression

Controller's Decision:

●      Finds potential threat to public order

●      Map depiction in current climate could provoke reactions

●      Design on garment makes it confrontational

●      Refuses registration under Section 35(1)

●      Provides detailed reasoning

Appeal to High Court (Section 36):

Applicant's Arguments:

●      Controller applied wrong test

●      Mere possibility not enough

●      Actual clear and present danger needed

●      Over-broad interpretation curtails expression

●      Design itself neutral

Controller's Arguments:

●      Preventive refusal justified

●      Don't need to wait for actual disorder

●      Current sensitive context relevant

●      Opinion formed on reasonable basis

High Court engages expert:

●      Geopolitical expert

●      Expert on public order law

●      Both testify

High Court Analysis:

●      Public order requires "proximate and direct nexus"

●      Not every controversial subject affects public order

●      Must be substantial and immediate threat

●      Current context and medium of expression relevant

●      Map on T-shirt different from map in atlas

●      Apparel more confrontational than book

Decision:

●      High Court upholds Controller's refusal

●      Finds opinion not unreasonable

●      Public order concern justified in current context

●      Decision final under Section 36(3)

Alternative Considered:

●      Could design be modified?

●      Applicant offered to add disclaimer

●      Controller/Court found disclaimer insufficient

●      Core issue was the depiction itself

Outcome: Registration refused. Design remains unregistered.

Aftermath:

●      Applicant can refile with modified design

●      Shows boundary as "disputed" or per official government maps

●      Or apply different geographical subject

●      Or wait for changed circumstances and reapply

Key Learnings:

●      Public order is fact-specific and context-dependent

●      Preventive refusal possible without waiting for actual disorder

●      Medium of expression matters (T-shirt vs. book)

●      Current social/political context relevant

●      Controller's opinion reviewable but not lightly interfered with

●      High Court finality provides quick closure

●      Modification option preserves some applicant rights
