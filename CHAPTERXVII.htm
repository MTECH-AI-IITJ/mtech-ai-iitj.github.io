Exam Notes: Chapter XVII - Use of Inventions for Government Purposes & Acquisition by Central Government

Section 99: Meaning of Use of Invention for Purposes of Government

  • Simple Explanation: This section defines when an invention is considered "used for government purposes." It includes:
    • Direct use by any government department (e.g., defense, healthcare).
    • Use by a third party authorized by the government (e.g., a contractor making equipment for the army).
    • Manufacture of products using the invention for government needs.
    • Import/export of the invention for government use.
  • Key Point: Government use is not limited to emergency situations; it applies to routine public services.

Section 100: Power of Central Government to Use Inventions for Government Purposes

  • Simple Explanation: The Central Government can use any patented invention without the patent owner’s permission, but must:
    1. Notify the patentee (in writing) about its intent to use the invention.
    2. Pay adequate remuneration (compensation) to the patentee.
  • Key Points:
    • This is not patent infringement (legal protection for government).
    • Use can start before or after patent grant if the invention is already published.
    • Remuneration is decided based on factors like invention value and government use scope.

Section 101: Rights of Third Parties in Respect of Use of Invention for Government Purposes

  • Simple Explanation: Protects third parties (e.g., licensees, suppliers) when the government uses an invention:
    • Existing rights: If a third party was legally using the invention before government use, their rights continue.
    • No additional rights: If the government authorizes a third party to use the invention, that party cannot claim extra rights beyond the government’s authorization.
  • Key Point: Government use does not cancel pre-existing agreements (e.g., licenses between patentee and a company).

Section 102: Acquisition of Inventions and Patents by the Central Government

  • Simple Explanation: The Central Government can fully acquire (take ownership of) an invention or patent if needed for public interest. Steps:
    1. Publish a notification in the Official Gazette (public declaration).
    2. The invention/patent becomes government property from the notification date.
    3. Pay compensation to the patentee (amount agreed or decided by the High Court).
  • Key Points:
    • Acquisition is permanent (unlike temporary use under Section 100).
    • Used for critical needs (e.g., national security, public health).

Section 103: Reference to High Court of Disputes as to Use for Government Purposes

  • Simple Explanation: If disputes arise over government use or acquisition (e.g., compensation amount), either party can:
    • Refer the case to the High Court for resolution.
    • The court decides on compensation, scope of use, or ownership rights.
  • Key Point: The High Court’s decision is final and binding on both government and patentee.

Chapter XVII: Use of Inventions for Government & Acquisition by Government - Simple Notes

This chapter gives special powers to the Government to use a patented invention without the patent owner's permission. This is to ensure that public interest and national needs are always prioritized over individual patent rights.

Section 99: Meaning of "Use for Purposes of Government"

  • Simple Meaning: This section defines what activities are considered "government use."
  • Key Points:
    • It's not just for the army or defense. It covers any use for:
      • Central Government: Any department or ministry.
      • State Governments: When authorized by the Central Govt.
      • Government Undertakings: Companies owned or controlled by the government.
    • It includes making, using, or even selling the invention to provide a service or product that the government has promised to the public (e.g., public health, public utility services).

Section 100: Power to Use Inventions for Purposes of Government

  • Simple Meaning: This is the main rule that allows the government to use a patent.
  • Key Points:
    • The government (or an authorized company) can use any patented invention without the owner's consent.
    • This can be done without a license or agreement from the patent holder.
    • The use can be free of charge, but the government will usually pay a royalty (a fee) to the patent owner. The amount can be agreed upon or decided by the court later.

Section 101: Rights of Third Parties

  • Simple Meaning: What happens if the government uses a patent that someone else has a license for?
  • Key Points:
    • A "third party" is anyone other than the patent owner, like a company that has bought a license to make and sell the invention.
    • If the government uses the patent, the rights of these license holders are also restricted.
    • However, the license holder is also entitled to receive payment (compensation) for the government's use, just like the patent owner.

Section 102: Acquisition of Inventions and Patents by Central Government

  • Simple Meaning: The government can completely take over (acquire) a patent.
  • Key Points:
    • This is more extreme than just "using" the patent. Here, the government becomes the new owner of the patent.
    • The government must publish a notification in the Official Gazette.
    • The government must pay compensation to the original patent owner for taking over their property (the patent).

Section 103: Reference to High Court

  • Simple Meaning: What if there's a dispute about payment or terms?
  • Key Points:
    • If the patent owner and the government cannot agree on the amount of royalty or compensation, either party can refer the dispute to the High Court.
    • The High Court will act as an umpire to decide what is a fair and reasonable payment.

Use Cases for Practice

Use Case for Sections 99 & 100: The Life-Saving Drug

  • Scenario: A pharmaceutical company holds a patent for a new, highly effective cancer drug, but sells it at an extremely high price, making it unaffordable for most citizens. The government wants to include this drug in its national health program to provide it for free in public hospitals.
  • Question: Can the government do this? Which section empowers it?
  • Answer: Yes. The government can invoke Section 100. Manufacturing or importing the drug for use in public hospitals qualifies as a "purpose of government" (public health under Section 99). The government can authorize a pharma company to manufacture the drug without the patent holder's permission, but it must pay them agreed-upon royalties.

Use Case for Section 101: The Licensed Manufacturer

  • Scenario: Company 'A' owns a patent for a water purification technology. It has given an exclusive license to Company 'B' to manufacture and sell devices based on this technology. The government, to tackle a drought, wants to use this technology in its relief efforts.
  • Question: What are the rights of Company 'B' (the third party) in this situation?
  • Answer: Under Section 101, the government's use will override the exclusive license of Company 'B'. The government can procure the devices from another manufacturer. However, Company 'B' is also entitled to seek compensation from the government for the loss of its licensed rights.

Use Case for Section 102: The Critical Defense Technology

  • Scenario: A private defense lab invents and patents a new stealth technology for aircraft. The government determines that this technology is vital for national security and must be solely controlled by the state to prevent it from falling into the wrong hands.
  • Question: What extreme step can the government take beyond just using the technology?
  • Answer: The government can use Section 102 to acquire the patent itself. This means the government becomes the new owner of the patent. The original inventor/lab will lose all ownership rights but will receive compensation from the government for the acquisition.

Use Case for Section 103: The Payment Dispute

  • Scenario: The government uses a patented solar panel design for a large public housing project. The patent owner claims the royalty should be ₹100 per panel. The government argues that ₹10 is fair. They cannot reach an agreement.
  • Question: How is this deadlock resolved?
  • Answer: As per Section 103, either the patent owner or the government can refer the dispute to the High Court. The High Court will hear both sides and determine what constitutes a fair and reasonable royalty amount for the use of the invention.

 

 

Chapter XVII: Use of Inventions for Government Purposes and Acquisition of Inventions by the Central Government

This chapter of the Patents Act deals with how the Indian government can use patented inventions and even acquire them for its own purposes. It's a crucial part of the law that ensures the government has access to important technologies, especially for public benefit, defense, or national security.

99. Meaning of Use of Invention for Purposes of Government

This section defines what it means for the government to "use an invention." It includes:

  • Manufacturing, making, or using the patented product.
  • Importing the patented product.
  • Exercising the patented process.

It clarifies that "for the purposes of Government" includes:

  • Defense purposes.
  • Internal security purposes.
  • Public health purposes, such as manufacturing medicines.
  • Any other purpose deemed important by the Central Government.

100. Power of Central Government to Use Inventions

This is the core section that gives the government the authority to use a patented invention without the patent holder's permission. Here are the key points:

  • The government can authorize anyone to use an invention for its purposes.
  • The government must pay the patent holder royalty for the use of the invention. This is a form of compulsory license, where the government is legally allowed to use the patent but must compensate the owner.
  • The terms and conditions of this use, including the royalty amount, are decided by the Central Government.
  • The government can do this even before the patent is granted, for a pending application.

Use Case: Imagine a pandemic breaks out and a company holds the patent for a life-saving vaccine. The government can use Section 100 to produce or import the vaccine on a large scale to protect the population, even if the patent holder cannot meet the demand. The government will then compensate the patent holder.

101. Rights of Third Parties

This section protects the rights of other parties (like a licensee) when the government uses an invention.

  • If a person had a license or a right to use the invention before the government's authorization, their rights are not affected.
  • However, if their use interferes with the government's use, the government's use takes priority. In such a case, the third party may also be eligible for compensation from the government.

102. Acquisition of Inventions and Patents

This section is different from Section 100. Instead of just using the invention, the government can acquire the entire patent and the invention itself. This means the government becomes the new owner.

  • This can only be done if the government believes it's necessary for a public purpose.
  • The government must notify the patent holder and offer them compensation.
  • Once the patent is acquired, it becomes public property, and the government can use it without any further obligation to the original owner.

Use Case: The government needs a specific technology for a new fighter jet. The technology is patented by a private company. To ensure full control and availability of the technology for national defense, the government can use Section 102 to acquire the entire patent, thereby owning the invention outright.

103. Reference to High Court

This section deals with disputes.

  • If there's a disagreement about the terms or amount of compensation for the government's use of an invention (under Section 100 or 102), the matter can be referred to the High Court.
  • The High Court will then decide the fair and reasonable amount of compensation. This ensures a transparent and just process for the patent holder.

Overview of Chapter XVII

This chapter of the Indian Patents Act, 1970 deals with how the Central Government can use or acquire patented inventions for government purposes, such as national defense, public health, or infrastructure, without needing permission from the patent holder — but with fair compensation.

Section 99: Meaning of Use of Invention for Purposes of Government

Simple Explanation:

  • The government (Central or any person authorized by it) can use a patented invention for its own purposes.
  • "Use" includes:
    • Making, using, selling, or importing the invention.
    • Using it in government projects, defense, public services, etc.
  • This can be done without the patentee’s permission, but the patentee must be paid compensation.

✅ Key Points:

  • Applies to Central Government and any person authorized by it.
  • Does not require prior permission from the patent holder.
  • Must be for government purposes (e.g., army, public hospitals, infrastructure).
  • Compensation must be paid to the patentee.

Simple Explanation:

  • The Central Government can use any patented invention for government purposes.
  • It can also authorize any person or company (e.g., a public sector unit) to make or use the invention on its behalf.
  • This power applies even if the patent is held by a private individual or company.
  • The patentee must be paid compensation based on the value and use.

✅ Key Points:

  • Government can authorize third parties (like PSUs) to use the invention.
  • No need to get license or consent from patentee.
  • Must pay adequate compensation.
  • Applies to existing and future patents.

Section 102: Acquisition of Inventions and Patents by the Central Government

Simple Explanation:

  • In urgent public interest (e.g., national emergency, public health crisis), the Central Government can take over a patent or invention.
  • The government becomes the owner of the patent.
  • The original patentee must be paid compensation.
  • The government can then use, license, or allow others to use the invention.

✅ Key Points:

  • Can happen in national emergencies or public interest.
  • Government acquires full ownership.
  • Must pay compensation to the patentee.
  • Rarely used – only in extreme cases.

Section 103: Reference to High Court of Disputes as to Use for Purposes of Government

✅ Key Points:

  • Disputes go to High Court, not lower courts.
  • Covers validity of use and compensation.
  • Ensures fairness to both government and patentee.

Chapter XVII: Use of Inventions for Purposes of Government and Acquisition of Inventions by Central Government

This chapter from the Indian Patents Act, 1970, deals with how the government can use or take over patented inventions for public or government needs. It balances the patent holder's rights with national interests, like defense, health, or emergencies. The government can use inventions without always needing permission, but usually pays fair compensation. Disputes go to the High Court.

Below are simple notes for each section, explained in easy language. For each section, I've included:

  • Notes: Key points broken down simply.
  • Use Cases for Practice: Real-world examples or hypotheticals to understand application.
  • MCQ Questions for Practice: 3 multiple-choice questions per section, with options. Answers are provided at the end of each section's MCQs for self-check.

Section 99: Meaning of Use of Invention for Purposes of Government

Notes:

  • This section defines what "use for government purposes" means in the chapter.
  • An invention is considered used for government if it's made, used, sold, or practiced by the Central Government, a State Government, or a government-owned company (undertaking).
  • This doesn't apply to certain government actions already allowed under Section 47 of the Act, like importing medicines for public distribution or using for experiments.
  • Basically, it's a broad definition to cover government needs without extra restrictions.

Use Cases for Practice:

  1. Public Health Scenario: During a pandemic, the government manufactures a patented vaccine using the invention's process and distributes it through state hospitals. This counts as "use for government purposes" since it's for a state government.
  2. Defense Example: A government defense factory uses a patented radar technology to build equipment for the army. This is government use, even if sold internally to another government department.
  3. Hypothetical: A state-run electricity company uses a patented solar panel design to generate power for public schools. This fits the definition, but if it's just importing for testing (under Section 47), this chapter doesn't apply.

MCQ Questions for Practice:

  1. What entities can "use" an invention for government purposes under Section 99?
    • A) Only the Central Government
    • B) Central Government, State Governments, and Government undertakings
    • C) Private companies authorized by patentee
    • D) Only international organizations
  2. Does Section 99 apply to government importation of medicines for public distribution?
    • A) Yes, always
    • B) No, because it's covered under Section 47
    • C) Only if the medicine is patented abroad
    • D) Yes, but only for State Governments
  3. An invention is "used for government purposes" if it is:
    • A) Only made, not sold
    • B) Made, used, exercised, or vended (sold)
    • C) Only used in private sectors
    • D) Exported to other countries

Answers: 1-B, 2-B, 3-B

Section 100: Power of Central Government to Use Inventions for Purposes of Government

Notes:

  • The Central Government (or anyone it authorizes in writing) can use any invention after a patent application is filed or granted, for government purposes.
  • If the government had already recorded, tested, or tried the invention before the patent's priority date (not learned from the patentee), they can use it for free—no royalty.
  • Otherwise, they must agree on payment terms with the patentee (before or after use), or the High Court decides. Payment is "adequate remuneration" based on the invention's value.
  • Authorization can be given even after the use happens (retroactive).
  • Government must inform the patentee about the use (unless it's an emergency, extreme urgency, or non-commercial).
  • Includes right to sell goods made from the invention on a non-commercial basis.
  • If there's an exclusive licensee or assignee, they also get notice, and terms apply to them.

Use Cases for Practice:

  1. Emergency Scenario: In a national disaster, the government authorizes a company to use a patented water purification tech without prior notice. Later, they negotiate payment, but no notice was needed due to urgency.
  2. Defense Testing Example: The government tested a drone invention in 2010, before the 2012 patent priority date. Now, they use it freely for army purposes without paying royalties.
  3. Hypothetical: A patented software for traffic management is used by a state police department (authorized by Central Govt). If no prior testing, payment is agreed; if disputed, High Court sets fair amount considering economic value.

    • B) Agreement or High Court, based on adequate remuneration and economic value
    • C) Patentee's demand only
    • D) No payment required

Section 101: Rights of Third Parties in Respect of Use of Invention for Purposes of Government

Notes:

  • When government uses a patented invention (or one with pending application), any existing license, assignment, or agreement between the patentee and third parties doesn't apply if it restricts government use or requires payments for such use.
  • Government use doesn't infringe copyrights in related documents or models.
  • If the patent was assigned in exchange for royalties based on use, government use is treated as authorized, and payments are shared between patentee and assignor (agreed or by High Court).
  • If there's an exclusive licensee who can use for government, payments are shared fairly (considering their development costs), agreed or by court.

Use Cases for Practice:

  1. License Restriction Scenario: A patentee has a license agreement with a company forbidding government sales. Government uses the invention for defense— the restriction is invalid, and no extra payment under the license.
  2. Shared Payment Example: A inventor assigns patent to a company for royalties on use. Government orders production; payments are split between inventor and company as per agreement or court.
  3. Hypothetical: An exclusive licensee spent money developing a patented medical device. Government uses it; court decides fair share of remuneration, factoring in licensee's expenses.

Section 102: Acquisition of Inventions and Patents by the Central Government

Notes:

  • If needed for a public purpose, Central Government can acquire the invention or patent by publishing a notification in the Official Gazette— it then automatically transfers to the government.
  • Notice must be given to the applicant/patentee and anyone registered as interested.
  • Compensation is agreed upon, or if not, decided by High Court. It considers costs incurred, patent term, how it was used before (including profits), and other factors.

Use Cases for Practice:

  1. National Security Scenario: Government acquires a patented encryption software for cyber defense by notification. They pay compensation based on development costs and prior profits.
  2. Public Utility Example: A patented clean water tech is acquired for nationwide rural supply. Court sets compensation if no agreement, looking at patent's remaining term.
  3. Hypothetical: During food shortage, government acquires a GM crop patent. Notice to patentee, compensation factors in profits from earlier commercial use.

Section 103: Reference to High Court of Disputes as to Use for Purposes of Government

Notes:

  • Disputes about government use (under Section 100), terms, payment shares, or acquisition compensation (Section 102) can be taken to High Court by either side.
  • Government can counter-claim to revoke the patent (on grounds like invalidity).
  • If disclosing invention details harms public interest, it can be shared confidentially to the other side's lawyer or expert.
  • Court considers any other benefits the person received from the invention.
  • Court can refer the case to a referee, commissioner, or arbitrator.
  • For inventions made by government employees in duty, Central Government decides the dispute (after hearing parties), instead of court.

Use Cases for Practice:

  1. Dispute Resolution Scenario: Patentee disagrees with remuneration for government use of a vaccine tech. They refer to High Court, where government counters by challenging patent validity.
  2. Confidential Info Example: In a defense patent dispute, sensitive test details are shared only with an expert, not publicly, to protect national interest.
  3. Hypothetical: A government employee invents a tool during work. Dispute on use terms is handled by Central Govt after hearing, not court.
