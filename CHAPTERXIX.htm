Section-wise Breakdown

Section 116: Appellate Board

  • Composition:
    • Chairperson + Vice-Chairpersons + Judicial Members + Technical Members.
    • Appointed by Central Government; qualifications prescribed.
  • Tricky Point: The Board must include technical members (patent experts) to ensure domain knowledge.

Section 117: Staff of Appellate Board

  • Support Staff: Officers/employees appointed by Central Government.
  • Tricky Point: Staff conditions of service are government-controlled, not autonomous.

Section 117A: Appeals to Appellate Board

  • Who Can Appeal: Any person aggrieved by Controller orders (e.g., refusal of patent, opposition decisions).
  • Time Limit: 3 months from Controller’s order (extendable by Board for "sufficient cause").
  • Form & Fee: Prescribed format + fee.
  • Tricky Point: No appeal for certain orders (e.g., refusal to extend time under Section 53).

Section 117B: Procedure and Powers of Appellate Board

  • Powers:
    • Civil court powers (CPC, 1908): Summon witnesses, demand documents, review decisions, dismiss for default.
    • Original powers: Grant relief under any patent provision (e.g., revocation, rectification).
  • Procedure: Follows principles of natural justice; self-regulated rules.
  • Tricky Point: Board can review its own decisions (unlike Controller).

Section 117C: Bar of Jurisdiction of Courts

  • Exclusivity: No civil court (except Supreme Court) can:
    • Entertain suits/proceedings in Board’s jurisdiction.
    • Grant injunctions in matters before the Board.
  • Tricky Point: Infringement suits remain with civil courts (not barred).

Section 117D: Rectification/Revocation Applications

  • Applications to Board:
    • Rectification of register (Section 50).
    • Revocation of patent (Section 64).
  • Procedure: Prescribed form + fee.
  • Tricky Point: Direct applications to Board (no need to approach Controller first).

Section 117E: Appearance of Controller

  • Representation: Controller may appear:
    • In person.
    • Through authorized officer.
    • Via registered patent agent.
  • Tricky Point: Controller cannot be represented by a non-agent lawyer.

Section 117F: Costs of Controller

  • Costs Award: Board may order Controller to pay costs if:
    • Controller appeared.
    • Appearance unnecessarily caused expense.
  • Tricky Point: Costs awarded only if Controller’s conduct was unjustified.

Section 117G: Transfer of Pending Proceedings

  • Automatic Transfer: Proceedings pending before courts/Controller on commencement of Patents (Amendment) Act, 2002, transferred to Board.
  • Tricky Point: Applies retrospectively to pending cases (not new filings).

Section 117H: Rule-Making Power

  • Autonomy: Board may make rules (with Central Government approval) for:
    • Procedure.
    • Conduct of business.
  • Tricky Point: Rules require government approval (not absolute autonomy).

    Answer: c) Directly before the Appellate Board (Section 117D).

    Answer: b) District Court (Section 117C bars injunctions but not infringement suits).

Exam Notes: Chapter XIX - Appeals to the Appellate Board

Overarching Context: The Appellate Board referred to in this chapter was the Intellectual Property Appellate Board (IPAB). The IPAB was abolished in 2021 (via Tribunals Reforms Act, 2021). Its jurisdiction and powers for patents have been transferred back to various High Courts. However, the principles, procedures, and powers outlined in this chapter are still fundamentally important for understanding how appeals and rectifications are handled, now by the High Courts. Expect exam questions on the law as written.

Section 116: Appellate Board

  • What it is: Establishes the Appellate Board (the IPAB was established u/s 83 of the Trade Marks Act, 1999 and had jurisdiction over patents u/s 116 of Patents Act).
  • Key Note: The Board is not a court but has the powers of a civil court.

Section 117: Staff of Appellate Board

  • Administrative: Deals with the appointment of officers and other employees. Typically less focus for substantive law questions.

Section 117A: Appeals to Appellate Board

  • Who can appeal? Any person aggrieved by a decision/order of the Controller.
  • What can be appealed?
    • Refusal of a patent u/s 15.
    • Decision on opposition u/s 25(4).
    • Decision on application for compulsory license u/s 84.
    • Decision on revocation u/s 85.
    • Refusal/Decision on surrender u/s 87.
  • Time Limit: Appeal must be filed within 3 months from the date of the decision/order. The Board has the power to condone a delay of up to 1 month if sufficient cause is shown.
  • Form: Appeal must be in prescribed form and accompanied by the prescribed fee.

Section 117B: Procedure and Powers of Appellate Board

  • Crucial Section: The Board is not bound by the procedure laid down by the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908.
  • Powers: It has the powers of a civil court for:
    • Receiving evidence.
    • Issuing summons.
    • Reviewing its own decisions.
    • Granting interlocutory orders (injunctions, stays).
    • Awarding costs.
    • Any other matter which may be prescribed.
  • Principles of Natural Justice: The Board shall guide itself by the principles of natural justice.

Section 117C: Bar of Jurisdiction of Courts, etc.

  • No Civil Court shall have jurisdiction to entertain any suit or proceeding in respect of any matter which the Appellate Board is empowered to determine.
  • No injunction shall be granted by any court or other authority in respect of any action taken or to be taken by the Board.

Section 117D: Procedure for Application for Rectification

  • An application for rectification of the register (e.g., to correct a clerical error, amend details, or as part of a revocation petition) can be made to the Appellate Board by a "person aggrieved".
  • The Board can decide on any question that may be necessary or expedient to decide in connection with the rectification.

Section 117E: Appearance of Controller in Legal Proceedings

  • The Controller has the right to appear and be heard in any proceedings before the Appellate Board.
  • The Controller must appear if so directed by the Board.
  • Unless directed otherwise, the Controller may not be made a party to the appeal.

Section 117F: Costs of Controller in Proceedings

  • The Central Government will bear the costs of the Controller for any proceedings before the Board.

Section 117G: Transfer of Pending Proceedings

  • This was a transitional provision. All pending appeals (e.g., those that would have gone to a High Court under the old law) stood transferred to the Appellate Board upon its establishment.

Section 117H: Power to Make Rules

  • The Board has the power to regulate its own procedure and practice.

Tricky Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

1. Under Section 117A, the maximum period of delay in filing an appeal that the Appellate Board can condone is:
a) 3 months
b) 1 month
c) 30 days
d) There is no upper limit; it is at the Board's discretion.
Answer: b) 1 month. The appeal must be filed within 3 months. The Board can only condone a delay of up to one more month (i.e., a total of 4 months from the order) if sufficient cause is shown.

2. Under Section 117E, in an appeal between a patent applicant and an opponent, the Controller:
a) Must always be made a respondent.
b) Cannot be made a party under any circumstances.
c) Has the right to appear and be heard but is not typically a party unless the Board directs.
d) Is represented by the Central Government's standing counsel automatically.
Answer: c) Has the right to appear and be heard but is not typically a party unless the Board directs. This balances the Controller's interest in the case with the need to keep proceedings between the main aggrieved parties.

3. The principles that guide the procedure of the Appellate Board under Section 117B are:
a) The Code of Civil Procedure, 1908.
b) The Indian Evidence Act, 1872.
c) The principles of natural justice.
d) The procedure prescribed by the Controller.
Answer: c) The principles of natural justice. The Board is not bound by the CPC or Evidence Act, though it has similar powers.

4. A civil suit challenging an order of the Controller refusing a patent application is:
a) The correct procedure.
b) Entertainable only by the District Court.
c) Barred by the jurisdiction of the Appellate Board under Section 117C.
d) Possible only with the consent of the Controller.
Answer: c) Barred by the jurisdiction of the Appellate Board under Section 117C. The correct remedy is an appeal to the Appellate Board (now the High Court), not a fresh civil suit.

5. An application for rectification of the patent register can be made by:
a) Any member of the public.
b) Only the patentee.
c) Only the Controller.
d) A person aggrieved.
Answer: d) A person aggrieved. This is a legal term implying a person who has suffered a legal grievance, not just any interested party.

Tricky Use Cases & Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1: The Delayed Appeal
Mr. X receives an order from the Controller rejecting his patent application on March 1, 2023. He is dissatisfied but gets busy with other matters. He finally prepares his appeal and files it with the Appellate Board on July 15, 2023. The Registry points out the delay. Can his appeal be heard?
Answer: The appeal was due by June 1, 2023 (3 months from March 1). It was filed on July 15, which is a delay of over 1.5 months. Under Section 117A(4), the Board can only condone a delay of up to 1 month (i.e., accept an appeal filed until July 1) if he shows "sufficient cause". Since he filed on July 15, which is beyond the condonable period, the Board has no power to condone this delay. The appeal is liable to be dismissed as time-barred.

Scenario 2: The Overealous Competitor
Company A, a competitor of Patentee B, believes B's patent is invalid. They file a petition for revocation before the Appellate Board. They are also involved in a separate commercial dispute with B and, to pressure them, file an application for an interim injunction in a District Court to stop B from asserting the patent until the revocation is decided. Should the District Court grant this injunction?
Answer: No. Under Section 117C, no court can grant an injunction in respect of any action taken or to be taken by the Appellate Board. The revocation proceeding is squarely before the Board. The correct course for Company A is to apply for an interim stay or injunction from the Appellate Board itself under its powers u/s 117B. The District Court's jurisdiction is barred.

Scenario 3: The Controller's Role
In an appeal against the grant of a patent (an opponent appealing u/s 25(4)), the appellant argues that the Controller made a fundamental error in interpreting the law. The respondent (patentee) argues the Controller was correct. What is the Controller's role in this appeal?
Answer: Under Section 117E, the Controller has a right to appear and be heard. Given that the appeal directly challenges the Controller's legal reasoning, it is highly likely the Board would either expect the Controller to appear or even formally direct him to do so to explain the rationale behind the decision. The Controller is the expert administrative authority, and his input assists the Board. His costs will be borne by the Central Government u/s 117F.

Chapter XIX: Appeals to the Appellate Board 🏛️

Section 116. Appellate Board

The Intellectual Property Appellate Board (IPAB) was a quasi-judicial body established to hear appeals against decisions of the Controller of Patents and the Registrar of Trademarks. It was dissolved on April 4, 2021. All pending cases were transferred to the High Courts. While this section is now obsolete, it's important to understand its historical role for context. The IPAB consisted of a Chairman and Technical Members.

Section 117. Staff of Appellate Board

This section is also obsolete as it dealt with the appointment of officers and employees of the IPAB. Their salaries and allowances were determined by the central government.

Section 117A. Appeals to Appellate Board

This section laid out the provisions for filing appeals. An appeal could be filed against any order or decision of the Controller of Patents by an aggrieved person. The appeal had to be filed within three months from the date of the order or decision, though the IPAB had the power to condone delays. The time limit for filing an appeal is now a crucial factor in patent law and is handled by the relevant High Courts.

Section 117B. Procedure and Powers of Appellate Board

This section detailed the powers of the IPAB, which were similar to those of a civil court. It could summon and enforce the attendance of witnesses, require the discovery and production of documents, and receive evidence on affidavits. The IPAB was not bound by the strict rules of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, but had to follow the principles of natural justice.

Section 117C. Bar of Jurisdiction of Courts, etc.

This section barred civil courts from exercising jurisdiction over any matter that the IPAB was empowered to decide. This was a critical provision designed to prevent parallel proceedings and ensure the IPAB was the sole authority for such appeals.

Section 117D. Procedure for application for rectification, etc., before Appellate Board

This section outlined the procedure for applications for the rectification of the register and other related matters. For instance, a person could apply to the IPAB for the revocation of a patent or the amendment of a patent specification.

Section 117E. Appearance of Controller in Legal Proceedings

The Controller of Patents had the right to appear and be heard in any proceedings before the IPAB that involved a decision made by them. This ensured the Controller's perspective was presented, especially in cases where their order was being challenged. The Controller was also entitled to a reasonable opportunity to be heard.

Section 117F. Costs of Controller in Proceedings before Appellate Board

This section ensured that the costs of the Controller in any proceeding before the IPAB were borne by the parties involved, as decided by the IPAB. This provision was meant to protect the Controller from undue financial burden when defending their decisions.

Section 117G. Transfer of Pending Proceedings to Appellate Board

This section was a transitional provision. It provided for the transfer of all appeals and other proceedings that were pending before any High Court to the IPAB after the latter's establishment. This ensured a smooth transition and centralized the patent litigation.

Section 117H. Power of Appellate Board to make rules

This section empowered the IPAB to make its own rules to regulate its procedure and the conduct of its business, in consultation with the central government.

Tricky MCQs 🧠

  1. A patent agent filed an appeal on behalf of their client against an order of the Controller of Patents. Due to a clerical error, the appeal was filed 95 days after the date of the order. According to the Patents Act, 1970 (as it was before the IPAB's dissolution), which of the following is correct? a) The appeal is automatically dismissed as it is filed after the statutory time limit of 90 days. b) The Appellate Board may condone the delay if sufficient cause is shown. c) The patent agent must file a fresh application to the Controller for a review of the order. d) The only recourse is to file a writ petition in the High Court. Answer: b) The Appellate Board (now the High Court) has the discretion to condone delays if a valid reason is provided. The statutory time limit is three months, which is approximately 90 days, but this power to condone delay is crucial.
  2. Which of the following matters could NOT be brought before the Appellate Board? a) An appeal against the refusal of a patent application. b) An application for the revocation of a patent. c) A petition for infringement of a patent. d) An appeal against an order of the Controller concerning a patent amendment. Answer: c) A petition for infringement of a patent. Infringement suits are heard by the High Court or a District Court, not the IPAB (or High Court on appeal). The IPAB/High Court on appeal dealt with appeals against the Controller's decisions.

Tricky Use Cases with Answers ⚖️

Use Case 1: The "Double-Decker" Appeal

A company, Innovate Corp., receives an adverse decision from the Controller of Patents regarding its patent application. The company's patent agent files an appeal with the High Court (after the IPAB dissolution) within the prescribed time limit. However, the company is also facing a patent infringement suit filed against it by a competitor in a different High Court. Innovate Corp. tries to file a motion to transfer the infringement suit to the same High Court hearing the appeal, arguing that both cases relate to the same patent.

Question: Is this a valid move? What is the likely outcome?

Answer: This is a tricky scenario because it involves two distinct types of proceedings. The appeal against the Controller's decision (regarding the patent's grant or refusal) is a statutory appeal. The infringement suit is a civil proceeding. The High Court hearing the infringement suit has jurisdiction over that matter. The High Court hearing the appeal from the Controller's decision has a different, specific jurisdiction. While both cases relate to the same patent, they are based on different legal provisions and have different objectives. The court will likely deny the transfer request because the two proceedings are fundamentally different and are governed by different legal principles and sections of the Patents Act. The bar on jurisdiction in the erstwhile Section 117C applied only to the matters that the IPAB was specifically empowered to handle, not general civil suits like infringement.

Tricky Scenarios with Answers 🤔

Scenario 1: The "Ghost" of the Appellate Board

A patent agent, Mr. Sharma, who has been practicing for a long time, advises his client to file a rectification of the patent register before the "Appellate Board" as he is used to the old system. The client correctly files the application with the relevant High Court. However, in the application, the client mistakenly refers to the "Appellate Board" as the forum.

Question: Will the application be dismissed on a technicality? What should be done?

Answer: No, the application will not be dismissed on this technicality. The courts are generally lenient on such clerical errors, especially when there has been a major statutory change. The legal maxim "ubi jus, ibi remedium" (where there is a right, there is a remedy) applies. The High Court has inherited the jurisdiction of the IPAB, so the forum is correct in substance. The applicant or their lawyer will be asked to correct the name of the forum in the application, and the court will proceed with the matter. This highlights the importance of staying updated with legal changes, especially for practicing patent agents.

TRICKY USE CASES WITH ANSWERS

Use Case 1: Appeal Against Compulsory License

Scenario: ABC Ltd was granted a compulsory license for a pharmaceutical patent. The patentee XYZ Pharma wants to challenge this order. Can they appeal to IPAB?

Answer: Yes, XYZ Pharma can appeal to IPAB under Section 117A as orders granting compulsory licenses under Sections 84, 91, 92 are specifically mentioned as appealable. The appeal must be filed within 3 months of the Controller's order granting the compulsory license.

Use Case 2: Interlocutory Order Challenge

Scenario: During patent examination, the Controller passed an interlocutory order directing the applicant to file additional documents. The applicant wants to appeal this order immediately.

Answer: No appeal lies against interlocutory orders. Section 117A specifically excludes interlocutory orders from appealable orders. The applicant must comply with the direction and can challenge the final order of refusal, if any.

Use Case 3: Jurisdiction Challenge

Scenario: A civil court entertained a suit for rectification of patent register while similar proceedings were pending before IPAB. Is this valid?

Answer: No, this is invalid. Section 117C bars the jurisdiction of civil courts over matters within IPAB's jurisdiction. The civil court should decline to entertain the suit as IPAB has exclusive jurisdiction over patent rectification matters.

Use Case 4: Time Limit Extension

Scenario: A patentee received the Controller's revocation order on 1st January but was traveling abroad. They filed appeal on 15th April (3.5 months later) requesting condonation of delay.

Answer: The appeal is time-barred as it exceeds the 3-month limit under Section 117A. While IPAB has discretionary powers, the statute provides a strict timeline. The patentee should have made arrangements for timely filing or sought extension before the expiry of limitation period.

Use Case 5: Controller's Participation

Scenario: In an appeal against grant of compulsory license, can the Controller participate and present arguments?

Answer: Yes, under Section 117E, the Controller has the right to appear and be heard in proceedings before IPAB, either in person or through a representative. This ensures the administrative perspective and public interest aspects are adequately presented.

TRICKY SCENARIOS WITH ANSWERS

Scenario 1: Multiple Appeal Rights

Question: A post-grant opposition was filed by Company A against Company B's patent. The Controller rejected the opposition. Company A filed an appeal to IPAB. Can Company B also file an appeal on different grounds?

Answer: No, Company B cannot file a separate appeal as they are the successful party before the Controller. However, they can file a cross-appeal or counter-objections if Company A's appeal raises issues that may affect their interests. Only "persons aggrieved" by the Controller's order can appeal.

Scenario 2: Transferred Proceedings Complexity

Question: A rectification proceeding was pending in High Court when IPAB was constituted. The matter was transferred to IPAB under Section 117G. The High Court had already recorded evidence. What happens to this evidence?

Answer: Under Section 117G, proceedings are transferred at the stage they have reached. The evidence recorded by the High Court would be considered by IPAB, and the proceeding would continue from where it was left off. IPAB may, if necessary, recall witnesses or seek additional evidence.

Scenario 3: Rule-Making Authority Conflict

Question: IPAB made rules regarding appeal procedure that seem to conflict with the Patents Rules. Which rules would prevail?

Answer: Under Section 117H, IPAB's rule-making power is subject to Central Government approval and must be consistent with the Act. If there's a conflict with existing Patents Rules, the matter should be resolved through proper consultation. Generally, specific provisions would prevail over general ones, but harmony should be maintained.

Scenario 4: Costs Against Public Interest

Question: In a compulsory license appeal where the Controller argued for public interest, IPAB dismissed the appeal. Can IPAB award costs against the Controller?

Answer: Yes, under Section 117F, IPAB has discretionary power to award costs to or against the Controller. However, when the Controller acts in public interest, courts generally don't award costs against public authorities unless there's frivolous or vexatious conduct.

Scenario 5: Writ Jurisdiction Overlap

Question: Can a party file a writ petition in High Court challenging IPAB's decision instead of going to Supreme Court under Article 136?

Answer: This is complex. Section 117C saves High Court's writ jurisdiction under Article 226, but typically, alternative statutory remedy (appeal to Supreme Court) should be exhausted first. High Court may entertain writ petition only in exceptional circumstances involving jurisdictional issues or fundamental procedural violations.

📘 CHAPTER XIX: APPEALS TO THE APPELLATE BOARD – EXAM NOTES

✅ Section 116. Appellate Board

  • The Central Government may constitute an Appellate Board.
  • It consists of a Chairperson and such number of Technical Members and Judicial Members as may be prescribed.
  • The Chairperson shall be appointed from among persons qualified for appointment as a Judge of a High Court or Chief Justice of a High Court.
  • Technical Members must have experience in science, engineering, or technology; Judicial Members must be qualified to be a District Judge.
  • Term: Not exceeding 5 years or until age of 65 (Chairperson), 62 (Members).
  • Reappointment allowed unless prohibited.

⚠️ Note: As per The Tribunals Reforms Act, 2021, the IPAB (Intellectual Property Appellate Board) was abolished w.e.f. April 4, 2021. Now, appeals lie directly to the High Court under Section 117A as amended.

📌 Current Position: The Appellate Board no longer exists. All pending matters transferred to High Courts. But for exam purposes, knowledge of pre-2021 structure is still tested.

✅ Section 117. Staff of Appellate Board

  • The Board can appoint officers and staff as deemed necessary.
  • Such appointments governed by regulations made by the Central Government.
  • The Secretary of the Board is responsible for administration.

🔍 Exam Tip: This section is rarely tested directly but important for procedural understanding.

✅ Section 117A. Appeals to Appellate Board (Repealed/Amended)

As originally enacted:

  • Any person aggrieved by a decision or order of the Controller could appeal to the Appellate Board within 3 months.
  • Examples of appealable orders:
    • Refusal of patent
    • Revocation of patent
    • Compulsory license
    • Amendment of specification
    • Opposition decisions (pre-grant/post-grant)

❗ After abolition of IPAB:

  • Now, appeals go directly to the High Court within three months under Section 117A (as substituted by Tribunals Reforms Act, 2021).

⚖️ Key Point: Only final orders are appealable; interlocutory orders not appealable unless specifically provided.

✅ Section 117B. Procedure and Powers of Appellate Board

  • The Board has powers of a civil court under CPC, including:
    • Summoning and enforcing attendance
    • Requiring discovery and production of documents
    • Receiving evidence on affidavit
    • Issuing commissions
    • Reviewing its own orders
  • Can regulate its own procedure.
  • Decisions by majority if more than one member.
  • Orders must be in writing with reasons.

📌 Important: The Board had revisional jurisdiction over Controller’s proceedings.

✅ Section 117C. Bar of Jurisdiction of Courts

  • No court shall have jurisdiction to entertain any suit or proceeding in respect of any matter which the Appellate Board is empowered to determine.
  • Also bars injunctions against proceedings before the Board.

⚠️ Exception: Writ jurisdiction under Articles 226/32 of Constitution remains available.

💡 Tricky Concept: While regular civil suits barred, constitutional remedies still open.

✅ Section 117D. Procedure for Application for Rectification, etc., before Appellate Board

  • Applications for rectification of register or revocation of patents were made to the Appellate Board.
  • However, after IPAB abolition:
    • These applications now go to the High Court.
  • Parties could adduce evidence, cross-examine, etc.
  • Power to amend claims during rectification existed subject to conditions.

🔁 Note: "Rectification" includes correction of error in name, address, ownership, invalidity, etc.

✅ Section 117E. Appearance of Controller in Legal Proceedings

  • The Controller may appear or be represented in any proceeding before the Appellate Board concerning:
    • Interpretation of the Act
    • Public interest
    • Administration of the Act

🎯 Purpose: Ensure uniform interpretation and protect public interest.

❗ The Controller is not a party but may assist the Board.

✅ Section 117F. Costs of Controller in Proceedings before Appellate Board

  • If the Controller appears, the costs incurred may be taxed and paid out of sums provided by Parliament.
  • Or, the Board may direct that such costs be borne by the parties in proportion it deems fit.

💬 Significance: Prevents misuse where frivolous appeals burden government resources.

✅ Section 117G. Transfer of Pending Proceedings to Appellate Board

  • The Central Government may direct transfer of any proceeding pending before one Appellate Board to another (if multiple Boards existed).
  • Also allows transfer of Controller’s proceedings to Appellate Board if deemed appropriate.

🔄 Post-abolition: All pending IPAB matters transferred to High Courts.

✅ Section 117H. Power of Appellate Board to Make Rules

  • The Board may make rules regulating its procedure, forms, maintenance of records, etc.
  • Subject to approval of Central Government.

⚠️ Rarely used; most procedures governed by Patent Rules.

Exam Notes on Chapter XIX: Appeals to the Appellate Board (Patents Act, 1970)

Chapter XIX of the Patents Act, 1970 deals with the establishment, functioning, and powers of the Intellectual Property Appellate Board (IPAB), which was the specialized tribunal for handling appeals related to patents (note: IPAB was abolished in 2021 by the Tribunals Reforms Act, and appeals now lie to High Courts, but these notes are based on the pre-2021 provisions as per the query). The chapter ensures efficient resolution of patent disputes by providing a dedicated appellate mechanism, barring court jurisdiction in certain matters, and outlining procedures. Below are detailed notes on each section, focusing on key provisions, purpose, and implications for exam preparation.

  • Section 116. Appellate Board This section establishes the IPAB. Key points:
    • The Appellate Board set up under Section 83 of the Trade Marks Act, 1999, serves as the IPAB for patents.
    • Composition: Chairman, Vice-Chairman, and other members (judicial and technical) appointed by the Central Government.
    • Qualifications: Chairman must be a former High Court Judge or have 5 years as Controller. Technical members need expertise in patent law, science, or technology.
    • Benches: IPAB functions through benches (one judicial + one technical member), sitting at places determined by the Chairman.
    • Purpose: To handle appeals and rectifications in IP matters.
    • Note: Emphasizes specialization in IP to ensure expert adjudication. Exam tip: Remember cross-reference to Trade Marks Act; questions may test on qualifications or bench composition.
  • Section 117. Staff of Appellate Board
    • The Central Government provides officers and staff to IPAB for efficient discharge of functions.
    • Appointment and conditions: Staff appointed by the President of IPAB with Central Government approval; salaries and service conditions prescribed by rules.
    • Purpose: Ensures administrative support for IPAB operations.
    • Note: Short section; often tested in MCQs for procedural details or government role.
  • Section 117A. Appeals to Appellate Board
    • Appealable matters: Lists orders appealable to IPAB, including refusal of application (s.15), opposition decisions (s.25), revocation (s.64), compulsory licensing (s.84-92A), etc.
    • Time limit: 3 months from the date of the order (extendable by IPAB on sufficient cause).
    • Exceptions: No appeal from Central Government orders or Controller's directions under certain sections (e.g., s.35 on secrecy).
    • Form: Appeal in prescribed form with fees.
    • Purpose: Provides remedy against Controller's decisions.
    • Note: Key for exams; memorize the list of appealable sections (e.g., 15, 16, 17, 19, 20, 25(1)-(4), 27, 28, 51, 54, 57, 60, 61, 63, 66, 68, 69, 70, 84, 85, 88, 91, 92, 92A). Tricky part: Appeals must be filed at the IPAB bench with territorial jurisdiction based on the appropriate office.
  • Section 117B. Procedure and powers of Appellate Board
    • Powers: IPAB has civil court powers under CPC, 1908 (summoning witnesses, discovery, affidavits, etc.). Not bound by CPC but guided by natural justice.
    • Procedure: Can regulate its own procedure; decisions by majority; Chairman can transfer cases between benches.
    • Purpose: Ensures fair and efficient hearings.
    • Note: IPAB can review its own decisions on clerical errors. Exam tip: Compare with s.77 (Controller's powers); questions may ask about IPAB's quasi-judicial nature.
  • Section 117C. Bar of jurisdiction of courts, etc.
    • No court or authority (except Supreme Court/High Court under writ jurisdiction) has jurisdiction over matters where appeal lies to IPAB.
    • Purpose: Centralizes IP disputes to avoid multiplicity of proceedings.
    • Note: Reinforces IPAB's exclusivity; often tested with scenarios on forum shopping or writ petitions.
  • Section 117D. Procedure for application for rectification, etc., before Appellate Board
    • Applications for rectification of register (e.g., under s.64 for revocation) filed to IPAB in prescribed form with fees.
    • Procedure: Notice to interested parties; hearing; IPAB can stay patent enforcement pending decision.
    • Purpose: Handles post-grant corrections or revocations.
    • Note: Links to s.64; exam questions may involve timing or who can apply (any interested person).
  • Section 117E. Appearance of Controller in legal proceedings
    • Controller can appear (or be represented) in any proceedings before IPAB where they have interest.
    • Notice: IPAB must notify Controller; Controller can submit written statements.
    • Purpose: Allows Controller to defend decisions.
    • Note: Short; tested on procedural rights.
  • Section 117F. Costs of Controller in proceedings before Appellate Board
    • Costs awarded to Controller in IPAB proceedings are payable to Central Government, not Controller personally.
    • Purpose: Protects public funds.
    • Note: Rarely standalone; combined with s.117E in questions.
  • Section 117G. Transfer of pending proceedings to Appellate Board
    • All pending appeals (except to High Courts) transferred to IPAB from the date it is established.
    • Purpose: Smooth transition when IPAB was set up (2003).
    • Note: Historical; questions may ask about transitional provisions.
  • Section 117H. Power of Appellate Board to make rules
    • IPAB can make rules (with Central Government approval) for its procedure, fees, forms, etc.
    • Rules laid before Parliament.
    • Purpose: Allows flexibility in operations.
    • Note: Links to Intellectual Property Appellate Board (Procedure) Rules, 2003.

Overall chapter notes: Focus on procedural timelines, appealable orders, and IPAB's powers. Cross-link with Chapters IV (examination), XI (opposition), and XIII (revocation). Post-2021 changes may be asked in advanced exams, but stick to 1970 Act for this.

  • Allows appeals against certain orders or decisions of the Controller or Central Government before IPAB, within prescribed time limits.

  • IPAB follows prescribed procedures and exercises powers similar to a civil court, including summoning witnesses and compelling document production.

  • Once jurisdiction is vested in IPAB, no other court or authority can entertain such matters, except Supreme Court appeals.

  • Anyone can apply to IPAB for rectification of the register of patents; ongoing proceedings are transferred to IPAB from courts.

Controller in Proceedings (Sections 117E–117F)

  • The Controller may appear and be heard in IPAB proceedings, and IPAB may award costs to or against the Controller, payable from government funds.

Transfer of Proceedings (Section 117G)

  • Pending proceedings before any court, regarding appeals, rectification, etc., are transferred to IPAB.

IPAB Rulemaking Powers (Section 117H)

  • IPAB is empowered to make its own procedural rules for patent matters.

Scenario 1: Bar of Jurisdiction

A patent owner loses a decision before the Controller and files an appeal in the local civil court. The defendant objects, citing Section 117C.
Analysis & Solution:
Section 117C specifically bars civil courts from adjudicating matters reserved for the Appellate Board. The court must transfer or refuse jurisdiction; the appeal must be filed with IPAB.

Scenario 2: Transfer of Pending Proceedings

A case for rectification of the Register of Patents is pending in the High Court when Chapter XIX comes into force.

As per Section 117G, all pending proceedings regarding rectification or appeal must be transferred to the IPAB. The court will forward the case files to IPAB.

The Appellate Board is asked to conduct video hearings for patent appeals, but there are no existing procedural rules for this method.

Under Section 117H, the Appellate Board itself has the power to make procedural rules for patent matters, including the conduct of video hearings if necessary.

Section 117F mandates that any cost awarded against the Controller in proceedings is payable out of Government funds, not personally by the Controller.

Examination notes: Chapter XIX Appeals to the Appellate Board (Sections 116-117H)

Note: The Intellectual Property Appellate Board (IPAB) was abolished by the Tribunal Reforms Act, 2021, and its functions are now transferred to the High Courts. However, since the request specifically mentions the Patent Agent 1970 examination, these notes will focus on the provisions related to the IPAB, which was the appellate body under the 1970 Act.

Key sections and provisions

  • Section 116. Appellate Board: The Appellate Board established under the Trade Marks Act, 1999, also functioned as the Appellate Board for the Patents Act, 1970.
  • Section 117. Staff of Appellate Board: This section covered the appointment and service conditions of the Appellate Board's staff.
  • Section 117A. Appeals to Appellate Board: This section outlined which decisions of the Controller or Central Government could be appealed to the Appellate Board. Appeals generally needed to be filed within three months, though extensions could be granted for sufficient cause. Certain government orders related to defense, public interest, or atomic energy were not appealable.
  • Section 117B. Procedure and powers of Appellate Board: The Appellate Board followed Natural Justice principles and could set its own procedures. It had Civil Court powers for receiving evidence and examining witnesses and was considered a Civil Court for certain Criminal Procedure Code purposes.
  • Section 117C. Bar of jurisdiction of courts, etc.: Other courts were barred from hearing matters appealable to the Appellate Board under Section 117A(2) or those under Section 117D.
  • Section 117D. Procedure for application for rectification, etc., before Appellate Board: This section described the process for seeking patent revocation or register rectification before the Appellate Board.
  • Section 117E. Appearance of Controller in legal proceedings: This section addressed the Controller's role in legal proceedings.
  • Section 117F. Costs of Controller in proceedings before Appellate Board: The Appellate Board had discretion over the Controller's costs but could not order the Controller to pay other parties' costs.
  • Section 117G. Transfer of pending proceedings to Appellate Board: Pending appeals and revocation/rectification cases (except counter-claims in infringement suits) from High Courts were transferred to the Appellate Board.
  • Section 117H. Power of Appellate Board to make rules: The Appellate Board could create rules for its procedures consistent with the Act.

Tricky use cases with answers

Exam Notes: Chapter XIX - Appeals to the Appellate Board (Sections 116-117H)

Key Provisions & Objectives

  • Purpose: Establishes the Appellate Board (now replaced by the Intellectual Property Appellate Board (IPAB), and post-2021, its functions transferred to Commercial Courts/High Courts under the Tribunals Reforms Act) as the primary forum for appeals against Controller orders, rectification, and revocation proceedings.
  • Jurisdiction: Exclusive appellate and original jurisdiction over patent disputes (except infringement suits).
  • Core Principles: Expedited resolution, specialized expertise, and exclusion of civil courts (except Supreme Court).
