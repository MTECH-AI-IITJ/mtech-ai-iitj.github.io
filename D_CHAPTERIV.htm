I. Tricky & Conceptual MCQs (Section 21)

MCQ Set 1: Core Legal Understanding

Q1. Exhibition of a design at an industrial exhibition will NOT invalidate its registration if:
 A. The design is exhibited only in India
 B. The exhibition is notified by Central Government
 C. Application is filed anytime before piracy
 D. Publication occurs only after registration

✅ Answer: B
 Explanation: Protection applies only to exhibitions notified by Central Government in Official Gazette.

Q2. Under Section 21, which of the following acts is expressly protected?
 A. Exhibition before filing without conditions
 B. Publication by proprietor during exhibition period
 C. Exhibition by third party without consent
 D. Commercial sale during exhibition

✅ Answer: B
 Explanation: Publication by exhibitor/proprietor during or after exhibition is protected subject to conditions.

Q3. “Privity or consent of the proprietor” becomes relevant when:
 A. The exhibitor is the proprietor
 B. The design is registered
 C. Exhibition is done by any other person
 D. The exhibition is outside India

✅ Answer: C
 Explanation: Protection extends even to third-party exhibition/publication without privity or consent.

MCQ Set 2: Time Limits & Procedure (Highly Examined)

Q4. The six-month period under proviso (b) is calculated from:
 A. Date of Gazette notification
 B. Date of exhibition closing
 C. Date of first exhibiting or first publication
 D. Date of application filing

✅ Answer: C
 Explanation: “Date of first exhibiting the design or article or publishing a description”.

Q5. If previous notice to Controller is not given but application is filed within six months, the result is:
 A. Registration valid
 B. Registration invalid
 C. Controller may condone delay
 D. Protection under Section 21 still applies

✅ Answer: B
 Explanation: Both conditions (a) & (b) are mandatory, not alternative.

Q6. Which form is required for previous notice to Controller?
 A. Prescribed under Designs Rules
 B. Any written communication
 C. Affidavit only
 D. No form prescribed

✅ Answer: A
 Explanation: Must be given in prescribed form under Designs Rules.

 A. Confidential disclosure

Q8. Publication “elsewhere” during or after exhibition by any person is protected provided:
 A. It is after registration

Part 1: Tricky & Comprehensive MCQs

Q1. "TechExpo 2023" is a major international technology fair held in New Delhi. An inventor, Mr. A, displays a novel ergonomic keyboard design at this expo on January 1, 2023. He files an application for design registration on June 30, 2023. However, it is later discovered that the Central Government never issued a notification in the Official Gazette extending the provisions of Section 21 to "TechExpo 2023". What is the legal status of Mr. A's design registration?

a) The registration is valid because the design was exhibited at a well-known international exhibition.
b) The registration is invalid as the disclosure at the exhibition constitutes prior publication, destroying novelty.
c) The registration is valid if Mr. A can prove he gave prior notice to the Controller.
d) The registration is valid only if the exhibition was held in India.

Answer: (b)
Explanation: The protection under Section 21 is not automatic for all exhibitions. It applies only to exhibitions specifically notified by the Central Government. Since TechExpo 2023 was not notified, the public display on January 1, 2023, acts as prior publication. The application filed on June 30 (beyond 6 months from January 1) would fail for lack of novelty. Condition (a) and (b) of the proviso are irrelevant if the main provision itself isn't triggered.

Q2. An Indian furniture designer exhibits a new chair design at the "NotifydExpo" (a duly notified exhibition) on March 15, 2023. A foreign competitor, who saw the design at the expo, independently publishes detailed photographs and an article about the chair design in an international design magazine on April 10, 2023. The Indian designer files her design application on August 1, 2023. Considering the competitor's actions, is her application valid?

a) No, the competitor's publication before the application date constitutes prior publication.
b) Yes, because Section 21 explicitly states that publication of a description by any person (even without consent) during/after the exhibition period does not invalidate the registration if conditions are met.
c) No, because the grace period only protects disclosures by the proprietor, not by third parties.
d) Yes, but only if she can sue the competitor for infringement first.

Answer: (b)
Explanation: This is a tricky but crucial protection offered by Section 21. The language is clear: "the exhibition of the design... or the publication of a description of the design by any person elsewhere during or after the period of the holding of the exhibition, without the privity or consent of the proprietor, shall not prevent the design from being registered...". Therefore, even unauthorized third-party publications stemming from the exhibition are shielded, provided the proprietor (the designer) complied with the notice and 6-month filing requirements.

Q3. A company exhibits a new automotive rim design at the "AutoExpo" (notified) on January 10. They also, as part of their marketing campaign, publish the design on their website accessible to the Indian public on January 5. They file the design application on July 3 of the same year. What is the most accurate assessment?

a) The application is valid as the exhibition was on January 10, and filing is within 6 months (by July 10).
b) The application is invalid because the website publication on January 5 is the "first exhibiting/publishing," and filing on July 3 is beyond 6 months from January 5.
c) The application is valid because the website publication is not an "exhibition" under the Act.
d) The grace period starts from the date of the notified exhibition, overriding any other prior disclosure.

Answer: (b)
Explanation: The proviso to Section 21 states that the application must be made "within six months from the date of first exhibiting the design or article or publishing a description of the design." The key term is "first". The website publication on January 5, being a public disclosure, is likely the first publication. The 6-month grace period runs from that date (Jan 5 -> July 4/5). Filing on July 3 is beyond 6 months from January 5, thus invalidating the claim to the grace period. The design may be considered publicly known from Jan 5.

Q4. Which of the following is NOT a mandatory condition for claiming the benefit of Section 21?

a) The exhibition must be an "industrial or other exhibition" notified by the Central Government.
b) The exhibitor must have obtained written consent from the Controller before exhibiting.
c) The exhibitor must give the Controller a previous notice in the prescribed form before the exhibition.
d) The application for registration must be filed within six months of the first exhibition/publication.

Answer: (b)
Explanation: The conditions are listed in the proviso: (a) previous notice to the Controller, and (b) filing within 6 months. The main body requires the exhibition to be notified. There is no requirement to obtain prior consent or permission from the Controller to exhibit; only a prior notice is required. This is a common point of confusion.

Hypothetical Previous Year Question:

Explanation: The grace period provided under Section 21 of the Designs Act, 2000 is 6 months, not 12 months. (A 12-month grace period is associated with the novelty criteria for patents under certain circumstances in the Patents Act, not for designs).

Part 3: Use Case

Scenario: "DesiCraft," an Indian handicrafts SME, is preparing to showcase its innovative new line of modular terracotta lamp designs at the "India International Trade Fair (IITF)," which is a duly notified exhibition under the Designs Act.

Action Plan for DesiCraft (Leveraging Section 21):

  1. Before the Exhibition: The company's IP manager fills out Form 5 (Notice of exhibition) as prescribed under the Designs Rules and submits it to the office of the Controller General of Designs before the IITF opens. This secures their intent to claim the grace period.
  2. At the Exhibition: DesiCraft confidently exhibits the lamps, collects market feedback, and gauges commercial interest without fear that this public display will ruin their chance to register the design.
  3. Post-Exhibition: The company has a critical deadline: 6 months from the first day the lamps were shown at IITF. They must use this time to finalize the design application, ensure the representations are perfect, and file Form 1 (Application for registration) along with the requisite fees well before the 6-month expiry.
  4. Risk Mitigated: Any visitor, blogger, or competitor who takes a picture and publishes it online during or after the fair cannot use that publication to challenge the novelty of DesiCraft's subsequently filed design application.

Part 4: Case Study

Case Title: The Dilemma of "QuickGadget Pvt. Ltd."

Facts:

  1. QuickGadget developed a novel foldable smartphone stand design.
  2. On February 1, 2023, they launched the product for sale on Amazon India.
  3. On March 1, 2023, they displayed the same stand at "InnoTech 2023," an exhibition officially notified by the Government.
  4. They filed a design application for the stand on August 25, 2023.

Problem: The Controller of Designs refused the application, citing "lack of novelty due to prior publication."

Arguments:

●      QuickGadget's Argument: They claim protection under Section 21. The design was exhibited at a notified exhibition (InnoTech) on March 1. They filed within 6 months of that date (by September 1). Therefore, the exhibition disclosure should not invalidate the registration.

●      Controller's Counter-Argument: Section 21's grace period runs from the "date of first exhibiting...or publishing." The first public disclosure/commercial offering was the sale on Amazon on February 1, 2023. This was before the exhibition and was not at a notified exhibition. The 6-month period for filing started on February 1, making the deadline August 1, 2023. The application filed on August 25 is beyond this period.

Legal Analysis & Outcome:
The Controller's refusal is legally sound. Section 21 offers a limited safe harbor for disclosures made at or after a notified exhibition. It does not "reset the clock" for any prior, non-exhibition related disclosures. The commercial sale on February 1 was an act that made the design available to the public in India, constituting prior publication under Section 4(b) of the Act. The grace period, if any, began on that date. QuickGadget failed to file within 6 months of that first disclosure.

Learning: The Section 21 grace period is not a substitute for timely filing. It is a specific exception for a specific type of disclosure (exhibition). Proprietors must ensure that the first-ever public disclosure of a design occurs either at a notified exhibition (after giving notice) or, if disclosed elsewhere first, a design application is filed within 6 months of that first disclosure, irrespective of any subsequent exhibition.

●      Alpha Tech followed the first proviso (gave previous notice).

●      The application was filed 7.5 months after the first exhibition.

THE DESIGNS ACT, 2000 - CHAPTER IV

INDUSTRIAL AND INTERNATIONAL EXHIBITIONS (Section 21)

COMPREHENSIVE MCQs

Basic Concepts

Q1. Which authority has the power to extend provisions of Section 21 to exhibitions? A) State Government B) Controller of Designs C) Central Government D) High Court

<details> <summary>Answer</summary> **C) Central Government** - Section 21 explicitly states that the Central Government extends these provisions by notification in the Official Gazette. </details>

Q2. What is the maximum time period within which an application for registration must be filed after exhibition to avail protection under Section 21? A) 3 months B) 6 months C) 12 months D) 18 months

<details> <summary>Answer</summary> **B) 6 months** - Proviso (b) mandates filing within six months from the date of first exhibiting the design/article or publishing its description. </details>

Tricky/Advanced Questions

Q3. An inventor displays his design at an approved exhibition on January 15, 2024. A competitor, without the inventor's consent, publishes the design description on February 10, 2024. The inventor files for registration on July 20, 2024. What is the status of the application? A) Valid - calculated from February 10 B) Invalid - 6-month period expired C) Valid - calculated from January 15 D) Invalid - lack of prior notice

<details> <summary>Answer</summary> **B) Invalid - 6-month period expired** - The 6-month period runs from the date of FIRST exhibiting (January 15), not from unauthorized publication. July 20 exceeds 6 months from January 15. </details>

Q4. Which of the following is NOT a mandatory requirement under Section 21 provisos? A) Previous notice to Controller in prescribed form B) Application within 6 months C) Exhibition at Government-approved venue D) Payment of double registration fee

<details> <summary>Answer</summary> **D) Payment of double registration fee** - There is no mention of any special fee structure in Section 21. </details>

Q5. Section 21 provides protection against invalidation due to exhibition. This protection extends to: A) Only the proprietor's own exhibition B) Only unauthorized exhibitions by third parties C) Both proprietor's exhibition and unauthorized exhibitions by others D) Only publications, not exhibitions

<details> <summary>Answer</summary> **C) Both proprietor's exhibition and unauthorized exhibitions by others** - The section covers exhibition/publication by the proprietor (with notice) AND exhibition/publication "by any person elsewhere...without the privity or consent of the proprietor." </details>

Q6. The phrase "without the privity or consent of the proprietor" refers to: A) Only the exhibitor's actions B) Only third-party actions C) Third-party actions that occur without proprietor's knowledge or permission D) Government actions

<details> <summary>Answer</summary> **C) Third-party actions that occur without proprietor's knowledge or permission** - "Privity" means knowledge/relationship, and "consent" means permission. The phrase protects against unauthorized third-party disclosures. </details>

Q7. A design is exhibited at an approved exhibition on March 1st. The exhibitor gives prior notice to the Controller on March 5th. Is the protection under Section 21 available? A) Yes, as notice was given during the exhibition B) No, notice must be "previous notice" (before exhibition) C) Yes, if application is filed within 6 months D) No, unless the Controller grants exemption

<details> <summary>Answer</summary> **B) No, notice must be "previous notice" (before exhibition)** - Proviso (a) requires "previous notice," meaning notice must be given BEFORE the exhibition occurs. </details>

Q8. Which of the following exhibitions would automatically qualify for Section 21 protection? A) Any international trade fair B) Any industrial exhibition in India C) Only exhibitions notified by Central Government in Official Gazette D) All exhibitions organized by industry associations

<details> <summary>Answer</summary> **C) Only exhibitions notified by Central Government in Official Gazette** - Section 21 applies only to exhibitions "to which the provisions of this section have been extended by the Central Government by notification in the Official Gazette." </details>

Q9. The "publication of a description of the design" under Section 21 includes: A) Only printed publications B) Only official patent/design journals C) Any form of public disclosure including digital media D) Only publications by the proprietor

<details> <summary>Answer</summary> **C) Any form of public disclosure including digital media** - The Act does not restrict the mode of publication. Modern interpretation includes all forms of public disclosure. </details>

Q10. An applicant exhibits a design at an approved exhibition with proper notice on June 1st. A third party independently develops and exhibits a similar design on July 1st at another exhibition. The applicant files on November 15th. What is the effect? A) Application invalid due to third-party disclosure B) Application valid; Section 21 protects against both disclosures C) Application valid only if third party is sued D) Application invalid as 6-month period is calculated from July 1st

<details> <summary>Answer</summary> **B) Application valid; Section 21 protects against both disclosures** - Section 21 protects against the applicant's own disclosure (with notice) and third-party disclosures (with or without consent). The 6-month period runs from June 1st (first exhibition = November 15th is within 6 months). </details>

USE CASES

Use Case 1: Successful Exhibition Protection

Scenario: Ms. Sharma, a furniture designer, creates an innovative modular sofa design. The "Delhi Furniture Expo 2024" has been notified by the Central Government under Section 21.

Actions:

●      February 1, 2024: Ms. Sharma sends previous notice to Controller in Form-3

●      February 15, 2024: She exhibits the sofa at Delhi Furniture Expo

●      March 1, 2024: A competitor photographs and posts the design on social media

●      July 10, 2024: Ms. Sharma files design registration application

Outcome:

●      The registration is valid and protected under Section 21

●      Neither her own exhibition (with notice) nor the unauthorized publication by competitor invalidates registration

●      Filing is within 6 months of first exhibition (February 15)

Legal Principle Applied: Section 21 protects against both authorized exhibition (with notice) and unauthorized third-party disclosure.

Use Case 2: Failure to Meet Requirements

Scenario: Mr. Kumar exhibits his innovative watch design at "India Watch Fair 2024" (notified exhibition) on March 1st, 2024.

Actions:

●      March 1: Exhibition begins without prior notice to Controller

●      March 10: Realizes mistake and sends notice to Controller

●      August 15: Files design registration application

Outcome:

●      Application is vulnerable to invalidation on two grounds:

1.     No "previous notice" (notice given after exhibition started)

2.     Filing beyond 6 months from first exhibition

Legal Principle Applied: Both provisos must be satisfied. Failure to meet either condition removes Section 21 protection.

Use Case 3: International Exhibition Complexity

Scenario: Indian designer exhibits jewelry design at "Milan Design Week 2024" (not notified under Section 21) on April 1st, then at "India Jewelry Expo 2024" (notified exhibition) on May 1st with proper notice.

Actions:

●      April 1: Exhibition in Milan (no notice, not notified exhibition)

●      May 1: Exhibition in India with previous notice to Controller

●      October 15: Files application in India

Outcome:

●      The April 1st Milan exhibition creates prior disclosure not protected by Section 21

●      Even though Indian exhibition had proper notice, the earlier unprotected disclosure may affect novelty

●      6-month period would run from April 1 (first exhibition), making October 15 filing late

Legal Principle Applied: Section 21 only protects exhibitions at notified venues. Prior unprotected disclosures can affect validity.

Use Case 4: Third-Party Independent Development

Scenario: Designer A exhibits textile pattern at notified exhibition on June 1st with proper notice. Designer B independently develops similar pattern and exhibits it on June 15th without Designer A's knowledge.

Actions:

●      June 1: Designer A's exhibition (with notice)

●      June 15: Designer B's independent exhibition

●      November 1: Designer A files application

Outcome:

●      Designer A's registration is valid under Section 21

●      Section 21 explicitly protects against "exhibition...by any person elsewhere...without the privity or consent of the proprietor"

●      However, if Designer B also files, priority would need to be determined based on actual creation dates

Legal Principle Applied: Section 21 protects against third-party disclosures but doesn't resolve priority disputes between independent creators.

CASE STUDY: THE MODULAR FURNITURE SAGA

Background

InnoDesign Pvt. Ltd. developed a revolutionary modular furniture system featuring interlocking components with a unique geometric design. The company wanted to test market reception before committing to design registration costs.

Timeline of Events

January 15, 2024:

●      InnoDesign learns that "India Home Expo 2024" (scheduled February 10-18) has been notified by Central Government under Section 21

●      Decision made to exhibit the design

January 25, 2024:

●      InnoDesign submits previous notice to Controller of Designs in prescribed Form-3

●      Notice includes: exhibition name, dates, design description, and applicant details

February 12, 2024:

●      Design exhibited at India Home Expo with great success

●      Media coverage includes photographs and descriptions in trade magazines

●      Several potential competitors take photos and videos

February 20, 2024:

●      A competitor "CopyCat Designs" starts manufacturing similar products

●      They argue the design is now in public domain due to exhibition

March 5, 2024:

●      Another competitor publishes detailed technical drawings of the design on their website

●      Claims they "reverse-engineered" it from exhibition photos

July 30, 2024:

●      InnoDesign files design registration application

●      References Section 21 protection and provides evidence of prior notice

September 2024:

●      CopyCat Designs files opposition claiming design lacks novelty due to February exhibition

●      InnoDesign responds citing Section 21 protection

Legal Issues

  1. Whether the exhibition destroyed novelty?

○      NO - Exhibition at notified venue with previous notice is protected

  1. Whether unauthorized publications by competitors affect validity?

○      NO - Section 21 explicitly protects against exhibitions/publications "without the privity or consent of the proprietor"

  1. Whether the 6-month deadline was met?

○      YES - First exhibition: February 12; Filing: July 30 (168 days = within 6 months)

  1. Whether all procedural requirements were satisfied?

○      YES - Previous notice given on January 25 (before exhibition)

○      Exhibition was at notified venue

○      Application filed within 6 months

Controller's Decision

Held:

  1. InnoDesign complied with both provisos of Section 21
  2. The exhibition and subsequent publications (authorized and unauthorized) do not invalidate the design
  3. CopyCat's opposition is dismissed
  4. Design registration granted to InnoDesign

  3. Protection is Comprehensive: Covers both proprietor's disclosure and third-party unauthorized acts
  4. Notification is Essential: Only exhibitions notified by Central Government qualify
  5. Evidence Preservation: Keep all documents (notice submission proof, exhibition dates, media coverage) for potential disputes
