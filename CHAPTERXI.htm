Exam Notes on Chapter XI: Restoration of Lapsed Patents

Section 60: Applications for Restoration of Lapsed Patents

  • Objective: Allows restoration of patents that lapsed due to non-payment of renewal fees.
  • Eligibility: Only the patentee or their legal representative may apply.
  • Time Limit: Application must be filed within 18 months from the date the patent ceased to have effect (due to non-payment).
  • Conditions:
    • Failure to pay renewal fees must be unintentional.
    • No undue delay in applying for restoration after the failure is discovered.
  • Formalities:
    • Submit Form 15 (prescribed form) with the Controller.
    • Pay the prescribed fee.
    • Include a statement explaining reasons for non-payment and delay.
  • Key Principle: Restoration is not automatic; the applicant must prove "unintentional failure" and "no undue delay."

Section 61: Procedure for Disposal of Applications

  • Initial Scrutiny: Controller verifies formal compliance (form, fee, statement).
  • Publication: Application is published in the Patent Office Journal if accepted.
  • Opposition:
    • Any person may oppose within 2 months of publication (extendable by Controller).
    • Grounds for opposition:
      • Failure to pay fees was intentional.
      • Undue delay in applying for restoration.
      • Restoration against public interest.
  • Hearing: Controller provides opportunities for both parties to present arguments.
  • Decision:
    • If satisfied, Controller restores the patent.
    • If opposed, Controller may refuse restoration after considering evidence.
  • Effect of Restoration: Patent is revived from the date of cessation, subject to Section 62 limitations.

Section 62: Rights of Patentees of Restored Patents

  • General Rule: Restored patent is deemed never to have lapsed.
  • Limitations on Rights:
    • No Damages for Past Infringement: Patentee cannot claim damages for infringement during the lapse period.
    • Good Faith Users Protected: Persons who, during the lapse period:
      • Made, used, or sold the invention in India in good faith.
      • Had no notice of the restoration application.
      • May continue these acts post-restoration without liability.
    • Post-Restoration Infringement: Patentee can enforce rights against acts occurring after restoration.
  • Public Interest: Restoration does not invalidate prior good-faith use or transactions.

Tricky MCQs with Answers

  1. Question: A patent lapsed on 1st January 2023 due to non-payment. The patentee applied for restoration on 30th June 2024. Is the application timely?
    a) Yes, as it is within 18 months.
    b) No, as it exceeds 18 months.
    c) Yes, if the delay was unintentional.
    d) No, unless the Controller extends the deadline.
    Answer: (b) No, as it exceeds 18 months.
    Reason: The 18-month deadline is mandatory (1st Jan 2023 to 30th June 2024 is 18 months, but the deadline is 1st July 2024; 30th June is within the period). Correction: 30th June 2024 is exactly 18 months from 1st Jan 2023, so it is timely. The correct answer should be (a) Yes, as it is within 18 months. However, the trick is that the applicant must still prove unintentional failure and no undue delay.*
  2. Question: After restoration, can the patentee sue a company that manufactured the invention during the lapse period?
    a) Yes, for infringement during the lapse period.
    b) No, if the company acted in good faith without notice of restoration.
    c) Yes, but only for acts after restoration.
    d) No, restoration voids all prior infringement claims.
    Answer: (b) No, if the company acted in good faith without notice of restoration.
    Reason: Section 62 protects good-faith users during the lapse period. The patentee cannot claim damages for past acts but can enforce rights against post-restoration infringement.*
  3. Question: Which ground is NOT valid for opposing a restoration application?
    a) The patentee intentionally delayed payment.
    b) Restoration would harm public health.
    c) The invention lacks novelty.
    d) The patentee delayed applying for restoration.
    Answer: (c) The invention lacks novelty.
    Reason: Opposition under Section 61 is limited to intentional failure, undue delay, or public interest. Novelty is a pre-grant issue and irrelevant to restoration.*

Tricky Use Cases with Answers

Use Case 1: Unintentional Failure vs. Undue Delay

Scenario: A patent lapsed on 1st March 2023. The patentee, hospitalized until 1st May 2023, applied for restoration on 1st October 2024 (19 months post-lapse). They proved non-payment was unintentional (due to hospitalization) but could not explain the 5-month delay after discharge.
Question: Should the Controller restore the patent?
Answer: No.
Reason: While non-payment was unintentional, the 5-month delay after discharge constitutes "undue delay" under Section 60. The 18-month deadline is strict, and the applicant must justify the entire delay period.*

Use Case 2: Good Faith User vs. Post-Restoration Infringement

Scenario: A patent lapsed on 1st January 2023. Company X manufactured the invention in India from 1st February 2023 to 1st December 2023, unaware of the lapse. The patent was restored on 1st January 2024. Company X continued manufacturing post-restoration.
Question: Can the patentee sue Company X for post-restoration infringement?
Answer: No.
Reason: Under Section 62, Company X is a "good faith user" who acted without notice of restoration. They may continue manufacturing post-restoration without liability, as their use began during the lapse period.*

Tricky Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1: International Use During Lapse

Situation: A patent lapsed on 1st June 2023. A U.S. company exported the invention to India on 1st August 2023. The patent was restored on 1st December 2023. The patentee sued for infringement.
Question: Is the U.S. company liable?
Answer: No for past acts; yes for future acts.
Reason: Section 62 protects good-faith users in India. The U.S. company’s export occurred during the lapse period, so no damages can be claimed. However, post-restoration exports would infringe, as the protection only covers acts done in India during the lapse.*

Scenario 2: Partial Payment of Renewal Fees

Situation: A patentee paid 50% of the renewal fee before the deadline, forgetting the balance. The patent lapsed. They applied for restoration within 18 months, arguing partial payment showed "intent" to maintain rights.
Question: Will restoration be granted?
Answer: Unlikely.
Reason: Section 60 requires full payment. Partial payment indicates negligence, not "unintentional failure." The Controller may deem this intentional non-compliance, barring restoration.*

Exam Notes: Chapter XI - Restoration of Lapsed Patents

This chapter provides a crucial safety net for patentees who fail to pay renewal fees, leading to the patent lapsing. It balances the patentee's interest in reviving their right with the public's interest in legal certainty.

Key Concepts & Triggers for Lapse

  • Lapse occurs due to non-payment of a renewal fee within the prescribed period (including the grace period with surcharge) under Section 53.
  • The patent is deemed to have lapsed from the date of the unpaid renewal fee, not the end of the grace period.

Section 60: Application for Restoration

  • Who can apply? The patentee or their legal representative.
  • When to apply? Within 18 months from the date of lapse (i.e., the date the renewal fee was due).
  • Ground for Application: The failure to pay the fee must be shown to be unintentional.
  • Content of Application (Form 15): Must state the circumstances that led to the failure to pay. It must be supported by evidence (typically an affidavit).
  • Required Actions: The application must include a statement of unpaid renewal fees and any additional prescribed fees. The unpaid renewal fee and the restoration application fee must be paid.

Section 61: Procedure for Disposal of Application

  1. Advertisement: The Controller will advertise the application in the Official Journal. This is to notify the public and potential opponents.
  2. Opposition Period: Any person (often a competitor) can oppose the restoration within 2 months of the advertisement.
  3. Grounds for Opposition:
    • The failure to pay the renewal fee was not unintentional.
    • There has been undue delay in making the application.
  4. Controller's Role: The Controller will hear both the applicant and the opponent (if any) before deciding.
  5. Possible Orders: The Controller may either:
    • Allow the application (order restoration), or
    • Refuse it, or
    • Refer the question to the Central Government if the invention is relevant for defense purposes.

Section 62: Rights of Patentees of Restored Patents

This is the most critical and trickiest section, dealing with the legal consequences of restoration.

  • General Principle: Upon restoration, the patent is revived and considered never to have lapsed.
  • Key Protection for the Public (Intervening Rights): To protect those who acted in good faith during the "lapsed" period, the rights of the patentee are restricted for acts done in the "intervening period".
  • The "Intervening Period": Defined as the period between the date of lapse and the date of publication of the application for restoration in the Official Journal.
  • Acts that are NOT infringing (The Exceptions):
    1. Any act done in respect of the invention in the intervening period.
    2. The continuation of that specific act (e.g., continued manufacture or sale of a product made during the intervening period) after restoration.
  • Acts that ARE infringing:
    • Any new act started after the restoration is published. For example, if a competitor starts manufacturing the patented product after the restoration notice is published, it would be an infringement.

Tricky Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

1. The 'intervening period' for the purpose of determining the rights of a restored patentee is defined as the period between:
a) The date the renewal fee was due and the date it was actually paid.
b) The date of lapse of the patent and the date of grant of the restoration application.
c) The date of lapse of the patent and the date of advertisement of the restoration application.
d) The date the renewal fee was due and the date of the Controller's order for restoration.
Answer: c) The date of lapse of the patent and the date of advertisement of the restoration application. (This is the precise legal definition per Section 62).

a) If the patentee can prove the invention is commercially successful.
b) If the failure to pay the renewal fee was unintentional.
c) If no third party has begun using the invention during the lapse period.
d) If the Controller is satisfied it is in the public interest.
Answer: b) If the failure to pay the renewal fee was unintentional. (This is the sole ground mentioned in S.60).

3. A competitor, Mr. X, began manufacturing a product that fell within the scope of a lapsed patent in January. The application for restoration was advertised in the Official Journal in March. The Controller ordered restoration in May. Can Mr. X continue manufacturing the product in June?
a) No, because the patent has been restored, making all acts of manufacture infringing.
b) Yes, because he began the act during the intervening period (lapse to advertisement).
c) Only if he obtains a license from the patentee after restoration.
d) No, because he should have opposed the restoration application.
Answer: b) Yes, because he began the act during the intervening period. (S.62 explicitly protects acts done in the intervening period and their continuation).

4. The time limit for filing an application for restoration of a lapsed patent is:
a) 12 months from the date of lapse.
b) 6 months from the expiry of the grace period for renewal.
c) 18 months from the date on which the patent ceased to have effect.
d) There is no time limit if the delay is explained.
Answer: c) 18 months from the date on which the patent ceased to have effect. (This is specified in S.60(1)).

Tricky Use Cases & Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1: The Overlooked Deadline
A patent lapsed on January 1, 2023, due to non-payment of the renewal fee. The patentee's in-house counsel was on long leave and the reminder was missed. The company realized the mistake on December 1, 2024. They filed an application for restoration on December 5, 2024, paying all due fees. Is the application valid?

Answer: No. The application was filed more than 18 months after the lapse (Jan 1, 2023 to Dec 5, 2024 is over 23 months). The 18-month period is a strict statutory deadline. The Controller has no discretion to extend it. The patent cannot be restored.

Scenario 2: The Strategic Competitor
Patent A lapses. The patentee files for restoration, which is advertised. Company B, a competitor, had begun using the invention after the lapse but before the advertisement. After the patent is successfully restored, Company B not only continues its existing production but also invests in and opens a brand new, larger factory dedicated solely to manufacturing the patented product.

Question: Is the operation of the new factory an infringement of the restored patent?

Answer: Yes. The original act of starting production is protected, and its continuation from the original factory is also protected. However, starting a new, separate act (building and operating a new factory) after the restoration was advertised constitutes a fresh commercial exploitation after the patent's rights are publicly known to be revived. This new act is not protected by the intervening rights clause and would be an infringement.

Scenario 3: The "Innocent" Opponent
A patent for a chemical process lapses. A chemical company, after conducting a thorough search confirming the lapse, spends significant capital to implement the process in its plant. Two months later, it sees the application for restoration advertised in the Official Journal.

Question: What is the best course of action for the chemical company to protect its investment?

Answer: It should file a notice of opposition to the restoration application under Section 61. The key ground would be to argue that the patentee's failure to pay was not unintentional (e.g., perhaps they had abandoned the patent strategically). If the opposition fails and the patent is restored, the company's rights are protected under Section 62 for the acts it did during the intervening period (lapse to advertisement). The opposition is its only chance to try and prevent the restoration altogether.

Chapter XI: Restoration of Lapsed Patents

Section 60: Applications for Restoration of Lapsed Patents

A lapsed patent can be restored under Section 60 of the Indian Patents Act, 1970. This section outlines the conditions and procedure for filing an application for restoration. A patent lapses if the renewal fee isn't paid within the prescribed time. ⏳ The patentee or their legal representative can apply for restoration. The application must be filed within 18 months from the date the patent ceased to have effect.

The application must include a statement that the failure to pay the renewal fee was unintentional. It must also explain the circumstances that led to this failure. The Controller then considers the application and, if satisfied, publishes the application in the official gazette.

Section 61: Procedure for Disposal of Applications for Restoration

Section 61 details the process the Controller follows when handling a restoration application. 🧐

  1. Advertisement: The Controller publishes the application to invite any opposition from the public.
  2. Opposition Period: Any person can file an opposition to the restoration within a specified period. The grounds for opposition must be related to the patentee's failure to pay the renewal fee.
  3. Hearing: The Controller may hold a hearing to decide the matter. Both the applicant and the opponent have the opportunity to present their case.
  4. Decision: The Controller decides whether to restore the patent. The restoration is typically granted if the failure to pay the fee was unintentional and not due to a deliberate act.

If the patent is restored, it's subject to certain conditions and protections for third parties.

Section 62: Rights of Patentees of Lapsed Patents Which Have Been Restored

Section 62 defines the rights of a patentee once their lapsed patent is restored. 🛡️ The restored patent's rights are not absolute. They are subject to certain limitations to protect third parties who may have started using the invention after the patent lapsed.

A restored patentee cannot sue for infringement that occurred during the period the patent was lapsed. Furthermore, any person who started manufacturing, using, or selling the patented invention in the interim period (between lapse and restoration) has the right to continue doing so. They also have the right to import or sell any articles made during that period. This is known as the "right of a person who has used the invention during the period of lapse."

Tricky MCQs

  1. A patentee fails to pay the renewal fee. The patent lapses on January 1, 2024. What's the latest date by which the patentee can apply for restoration?
    • A. January 1, 2025
    • B. June 30, 2025
    • C. July 1, 2025
    • D. January 1, 2026

Answer: C. The application for restoration must be filed within 18 months from the date the patent ceased to have effect. 18 months from January 1, 2024, is June 30, 2025. The application must be filed on or before this date. Therefore, the last day is June 30, 2025, but the question asks for the latest date. It can be tricky, as the application can be filed on the last day. The date after June 30, 2025, would be July 1, 2025. This question's phrasing is designed to trip you up. The correct answer would be June 30, 2025. However, if the options are as given, the spirit of the question is to know the 18-month deadline, which ends on June 30, 2025. Let's re-examine the law: The patent "lapsed on January 1, 2024." The 18-month period would end on July 1, 2025. Therefore, the latest date is July 1, 2025.

  1. A restored patent's rights are retroactive to the date of lapse. True or False?

Answer: False. The rights are not retroactive concerning infringement during the lapsed period. This is a key limitation under Section 62.

  1. Under what circumstance can a person continue to use a restored invention?
    • A. If they started using it before the patent was granted.
    • B. If they started using it after the patent was granted but before it lapsed.
    • C. If they started using it during the period the patent was lapsed.
    • D. If they started using it at any time, with permission from the patentee.

Answer: C. This is the main provision of Section 62 to protect third-party rights.

Tricky Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1: A company, "Innovate Co.," discovers that a competitor, "Tech Corp," is manufacturing an invention that Innovate Co.'s patent has lapsed on. Tech Corp started manufacturing after the lapse. Innovate Co. successfully restores its patent. Can Innovate Co. sue Tech Corp for damages for the period the patent was lapsed?

Answer: No. According to Section 62, the patentee cannot sue for infringement that occurred during the period of lapse. Tech Corp also has a right to continue manufacturing and selling the product.

Scenario 2: A patent agent submits an application for restoration, but the Controller finds that the patentee had deliberately let the patent lapse because they were in a financial crisis and wanted to save on renewal fees. Is the Controller likely to grant the restoration?

Answer: No. The Controller must be satisfied that the failure to pay the renewal fee was "unintentional." A deliberate act, even if for financial reasons, would not be considered unintentional. The application will likely be rejected.

CHAPTER XI - RESTORATION OF LAPSED PATENTS

Complete Study Guide for Patent Agent Examination

SECTION 60: Applications for Restoration of Lapsed Patents

Key Provisions:

Section 60(1) - Where a patent has ceased to have effect by reason of failure to pay any renewal fee within the prescribed time or within the period of grace, the patentee or his legal representative may apply to the Controller for restoration of the patent.

Section 60(2) - Application must be made within 18 months from the date on which the patent ceased to have effect, accompanied by:

  • Statement setting out circumstances leading to failure to pay renewal fee
  • Payment of restoration fee and unpaid renewal fee

Section 60(3) - Controller may require applicant to advertise the application in prescribed manner.

Critical Points:

  • Time Limit: 18 months from lapse (ABSOLUTE deadline)
  • Who can apply: Patentee or legal representative only
  • Grounds: Non-payment of renewal fee (within time or grace period)
  • Requirements: Statement of circumstances + fees + possible advertisement

SECTION 61: Procedure for Disposal of Applications for Restoration

Key Provisions:

Section 61(1) - If Controller is satisfied that:

  • Failure to pay renewal fee was unintentional
  • There has been no undue delay in making application
  • Patent shall be restored

Section 61(2) - Notice of restoration shall be published in Official Gazette

Section 61(3) - Controller may impose conditions on restoration

Critical Points:

  • Test: "Unintentional failure" + "No undue delay"
  • Discretionary power: Controller may impose conditions
  • Publication: Mandatory in Official Gazette
  • Effect: Patent restored with retrospective effect

SECTION 62: Rights of Patentees of Lapsed Patents Which Have Been Restored

Key Provisions:

Section 62(1) - Rights of patentee shall not extend to:

  • Any act done in good faith during the period when patent was lapsed
  • Preparations made in good faith during lapse period

Section 62(2) - Person who during lapse period:

  • In good faith began working invention in India, or
  • Made effective and serious preparations for working
  • May continue to work invention

Section 62(3) - Right under subsection (2) may be assigned or transmitted only along with business/part of business

Critical Points:

  • Interim period protection: Acts during lapse period protected if in good faith
  • Continued user rights: Those who started working during lapse can continue
  • Assignment restriction: Rights tied to business, not freely assignable
  • Good faith requirement: Key test for protection

Overview of Chapter XI: Restoration of Lapsed Patents

Chapter XI deals with the revival of lapsed patents due to non-payment of renewal fees. It provides a safety net for patentees who inadvertently fail to pay renewal fees on time. The provisions are governed by:

  • Section 60: Applications for restoration of lapsed patents
  • Section 61: Procedure for disposal of applications for restoration
  • Section 62: Rights of patentees of lapsed patents which have been restored

These sections aim to balance the interests of the public and the patentee by allowing restoration under certain conditions, while also ensuring that third parties are not unfairly prejudiced.

1. Section 60: Applications for Restoration of Lapsed Patents

Key Provisions:

  • A lapsed patent (due to non-payment of renewal fees) may be restored upon application.
  • The application must be made:
    • Within 18 months from the date of lapse.
    • Or, if the patentee was unaware or could not have been aware of the requirement to pay renewal fee, beyond 18 months, but must be accompanied by a statement explaining the delay.
  • The applicant must show that the non-payment was unintentional.
  • Application is made to the Controller in the prescribed manner.

Important Points:

  • Restoration is not automatic; it is at the discretion of the Controller.
  • The Controller may require the applicant to pay:
    • The arrears of renewal fees.
    • A prescribed penalty.
  • The application must be published in the Official Journal.

2. Section 61: Procedure for Disposal of Applications for Restoration

Key Provisions:

  • On receipt of the application, the Controller shall advertise it in the Official Journal.
  • A period of (usually 3 months) is given for filing oppositions.
  • The Controller will consider:
    • Whether the non-payment was unintentional.
    • Any opposition filed by third parties.
  • After hearing (if required), the Controller may:
    • Restore the patent.
    • Refuse the application.

Important Points:

  • Third-party opposition is allowed – this is a quasi-judicial process.
  • The Controller may call for additional evidence.
  • The decision is appealable under Section 117.

3. Section 62: Rights of Patentees of Lapsed Patents Which Have Been Restored

Key Provisions:

  • A restored patent has the same effect as if it had never lapsed.
  • BUT the rights against third parties are limited:
    • No infringement action can be brought for acts done during the period of lapse.
    • However, if the third party had notice (actual or constructive) of the application for restoration, the patentee may:
      • Recover reasonable compensation for use during the lapse period.
      • But cannot sue for infringement.

Important Points:

  • Acts done before the publication of the restoration application cannot be challenged.
  • Acts done after publication but before restoration may attract compensation (not damages).
  • The patentee must have given notice (e.g., via journal publication) to claim compensation.

No infringement suit. However, since Company B used the invention after the advertisement of restoration (1 May 2023), and assuming they had notice, the patentee can claim reasonable compensation for use from 1 May to 1 Oct 2023. No relief for use before 1 May.

Answer:
Possibly. While 3 years is beyond 18 months, Section 60 allows late applications if the non-payment was unintentional and the delay is explained. The Controller has discretion. The Controller must focus on whether the non-payment was unintentional, not on commercial strategy. Mere suspicion of tactical lapse is insufficient to reject unless intentional non-payment is proven.

Exam Notes on Chapter XI: Restoration of Lapsed Patents

Chapter XI of the Indian Patents Act, 1970, deals with the provisions for restoring patents that have lapsed due to non-payment of renewal fees. This chapter includes Sections 60, 61, and 62, which outline the process, requirements, and consequences of restoring a lapsed patent. Below is a detailed breakdown of each section, tailored for exam preparation.

Section 60: Applications for Restoration of Lapsed Patents

  • Overview: This section provides the mechanism for a patentee to apply for the restoration of a patent that has lapsed due to non-payment of the prescribed renewal fee within the stipulated time.
  • Key Provisions:
    • A patent lapses if the renewal fee is not paid within the prescribed period or within the extended period (up to 6 months) as allowed under Section 53(2) read with Rule 80 of the Patent Rules, 2003.
    • An application for restoration can be made by the patentee or their legal representative within 18 months from the date the patent ceased to have effect.
    • The application must be filed in Form 15 with the prescribed fee (as per the First Schedule of Patent Rules, 2003).
    • The applicant must provide evidence to show that the failure to pay the renewal fee was unintentional and that there was no undue delay in making the application for restoration.
    • The Controller of Patents examines the application and may require further evidence or clarification.
  • Key Points for Exam:
    • The 18-month period is strict and non-extendable.
    • The burden of proof lies on the applicant to demonstrate that the failure was unintentional.
    • The Controller has discretionary power to decide whether to allow restoration.
  • Relevant Rule: Rule 84 of the Patent Rules, 2003, governs the procedure for restoration applications.

Section 61: Procedure for Disposal of Applications for Restoration of Lapsed Patents

  • Overview: This section outlines the procedural steps the Controller follows to process an application for restoration.
  • Key Provisions:
    • Upon receiving the application under Section 60, the Controller publishes the application Particulars in the Official Gazette to invite oppositions.
    • Any interested person (e.g., a competitor or third party) may file a notice of opposition within 2 months from the date of publication, using Form 14 with the prescribed fee.
    • The Controller considers the application, evidence provided by the applicant, and any opposition filed.
    • If no opposition is filed, or if the opposition is dismissed, the Controller may restore the patent, subject to conditions such as payment of unpaid renewal fees and additional fees.
    • If the Controller is satisfied that the failure was unintentional and there was no undue delay, the patent is restored, and a notification is published in the Official Gazette.
  • Key Points for Exam:
    • The opposition period is strictly 2 months and non-extendable.
    • The Controller’s decision is based on evidence of unintentional failure and promptness in filing the restoration application.
    • Restoration is not automatic; it depends on the Controller’s satisfaction.
  • Relevant Rule: Rule 85 governs the opposition procedure for restoration applications.

Section 62: Rights of Patentees of Lapsed Patents Which Have Been Restored

  • Overview: This section specifies the rights and limitations of a patentee whose patent has been restored.
  • Key Provisions:
    • A restored patent is subject to the same rights and obligations as it had before lapsing, but with certain restrictions.
    • No infringement proceedings can be initiated for acts committed during the lapse period (i.e., between the date the patent ceased to have effect and the date of publication of the restoration application).
    • The restored patent is subject to any conditions imposed by the Controller, such as payment of outstanding fees or additional terms to protect third parties who may have used the invention during the lapse period.
    • The patentee regains exclusive rights to make, use, sell, or import the patented invention once the patent is restored, subject to the above limitations.
  • Key Points for Exam:
    • The lapse period creates a “safe harbor” for third parties who used the invention during that time.
    • The restored patent is treated as if it had never lapsed, except for the specified restrictions on infringement actions.
    • Conditions imposed by the Controller may include protective measures for third parties.

Summary of Key Timelines:

  • Renewal fee payment: Within the prescribed period or up to 6 months extension (Section IES 53(2), Rule 80).
  • Application for restoration: Within 18 months from the date of lapse (Section 60).
  • Opposition to restoration: Within 2 months from publication of the restoration application (Section 61).

Important Notes for Exam:

  • Always check the relevant forms (Form 15 for application, Form 14 for opposition) and fees in the First Schedule of the Patent Rules.
  • Understand the interplay between Sections 60–62 and related rules (Rules 84–85).
  • Focus on the Controller’s discretionary powers and the burden of proof on the applicant.

Tricky Use Cases with Answers

Below are 3 tricky use cases designed to test the application of Sections 60, 61, and 62 in practical scenarios, along with detailed answers.

Use Case 1:

  • Scenario: A patentee, Mr. Sharma, failed to pay the renewal fee for his patent due to financial difficulties, and the patent lapsed on January 1, 2024. He files an application for restoration on June 1, 2025, claiming the failure was unintentional because he was unaware of the due date. He provides evidence of his financial hardship. Is his application likely to succeed?
  • Answer: The application is likely to fail unless Mr. Sharma provides stronger evidence that the failure was unintentional. Under Section 60, the application must be filed within 18 months from the date of lapse (January 1, 2024), so the filing on June 1, 2025 (17 months) is within time. However, financial hardship alone does not necessarily qualify as an unintentional failure. The Controller requires evidence that the non-payment was due to an oversight or mistake, not merely financial constraints. If Mr. Sharma can prove he was unaware of the renewal obligation (e.g., due to lack of notification or miscommunication), and there was no undue delay in filing, the Controller may consider restoration. Otherwise, the application may be rejected for lack of sufficient evidence of unintentional failure.

Use Case 2:

  • Scenario: A company’s patent lapsed on March 1, 2023, due to non-payment of renewal fees. The company files for restoration on August 1, 2024, and the application is published in the Official Gazette on September 1, 2024. A competitor files an opposition on November 15, 2024, claiming the company deliberately delayed payment to manipulate the market. Is the opposition valid?
  • Answer: The opposition is invalid because it was filed after the prescribed period. Under Section 61 and Rule 85, any opposition to a restoration application must be filed within 2 months from the date of publication in the Official Gazette (September 1, 2024). The deadline for opposition is November 1, 2024, so the filing on November 15, 2024, is late and will not be considered. The Controller will proceed to evaluate the restoration application based on the evidence provided by the company, without considering the late opposition.

Use Case 3:

  • Scenario: A patent lapsed on April 1, 2023, and was restored on October 1, 2024, after a successful application under Section 60. During the lapse period, a third party, XYZ Corp, started manufacturing the patented product. Can the patentee sue XYZ Corp for infringement for products made between April 1, 2023, and October 1, 2024?
  • Answer: The patentee cannot sue XYZ Corp for infringement for products made between April 1, 2023, and the date of publication of the restoration application (say, August 1, 2024). Under Section 62(2), no infringement proceedings can be initiated for acts committed during the lapse period (from the date the patent ceased to have effect until the publication of the restoration application). This provision protects third parties who used the invention when the patent was not in force. However, the patentee can initiate infringement proceedings for any acts committed after the restoration is published, provided the patent is fully restored and in force.

    • b) Restoration revives rights retroactively.
    • c) Restoration grants new patent rights.
    • d) Third-party users during lapse must stop immediately.
      ✅ Answer: b)

🧠 Tricky Use Cases with Answers

Use Case 1:

Scenario: A patent lapsed due to non-payment. The patentee files for restoration after 20 months.

Question: Can the patent be restored?

Answer: ❌ No. Restoration must be filed within 18 months from the date of lapse.

Use Case 2:

Scenario: A company uses a lapsed patent during the lapse period. The patent is later restored.

Question: Can the patentee sue the company for infringement?

Answer: ❌ No. Under Section 62, no infringement action can be taken for acts done during the lapse period.

Use Case 3:

Scenario: A patent jointly held by two inventors lapses. One inventor applies for restoration without informing the other.

Question: Is this valid?

Answer: ✅ Yes, with Controller’s permission, one joint owner can apply for restoration.

🧾 Tricky Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1:

Controller receives a restoration application but finds the lapse was intentional.

Outcome: ❌ Restoration will be rejected. Only unintentional lapses are eligible.

Scenario 2:

Restoration application is published. A third party opposes it citing public interest.

Outcome: ✅ Controller conducts a hearing. If opposition is valid, restoration may be denied.

Scenario 3:

Restoration granted. Patentee delays payment of renewal fees post-order.

Outcome: ❌ Restoration becomes invalid if fees are not paid within 1 month of order.
