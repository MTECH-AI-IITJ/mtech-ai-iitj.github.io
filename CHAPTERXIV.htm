Exam Notes: Chapter XIV - Patent Office and Its Establishment (Sections 73-76)

Key Concepts:

  1. Section 73: Controller and Other Officers
    • Establishes the Controller-General of Patents, Designs, and Trademarks as the head of the Patent Office.
    • Appointed by the Central Government.
    • Powers include: Granting patents, revocation, opposition proceedings, and administrative control.
    • Other officers (e.g., Deputy/Assistant Controllers) exercise delegated powers but cannot hear appeals (now under IPAB/High Court post-IPAB abolition).
  2. Section 74: Patent Office and Its Branches
    • Patent Office headquartered in Kolkata with branches in Delhi, Mumbai, and Chennai.
    • Jurisdiction defined by the Central Government (e.g., applications filed based on applicant’s location).
    • Filing flexibility: Applications can be submitted to any branch, but jurisdictional rules apply for proceedings.
  3. Section 75: Restrictions on Employees
    • During employment:
      • Cannot acquire rights/interest in patents (except via inheritance/bequest).
      • Cannot participate in proceedings where they have a financial interest.
    • Post-employment:
      • Barred for 1 year from acting/pleading in proceedings they handled during employment.
  4. Section 76: Confidentiality Obligations
    • Officers/employees cannot disclose patent application details:
      • Pre-publication: Absolute prohibition (confidential until 18-month publication).
      • Post-publication: Only disclose information in the official file (e.g., granted patent details).
    • Violation may lead to disciplinary action or penalties.

Exam Notes: Chapter XIV - Patent Office and Its Establishment

This chapter is administrative but crucial. It establishes the framework for the patent-granting authority in India and includes critical ethical and confidentiality rules for its employees.

Section 73: Controller and other officers

  • Key Function: The Central Government appoints the Controller General of Patents, Designs and Trademarks.
  • Hierarchy: The Controller General is the head of the patent office.
  • Delegation of Power: The Controller General can delegate any function (e.g., examination, hearing oppositions) to Subordinate Officers (Examiners, Assistant Controllers, Deputy Controllers, Joint Controllers).
  • Exam Tip: The term "Controller" used throughout the Act can refer to the Controller General or any subordinate officer to whom powers have been delegated.

Section 74: Patent office and its branches

  • Head Office: The Patent Office has its head office (currently in Kolkata).
  • Branches: The Central Government can establish branch offices (currently in Delhi, Mumbai, Chennai) to facilitate the Act's administration.
  • Seal: Each office (head and branch) has its own seal for official documents.
  • Jurisdiction: The territorial jurisdiction for filing patents is based on the applicant's address:
    • Kolkata: West Bengal, Bihar, Jharkhand, Orissa, Assam, NE States, Sikkim, Andaman & Nicobar.
    • Mumbai: Maharashtra, Gujarat, MP, Chhattisgarh, Goa, Daman & Diu, Dadra & Nagar Haveli.
    • Chennai: AP, Telangana, Karnataka, Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Pondicherry, Lakshadweep.
    • Delhi: All other states/UTs (e.g., Delhi, Haryana, HP, J&K, Punjab, Rajasthan, UP, Uttarakhand).

Section 75: Restriction on employees as to right or interest in patents

  • Prohibition: Officers and employees of the patent office cannot acquire or hold any right or interest (directly or indirectly) in any patent issued by the office.
  • Exception: They can acquire rights through inheritance or bequest (as a legal heir or beneficiary in a will).
  • Purpose: To prevent conflict of interest and ensure impartiality. An officer involved in granting a patent should not stand to gain from it.

Section 76: Officers and employees not to furnish information

  • Confidentiality Duty: Officers and employees are prohibited from disclosing any information related to:
    1. An application for a patent (until it is published u/s 11A).
    2. Any specification or document related to such an application.
  • Exceptions (When disclosure is permitted):
    • When required by the Act itself (e.g., for publication, opposition proceedings).
    • When required by a court order.
    • When required to be communicated to the Central Government.
  • Purpose: To protect the confidentiality of the invention before publication, which is a fundamental right of the applicant.

Tricky Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

1. Mr. Sharma, a Deputy Controller at the Mumbai patent office, inherits a 5% share in a family-run company that holds a patent. The patent was granted by the Delhi branch two years ago. Under Section 75, what is the legal status of Mr. Sharma's inheritance?
a) It is illegal, and he must immediately divest his shares.
b) It is permissible only if he gets written permission from the Controller General.
c) It is permissible as it was acquired through inheritance.
d) It is permissible only if the patent was not granted by his own branch.

Answer: c) It is permissible as it was acquired through inheritance.

  • Why it's tricky: It tests the specific exception to the rule. The prohibition does not apply to rights acquired through inheritance or bequest, regardless of which office granted the patent.

2. An examiner in the patent office is reviewing a patent application filed by "XYZ Pharma." His brother is the lead scientist at XYZ Pharma. What is the examiner's primary ethical obligation under the Act?
a) To fast-track the application since he has insider knowledge of its validity.
b) To immediately inform his superior and seek to be recused from handling the application to avoid a conflict of interest.
c) To process the application as normal but inform the applicant of the relationship.
d) There is no obligation as the restriction only applies to financial interests, not familial ones.

Answer: b) To immediately inform his superior and seek to be recused from handling the application to avoid a conflict of interest.

  • Why it's tricky: While Section 75 specifically mentions "right or interest," ethical conduct and the principles of natural justice demand that any situation creating a potential or perceived conflict of interest (like a close familial relationship) must be disclosed to maintain the integrity of the process.

3. Before the mandatory publication period (18 months), a third party approaches a patent office employee, offering a bribe for information on a specific, unpublished patent application. The employee refuses. Which section directly prohibits the action attempted by the third party?
a) Section 73
b) Section 74
c) Section 75
d) Section 76

Answer: d) Section 76

  • Why it's tricky: It tests the precise application of the sections. Section 76 is the specific provision that bars employees from furnishing information about an unpublished application. The other sections deal with appointment, office location, and financial interests.

4. A patent agent, representing a client, requests an informal update on the examination status of their unpublished application from an acquaintance who works at the patent office. The employee can:
a) Provide a verbal update as it's just a status, not the technical details.
b) Provide a written update if no money is exchanged.
c) Refuse to provide any information, citing Section 76.
d) Provide the information only if the request is made in writing by the patent agent.

Answer: c) Refuse to provide any information, citing Section 76.

  • Why it's tricky: It presents a common, seemingly harmless scenario. The law under Section 76 is absolute: no information can be furnished before publication, except through the official channels mandated by the Act. "Any information" includes even the simple status of the application.

5. The territorial jurisdiction for filing a patent application for an inventor based in Bangalore is determined by:
a) The location of the head office of the patent office.
b) The subject matter of the invention.
c) The place of business of the applicant/inventor.
d) The choice of the applicant, who can file at any branch.

Answer: c) The place of business of the applicant/inventor.

  • Why it's tricky: It checks the understanding of the practical rule under Section 74. While the office has branches, an applicant cannot choose arbitrarily; they must file at the branch that has territorial jurisdiction over their place of residence or business.

Tricky Scenarios & Use Cases for Practice

Scenario 1: The Inherited Patent
Ms. Ananya, a newly appointed Patent Examiner in Chennai, discovers that her late grandfather's will bequeaths her a portfolio of assets that includes two patents. One of these patents is currently undergoing a post-grant opposition at the Delhi branch.

  • Questions:
    1. Does Ms. Ananya violate Section 75 by inheriting these patent rights?
    2. What immediate steps must she take upon learning of this inheritance?
    3. Can she continue to work on files related to the patent in opposition in Delhi? Why or why not?
  • Suggested Analysis:
    1. No, she does not violate S.75, as the acquisition is through bequest (inheritance), which is a clear exception.
    2. She must immediately disclose this fact in writing to the Controller General. This is a mandatory ethical disclosure to ensure transparency and avoid any future allegations of conflict of interest.
    3. No, she must be recused. Even though the acquisition is legal, she now has a financial interest in the validity of that patent. Allowing her to work on any related proceedings (even in a different branch) would be a clear conflict of interest and violate the principle of impartiality.

Scenario 2: The Inquisitive Journalist
A journalist contacts the Head of the Kolkata patent office, demanding information under the Right to Information (RTI) Act about a specific, unpublished patent application filed by a major tech company, claiming it is a matter of public interest.

  • Questions:
    1. Is the Head of the office obligated to provide this information under the RTI Act?
    2. Which section of the Patents Act would they rely on to refuse the request?
    3. Are there any circumstances under which this information could be disclosed before publication?
  • Suggested Analysis:
    1. No. The RTI Act has exemptions for information that is confidential or covered by intellectual property law. The Patents Act specifically mandates confidentiality for unpublished applications.
    2. They would rely on Section 76, which prohibits furnishing such information.
    3. The exceptions in S.76 are: (a) if required by the Act itself, or (b) by an order of a court. A journalistic request or an RTI query does not qualify as an exception. Only a direct court order could compel disclosure.

Scenario 3: The Consulting Offer
Mr. Kapoor, a retired Deputy Controller from the Delhi patent office, is offered a high-paying consulting role by a law firm. The firm wants him to advise on strategies for navigating the patent prosecution process for their clients.

  • Questions:
    1. Are there any restrictions on Mr. Kapoor's employment after retirement?
    2. Can he use his general knowledge of office procedures?
    3. What would be illegal for him to do in his new role, based on the principles of Chapter XIV?
  • Suggested Analysis:
    1. While the Act doesn't explicitly bar post-retirement employment, standard government conduct rules often impose a "cooling-off" period. The key restriction from the Act is on using confidential information.
    2. Yes, he can use his general knowledge and expertise gained from his career (e.g., how to properly draft forms, general timelines). This is considered public domain skill.
    3. It would be illegal for him to:
      • Disclose any confidential information from any application he worked on or had access to during his tenure (S.76 applies even after retirement).
      • Use undue influence over his former subordinates to gain favours for his clients.
      • The principle behind S.76 (confidentiality) is perpetual and not limited to the term of employment.

 

 

 

 

CHAPTER XIV: PATENT OFFICE AND ITS ESTABLISHMENT

Exam Notes

This chapter covers the administrative structure and rules governing the Indian Patent Office. Key sections to remember are:

  • Section 73: Controller and other officers. This section establishes the Controller General of Patents, Designs and Trademarks (Controller) as the head of the Patent Office. It also allows for the appointment of other officers, such as the Deputy Controller, Assistant Controller, and Examiners. The Controller has the final say on all patent matters, subject to appeal.
  • Section 74: Patent office and its branches. This section establishes the Patent Office and allows for the creation of branch offices. The head office is in Kolkata, with other branches in Delhi, Mumbai, and Chennai. All patent applications can be filed at any of these offices, but each branch has a specific territorial jurisdiction.
  • Section 75: Restriction on employees of patent office as to right or interest in patents. This is a crucial section that prevents a conflict of interest. It prohibits employees of the Patent Office from acquiring any direct or indirect right or interest in a patent, other than by inheritance or as a legal representative. This ensures that employees are not biased in their decisions.
  • Section 76: Officers and employees not to furnish information, etc. This section mandates the confidentiality of information. It prohibits employees from disclosing any information relating to patent applications or patents to unauthorized persons. Information can only be disclosed under specific circumstances, such as when it's required by law or by the Controller.

Tricky MCQs

  1. Question: According to Section 73 of the Patents Act, 1970, who is the head of the Indian Patent Office? A) Registrar of Patents B) Director General of Patents C) Controller General of Patents, Designs and Trademarks D) Chief Patent Officer Answer: C) Controller General of Patents, Designs and Trademarks. The common mistake is to confuse the title or assume a more generic name.
  2. Question: An employee of the Patent Office can acquire a patent if: A) The invention was developed outside of working hours. B) It is a joint application with a non-employee. C) The interest is acquired by inheritance or as a legal representative. D) The Controller gives special permission. Answer: C) The interest is acquired by inheritance or as a legal representative. This is the only exception explicitly mentioned in Section 75.
  3. Question: An examiner in the Patent Office is approached by a journalist for details on a pending patent application. The examiner can: A) Provide a summary of the application but not the full details. B) Disclose the information if the applicant has given written permission. C) Not disclose any information unless required by law or directed by the Controller. D) Disclose the information as long as the journalist agrees to an embargo. Answer: C) Not disclose any information unless required by law or directed by the Controller. Section 76 is very strict about confidentiality.

Tricky Scenarios & Use Cases

  1. Scenario: An examiner at the Patent Office, Mr. Sharma, learns about a groundbreaking invention during his review of an application. He later resigns and files a patent application for a similar, but not identical, invention.
    • Legal Implication: Mr. Sharma has not violated Section 75, as he is no longer an employee. However, he may have violated Section 76 if he used confidential information from the previous application. This scenario highlights the difference between a direct interest (prohibited by Section 75) and the misuse of confidential information (prohibited by Section 76). The key is the source of the idea and whether it came from his official duties.
  2. Scenario: The Controller needs to get information from the Patent Office's records for a court case. The officer in charge refuses, citing Section 76.
    • Legal Implication: The officer is incorrect. Section 76 has an exception: information can be disclosed if required by law or by the Controller's specific direction. The Controller, being the head of the office, can request this information for official purposes.
  3. Scenario: A patent employee's uncle passes away, leaving him a share in a company that holds several patents.
    • Legal Implication: This is a classic test of Section 75. The employee can legally accept this inheritance as it is one of the two permitted exceptions to the rule. This scenario highlights the legal exceptions and the importance of knowing them precisely.
  4. Scenario: A company is involved in a legal dispute and needs to know the filing details of a competitor's patent application. They approach a clerk at the Patent Office and offer a bribe.
    • Legal Implication: This is a clear violation of Section 76 by the clerk if they provide the information. The clerk is prohibited from furnishing such information to unauthorized parties. The company's action would also constitute a criminal offense related to bribery. This shows the practical application of the confidentiality clause and the potential for legal and ethical breaches.

Section 73: Controller and Other Officers

Key Points:

  • Controller of Patents: Chief executive officer of the Patent Office
  • Appointment: Central Government appoints the Controller and other officers
  • Qualifications: Must possess prescribed qualifications (technical/legal expertise)
  • Powers: Administrative, quasi-judicial, and executive powers
  • Hierarchy: Controller > Joint Controller > Deputy Controller > Assistant Controller > Examiner

Important Legal Provisions:

  • Controller has powers of a civil court under Code of Civil Procedure, 1908
  • Can summon witnesses, examine on oath, receive evidence on affidavits
  • Powers include discovery and inspection of documents
  • Can issue commissions for examination of witnesses

Section 74: Patent Office and Its Branches

Key Points:

  • Head Office: Located at prescribed place (currently Mumbai)
  • Branch Offices: Can be established at various locations as needed
  • Jurisdiction: Each branch office has territorial jurisdiction
  • Functions: Examination, opposition proceedings, revocation matters
  • Current Structure:
    • Head Office: Mumbai
    • Branch Offices: Delhi, Chennai, Kolkata, Ahmedabad

Territorial Distribution:

  • Each office covers specific states/territories
  • Applications filed based on applicant's address or place of business
  • Transfer of applications between offices possible in certain circumstances

Section 75: Restriction on Employees - Rights/Interest in Patents

Key Points:

  • Prohibition: Patent office employees cannot acquire rights in patents
  • Scope: Applies during employment and for prescribed period after cessation
  • Exceptions: Patents applied for before joining service (with disclosure)
  • Penalty: Dismissal and other disciplinary action
  • Rationale: Prevents conflict of interest and maintains impartiality

Detailed Restrictions:

  • Cannot file patent applications (directly/indirectly)
  • Cannot acquire patents by assignment/transmission
  • Cannot have financial interest in patent-related businesses
  • Cannot act as patent agent or representative

Section 76: Non-disclosure Obligations

Key Points:

  • Confidentiality: Officers cannot furnish information except as authorized
  • Official Secrets: Information obtained in official capacity is protected
  • Exceptions: Information disclosure as per law or court orders
  • Penalty: Legal action under Official Secrets Act
  • Duration: Obligation continues even after cessation of employment

📘 CHAPTER XIV: PATENT OFFICE AND ITS ESTABLISHMENT

Sections 73 to 76
Relevant Provisions: Controller, Patent Office, Restrictions on Employees

✅ Exam Notes: Key Concepts & Legal Provisions

Section 73: Controller and Other Officers

  • The Controller-General of Patents, Designs & Trade Marks is the Controller under the Patents Act.
  • Appointed by the Central Government.
  • The Controller is competent to exercise powers and perform functions under the Act.
  • May appoint Deputy Controllers, Assistant Controllers, and other officers.
  • Delegation of Powers: The Controller may delegate certain functions to Deputy/Assistant Controllers, except powers specifically reserved for the Controller (e.g., revocation of patents, certain opposition decisions).
  • Administrative Head: The Controller is the administrative head of the Patent Office.

Key Point: Only the Controller can perform non-delegable functions — e.g., decisions under Sections 64 (revocation), 11B (compulsory licensing review), etc.

Section 74: Patent Office and its Branches

  • The Patent Office is established by the Central Government.
  • Head Office: Kolkata (since 1856, originally under the British regime).
  • Branch Offices: Chennai, Mumbai, Delhi, and Ahmedabad — functional since 2009.
  • Each branch handles patent applications based on applicant’s address or agent’s address (jurisdiction based on territorial limits).
  • Jurisdiction:
    • Delhi → Northern & Western India
    • Mumbai → Western India
    • Chennai → Southern India
    • Kolkata → Eastern & North-Eastern India

Important: The location of the applicant/agent determines the appropriate office for filing.

Section 75: Restriction on Employees as to Right or Interest in Patents

  • No employee of the Patent Office shall:
    • Acquire or hold any interest in a patent or application.
    • Be interested directly or indirectly in the business of a patent agent.
  • Applies to Controller, Deputy/Assistant Controllers, examiners, clerks, etc.
  • Purpose: Prevent conflict of interest and ensure impartiality.
  • Exception: An employee may inherit a patent but must divest themselves of interest within three months.

Note: Violation may lead to disciplinary action or removal.

Section 76: Officers and Employees not to Furnish Information

  • No officer or employee of the Patent Office shall:
    • Disclose confidential information acquired in the course of official duties.
    • Provide unauthorized information about pending applications (before publication).
  • Applies even after leaving service.
  • Exception: Disclosure authorized by law or with Controller’s permission.
  • Penalty: Contravention may lead to fine, imprisonment up to 2 years, or both (under Section 126).

Key: Applies to pre-publication phase (before 18 months). Post-publication, information is public.

❓ Tricky MCQs (Multiple Choice Questions)

Q1. Which of the following functions cannot be delegated by the Controller to a Deputy Controller?

A) Examination of patent applications
B) Hearing of pre-grant opposition
C) Decision on revocation of a patent under Section 64
D) Grant of a patent

Answer: C
Explanation: Revocation under Section 64 is a tribunal-like function and can only be initiated by the High Court or Appellate Board (now IPAB), but the Controller has limited powers. However, final revocation orders are not within the Controller’s routine delegated powers. More importantly, certain quasi-judicial functions like revocation petitions under Section 64 (when filed before the Controller) are non-delegable. Hence, C is correct.

Q2. Mr. A is an Examiner at the Patent Office in Chennai. His brother files a patent application and appoints Mr. A’s wife as the patent agent. Mr. A does not work on the application. Is there a violation of Section 75?

A) No, because Mr. A did not act on the case
B) Yes, because indirect interest is prohibited
C) No, because family members can act independently
D) Yes, only if Mr. A benefits financially

Answer: B
Explanation: Section 75 prohibits indirect interest. Even if Mr. A doesn’t work on it, having a close family member acting as agent in a case may create a perception of conflict. While not automatically a breach, any indirect interest could be interpreted broadly. However, actual violation requires personal benefit or active interest. But since the section says “directly or indirectly”, B is the best answer in a tricky context.

Trick: The law prohibits interest, not mere association. But examiners must recuse and report such situations.

Q3. A patent application was filed in Mumbai, but the applicant later shifts to Assam. The application is still pending. Can the applicant request transfer to Kolkata branch?

A) No, jurisdiction is fixed at the time of filing
B) Yes, with Controller’s permission
C) Yes, automatically upon change of address
D) Only after publication

Answer: A
Explanation: Jurisdiction is determined at the time of filing based on the applicant’s address or agent’s address. It does not change later. So, even if the applicant moves, the office remains the same.

Q4. An ex-employee of the Patent Office, now working in a private IP firm, accesses a pre-published application file for research. He never worked on it. Is this a violation of Section 76?

A) No, because he is no longer employed
B) Yes, because the duty of confidentiality continues after employment
C) Only if he discloses it to a third party
D) No, if used for academic research

Answer: B
Explanation: Section 76 applies even after retirement or resignation. Unauthorized access or use of confidential information (especially pre-publication) is a continuing obligation. So, accessing such files without authority is a violation.

Q5. Which of the following is not a branch office of the Indian Patent Office?

A) Hyderabad
B) Delhi
C) Mumbai
D) Chennai

Answer: A
Explanation: The four branches are Delhi, Mumbai, Chennai, Kolkata. Hyderabad is not a Patent Office branch.

🧩 Tricky Scenarios & Use Cases (For Practice)

Scenario 1: Conflict of Interest

Ms. Priya is a Deputy Controller at the Kolkata Patent Office. Her husband owns a patent consultancy firm that files 50+ applications annually. She has no financial stake but occasionally discusses general patent trends at home. One application filed by her husband’s firm comes up for examination in another department.

Question: Does this violate Section 75? Should she recuse herself? What safeguards should exist?

Analysis:

  • Section 75 prohibits direct or indirect interest in patent agent business.
  • If her husband runs a patent agency, and she is a Controller, this is a clear conflict even without financial stake.
  • She must declare the interest and recuse from any related decisions.
  • Best practice: rotation, disclosure, and institutional ethics committee.

Scenario 2: Unauthorized Disclosure

Mr. Raj, a former examiner, now works in a corporate IP team. He remembers technical details of a pending application he examined 2 years ago (not yet published). He uses this knowledge to advise his company to design around the invention.

Question: Is this a violation of Section 76?

Answer: Yes.

  • Section 76 imposes lifelong confidentiality on information obtained in official capacity.
  • Using such knowledge for competitive advantage is unauthorized use and may attract penalty under Section 126.

Scenario 3: Delegation of Powers

The Controller is on leave. A Deputy Controller signs an order revoking a patent under Section 64, citing lack of inventive step.

Question: Is the order valid?

Answer: No.

  • Revocation under Section 64 is a quasi-judicial function that cannot be delegated to Deputy Controller.
  • Only the Controller (or IPAB) can hear and decide revocation petitions.
  • The order is ultra vires and can be challenged in court.

Scenario 4: Inherited Patent

Mr. Sen, a patent examiner, inherits a patent from his uncle. He does not disclose it and does not sell it.

Question: What are the legal implications under Section 75?

Answer:

  • He may inherit the patent, but must divest himself of interest within 3 months.
  • Failure to do so is a violation of Section 75.
  • He must report to the Controller and transfer the patent to avoid disciplinary action.

Scenario 5: Jurisdictional Error

A company in Gujarat files a patent application through an agent based in Chennai. The application is filed at the Delhi office by mistake.

Question: What happens?

Answer:

  • The application may be transferred to the Mumbai office (since Gujarat falls under Mumbai jurisdiction).
  • The Controller may allow transfer under procedural rules.
  • But filing at the wrong office does not invalidate the application if corrected early.
