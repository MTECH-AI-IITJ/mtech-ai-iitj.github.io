Exam Notes: Chapter VI - Anticipation (Sections 29-34, Patents Act, 1970)

Section 29: Anticipation by Previous Publication

  • Definition: An invention is anticipated if it was published (in any form: written, oral, electronic, etc.) before the priority date of the patent application.
  • Key Points:
    • Publication must be accessible to the public (e.g., journals, patents, books, online databases).
    • Location: Publication anywhere in the world anticipates if available in India.
    • Enabling Disclosure: Publication must enable a person skilled in the art to replicate the invention.
  • Exception: If publication is not enabling (e.g., vague, incomplete), it does not anticipate.

Section 30: Anticipation by Previous Communication to Government

  • Definition: Anticipation occurs if the invention was communicated to the Government (or authorized entity) before the priority date.
  • Key Points:
    • Communication must be in confidence (e.g., for national security, public interest).
    • No Anticipation if:
      • Communication was made without consent of the applicant, or
      • Government uses/publishes the invention without consent.
  • Tricky Aspect: Confidentiality is critical; public disclosure by Government without consent does not anticipate.

Section 31: Anticipation by Public Display, etc.

  • Definition: Anticipation occurs if the invention was publicly displayed (e.g., exhibitions, trade fairs) before the priority date.
  • Key Points:
    • No Anticipation if:
      • Display was at an officially notified exhibition (by Government Gazette),
      • Display was by the inventor/legal representative,
      • Display occurred within 6 months before filing.
  • Tricky Aspect: Non-notified exhibitions or displays by third parties anticipate.

Section 32: Anticipation by Public Working

  • Definition: Anticipation occurs if the invention was publicly worked (used/commercialized) in India before the priority date.
  • Key Points:
    • No Anticipation if:
      • Public working was by the applicant for reasonable trial/experiment (non-commercial).
      • Working was secret (not accessible to public).
  • Tricky Aspect: "Reasonable trial" excludes commercial sales; working by third parties always anticipates.

Section 33: Anticipation by Use/Publication After Provisional Specification

  • Definition: Anticipation occurs if the invention was used or published after filing a provisional specification but before filing the complete specification.
  • Key Points:
    • No Anticipation if:
      • Use/publication is in accordance with the provisional specification.
      • Example: Publishing details matching the provisional spec during the 12-month window.
  • Tricky Aspect: Deviations from the provisional spec (e.g., new features) anticipate.

Section 34: No Anticipation if Circumstances Only as per Sections 29-32

  • Definition: An invention is not anticipated if the circumstances fall exclusively under Sections 29-32.
  • Key Points:
    • Savings Clause: Protects inventors if anticipation is only due to:
      • Publication (Sec 29),
      • Government communication (Sec 30),
      • Public display (Sec 31),
      • Public working (Sec 32).
    • Exception: If anticipation arises from other sources (e.g., prior use by third parties), this section does not apply.

Introduction to Chapter VI: Anticipation

This chapter defines the specific circumstances under which an invention is considered not new (i.e., "anticipated") and therefore unpatentable. The core principle is that for an invention to be patentable, it must be novel. If the invention has already been made available to the public in any form before the "priority date" of the patent application, it is anticipated. The sections (29-34) outline these specific scenarios and their exceptions.

1. Exam Notes: Section-by-Section Breakdown

Section 29: Anticipation by previous publication

  • What it is: An invention is anticipated if it was published in any document (anywhere in the world) before the priority date of the patent claim.
  • Key Conditions:
    1. The document must be publicly available (e.g., in a library, journal, database, online).
    2. The publication must contain a clear and complete description of the invention such that a person skilled in the art could perform it. A mere vague or obscure hint is not enough.
    3. The subject matter must be the same. If the prior publication discloses all the essential features of the claimed invention, it is anticipated.
  • Doctrine of Pari Materia: The prior document and the patent specification must be read as a whole by a person skilled in the art, not in isolation.

Section 30: Anticipation by previous communication to Government

  • What it is: An invention is anticipated if it was previously communicated to the Government or any person authorized by the Government to investigate the invention.
  • Key Conditions:
    1. The communication must contain a full and complete description of the invention.
    2. This is relevant for inventions made by government employees or contractors where the government is the first to be informed.
  • Note: This is a less common ground for anticipation in public exams but important to know.

Section 31: Anticipation by public display, etc.

  • What it is: An invention is anticipated if it was displayed at a recognised exhibition or its description was read before a learned society.
  • Key Conditions & Exception:
    1. The display or reading must be public.
    2. Crucial Exception (Proviso): This is NOT anticipation if the applicant:
      • Exhibited the invention at an international or officially recognised exhibition.
      • Filed the application within SIX MONTHS from the date of exhibiting it.
      • Includes a statement to this effect in the complete specification.
    • This is the "Exhibition Grace Period".

Section 32: Anticipation by public working

  • What it is: An invention is anticipated if it was publicly worked in India to a scale reasonable for experimental purposes.
  • Key Conditions:
    1. "Working" means the actual practical use of the invention.
    2. The working must be public, not secret or confidential.
    3. The working must be more than just a secret trial or experiment. If the public can see and understand the invention from its use, it is anticipated.
  • Exception: Reasonable trial or experiment, if kept confidential, does not constitute anticipation.

Section 33: Anticipation by use and publication after provisional specification

  • What it is: This is a critical and often tested section. It protects the applicant during the period between filing a provisional and complete specification.
  • Key Conditions:
    1. After filing a provisional specification, the applicant (or someone deriving title from them) might use the invention or publish it.
    2. Such use or publication WILL NOT be considered anticipation if the complete specification is filed within 12 months.
    3. However, this protection is only against the applicant's own actions. It offers no protection against a third party who independently publishes or uses the invention during this period.

Section 34: No anticipation if circumstances are only as described in sections 29,30,31 and 32

  • What it is: This is the "saving clause". It states that an invention is NOT anticipated merely because it fell within the scope of S.29, 30, 31, or 32 if:
    1. The publication, communication, display, or working was without the consent of the true inventor (or their predecessor in title).
    2. The information was obtained from the true inventor and published in breach of confidence.
    3. The true inventor displayed or worked it as a trial experiment.
  • Purpose: To protect the true inventor from having their invention stolen and disclosed by others, or from their own innocent experimental actions.

2. Tricky Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

1. An inventor, Mr. A, files a provisional specification on Jan 1, 2023. On June 1, 2023, he publishes a paper detailing the invention in a scientific journal. He files his complete specification on Dec 15, 2023. Will his own publication be considered prior art against his application?
a) Yes, because it was published before the complete specification.
b) Yes, because any publication before the grant of the patent is prior art.
c) No, because Section 33 protects the applicant from their own publication after a provisional filing.
d) No, because he filed the complete specification within 12 months.

Answer: c) Section 33 explicitly states that use or publication by the applicant after the provisional specification is not anticipation, provided the complete spec is filed within 12 months.

2. An invention was publicly demonstrated at the India International Trade Fair, a government-recognized exhibition, on November 1, 2022. What is the last date by which the inventor must file a patent application to avoid anticipation under Section 31?
a) November 1, 2022
b) December 1, 2022
c) May 1, 2023
d) November 1, 2023

Answer: c) Section 31 provides a grace period of 6 months from the date of display. Nov 1, 2022 + 6 months = May 1, 2023.

3. A competitor, Mr. X, unlawfully obtains confidential documents describing Mr. Y's secret invention and publishes them. Mr. Y then files a patent application. Which section can Mr. Y use to defend the novelty of his invention?
a) Section 29
b) Section 31
c) Section 33
d) Section 34

Answer: d) Section 34 is the saving clause that states there is no anticipation if the publication was without the consent of the true inventor.

4. The 'public working' of an invention under Section 32 would NOT constitute anticipation if:
a) It was worked on a large commercial scale.
b) It was worked only to a reasonable extent for experimental purposes.
c) It was worked secretly in a private laboratory.
d) It was worked publicly but for a very short period.

Answer: c) The key word is "public" working. Secret trials or experiments do not amount to public working and thus do not anticipate the invention. Option b is a trick; the section says working to a reasonable extent does constitute anticipation if it's public.

5. The protection offered under Section 33 (after filing a provisional specification) extends to:
a) Any publication by any person anywhere in the world.
b) Only the applicant's own actions of use and publication.
c) The actions of the applicant and all third parties.
d) Only publications within India.

Answer: b) This is a crucial distinction. Section 33 only protects against the applicant's own subsequent actions. It provides no shield against independent third-party actions during the provisional period.

3. Tricky Use Cases & Scenarios

Scenario 1: The Academic Researcher

Dr. Sharma files a provisional application for a new chemical process. Eight months later, she presents her findings at an international conference, and the proceedings are published. She files her complete specification two months after the conference (10 months after the provisional). A patent examiner cites her own conference paper as prior art under S.29.

  • Issue: Is the invention anticipated?
  • Analysis: Apply Section 33. The publication was by the applicant herself after the provisional date. Since she filed the complete specification within 12 months (10 < 12), her own publication is not considered anticipation. Her application is safe from her own actions.

Scenario 2: The Leaked Prototype

An engineer at Alpha Corp. gives a prototype of a new drone to Beta Corp. under a strict Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA) for testing. Beta Corp. breaches the NDA and demonstrates the drone at a public tech show. Two weeks later, Alpha Corp. files a patent application.

  • Issue: Does the public display by Beta Corp. anticipate Alpha's invention?
  • Analysis: Apply Section 34. The public display (S.31) was done without the consent of the true inventor (Alpha Corp.). Therefore, under S.34, this display will not be considered as anticipation. Alpha's application is valid.

Scenario 3: The Independent Publisher

Mr. Kapoor files a provisional application for a new software algorithm on January 10. Unaware of Mr. Kapoor's work, a university research group in Germany publishes an identical algorithm in a journal on March 15. Mr. Kapoor files his complete specification on December 20.

  • Issue: Is Mr. Kapoor's invention novel?
  • Analysis: This tests the limits of Section 33. The protection of S.33 only applies to the applicant's own actions. The German publication is an independent third-party action. The priority date for novelty is the date of the provisional filing (Jan 10). The German publication (Mar 15) is after the priority date. Therefore, it is not prior art under S.29, and the invention is novel. The critical point is that the priority date is the date of the first filing, not the complete specification date.

Scenario 4: The Field Trial

A agro-tech company tests a new genetically modified seed on a small, fenced-off plot of land next to a public highway. The plants are clearly visible from the road, and their unique properties could be identified by an expert agronomist passing by. The company then files a patent.

  • Issue: Is the invention anticipated by public working (S.32) or display (S.31)?
  • Analysis: This is a grey area. The company will argue it was an experimental trial. However, if the invention was capable of being viewed and understood by a member of the public (especially an expert), it could be considered public working or display. The lack of active measures to maintain secrecy (e.g., opaque fencing, greenhouses) weakens their case. This could be grounds for anticipation.

Exam Notes: Anticipation under The Patents Act, 1970 📝

Overview

Anticipation refers to the disclosure of an invention before the date of filing a patent application for that invention. An invention is not considered new if it's anticipated, and therefore it can't be patented. The provisions on anticipation are detailed in Chapter VI, Sections 29-34 of The Patents Act, 1970.

Section 29: Anticipation by Previous Publication

  • An invention is anticipated if it has been published before the priority date of the patent application.
  • Publication can be in any document, including patent specifications, books, journals, or online publications.
  • Key exception: A document published on or after the priority date of the patent application does not anticipate the invention.

Section 30: Anticipation by Previous Communication to Government

  • An invention is not anticipated if it was communicated to the Government for the purpose of an investigation or trial, provided the communication was made in confidence.
  • This provision protects inventors who need to disclose their invention to the government to get funding or approval.

Section 31: Anticipation by Public Display, etc.

  • An invention is not anticipated if it was displayed at an exhibition or read at a learned society within 12 months before the filing date.
  • The inventor must give notice to the Controller of Patents before the display or reading, as specified in the rules.
  • This is a grace period that allows inventors to disclose their invention publicly without losing their right to patent.

Section 32: Anticipation by Public Working

  • The public working of an invention for the purpose of reasonable trial is not considered anticipation.
  • This is a limited exception, and the working must be genuinely for trial and not for commercial gain.
  • The working must be conducted within 12 months of the filing date.

Section 33: Anticipation by Use and Publication after Provisional Specification

  • If a complete specification is filed after a provisional specification, any use or publication of the invention after the filing of the provisional specification does not anticipate the invention.
  • The priority date is the date of the provisional specification.

Section 34: No Anticipation if Circumstances are Only as Described

  • This section consolidates the exceptions and clarifies that an invention is not anticipated if the disclosure was made under the specific circumstances described in Sections 29, 30, 31, and 32.
  • This provision essentially provides a safe harbor for inventors who make certain types of disclosures.

Tricky MCQs 🧠

  1. An inventor publicly displays his invention at a trade fair on January 1, 2025. He files a patent application for the invention on December 20, 2025. Is the invention anticipated?
    • A) Yes, because it was publicly displayed before the filing date.
    • B) No, because the public display was within 12 months of the filing date.
    • C) Yes, because a public display always anticipates an invention.
    • D) No, because a trade fair is not considered a public display.

Answer: B) No, because the public display was within 12 months of the filing date. Explanation: Section 31 provides a grace period of 12 months for public displays, provided the inventor has given prior notice.

  1. A company discloses its invention in a confidential proposal to the Ministry of Defence. The company subsequently files a patent application for the invention. Is the invention anticipated?
    • A) Yes, because disclosure to a government body is a form of publication.
    • B) No, because the communication was made in confidence for the purpose of investigation.
    • C) Yes, because the government is not bound by confidentiality agreements.
    • D) No, because this is an exception only for academic disclosures.

Answer: B) No, because the communication was made in confidence for the purpose of investigation. Explanation: Section 30 explicitly protects confidential communication to the Government for the purpose of an investigation or trial.

Tricky Use Cases & Scenarios 🤯

  • Scenario 1: The Accidental Public Working. A start-up is testing its new automated cleaning device in a public park. The device is not yet fully functional and is clearly marked "FOR TRIAL PURPOSES ONLY." A rival company sees the device and publishes an article about its working. Can the start-up still file a patent?
    • Analysis: This falls under Section 32 (public working). The key is whether the working was genuinely for a "reasonable trial" and not for commercial use. The fact that the device was not fully functional and was marked for trial purposes strengthens the argument that it was a non-anticipating public working. The publication by the rival company would not anticipate the invention because the initial disclosure was protected under the grace period.
  • Scenario 2: The E-commerce Publication. An inventor publishes a detailed description of her invention, a new type of solar panel, on her personal blog and an e-commerce website to gauge public interest. She removes the posts after a week but decides to file a patent application six months later. Is the invention anticipated?
    • Analysis: This is a classic Section 29 (previous publication) case. The publication on a blog and e-commerce site is a public disclosure. Unlike a formal exhibition or learned society presentation, there is no grace period for this type of publication. The invention is likely anticipated, as the inventor has already made the details publicly available without the protection of Sections 31 or 32.

Exam Notes: CHAPTER VI – ANTICIPATION (Patents Act, 1970)
Relevant Sections: 29 to 34

Overview of Chapter VI: Anticipation

Chapter VI of the Patents Act, 1970 deals with anticipation, which refers to prior disclosure of the invention that may invalidate a patent application. An invention is not patentable if it has been anticipated by prior art. The key principle is "novelty" — the invention must be new.

Section 29: Anticipation by Previous Publication

Key Points:

  • An invention is anticipated if:
    • It was published in India (or elsewhere) before the priority date of the patent application.
    • The publication includes a written description sufficient to enable a person skilled in the art to carry out the invention.
  • Applies to any document, including:
    • Journals
    • Books
    • Theses
    • Patents (Indian or foreign)
    • Websites (if accessible before priority date)
  • "Publication" means made available to the public without confidentiality.
  • "Before the priority date": Includes the date of filing of the provisional or complete specification.
  • Test: Whether the prior document discloses the same invention with sufficient clarity and detail.

Landmark Case:

  • Bishwanath Prasad Radhey Shyam v. Hindustan Metal Industries (1982) – Clarified that mere suggestion or teaching in prior art may not anticipate unless it enables the invention.

Section 30: Anticipation by Previous Communication to Government

Key Points:

  • If the invention was communicated to the Government or any Government department before the priority date, it may anticipate the patent.
  • Applies only if:
    • The communication was not in confidence.
    • The information was made publicly available by the Government.
  • Mere submission to a government body under confidentiality does not anticipate.
  • Example: A research report submitted to DST (Department of Science and Technology) under a confidential project is not anticipatory.

Section 31: Anticipation by Public Display, etc.

Key Points:

  • Anticipation occurs if the invention was:
    • Publicly displayed
    • Used in public
    • Offered for sale
    • Used for public purpose
    • Otherwise published or used in India before the priority date.
  • "Public" means accessible to any member of the public, not necessarily widely known.
  • Example: Exhibiting a prototype at an engineering fair without confidentiality = anticipation.

Section 32: Anticipation by Public Working

Key Points:

  • If the invention was worked in public in India for more than 12 months before the filing date, it is anticipated.
  • "Public working" means:
    • Manufacturing or using the invention openly.
    • Not under secrecy or experimental conditions.
  • Exception: Working done in secrecy or for experimental purposes does not count as public working.
  • 12-month grace period: If public working started less than 12 months before filing, it may not anticipate (but this is narrow and risky).

Section 33: Anticipation by Use and Publication After Provisional Specification

Key Points:

  • Once a provisional specification is filed, any use or publication of the invention by the applicant or someone who obtained knowledge from them does not anticipate the invention.
  • This is a safe harbor for inventors.
  • Conditions:
    • The use/publication must stem from the provisional filing.
    • Third-party independent disclosure still counts as anticipation.
  • Allows inventors to test, market, or disclose after provisional filing without losing novelty.

Section 34: No Anticipation Unless Circumstances Fall Under Sections 29–32

Key Points:

  • Only disclosures falling under Sections 29, 30, 31, or 32 can anticipate an invention.
  • Excludes certain disclosures:
    • Disclosures due to breach of confidence
    • Displays at official international exhibitions (e.g., under Article 11 of Paris Convention) — covered under Rule 20(9) of Patents Rules, 2003, which allows a 12-month grace period.
  • Important: Section 34 acts as a limiting clause — if the disclosure doesn’t fall under 29–32, it cannot be used to challenge novelty.

  • A company launches a product. Competitor reverse-engineers it, discovers the invention, and files a patent.
  • Question: Can the competitor get a patent?
  • Answer: No — the invention was publicly worked (Section 32) and already disclosed. Also, lack of novelty.

  • Answer: No — under Section 34, if the disclosure was due to unauthorized act, it does not anticipate, provided the company files within 12 months (Rule 20(9)).

  • Answer: Yes — if no confidentiality, and used publicly for >12 months → anticipation.

  • An inventor presents at an international exhibition (recognized under Paris Convention) on June 1, 2023. Files Indian patent on July 1, 2024 (13 months later).

  • Answer: Yes — Rule 20(9) allows only 12 months grace period. Filed after 13 months → anticipation applies.

Exam Notes on CHAPTER VI ANTICIPATION (Indian Patents Act, 1970)

Chapter VI of the Indian Patents Act, 1970, deals with anticipation, which is a key ground for determining novelty of an invention. Anticipation occurs when an invention is not new because it has been disclosed in the prior art. However, sections 29-34 outline exceptions where certain disclosures or uses do not constitute anticipation, providing grace periods or protections for inventors. These sections are crucial for patent examination, opposition, and revocation proceedings. Below is a structured summary of each section, presented in a table for clarity.

Section

Key Provisions

Key Exceptions/Conditions

Section 29: Anticipation by previous publication

An invention is not anticipated by a prior publication if: - It was published in an Indian patent specification filed before January 1, 1912. - The publication was obtained from the inventor without consent, and the patent application was filed as soon as reasonably practicable after learning of it (unless commercially worked in India beyond reasonable trial). - Another application was made in contravention of the true inventor's rights, or the invention was used/published without consent after that filing.

Protects against unauthorized publications or abusive filings. Proviso excludes cases where the invention was commercially exploited in India.

Section 30: Anticipation by previous communication to Government

An invention is not anticipated by communication to the Government (or authorized persons) for investigation, or any actions taken for that purpose.

Safeguards disclosures made for official evaluations, e.g., during security reviews or merit assessments. No time limits apply.

Section 31: Anticipation by public display, etc.

An invention is not anticipated by: - Display or use at a notified industrial exhibition with the inventor's consent. - Publication resulting from such display. - Unauthorized use during the exhibition period. - Description in a paper read before a learned society or published in its transactions with consent. Application must be filed within 12 months (as per amended Act) of the exhibition opening or paper reading/publication.

Provides a grace period for exhibitions and academic presentations. Central Government notifies eligible exhibitions.

Section 32: Anticipation by public working

An invention is not anticipated by public working within 1 year before the priority date if done by the inventor/applicant (or with consent) solely for reasonable trial, and public working was necessary due to the invention's nature.

Allows experimental public use without losing novelty, e.g., testing large machinery that can't be done privately.

Section 33: Anticipation by use and publication after provisional specification

Where a provisional specification is filed: - Use or publication after the provisional but before the complete specification does not anticipate if by the applicant or to protect their interest. - For amendments to complete specification, use/publication after filing but before amendment does not anticipate.

Protects interim disclosures between provisional and complete filings, ensuring the patent isn't invalidated by the inventor's own actions during this period.

Section 34: No anticipation if circumstances are only as described in sections 29,30,31 and 32

The Controller cannot refuse a patent, and a patent cannot be revoked/invalidated, solely based on circumstances exempted under sections 29-32.

Overarching protection reinforcing the exceptions; ensures these safe harbors are absolute against anticipation claims.

These notes are based on the exact wording from the Act. Key takeaway: These sections balance protecting inventors from self-disclosure while maintaining the novelty requirement. In practice, evidence of consent, timing, and purpose is critical in hearings.

  4. Post-Provisional Third-Party Publication (Section 33): After provisional, a competitor (unaware) publishes similar idea. Applicant files complete. Use: Anticipated if not by/on behalf of applicant. Tricky: If the competitor derived from applicant's leak, might fall under Section 29(3) as contravention.
  5. Government Investigation Leak (Section 30): Invention communicated for defense review; a contractor accidentally discloses. Use: Not anticipated if "in consequence" of investigation. Tricky: If the disclosure is malicious, may not qualify—requires proving link to official purpose.

Tricky Scenarios

These scenarios test edge cases, often seen in oppositions.

  1. Dual Disclosure Scenario: Inventor displays at a notified exhibition (Section 31) and also communicates to Government (Section 30). A third party uses during exhibition without consent. Files 11 months later. Scenario: Not anticipated, but tricky if exhibition publication overlaps with Government report—must prove each exception separately.
  2. Abusive Filing by Employee (Section 29(3)): Employee files in own name after learning from employer. Employer files later. Scenario: Not anticipated for employer if proven contravention. Tricky: If employee published before employer's filing, evidence of "without consent" is key; absence could invalidate.
  3. Amendment After Publication (Section 33(2)): Complete specification amended post-filing; a publication matches the amendment but occurred after original filing. Scenario: Not anticipated for the amended part. Tricky: If the publication is the inventor's own, but pre-amendment, it might still raise obviousness issues outside anticipation.
  4. International Grace Period Overlap (Section 31): Invention displayed at foreign exhibition (notified in India), paper read at society 6 months later. Files 13 months from exhibition but 7 months from paper. Scenario: Anticipated, as grace period runs from each event separately. Tricky: Multiple events require tracking individual timelines.
  5. Commercial Trial Masquerade (Section 32): Public working labeled "trial" but generates revenue. Within 1 year. Scenario: Anticipated if revenue indicates commercial intent. Tricky: Courts examine if sales were incidental to trial (e.g., disposing tested products) or primary.

Here’s a comprehensive study guide for Chapter VI – Anticipation (Sections 29 to 34) of the Indian Patents Act, 1970, tailored for Patent Agent Exam preparation. This includes:

📘 Exam Notes: Chapter VI – Anticipation

Section 29 – Anticipation by Previous Publication

  • If an invention has been published in India or elsewhere before the priority date, it is not patentable.
  • Exceptions: Disclosure without consent of the inventor, or publication in a journal after filing provisional specification.

Section 30 – Anticipation by Previous Communication to Government

  • Disclosure to the government or authorized person for investigation or testing does not count as anticipation.

Section 31 – Anticipation by Public Display, etc.

  • Display at exhibitions, publication in conference proceedings, or use for demonstration does not count as anticipation if prior notice was given to the Controller.

Section 32 – Anticipation by Public Working

  • Public working for reasonable trial or experiment before the priority date is not anticipation.

Section 33 – Anticipation by Use and Publication After Provisional Specification

  • Use or publication after filing a provisional specification but before complete specification is not anticipation.

Section 34 – No Anticipation if Circumstances Are Only as Described in Sections 29–32

  • Reinforces that disclosures under Sections 29–32 do not invalidate the patent.
