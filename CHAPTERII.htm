Exam Notes: Chapter II - Inventions Not Patentable

Section 3: What are not inventions

  • Key Principle: Certain subject matters are excluded from patentability even if they are novel and inventive.
  • Sub-clauses:
    • (a) Frivolous inventions or contrary to natural laws:
      E.g., perpetual motion machines (violates laws of thermodynamics).
    • (b) Contrary to public order/morality or harmful:
      E.g., devices for illegal activities, biological weapons.
    • (c) Mere discoveries:
      • Scientific principles (e.g., Newton’s laws).
      • Naturally occurring substances (e.g., discovering a new mineral).
      • Exception: Isolated/purified forms of natural substances may be patentable if they exhibit new properties.
    • (d) New forms of known substances without enhanced efficacy:
      • New polymorphs, salts, or derivatives of known drugs.
      • Key test: Must show enhanced therapeutic efficacy (e.g., higher bioavailability leading to better treatment).
      • Exception: New uses of known substances are patentable if they involve a new process/product.
    • (e) Mere admixtures:
      E.g., mixing two known drugs without synergistic effects.
    • (f) Mere rearrangement of known devices:
      E.g., combining a radio and clock without new functionality.
    • (g) Agricultural/horticultural methods:
      E.g., new farming techniques, plant grafting methods.
    • (h) Methods of treatment:
      • Surgical, diagnostic, or therapeutic methods for humans/animals.
      • Exception: Instruments used in treatment (e.g., surgical tools) are patentable.

Section 4: Inventions relating to atomic energy

  • Key Principle: All inventions related to atomic energy are non-patentable.
  • Scope: Includes nuclear reactors, fissile materials, and processes for atomic energy production.
  • Rationale: National security and public interest (Atomic Energy Act, 1962).

Section 5: [Omitted]

  • No longer applicable (originally dealt with food/drug patents).

Tricky MCQs

  1. Which of the following is patentable under Section 3?
    a) A new method of grafting mango trees.
    b) A new polymorph of aspirin with 10% higher bioavailability but no improved therapeutic effect.
    c) A device for detecting earthquakes using a new scientific principle.
    d) A new surgical instrument for heart surgery.
    Answer: (d)
    Reason: (a) is agricultural method (3g); (b) lacks enhanced efficacy (3d); (c) is a scientific principle (3c); (d) is an instrument (not a treatment method).
  2. A scientist isolates a new compound from a plant that kills mosquitoes. Is this patentable?
    a) Yes, as it is a new compound.
    b) No, as it is a naturally occurring substance.
    c) Yes, if it shows enhanced efficacy over existing insecticides.
    d) No, as it relates to atomic energy.
    Answer: (b)
    Reason: Mere discovery of a naturally occurring substance (3c). Exception: If the compound is purified and shows new properties, it may be patentable.
  3. Under Section 3(d), which is NOT patentable?
    a) A new salt of a known drug with 50% higher absorption rate.
    b) A new use of a known drug for treating cancer.
    c) A new process for manufacturing a known drug using a new catalyst.
    d) A new drug derived from a known compound with proven higher efficacy.
    Answer: (a)
    Reason: (a) lacks enhanced therapeutic efficacy (Novartis judgment); (b) new use is patentable; (c) new process with new reactant is patentable; (d) enhanced efficacy makes it patentable.
  4. An invention claims a process for enriching uranium. Is it patentable?
    a) Yes, if it is novel.
    b) No, under Section 4.
    c) Yes, if it is for peaceful purposes.
    d) No, under Section 3(b).
    Answer: (b)
    Reason: Relates to atomic energy (Section 4).

Tricky Use Cases with Answers

  1. Use Case: A company creates a new drug by mixing two known antibiotics. The mixture shows no synergistic effect but reduces pill burden.
    Answer: Not patentable under Section 3(e) (mere admixture without enhanced properties).
    Trick Point: "Reduced pill burden" is a convenience, not enhanced efficacy.
  2. Use Case: An inventor claims a method of diagnosing diabetes using a new AI algorithm.
    Answer: Not patentable under Section 3(h) (diagnostic method).
    Trick Point: The AI software itself may be patentable, but the diagnostic method is excluded.
  3. Use Case: A researcher discovers that turmeric (known substance) heals wounds faster when applied in a new gel form.
    Answer: Not patentable under Section 3(d) (new form without enhanced efficacy).
    Trick Point: "Faster healing" must be proven to be significantly better than existing turmeric applications.
  4. Use Case: A new machine combines a known tractor and a known harvester. The combination allows simultaneous plowing and harvesting.
    Answer: Patentable.
    Reason: Not a "mere arrangement" (3f) as it creates new functionality (synergistic effect).

Tricky Scenarios with Answers

  1. Scenario: A pharmaceutical company files a patent for a new crystal form of an existing drug. The new form dissolves faster but has the same therapeutic effect.
    Answer: Not patentable under Section 3(d).
    Reason: Faster dissolution does not equate to enhanced therapeutic efficacy (per Novartis v. Union of India).
  2. Scenario: An inventor claims a process for treating cancer by radiating patients with a new frequency of microwaves.
    Answer: Not patentable under Section 3(h).
    Reason: Method of treatment for humans. The microwave device itself may be patentable.
  3. Scenario: A scientist patents a genetically modified bacterium that produces insulin.
    Answer: Patentable.
    Reason: The bacterium is not naturally occurring (excluded from 3c) and is a new product with enhanced efficacy.
  4. Scenario: A farmer develops a new irrigation system using solar-powered sensors.
    Answer: Patentable.
    Reason: It is a device, not an agricultural method (3g excludes methods, not devices).

Key Takeaways

  • Section 3(d) is the most litigated: Enhanced efficacy (therapeutic) is mandatory for new forms of known drugs.
  • Treatment methods (3h) and atomic energy (4) are absolute exclusions.
  • Naturally occurring substances (3c) are excluded unless isolated/purified with new properties.
  • Agricultural methods (3g) are excluded, but related devices (e.g., new tractors) are patentable.
  • Mere combinations (3e, 3f) require proof of synergistic effects or new functionality.

 

 

 

 

Chapter II: Inventions Not Patentable - Exam Notes

This chapter is the cornerstone of understanding what cannot be patented in India, as defined by Sections 3, 4, and the omitted Section 5.

Section 3: What are not inventions

This section lists discoveries or inventions which are not considered inventions within the meaning of the Act. Even if they are novel and inventive, they are statutorily barred from patentability.

Clause

Provision

Explanation & Key Points

Leading Case Laws

(a)

Frivolous inventions or contrary to well-established natural laws.

E.g., Perpetual motion machines. The invention must not violate fundamental laws of physics (thermodynamics, gravity).

(b)

Inventions contrary to public order or morality or which cause serious prejudice to health/environment.

A balancing test is used. E.g., inventions related to gambling, torture devices, biological weapons, terminator gene technology.

Monsanto v. Coramandal

(c)

Discovery of a scientific principle or the discovery of any living or non-living substance occurring in nature.

Discovery vs. Invention: Finding something that already exists (e.g., a new plant, microbe, or property of a material) is a discovery. Isolating or purifying it may be patentable if it results in a new industrial application.

Dimminaco A.G. v. Controller (Purification of a live vaccine was held patentable). Funk Bros. Seed Co. v. Kalo Inoculant Co. (US SC: discovery of naturally occurring bacteria mix not patentable)

(d)

Discovery of a new form of a known substance which does not result in the enhancement of the known efficacy of that substance.

Key for Pharmaceuticals: Mere discovery of a new form (salts, esters, polymorphs, derivatives) of a known drug is not patentable unless it significantly enhances the known therapeutic efficacy. Mere improved physical characteristics (solubility, stability) are not sufficient.

Novartis v. Union of India (The Glivec case - Beta crystalline form of Imatinib Mesylate was held non-patentable as it did not demonstrate enhanced therapeutic efficacy).

(e)

Substance obtained by a mere admixture resulting only in the aggregation of the properties of the components.

E.g., A mixture of sugar and tea. Patentable if it shows a synergistic effect (the combined effect is greater than the sum of individual effects).

Hindustan Lever v. Godrej (Soap mixture showing synergistic cleansing effect was held patentable).

(f)

Mere arrangement or re-arrangement or duplication of known devices.

E.g., A clock with two known alarm mechanisms. Must show a new, non-obvious functional interaction between the parts.

(g)

A method of agriculture or horticulture.

This is a broad exclusion. E.g., Method of sowing, pruning, manuring. However, agricultural equipment is patentable.

Narendra Kumar v. Abhimanyu

(h)

Any process for the medicinal, surgical, curative, prophylactic, diagnostic, therapeutic or other treatment of human beings or animals.

Treatment methods are not patentable. However, apparatus/ devices used for treatment are patentable. So are substances (drugs) and their manufacturing processes.

Bajaj Auto Ltd. v. TVS Motor Company Ltd.

(i)

Plants and animals in whole or any part thereof, including seeds, varieties, and species.

Biological processes for their production or propagation are also not patentable. Protection is available under the Protection of Plant Varieties and Farmers' Rights Act, 2001.

(j)

Mathematical or business method or a computer program per se or algorithms.

"Per se" is crucial. A pure algorithm or software is not patentable. However, if the software has a technical application to a hardware component and solves a technical problem, it may be patentable.

Ferid Allani v. Union of India (If a computer program has a technical effect or technical contribution, it is patentable).

(k)

A literary, dramatic, musical or artistic work.

This is the domain of copyright law, not patent law.

(l)

A mere scheme or rule or method of performing mental act or method of playing game.

E.g., A new set of rules for a card game or a new strategy for business management.

(m)

Presentation of information.

E.g., A new way of displaying data on a screen. This is often covered by copyright or design law.

(n)

Topography of integrated circuits.

Protected under the *Semiconductor Integrated Circuits Layout-Design Act, 2000*.

(o)

Traditional Knowledge.

Any invention which is an "aggregation or duplication of known properties of traditionally known component or components." Prevents biopiracy.

Section 4: Inventions relating to atomic energy not patentable

  • No patent shall be granted for an invention relating to atomic energy.
  • The invention must fall within Sub-section (1) of Section 20 of the Atomic Energy Act, 1962.
  • This includes inventions concerning:
    • The use of atomic energy.
    • The use of radioactive substances.
  • This is a blanket prohibition for reasons of national security.

Section 5: [Omitted]

  • Originally, this section barred product patents for substances intended for use, or capable of being used, as food, medicine, or drugs, and for substances prepared by chemical processes (including alloys, optical glass, semiconductors).
  • Only the processes for manufacturing such substances were patentable.
  • This section was omitted by the Patents (Amendment) Act, 2005, to make India TRIPS-compliant.
  • Current Status: Product patents are now allowed for drugs and food. This is why pharmaceutical product patents are now granted in India.

Tricky Use Cases with Answers

Use Case 1: The "Smart" Soil
An inventor creates a soil mixture containing a new, naturally occurring bacteria (discovered in the Amazon) mixed with clay and organic fertilizer. The mixture significantly improves water retention and crop yield.

  • Q: Is this mixture patentable?
  • A: Possibly not. The bacteria itself is a discovery of a living substance (S. 3(c)). The mixture could be challenged as a mere admixture (S. 3(e)) unless it can be shown to have a synergistic effect beyond the aggregation of its components' properties (e.g., the bacteria only works in the presence of that specific clay).

Use Case 2: The Surgical Robotic Arm
A company develops a robotic arm that uses a novel AI-driven algorithm to assist a surgeon in performing minimally invasive heart surgery with sub-millimeter precision.

  • Q: Is this invention patentable?
  • A: Yes. While the method of surgery itself is not patentable (S. 3(h)), the apparatus (the robotic arm) is a patentable product. The algorithm, while falling under S. 3(k), is acceptable because it is not claimed "per se" but as an integral part of a technical apparatus solving a technical problem (precision in surgery).

Use Case 3: A New Crystal for Drug Delivery
A scientist develops a new co-crystal of a known anticancer drug with a benign acid. This co-crystal does not make the drug more potent at killing cancer cells (therapeutic efficacy is the same), but it makes the drug stable at room temperature, eliminating the need for expensive cold-chain storage.

  • Q: Is this co-crystal patentable under Section 3(d)?
  • A: This is a grey area and would be a tough case. The Controller may argue that "efficacy" refers specifically to "therapeutic efficacy" (ability to treat the disease), which has not enhanced. The applicant would have to argue that the stability is a property that enhances the "efficacy" of the drug as a commercial product by making it accessible, or that it improves delivery. The precedent from Novartis leans towards a strict interpretation of "therapeutic efficacy".

Tricky Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1: The Traditional Remedy
An Indian company isolates the active compound from Neem leaves, characterizes it, and patents it in the US as a powerful natural pesticide. They then file for the same patent in India.

  • Q: Would this patent be granted in India? Why or why not?
  • A: No. This would likely be rejected under Section 3(p) as traditional knowledge. The use of Neem as a pesticide is well-documented in ancient Indian texts. The isolation of the active compound could be seen as an aggregation of known properties of a traditionally known component.

Scenario 2: The Atomic Battery
A researcher invents a revolutionary battery that uses a safe, low-energy radioactive isotope to generate electricity for decades. It's designed for use in pacemakers.

  • Q: Discuss the patentability of this invention in India.
  • A: The invention is clearly related to atomic energy (use of a radioactive substance). Regardless of how novel, inventive, or useful it is, it would be statutorily barred from patentability under Section 4 of the Patents Act, read with Section 20(1) of the Atomic Energy Act, 1962.

Scenario 3: The Diagnostic Method App
A startup creates a mobile app that asks users a series of questions about their symptoms, compares them against a massive medical database using a unique algorithm, and provides a probable diagnosis.

  • Q: Is this app patentable?
  • A: Highly unlikely. The core invention is a diagnostic method (S. 3(h)) performed on humans. The algorithm, being a computer program per se (S. 3(k)), is also not patentable. While the user interface could be protected by copyright or design law, the core functionality is barred from patent protection.

Chapter II: Inventions Not Patentable

This chapter of the Patent Act, 1970, lays down the specific categories of inventions that are not patentable in India, even if they meet the basic criteria of novelty, inventive step, and industrial applicability. These exclusions are critical for a patent agent to know.

  • Section 3: What are not inventions 🚫

This section lists 16 categories of subject matter that are not considered "inventions" under the Act. They are not patentable because they are seen as either lacking an inventive step, being frivolous, being contrary to public order or morality, or falling into a category that is not considered a true technological invention.

    • (a) Frivolous or obvious inventions: Anything that claims to be a perpetual motion machine or is contrary to well-established natural laws. For example, a device that claims to create energy from nothing.
    • (b) Inventions contrary to public order or morality: This includes inventions that are illegal, immoral, or cause serious prejudice to human, animal, plant life, or the environment. For example, a weapon of mass destruction.
    • (c) Mere discovery of a scientific principle or abstract theory: A discovery is a finding of something that already exists in nature, not an invention. For example, discovering a new planet or a new property of a material. .
    • (d) Mere discovery of a new form of a known substance: Unless this new form results in an enhancement of known efficacy of the substance. This is particularly relevant for pharmaceutical patents. For example, a new crystalline form of a drug is not patentable unless it shows improved bioavailability. The explanation to this clause clarifies that salts, esters, polymorphs, etc., of a known substance are considered the same substance unless they demonstrate enhanced efficacy.
    • (e) A substance obtained by mere admixture: This applies to a substance that is a simple mixture of two or more known substances, resulting only in the aggregation of the properties of the components. For example, a simple mixture of two different paints to get a new colour, where the new colour is the only result.
    • (f) Arrangement or re-arrangement of devices: This applies to the mere arrangement or re-arrangement or duplication of known devices each functioning independently of one another in a known way. For example, arranging a clock, a radio, and a calculator on a single board.
    • (h) A method of agriculture or horticulture: Methods for cultivating crops or growing plants are not patentable. For example, a new way to water plants to increase yield.
    • (i) Any process for medicinal, surgical, curative, prophylactic, or other treatment of human beings or animals: This exclusion is for methods of treatment, not for the products used in the treatment. For example, a new surgical technique is not patentable, but a new surgical instrument or drug is.
    • (j) Plants and animals in whole or in part, including seeds, varieties, and species: This explicitly excludes living organisms from patentability. However, microorganisms are not excluded.
    • (k) A mathematical or business method or a computer program per se or algorithms: This is a crucial exclusion. While a program on its own isn't patentable, a program used to control a machine or achieve a technical effect might be.
    • (l) A literary, dramatic, musical, or artistic work or any other aesthetic creation whatsoever including cinematographic works and television productions: These are protected by copyright law, not patent law.
    • (m) A mere scheme or rule or method of performing a mental act or method of playing a game: For example, a new method for memorizing data or a new chess strategy.
    • (n) Presentation of information: Simply presenting information in a new format is not patentable. For example, a new way to display a chart.
    • (o) Topography of integrated circuits: These are protected under a separate law, the Semiconductor Integrated Circuits Layout-Design Act, 2000.
    • (p) An invention which is an aggregation or duplication of known properties of traditionally known components: This is a specific exclusion, often referred to as the "traditional knowledge" exclusion, to prevent the patenting of things already known in a community's traditional practices.
  • Section 4: Inventions relating to atomic energy not patentable ⚛️

This section is an absolute prohibition. Any invention that relates to atomic energy is not patentable. This is a matter of national security and international agreements. The decision on what constitutes "atomic energy" is determined by the Central Government. This exclusion overrides all other patentability criteria.

  • Section 5: [Omitted]

This section was omitted by the Patents (Amendment) Act, 2005. Before its omission, it provided a special provision for patents relating to substances intended for use as food, medicine, or drugs. It allowed for process patents only, not product patents, in these fields. The omission of this section brought India's patent law into compliance with its obligations under the TRIPS Agreement, allowing for product patents in pharmaceuticals and agrochemicals.

🧠 Tricky MCQs

  1. A pharmaceutical company develops a new, more stable crystalline form of a known anti-cancer drug. This new form is shown to have significantly higher bioavailability, allowing for a lower, more effective dose. Is this new form patentable in India? a) No, because it is a mere discovery of a new form of a known substance. b) No, because the original drug is already patented. c) Yes, because it results in the enhancement of known efficacy. d) Yes, but only if the original patent has expired.
    • Correct Answer: (c). This question tests the "enhanced efficacy" clause of Section 3(d). The new crystalline form is patentable because it provides a new, tangible benefit (higher bioavailability) that the original form did not have.
  2. A software company develops a new algorithm that significantly improves the efficiency of a robot's navigation system. The patent claim is for the algorithm itself. Is this invention patentable in India? a) Yes, because the algorithm has a clear technical effect. b) No, because a computer program per se is not patentable. c) Yes, because it's a new and inventive algorithm. d) No, because it is a mathematical method.
    • Correct Answer: (b). The question specifies that the claim is for the "algorithm itself." According to Section 3(k), a "computer program per se" or an "algorithm" is not patentable. However, if the claim was for the robot system incorporating the algorithm to achieve a technical effect (e.g., improved navigation), it might be patentable.
  3. A scientist discovers a new species of bacteria that produces a compound effective against a specific plant disease. The scientist's patent claim is for the bacteria itself. Is this patentable? a) Yes, because it's a new species of bacteria with a useful application. b) No, because plants and animals are not patentable. c) No, because it's a mere discovery of something already existing in nature. d) Yes, because microorganisms are not explicitly excluded under Section 3(j).
    • Correct Answer: (d). This is a tricky one. While Section 3(j) excludes "plants and animals in whole or in part," it does not explicitly exclude microorganisms. Indian case law has established that a new microorganism that is isolated and cultured can be patentable. The exclusion in 3(j) is for higher life forms.

🧐 Tricky Use Cases

Use Case 1: A company files a patent for a new "health drink" that is a simple mixture of three well-known herbs (A, B, and C). The company claims that the mixture is patentable because it has a combined effect of improving immunity, which is the sum of the individual known properties of the herbs.

  • Answer: The patent application will likely be rejected under Section 3(e). The invention is a "substance obtained by mere admixture" and results only in the aggregation of the properties of the components. The company is not demonstrating a new, synergistic, or unexpected property that goes beyond the sum of the individual herbs' known benefits. A simple mixture of known ingredients without a new or unexpected effect is not patentable.

Use Case 2: A medical doctor invents a new surgical procedure to perform a specific type of heart bypass surgery more efficiently. The doctor files a patent application for this procedure.

  • Answer: The patent application will be rejected under Section 3(i). This section explicitly excludes "any process for medicinal, surgical, curative, prophylactic or other treatment of human beings or animals" from patentability. The rationale is to prevent the monopolization of medical and surgical procedures, ensuring that doctors are free to use the best available techniques to treat patients.

🔎 Tricky Scenarios

Scenario 1: A company develops a new type of solar panel that is structurally the same as a known one, but is now coated with a newly discovered chemical that makes it 50% more efficient in converting sunlight to electricity. The company files a patent for the "new solar panel."

  • Answer: This is a tricky one. The claim for the "new solar panel" might be challenged as an "arrangement or re-arrangement of known devices" under Section 3(f) if the claim is poorly drafted. However, the core of the invention lies in the new chemical coating which provides a new and unexpected technical effect (50% increase in efficiency). A well-drafted patent application would focus the claims on the new coating technology or the combination of the coating with the panel to achieve the new technical effect. This would overcome the rejection under Section 3(f) and establish patentability.

Scenario 2: A researcher discovers a new species of algae in a deep-sea vent. This algae produces a unique enzyme that can break down plastic. The researcher files a patent for the algae itself and the enzyme.

  • Answer: The claim for the algae itself would likely be rejected under a strict interpretation of Section 3(j), as it is a living organism. However, the claim for the enzyme and the process for producing it (e.g., using the isolated algae culture) would likely be patentable. The enzyme is a non-living substance with a useful application, and the process of isolating and using it is a technical invention.

📚 Related Questions from The Patent Act 1970 (Previous Years)

    • Answer: Before its omission in 2005, Section 5 allowed for process patents only for substances intended for use as food, medicine, or drugs. This provision was in the original Act and has been the subject of significant legal interpretation, particularly in pharmaceutical cases. This is a core concept that has remained consistent.
  3. Under the Act, is a method for treating a plant disease patentable?
    • Answer: This falls under Section 3(h), which excludes "a method of agriculture or horticulture." A method for treating a plant disease would be considered part of horticulture, and therefore not patentable. The focus of the Act is to avoid monopolies on basic agricultural practices.

  • Discovery vs Invention: A discovery reveals what already exists in nature; an invention creates something new
  • Natural laws, mathematical formulas, abstract theories are not patentable
  • Example: Discovery of DNA structure is not patentable, but a method of using DNA for genetic engineering may be

(b) Discovery of Any Living Thing or Non-living Substance Occurring in Nature

  • Naturally occurring substances cannot be patented
  • Exception: If the substance is extracted or produced by a special process, the process may be patentable
  • Example: Turmeric cannot be patented, but a process for extracting curcumin from turmeric may be

(c) Mere Discovery of a New Form of Known Substance

  • New forms must show enhanced efficacy to be patentable
  • Includes salts, esters, ethers, polymorphs, metabolites, pure forms, particle sizes, isomers, mixtures
  • Key Requirement: Must demonstrate significantly enhanced efficacy
  • Example: A new crystalline form of an existing drug must show better bioavailability or stability

(d) Mere Use of Known Process, Machine or Apparatus

  • Unless it results in a new product or employs at least one new reactant
  • Example: Using a known centrifuge for a new separation process may not be patentable unless it produces a novel product

(e) Mere Arrangement, Rearrangement or Duplication of Features

  • Simple combinations of known elements without synergistic effect
  • Example: Combining a pen and a torch without any technical advantage

(f) Method of Agriculture or Horticulture

  • Traditional farming methods, plant breeding techniques
  • Exception: Biotechnological processes may be patentable if they involve technical steps

(g) Medical Methods

  • Any process for medicinal, surgical, curative, prophylactic, diagnostic, therapeutic treatment
  • Treatment of animals also excluded
  • Exception: Products (drugs, devices) are patentable, but methods of treatment are not

(h) Business Methods and Computer Programs Per Se

  • Pure business methods without technical content
  • Computer programs by themselves (per se) are not patentable
  • Exception: Computer programs with technical applications or embedded in hardware may be considered

(i) Mathematical Methods and Algorithms

  • Pure mathematical formulas, algorithms
  • Exception: If applied to solve a technical problem, may be patentable

(j) Literary, Dramatic, Musical or Artistic Works

  • Covered under copyright, not patents
  • Includes databases, compilations

(k) Mental Acts, Playing Games, and Business Transactions

  • Pure mental processes, game rules, business schemes
  • Exception: Technical implementation of games (gaming software/hardware) may be patentable

(l) Presentation of Information

  • Mere display or arrangement of information
  • Example: A new format for displaying data without technical means

Important Judicial Interpretations

Novartis AG v. Union of India (2013)

  • Supreme Court clarified that for new forms under Section 3(d), "significantly enhanced efficacy" must be demonstrated
  • Beta crystalline form of Imatinib Mesylate was rejected for lack of enhanced efficacy

Ferid Allani v. Union of India (2019)

  • Delhi High Court held that computer programs with technical applications are patentable

Scope of Exclusion

  • Inventions relating to production, control, use of atomic energy

Exam Notes on CHAPTER II: INVENTIONS NOT PATENTABLE

Section 3: What are not inventions

This section provides an exhaustive list of subject matters that do not qualify as inventions under the Patents Act, 1970. It serves as a negative list to exclude certain types of innovations from patentability, ensuring patents are granted only for true technical advancements. The list is divided into clauses (a) to (p), each addressing specific exclusions. Key points to remember for exams:

  • Purpose: To prevent patenting of frivolous, immoral, or non-technical matters; aligns with TRIPS Article 27 for exclusions on public policy grounds.
  • Key Clauses with Examples:
    • (a) Frivolous inventions or those contrary to well-established natural laws (e.g., perpetual motion machine).
    • (b) Inventions whose use/exploitation is contrary to public order/morality or causes prejudice to human/animal/plant life, health, or environment (e.g., device for human cloning).
    • (c) Mere discovery of scientific principle, abstract theory, or any living/non-living substance in nature (e.g., discovering gravity or isolating a natural gene without modification).
    • (d) Mere discovery of new form/property/use of known substance without enhanced efficacy; mere use of known process unless it yields new product/new reactant (e.g., new salt of known drug without better efficacy). Explanation: Derivatives like salts, polymorphs, isomers are same substance unless efficacy differs significantly – critical for pharma patents.
    • (e) Substance from mere admixture aggregating properties (e.g., mixing known painkillers without synergy).
    • (f) Mere arrangement/re-arrangement/duplication of known devices functioning independently (e.g., combining known fan and heater without novel interaction).
    • (g) Omitted by 2002 Amendment.
    • (h) Method of agriculture/horticulture (e.g., new planting technique).
    • (i) Processes for medicinal/surgical/curative/prophylactic/diagnostic/therapeutic treatment of humans/animals to free them from disease or increase economic value (e.g., new surgical method; but devices/apparatus are patentable).
    • (j) Plants/animals (whole/parts), seeds, varieties, species, essentially biological processes for production/propagation (e.g., genetically modified plant via natural breeding; but microorganisms and non-biological processes are patentable).
    • (k) Mathematical/business methods, computer programs per se, algorithms (e.g., software algorithm alone; but technical applications may qualify if not "per se").
    • (l) Literary/dramatic/musical/artistic works, cinematographic/TV productions (protected under copyright).
    • (m) Mere scheme/rule/method of mental act/playing game (e.g., new chess rule).
    • (n) Presentation of information (e.g., new data format without technical effect).
    • (o) Topography of integrated circuits (protected under separate law).
    • (p) Traditional knowledge or aggregation/duplication of known properties of traditional components (e.g., known herbal remedy repackaged).
  • Exam Tips: Focus on pharma/biotech exclusions (d, i, j, p); case laws like Novartis (3(d) efficacy) are often tested. Objections under Section 3 are absolute bars – no overcoming via amendments. Cross-reference with Section 2(1)(j) for invention definition (novel, inventive, industrial application).

Section 4: Inventions relating to atomic energy not patentable

  • Key Provision: No patent for inventions relating to atomic energy falling under Section 20(1) of the Atomic Energy Act, 1962.
  • Scope: Covers production, control, use, or disposal of atomic energy/radioactive substances. "Atomic energy" includes energy from fission/fusion/radioactive decay.
  • Procedure: Applications screened by Controller; if suspected, referred to Central Government/Atomic Energy Regulatory Board (AERB) for review. If confirmed, application refused or revoked.
  • Rationale: National security and public safety; aligns with international non-proliferation treaties.
  • Exam Tips: Rare but tested in MCQs on exclusions; distinguish from Section 3(b) (prejudice to life/environment). No exceptions – absolute bar.

Section 5: [Omitted]

  • Historical Context: Omitted by Patents (Amendment) Act, 2005 (effective 2005 to comply with TRIPS).
  • Previous Provision: Limited patents for food, medicines, drugs, chemicals to processes only (no product patents). Methods/processes for manufacture were patentable, but substances themselves were not.
  • Current Impact: Product patents now allowed for food/pharma/chemicals, subject to Section 3(d). This shift enabled evergreening scrutiny.
  • Exam Tips: Know omission date and reason (TRIPS compliance); contrast pre-2005 vs. post-2005 regime.

Indian Patents Act, 1970 - Chapter II: Inventions Not Patentable

Section 3: What are not inventions

Section 3 of the Patents Act defines subject matter that, even if novel and inventive, is not considered an "invention" for the purpose of patentability in India. These exclusions are in place to prevent the monopolization of certain areas or to maintain India's public policy goals. 

Key exclusions under Section 3

Section 3 outlines several categories of subject matter that are not considered inventions. These include things that are frivolous or contrary to natural laws, as well as those contrary to public order or morality. Mere discoveries of scientific principles, abstract theories, or substances found in nature are also excluded. Additionally, discovering a new form of a known substance without enhancing its efficacy, or a new property, use, or a known process that doesn't result in a new product or use a new reactant, is not patentable. Simple mixtures of known substances that only combine their properties, and mere arrangements or re-arrangements of known devices functioning in their usual ways, are also excluded. Methods of agriculture, horticulture, and processes for treating humans or animals are not patentable. Plants and animals (with the exception of microorganisms) and essentially biological processes for their production or propagation are excluded. Mathematical or business methods, computer programs per se, and algorithms are not patentable. Literary, dramatic, musical, or artistic works and other aesthetic creations are also excluded. Further exclusions cover mere schemes or methods for performing mental acts or playing games, presentations of information, topography of integrated circuits, and traditional knowledge. 

Section 4: Inventions relating to atomic energy not patentable

Section 4 prohibits the patenting of inventions related to atomic energy. 

Key features of Section 4

This section states that no patent will be granted for inventions related to atomic energy as defined in the Atomic Energy Act, 1962. The Central Government retains ownership of all such inventions, making them ineligible for private patents. The definition of atomic energy in the Act is broad, encompassing various materials and processes related to its creation, management, or utilization. This exclusion is aimed at safeguarding national security and public safety by ensuring government control over inventions in this critical field.

Tricky use cases with answers

Section 3

  1. Use Case: A scientist discovers a new species of antibiotic-producing bacteria in a remote forest. She isolates the bacteria and identifies the specific gene responsible for antibiotic production. Can she patent the newly discovered bacteria or the isolated gene?
    • Answer: The newly discovered bacteria is not patentable as it is a naturally occurring living thing (Section 3(c)). The isolated gene per se is also a discovery of something existing in nature. However, a genetically modified microorganism using this gene might be patentable under certain conditions, as Section 3(j) allows for patenting microorganisms that are not merely discovered. Patentability depends on demonstrating human intervention leading to a new and inventive step.
  1. Use Case: A software company develops a new software application for managing customer relationships. The application utilizes a novel algorithm for predicting customer behavior and optimizing sales strategies.
    • Answer: The software application itself is generally not patentable under Section 3(k) as a "computer program per se". However, if the algorithm provides a technical effect and solves a technical problem beyond a mere business or mathematical method, those specific technical aspects might be patentable. The key is to show a technical contribution, not just an abstract idea or business method. 

Section 4

  1. Use Case: A research team develops a new type of radiation-shielding material that uses a novel combination of elements. While the material is intended for use in nuclear facilities, it also has potential applications in medical imaging and space exploration.
    • Answer: The patentability is complex due to the intended use in nuclear facilities, which could fall under Section 4 if it relates to atomic energy as defined by the Atomic Energy Act, 1962. The Patent Office would likely consult with the Department of Atomic Energy (DAE). However, if the invention has significant technical applications outside the restricted scope of atomic energy, a patent might be possible for those specific applications, provided a reasoned order, not just a DAE directive, supports any rejection. 

Tricky scenarios with answers

Section 3

  1. Scenario: An inventor develops a revolutionary method for treating a specific disease. The method involves a unique sequence of diagnostic tests followed by a personalized drug regimen tailored to the patient's genetic profile. Can this entire method be patented?
    • Answer: No, the entire method is likely not patentable because Section 3(i) excludes methods of diagnosis and treatment of human beings. While the diagnostic tests and drug compositions might be patentable, the method of performing the diagnosis or treatment is generally excluded for public access to medical practices.
  1. Scenario: A company develops a new type of online banking system that allows users to manage their finances through a unique graphical interface. The system employs a novel method for securing transactions and detecting fraud.
    • Answer: The online banking system as a whole might be considered a business method, excluded under Section 3(k). However, if the novel method for securing transactions and detecting fraud involves a technical advancement and has a technical effect beyond mere business operations, those technical aspects might be patentable. The key is demonstrating a concrete technical solution to a technical problem. 

Section 4

  1. Scenario: A company invents a new method for detecting minute traces of radioactive materials in environmental samples. This method is highly sensitive and could be used for both environmental monitoring and detecting illicit nuclear material.
    • Answer: This invention would likely be examined under Section 4 because it relates to detecting radioactive materials. The Patent Office would likely consult with the Department of Atomic Energy (DAE). Despite its environmental benefits, the relationship to radioactive substances and potential use in detecting illicit nuclear material could lead to non-patentability to ensure national security and prevent misuse.
