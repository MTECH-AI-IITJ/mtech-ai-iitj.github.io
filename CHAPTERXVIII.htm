Exam Notes: Chapter XVIII - Suits Concerning Infringement of Patents (Sections 104-111)

Key Concepts Simplified

  1. Jurisdiction (Section 104)
    • Simple Explanation: Only a District Court (or higher) can hear patent infringement suits. No lower court (e.g., Small Causes Court) can handle these cases.
    • Key Point: Suits must be filed where the defendant resides, works, or where the infringement occurred.
  2. Burden of Proof (Section 104A)
    • Simple Explanation:
      • For product patents: The plaintiff (patent holder) must prove infringement.
      • For process patents: The defendant must prove they did not use the patented process (since the plaintiff can’t easily access the defendant’s manufacturing methods).
    • Key Point: Shifts burden to defendant in process patent cases.
  3. Declaration of Non-Infringement (Section 105)
    • Simple Explanation: If someone is threatened with a lawsuit (e.g., via legal notice), they can ask the court to declare that their product/process does not infringe the patent.
    • Key Point: Protects against baseless threats.
  4. Relief for Groundless Threats (Section 106)
    • Simple Explanation: If a patent holder threatens infringement without valid grounds, the threatened person can sue for:
      • Declaration that threats are unjustified.
      • Injunction to stop threats.
      • Damages for losses (e.g., business disruption).
    • Key Point: Penalizes false threats.
  5. Defences in Infringement Suits (Section 107)
    • Simple Explanation: Defendants can argue:
      • The patent is invalid (e.g., invention was obvious or not new).
      • They did not infringe (e.g., their product is different).
      • The patent holder is not entitled to the patent (e.g., stolen invention).
      • Research use (allowed under law).
    • Key Point: Multiple defences available.
  6. Acts Not Considered Infringement (Section 107A)
    • Simple Explanation: These acts are exempt from infringement:
      • Using a patented invention for research or government regulatory approval (e.g., drug testing).
      • Importing patented products from an authorized seller.
      • Experimental use (e.g., academic studies).
    • Key Point: Safeguards innovation and public interest.
  7. Reliefs in Infringement Suits (Section 108)
    • Simple Explanation: If infringement is proven, the court can order:
      • Injunction (stop infringement).
      • Damages (compensation for losses) or account of profits (hand over profits from infringement).
      • Seizure/destruction of infringing goods.
    • Key Point: Aims to restore the patent holder’s rights.
  8. Rights of Exclusive Licensee (Section 109)
    • Simple Explanation: An exclusive licensee (sole user of the patent) can sue infringers independently without the patent holder’s involvement.
    • Key Point: Empowers licensees to enforce rights.
  9. Rights of Compulsory Licensee (Section 110)
    • Simple Explanation: A compulsory licensee (government-granted license) can sue infringers but must notify the patent holder, who can join the suit.
    • Key Point: Balances public access with patent rights.
  10. Restriction on Damages (Section 111)
    • Simple Explanation: No damages or profits can be claimed for infringement that occurred before the patent application was published. Only an injunction (stop order) is allowed for pre-publication infringement.
    • Key Point: Protects users unaware of the pending patent.

Chapter XVIII: Suits Concerning Infringement of Patents - Exam Notes

This chapter deals with what happens when someone's patent rights are violated (infringed) and how they can go to court to fix it.

Section 104: Jurisdiction

  • Simple Meaning: This section tells you which court has the power to hear a patent infringement case.
  • Key Points:
    • A patent infringement suit must be filed in a District Court or a High Court that has the authority to handle it.
    • The court must be located either:
      1. Where the defendant (the person being sued) lives or works.
      2. Where the cause of action (i.e., where the infringement actually happened) took place.

Use Case: A company in Mumbai holds a patent. They discover a shop in Delhi selling products that infringe their patent. They can file the lawsuit either in Delhi (where the infringement is happening) or in the city where the owner of the shop lives/operates their business.

Section 104A: Burden of Proof in Case of Suits Concerning Infringement

  • Simple Meaning: This is a special rule about who has to prove what in court.
  • Key Points:
    • Normally, the person who files the case (the patent owner) has to prove that infringement occurred.
    • However, for process patents (a patent on a method of making something), the burden of proof can shift to the defendant.
    • If the product made by the alleged infringer is identical to the product made by the patented process, the court can order the defendant to prove that their process is different and does not infringe the patent.

Use Case: Company A has a patent for a special process to create super-strong glass. Company B starts selling identical super-strong glass. In court, Company A can show the glasses are the same and ask the judge to force Company B to reveal its manufacturing process to prove it's not using Company A's patented method.

Section 105: Power of Court to Make Declaration as to Non-Infringement

  • Simple Meaning: This allows a potential defendant to go to court first and ask for a declaration that their product/process does NOT infringe a patent.
  • Key Points:
    • It's a proactive tool for companies to get legal clarity and avoid baseless lawsuits.
    • The person applying must have written to the patent owner asking for acknowledgment of non-infringement, and the patent owner must have refused or failed to give it.

Use Case: A startup develops a new software tool. They are worried it might be seen as infringing a big tech company's broad patent. Before launching, they can ask the court to declare that their tool does not infringe, giving them confidence to proceed.

Section 106: Power of Court to Grant Relief in Cases of Groundless Threats

  • Simple Meaning: This protects people from bullying. If someone is making empty legal threats of patent infringement to scare you, you can sue them.
  • Key Points:
    • A person who receives such threats can go to court to stop the threats.
    • They can also ask for damages for any losses caused by the threats.
    • The person making the threats can only avoid this if they can prove that their threats were justified and that the acts actually do infringe their patent.

Use Case: A large company sends threatening letters to all the competitors of a small business, claiming they are infringing a patent, even though they aren't. The competitors can sue the large company to make them stop the threats and pay for the harm caused.

Section 107: Defences in Suit for Infringement

  • Simple Meaning: This lists the valid arguments a defendant can use to fight an infringement lawsuit.
  • Key Points: Common defences include:
    • The patent is invalid (it should never have been granted).
    • The defendant has a license to use the patent.
    • The act falls under exceptions (like for research or use on a foreign ship).

Use Case: A generic medicine manufacturer is sued for infringement. Their defence is that the patent held by the big pharma company is invalid because the drug was not actually novel when it was patented.

Section 107A: Certain Acts Not to Be Considered as Infringement (Bolar Provision)

  • Simple Meaning: It is not infringement to make, use, or sell a patented invention for the purpose of developing information to get regulatory approval (e.g., from the FDA).
  • Key Points:
    • This is crucial for generic drug companies. It allows them to develop and test copies of a drug before the original patent expires so they can launch immediately after.
    • This ensures cheaper generic medicines are available to the public as soon as possible.

Use Case: A company makes a sample of a patented drug six months before the patent expires. They use it to conduct tests required to get government approval to sell the drug. This act is not considered infringement.

Section 108: Reliefs in Suits for Infringement

  • Simple Meaning: If the patent owner wins the case, these are the rewards or remedies the court can give them.
  • Key Points: The court can grant:
    • An injunction (a court order to stop the infringing activity immediately).
    • Damages (money to compensate for the loss suffered).
    • An account of profits (forcing the infringer to hand over all the profits they made from the infringement).
    • Seizure of the infringing goods.

Use Case: After winning an infringement case, the court orders the infringing company to 1) immediately stop production, 2) destroy all existing stock, and 3) pay ₹1 crore in damages to the patent owner.

Section 109: Right of Exclusive Licensee to Take Proceedings

  • Simple Meaning: An exclusive licensee (someone who has the sole right to use the patent, often to the exclusion of even the patent owner) has the right to sue for infringement in their own name.
  • Key Points:
    • They must make the actual patent owner a party to the suit (usually as a co-plaintiff).

Use Case: A university patents a new battery technology and gives an exclusive license to Company X to manufacture it. If Company Y starts infringing, Company X can directly file a lawsuit against Company Y.

Section 110: Right of Licensee Under Section 84 to Take Proceedings

  • Simple Meaning: A compulsory licensee (someone granted a license by the government without the patent owner's full consent, under S.84) can also sue for infringement, but only if the patent owner refuses to do so.
  • Key Points:
    • They must first call upon the patent owner to take legal action.
    • If the patent owner refuses or neglects to sue within a certain time, the compulsory licensee can then sue in their own name.

Use Case: Company Z is a compulsory licensee for a life-saving drug. They notice another company infringing. They write to the patent owner asking them to sue. The patent owner ignores the letter. After 3 months, Company Z can file the infringement suit itself.

Section 111: Restriction on Power of Court to Grant Damages

  • Simple Meaning: You can't get damages for infringement that happened before the date of publication of the patent application or during the period when the patent had lapsed (e.g., for non-payment of renewal fees) and was later restored.

Use Case: A patent application was published on January 1, 2023, and granted on July 1, 2023. The patent owner discovers infringement that started on March 1, 2023 (between publication and grant). They can sue, but they cannot claim monetary damages for the period before the grant date (July 1). They might only get an injunction.

Here are exam notes in simple language, along with practice MCQs and use cases for each section of Chapter XVIII, Suits Concerning Infringement of Patents from the Indian Patents Act.

104. Jurisdiction

Simple Notes: This section explains which courts have the power to hear a patent infringement case. A lawsuit for patent infringement must be filed in a District Court or a High Court. A very important point is that if the person being sued (the defendant) files a "counter-claim" asking for the patent to be canceled (revoked), the entire case is then moved to the High Court for a decision.

Use Case: Company A sues Company B in a District Court for infringing on its patent. Company B responds by not only denying infringement but also filing a counter-claim, arguing that Company A's patent is not valid and should be revoked. Because of this counter-claim, the District Court cannot continue to hear the case, and it is automatically transferred to the High Court.

Practice MCQ: A company wants to file a suit against a competitor for patent infringement. In which court can they file the suit? A) District Court B) High Court C) Supreme Court D) Both A and B Correct Answer: D) Both A and B

104A. Burden of proof in case of suits concerning infringement

Simple Notes: Generally, the person claiming infringement (the patent holder) has to prove it. However, this section makes an exception for a process patent. If a company has a patent for a process to make a new product, and a competitor starts selling an identical product, the court can tell the competitor to prove that their process is different. This is a shift in the "burden of proof." This happens only if the patent holder has made reasonable efforts to figure out the competitor's process and failed, and they can first prove that the competitor's product is identical to theirs.

Use Case: Company Alpha has a patent for a special process to create a new type of lightweight plastic. Company Beta starts selling an identical-looking lightweight plastic. Company Alpha suspects infringement but cannot figure out how Beta is making the plastic. Alpha sues Beta. The court, seeing that the products are identical and Alpha has been unable to determine the process, orders Company Beta to prove that the process they use to make the plastic is different from Company Alpha's patented process.

Practice MCQ: Under what conditions can the burden of proof shift from the patent holder to the defendant in a patent infringement suit? A) When the patent is for a product, not a process. B) When the defendant is a large corporation. C) When the patent is for a process to obtain a new product, the products are identical, and the patent holder has been unable to determine the defendant's process after reasonable efforts. D) The burden of proof never shifts to the defendant. Correct Answer: C) When the patent is for a process to obtain a new product, the products are identical, and the patent holder has been unable to determine the defendant's process after reasonable efforts.

105. Power of court to make declaration as to non-infringement

Simple Notes: This is a way for a company to proactively get legal clarity. If a company is worried that its product or process might infringe on someone else's patent, it can sue the patent holder to get a court declaration that their actions do not infringe. This is a way for the company to protect itself and avoid future lawsuits. To do this, the company must first ask the patent holder in writing for a clear statement of non-infringement, and the patent holder must have refused or ignored the request. The court will not consider the patent's validity in this type of suit.

Use Case: A startup is developing a new gadget. They believe it might be similar to a patented invention by a larger company, but they don't think it infringes. To get legal certainty before they launch, they write to the patent holder asking for a written acknowledgment that their gadget does not infringe. The patent holder ignores the request. The startup can then file a suit under Section 105 to get a court declaration of non-infringement.

Practice MCQ: A company can file a suit for a declaration of non-infringement under Section 105 if: A) The patent holder has threatened them with legal action. B) They have applied in writing to the patent holder for a declaration and the patent holder has refused or neglected to give it. C) The patent holder has already filed a suit for infringement against them. D) The patent is more than five years old. Correct Answer: B) They have applied in writing to the patent holder for a declaration and the patent holder has refused or neglected to give it.

106. Power of court to grant relief in cases of groundless threats of infringement proceedings

Simple Notes: This section protects people from unjustified threats of being sued for patent infringement. If a patent holder (or anyone else) threatens a person with an infringement lawsuit without a valid reason, the person being threatened can sue for relief. The court can declare the threats unjustifiable, issue an injunction to stop the threats, and even award damages. However, merely notifying someone that a patent exists is not considered a threat.

Use Case: A large corporation sends threatening letters to several small businesses, claiming they are infringing on a patent that the corporation actually lost in court a year ago. The small businesses can file a suit under Section 106 to stop these groundless threats and possibly claim damages for the harm caused to their businesses.

Practice MCQ: Which of the following is NOT a type of relief a court may grant for a groundless threat of infringement? A) A declaration that the threats are unjustifiable. B) An injunction against the continuance of the threats. C) An order to revoke the patent. D) Damages for any loss suffered. Correct Answer: C) An order to revoke the patent.

107. Defences, etc., in suit for infringement

Simple Notes: This section lists the defenses an alleged infringer can use in a lawsuit. The most common and powerful defense is that the patent itself is invalid and should be revoked. Any reason a patent can be revoked under Section 64 (e.g., lack of novelty, lack of inventive step) can be used as a defense in an infringement suit.

Use Case: Company X sues Company Y for infringing its patent on a specific type of furniture hinge. In its defense, Company Y argues that the hinge design was actually described in a design magazine from 1990, meaning the patent lacked "novelty" when it was granted. This is a valid defense under Section 107.

Practice MCQ: A defendant in a patent infringement suit can use any ground for revocation of the patent under Section 64 as a ground for their defense. A) True B) False Correct Answer: A) True

107A. Certain acts not to be considered as infringement

Simple Notes: This section outlines specific actions that are not considered patent infringement, even if they involve a patented invention. The most notable exception is the "Bolar provision," which allows a third party to use a patented invention (like a drug) for research and development purposes, specifically for obtaining regulatory approvals to market a generic version after the patent expires. This is also known as a "research exemption." Additionally, the import of patented products from someone authorized to sell them is not considered an infringement.

Use Case: A generic drug company, Beta Pharma, uses a patented drug molecule to conduct clinical trials and other tests required by the government to get regulatory approval to sell a generic version. They do this while the patent is still active. This act, done solely to gather information for regulatory submission, is protected under Section 107A and does not constitute patent infringement.

Practice MCQ: Using a patented invention for research and development to get regulatory approval for a generic product is: A) An act of patent infringement. B) Not considered an infringement under the "Bolar provision." C) An infringement, but with a reduced penalty. D) Only allowed after the patent has expired. Correct Answer: B) Not considered an infringement under the "Bolar provision."

108. Reliefs in suits for infringement

Simple Notes: This section lists the remedies a court can grant to a successful patent holder in an infringement suit. The primary reliefs are:

  1. An injunction to stop the infringer from continuing the infringing activities.
  2. Either damages (to compensate for the patent holder's losses) or an account of profits (to make the infringer hand over the profits they made from the infringement). The patent holder can choose which one.
  3. The court can also order the seizure, forfeiture, or destruction of the infringing goods.

Use Case: A court finds that Company A has infringed on Company B's patent for a smartphone camera. The court issues an injunction preventing Company A from selling the infringing phone models. Additionally, Company B, as the patent holder, chooses to receive an "account of profits," so the court orders Company A to hand over all the profits it made from selling the infringing phones.

Practice MCQ: In a patent infringement suit, the court can grant a patent holder a choice between which two reliefs? A) An injunction and damages. B) Damages and an account of profits. C) Seizure of goods and an injunction. D) An injunction and an account of profits. Correct Answer: B) Damages and an account of profits.

109. Right of exclusive licensee to take proceedings against infringement

Simple Notes: An exclusive licensee is someone who has been given the sole right to use a patent by the patent holder. This section gives an exclusive licensee the same right as the original patentee to sue for infringement. In a suit filed by an exclusive licensee, the court will consider the losses suffered by the licensee and the profits made by the infringer that relate to the licensee's rights. The original patentee must also be joined as a defendant in the lawsuit unless they are a co-plaintiff.

Use Case: Patentee A grants Company B an exclusive license to manufacture and sell their patented product in India. When Company C starts selling an infringing product, Company B, as the exclusive licensee, can file a lawsuit against Company C for infringement. The court would then consider the financial losses and harm suffered by Company B.

Practice MCQ: Who, besides the patentee, has the right to file a suit for patent infringement under Section 109? A) Any person B) A non-exclusive licensee C) An exclusive licensee D) The Government Correct Answer: C) An exclusive licensee

110. Right of licensee under section 84 to take proceedings against infringement

Simple Notes: This section deals with compulsory licenses. A compulsory license is a license granted by the government to a company to use a patent without the patent holder's permission, often for public health reasons, if the patent holder is not meeting the public's needs. If a person is granted a license under Section 84, they can ask the patent holder to sue for infringement. If the patent holder fails to do so within two months, the compulsory licensee can file the lawsuit themselves.

Use Case: The government grants Company P a compulsory license to produce a life-saving drug patented by Company Q, as Company Q is not supplying enough of the drug at an affordable price. Company R starts selling an infringing version of the drug. Company P can ask Company Q to sue Company R. If Company Q does not act within two months, Company P can then file the infringement suit on its own.

Practice MCQ: If a person holding a compulsory license under Section 84 wants to sue for infringement, they must first: A) Get the court's permission. B) Wait for the patent to expire. C) Ask the patentee to take action and wait for two months. D) Pay a royalty to the patentee. Correct Answer: C) Ask the patentee to take action and wait for two months.

111. Restriction on power of court to grant damages or account of profits for infringement

Simple Notes: This section puts limits on when a court can award monetary relief (damages or account of profits) in an infringement suit. The court cannot award these if the defendant can prove they were not aware and had no reasonable reason to believe the patent existed at the time of infringement. Also, damages or profits cannot be awarded for any infringement that happened during a time when the patent's renewal fees were not paid. The court can still, however, grant an injunction to stop the infringement.

Use Case: Company A sues Company B for patent infringement. Company B shows in court that the patent was not published in any official gazette, and they had no way of knowing it existed. The court may still grant an injunction to stop Company B's activities, but it may refuse to award damages or an account of profits to Company A because of Company B's lack of knowledge.

Practice MCQ: Under what circumstance can the court NOT grant damages or an account of profits in an infringement suit? A) The defendant was unaware of the patent's existence and had no reasonable grounds to believe it existed. B) The defendant is a small business. C) The patent is for a process and not a product. D) The patent holder has a non-exclusive license. Correct Answer: A) The defendant was unaware of the patent's existence and had no reasonable grounds to believe it existed.

Exam Notes in Simple Language

Section 104: Jurisdiction

Simple Explanation:

  • This section decides which court can hear patent infringement cases
  • Generally, the District Court where the defendant lives or does business can hear the case
  • The court where the cause of action arose (where infringement happened) also has jurisdiction

Key Points:

  • Multiple courts may have jurisdiction
  • Plaintiff can choose the most convenient court
  • High Courts also have jurisdiction in patent matters

Section 104A: Burden of Proof in Infringement Suits

Simple Explanation:

  • In patent cases, the person claiming infringement must prove it
  • However, if the patent is for a process, and the defendant makes the same product, the burden shifts
  • The defendant must then prove they didn't use the patented process

Key Points:

  • Normal rule: Plaintiff proves infringement
  • Exception: Process patents - defendant must prove non-infringement
  • Applies when identical products are made

Section 105: Declaration of Non-Infringement

Simple Explanation:

  • Anyone can ask the court to declare that their actions don't infringe a patent
  • This helps people get clarity before starting business activities
  • Prevents unnecessary legal disputes

Key Points:

  • Preventive legal remedy
  • Court can declare activities as non-infringing
  • Useful for business planning

Section 106: Protection Against Groundless Threats

Simple Explanation:

  • If someone falsely threatens patent infringement action, the threatened party can sue them
  • Court can give damages and stop the false threats
  • Protects businesses from unfair intimidation

Key Points:

  • Remedy against false threats
  • Can claim damages
  • Court can issue injunctions against threats

Section 107: Defences in Infringement Suits

Simple Explanation:

  • Lists what defences can be used when sued for patent infringement
  • Main defences include: patent is invalid, no actual infringement occurred, prior use rights

Key Points:

  • Patent invalidity is a complete defence
  • Prior user rights protect earlier users
  • Non-infringement can be proved

Section 107A: Acts Not Considered Infringement

Simple Explanation:

  • Certain activities are specifically excluded from being called infringement
  • Includes research, experimentation, and some government uses
  • Protects legitimate activities

Key Points:

  • Research and experimentation exempted
  • Government use may be exempted
  • Bona fide activities protected

Section 108: Reliefs in Infringement Suits

Simple Explanation:

  • Lists what remedies a patent owner can get if infringement is proved
  • Includes: stopping the infringement (injunction), damages, account of profits, seizure of infringing goods

Key Points:

  • Injunction (stop infringement)
  • Monetary compensation (damages/profits)
  • Seizure and destruction of infringing products

Section 109: Rights of Exclusive Licensee

Simple Explanation:

  • An exclusive licensee can sue for infringement just like the patent owner
  • They have the same rights to protect the patent
  • Must follow proper legal procedures

Key Points:

  • Exclusive licensee = same rights as owner
  • Can sue independently
  • Must give notice to patent owner

Section 110: Rights of Compulsory Licensee

Simple Explanation:

  • Even compulsory licensees (forced by government) can sue for infringement
  • Protects their legitimate business interests
  • Limited compared to exclusive licensees

Key Points:

  • Compulsory licensees can also sue
  • Must show their interests are affected
  • Subject to certain conditions

Section 111: Restrictions on Damages/Profits

Simple Explanation:

  • Court cannot always give full damages or profits
  • If patent wasn't properly marked or advertised, damages may be reduced
  • Encourages proper patent marking

Key Points:

  • Patent marking affects damages
  • No damages before notice of infringement
  • Encourages compliance with marking requirements

Exam Notes – Chapter XVIII: Suits Concerning Infringement of Patents (Simple Language)
Under the Patents Act, 1970 (India)

Overview

This chapter deals with legal actions (suits) that can be taken when someone uses a patented invention without the permission of the patent holder. It covers where to file a suit, who can file it, what defenses are allowed, and what remedies are available.

1. Section 104: Jurisdiction

Meaning in Simple Language:
You can file a patent infringement lawsuit only in a District Court or a High Court that has jurisdiction (authority) over the area where:

  • The infringer resides, or
  • The infringer carries on business, or
  • The infringement occurred.

Important Point:
Only courts at the District level or higher can hear patent cases. Lower courts (like small claims courts) cannot.

✅ MCQ Practice – Section 104

Q1. Where can a patent infringement suit be filed?
A) Any magistrate court
B) Only Supreme Court
C) District Court or High Court having jurisdiction
D) Consumer Forum

✅ Answer: C

Q2. Which of the following is NOT a valid ground for jurisdiction in a patent infringement suit?
A) Defendant resides in the area
B) Infringement took place in the area
C) Plaintiff resides in the area
D) Defendant carries on business in the area

✅ Answer: C

🛠️ Use Case – Section 104

Situation:
Rahul (patent holder) lives in Delhi. A company in Chennai is making and selling his patented product without permission.

Where can Rahul file the suit?
👉 He can file in a District or High Court in Chennai, because that’s where the infringement and the defendant's business are located.
He cannot file in Delhi just because he lives there.

2. Section 104A: Burden of Proof in Suits (Special Rule for Process Patents)

Meaning in Simple Language:
If the patent is for a process (method) to make a product, and the product is new, then:

  • The defendant (accused) must prove they did not use the patented process.
  • This is only for new products made by a patented process.

Why?
It's hard for the patent holder to prove how someone made a product. So the law shifts the burden to the accused.

✅ MCQ Practice – Section 104A

Q3. Section 104A applies when:
A) Any product is copied
B) A new product is made by a patented process
C) A machine is used without permission
D) Software is copied

✅ Answer: B

Q4. Who has the burden of proof under Section 104A?
A) The patent holder
B) The court
C) The accused infringer
D) The government

✅ Answer: C

🛠️ Use Case – Section 104A

Situation:
Priya has a patent for a new chemical process to make a cancer drug. A company starts selling the same drug. The drug is new (not known before).

What happens?
Priya shows the drug is new and her process is patented.
Now, the company must prove they used a different process.
If they can’t, they are infringing.

3. Section 105: Declaration of Non-Infringement

Meaning in Simple Language:
If someone fears they might be sued for patent infringement (e.g., they get a warning letter), they can go to court and ask:
"Am I infringing this patent?"
The court can give a declaration (official statement) saying NO, you are not infringing.

Conditions:

  • The person must have asked the patent holder for a written promise not to sue.
  • The patent holder must have refused or ignored the request.

✅ MCQ Practice – Section 105

Q5. A declaration of non-infringement can be sought by:
A) The patent holder
B) Someone threatened with a lawsuit
C) A government agency
D) A competitor with no connection

✅ Answer: B

Q6. Before seeking a declaration, the person must:
A) File a police complaint
B) Ask the patentee for a written assurance not to sue
C) Stop using the product
D) Pay royalty

✅ Answer: B

🛠️ Use Case – Section 105

Situation:
A startup receives a letter from a patent holder saying, “You are using our patented tech. Stop or we’ll sue.” The startup believes they are not infringing.

What can they do?
They can write to the patent holder: “Please give us a written assurance you won’t sue.”
If the patent holder refuses, the startup can go to court and get a declaration of non-infringement to protect itself.

4. Section 106: Groundless Threats of Infringement Proceedings

Meaning in Simple Language:
If someone threatens to sue for patent infringement without good reason, the person being threatened can go to court and ask for:

  • Stop the threats (injunction),
  • Compensation (damages) for losses suffered.

Example: Sending warning letters when no patent exists or when no infringement is happening.

✅ MCQ Practice – Section 106

Q7. Section 106 protects against:
A) Actual infringement
B) False or baseless threats of infringement
C) Patent expiry
D) Poor patent drafting

✅ Answer: B

Q8. Relief under Section 106 includes:
A) Imprisonment of the threatener
B) Damages and injunction against threats
C) Free license to use the patent
D) Patent cancellation

✅ Answer: B

🛠️ Use Case – Section 106

Situation:
A company sends letters to several small businesses saying, “You are infringing our patent. Pay us ₹10 lakh or we’ll sue.” But the patent is invalid or not related to the products.

What can the businesses do?
They can file a suit under Section 106 to stop the threats and claim compensation for fear, loss of customers, or legal costs.

5. Section 107: Defences in Suit for Infringement

Common Defences (Reasons why someone is NOT infringing):

  • The patent is invalid (should not have been granted).
  • The act was done privately and not for commercial gain.
  • The use was for research or experiments.
  • The product was patented in India and sold by the patentee (exhaustion of rights).
  • The use is under Bolar exemption (to get regulatory approval before patent expiry).
  • The defendant was using the invention before the patent was filed (prior user rights).

✅ MCQ Practice – Section 107

Q9. Which of the following is a valid defence in infringement?
A) The product is popular
B) The defendant used the invention before the patent filing date
C) The patent is very expensive
D) The patentee is a foreigner

✅ Answer: B

Q10. Using a patented drug for research is:
A) Always infringement
B) Not infringement under Section 107
C) Punishable by fine
D) Allowed only after patent expiry

✅ Answer: B

🛠️ Use Case – Section 107

Situation:
A scientist uses a patented molecule in lab experiments to develop a new drug.

Is this infringement?
👉 No. Use for research is a valid defence under Section 107.

6. Section 107A: Acts Not Considered as Infringement

Key Exceptions (Safe Zones):

  • Bolar Provision: Making, using, or importing a patented drug before expiry to get regulatory approval (e.g., for generic launch right after expiry).
  • Extensive Experimental Use: Using the invention for research or testing.
  • Prior Commercial Use: If someone was already using the invention before the patent was filed, they can continue (but can’t transfer the right).

✅ MCQ Practice – Section 107A

Q11. Under Section 107A, making a patented drug before expiry for regulatory filing is:

✅ Answer: B

Q12. The 'Bolar provision' helps:
A) Stop generics
B) Allow generics to prepare before patent expiry
C) Extend patent term
D) Ban exports

✅ Answer: B

🛠️ Use Case – Section 107A

Situation:
A generic drug company wants to launch a medicine the day after the patent expires. They need approval from the drug regulator.

Can they make samples and test before expiry?
👉 Yes. Section 107A allows this. No infringement.

7. Section 108: Reliefs in Suits for Infringement

If the court finds infringement, it can give:

  • Injunction (order to stop the infringing act)
  • Damages (money compensation)
  • Account of profits (infringer must pay profits earned from the invention)

Note: Injunctions can be interim (temporary) or permanent.

✅ MCQ Practice – Section 108

Q13. Which relief is NOT available under Section 108?
A) Injunction
B) Damages
C) Death penalty
D) Account of profits

✅ Answer: C

B) Injunction
C) Royalty
D) License

✅ Answer: B

🛠️ Use Case – Section 108

Situation:
Court finds that Company X copied a patented phone feature.

Relief to patentee:

  • Court orders Company X to stop selling the phone (injunction)
  • Pays ₹5 crore as damages
  • Also gives all profits made from the sales (account of profits)

8. Section 109: Right of Exclusive Licensee to Sue

Meaning:
An exclusive licensee (person/company given sole right to use the patent) can file an infringement suit in their own name, without the patentee, if:

  • The license agreement allows it, or
  • The patentee refuses or fails to sue.

But the patentee must be made a party to the suit (included in the case).

✅ MCQ Practice – Section 109

A) Any licensee

C) Exclusive licensee (under certain conditions)
D) Government only

✅ Answer: C

Q16. The patentee must be:
A) Paid 50% of damages
B) Made a party to the suit by exclusive licensee
C) Replaced in the patent
D) Not involved

✅ Answer: B

🛠️ Use Case – Section 109

Situation:
ABC Ltd. has an exclusive license to use a patented engine in India. XYZ Ltd. starts copying it.

ABC can sue XYZ on its own, but must include the patent holder in the case.

9. Section 110: Right of Licensee under Section 84 to Sue

Meaning:
If a compulsory license was granted under Section 84 (because patent is not worked in India), that licensee can sue for infringement — but only if the patentee joins the suit or gives consent.

Note: Compulsory license = Govt. allows someone to use the patent without owner’s consent, usually to ensure public access.

✅ MCQ Practice – Section 110

Q17. A compulsory licensee can sue for infringement only if:
A) They pay extra royalty
B) The patentee is a party or gives consent
C) The court allows it unconditionally
D) The product is exported

✅ Answer: B

Q18. Section 84 deals with:
A) Patent filing
B) Compulsory licensing
C) Patent renewal
D) International patents

✅ Answer: B

🛠️ Use Case – Section 110

Situation:
Govt. grants a compulsory license to a company to make a life-saving drug because the patentee isn’t selling it affordably.

Now, another company starts copying the drug.

Can the compulsory licensee sue?
👉 Yes, only if the patentee agrees or joins the lawsuit.

10. Section 111: Restriction on Damages or Profits

Meaning:
The court cannot award damages or profits for infringement that happened before the patent was granted.

Why?
Because the patent didn’t legally exist before the grant date.

Exception: If the patent claims priority from an earlier application, and the invention was published, then damages can be claimed from the publication date — but only if the infringer was notified.

🛠️ Use Case – Section 111

Situation:
A patent was granted in 2024. Someone made the product in 2023.

Can the patentee claim damages for 2023?
👉 No, because patent didn’t exist.
But if the application was published in 2022, and the infringer was notified, and priority was claimed, limited damages may apply from publication.

CHAPTER XVIII: SUITS CONCERNING INFRINGEMENT OF PATENTS

Below are simplified exam notes for Chapter XVIII of the Indian Patents Act, 1970, covering suits related to patent infringement. Each section includes a brief explanation, a use case for practice, and multiple-choice questions (MCQs) for self-assessment.

1. Section 104: Jurisdiction

Explanation:

  • Patent infringement cases must be filed in a District Court or a High Court with jurisdiction.
  • If the defendant countersues to revoke the patent, the case may be transferred to a High Court.
  • No case can be filed in a court lower than a District Court.

Use Case:

  • Scenario: A company, Tech Innovations, files a patent infringement case against a competitor in a local magistrate court. The competitor argues that the case should be dismissed because the court lacks jurisdiction.
  • Practice Question: Why is the magistrate court not the right place to file this case? What should Tech Innovations do to correct this?

MCQs:

  1. Where can a patent infringement suit be filed? a) Any court in India b) District Court or High Court c) Magistrate Court d) Supreme Court only Answer: b) District Court or High Court
  2. What happens if a defendant in a patent infringement suit files a counterclaim for patent revocation? a) The case is dismissed b) The case may be transferred to a High Court c) The case stays in the District Court d) The case is moved to a Magistrate Court Answer: b) The case may be transferred to a High Court

2. Section 104A: Burden of Proof in Case of Suits Concerning Infringement

Explanation:

  • In infringement cases involving process patents (e.g., a method to manufacture something), the defendant must prove they did not infringe.
  • If the product made by the defendant is identical to the patented product, the court assumes infringement unless the defendant proves otherwise.

Use Case:

  • Scenario: PharmaCorp holds a patent for a process to produce a specific medicine. They sue Medico Ltd. for making the same medicine. Medico Ltd. claims they used a different process.
  • Practice Question: Who has the burden of proof in this case, and what must they demonstrate to avoid liability?

MCQs:

  1. In a process patent infringement case, who bears the burden of proof? a) The patent holder b) The defendant c) The court d) Both parties equally Answer: b) The defendant
  2. What does the court assume if the defendant’s product is identical to the patented product? a) No infringement occurred b) Infringement occurred unless proven otherwise c) The patent is invalid d) The case is dismissed Answer: b) Infringement occurred unless proven otherwise

3. Section 105: Power of Court to Make Declaration as to Non-Infringement

Explanation:

  • A person can ask the court to declare that their product or process does not infringe a patent.
  • They must first notify the patent holder, who can contest the declaration.
  • The court decides based on evidence whether infringement exists.

Use Case:

  • Scenario: Startup Gadgets wants to launch a new device but is unsure if it infringes a competitor’s patent. They approach the patent holder, who refuses to clarify. Startup Gadgets files a suit for a non-infringement declaration.
  • Practice Question: What steps must Startup Gadgets follow before filing the suit, and what will the court evaluate?

MCQs:

  1. What must a person do before filing for a non-infringement declaration? a) File a case directly in court b) Notify the patent holder c) Pay a fee to the patent office d) Publish a public notice Answer: b) Notify the patent holder
  2. What does the court decide in a non-infringement declaration case? a) Whether the patent is valid b) Whether the product/process infringes the patent c) Whether the patent holder is liable d) Whether the product is marketable Answer: b) Whether the product/process infringes the patent

4. Section 106: Power of Court to Grant Relief in Cases of Groundless Threats of Infringement Proceedings

Explanation:

  • If someone threatens another with a patent infringement lawsuit without valid grounds, the threatened party can seek court relief.
  • The court can stop the threats and award damages if the threats are unjustified.

Use Case:

  • Scenario: BigTech sends a legal notice to SmallCo, claiming SmallCo’s product infringes their patent. SmallCo believes the threat is baseless and files a suit under Section 106.
  • Practice Question: What remedies can SmallCo seek, and what must they prove to succeed in court?

MCQs:

  1. What can a person do if they receive groundless threats of patent infringement? a) Ignore the threats b) File a suit for relief in court c) Pay the patent holder to settle d) Surrender their product Answer: b) File a suit for relief in court
  2. What relief can the court provide in cases of groundless threats? a) Revoke the patent b) Stop the threats and award damages c) Transfer the patent to the threatened party d) Dismiss the patent holder’s claims automatically Answer: b) Stop the threats and award damages

5. Section 107: Defences, etc., in Suit for Infringement

Explanation:

  • In an infringement lawsuit, the defendant can argue that the patent is invalid (e.g., not novel or obvious).
  • They can also claim their actions do not infringe the patent or fall under permitted acts (like experimental use).

Use Case:

  • Scenario: InnoTech is sued for patent infringement by PatentCorp. InnoTech argues that the patent is invalid because it was already known in the industry.
  • Practice Question: What defenses can InnoTech raise, and how might they challenge the patent’s validity?

MCQs:

  1. What is a valid defense in a patent infringement suit? a) The patent is invalid b) The defendant made more profits c) The plaintiff delayed the lawsuit d) The product is of better quality Answer: a) The patent is invalid
  2. Which of the following can a defendant claim in an infringement suit? a) The patent holder is not registered b) Their actions are permitted under the law c) They were unaware of the patent d) The patent is too expensive Answer: b) Their actions are permitted under the law

6. Section 107A: Certain Acts Not to be Considered as Infringement

Explanation:

  • Certain acts, like importing patented products for research or exporting medicines to developing countries (under specific conditions), are not considered patent infringement.
  • These are often called "Bolar provisions" or parallel import exemptions.

Use Case:

  • Scenario: MediPharma imports a patented drug to conduct clinical trials for a generic version. The patent holder sues for infringement.
  • Practice Question: Can MediPharma use Section 107A as a defense? What conditions must they meet?

MCQs:

  1. Which act is not considered patent infringement under Section 107A? a) Selling patented products commercially b) Importing for research purposes c) Copying the patented product for profit d) Distributing patented products without permission Answer: b) Importing for research purposes
  2. What is a purpose for which importing patented products is allowed under Section 107A? a) Commercial sale b) Research and development c) Personal use d) Advertising Answer: b) Research and development

7. Section 108: Reliefs in Suits for Infringement

Explanation:

  • If infringement is proven, the court can grant remedies like an injunction (to stop the infringing act), damages, or an account of profits.
  • The court may also order the destruction of infringing goods.

Use Case:

  • Scenario: A court finds that CopyTech infringed a patent by manufacturing a patented device. The patent holder seeks an injunction and damages.
  • Practice Question: What remedies can the patent holder request, and what factors might the court consider in awarding damages?

MCQs:

  1. What remedy can a court grant in a patent infringement case? a) Imprisonment of the infringer b) Injunction to stop the infringement c) Patent transfer to the infringer d) Public apology by the infringer Answer: b) Injunction to stop the infringement
  2. What can the court order regarding infringing goods? a) Sell them to the public b) Destroy them c) Donate them to charity d) Store them indefinitely Answer: b) Destroy them

8. Section 109: Right of Exclusive Licensee to Take Proceedings Against Infringement

Explanation:

  • An exclusive licensee (someone given exclusive rights to use the patent) can file an infringement lawsuit.
  • The patent holder must be included in the lawsuit unless they agree otherwise.

Use Case:

  • Scenario: BioTech, an exclusive licensee of a patented vaccine, discovers that a competitor is selling a similar vaccine. BioTech wants to sue for infringement.
  • Practice Question: Can BioTech file the lawsuit alone, or must they involve the patent holder? What steps should they take?

MCQs:

  1. Who can file a lawsuit for patent infringement under Section 109? a) Any licensee b) An exclusive licensee c) A non-exclusive licensee d) The public Answer: b) An exclusive licensee
  2. What is required when an exclusive licensee files an infringement suit? a) The patent holder must be included in the suit b) The licensee must surrender their license c) The court’s permission is not needed d) The patent must be revoked Answer: a) The patent holder must be included in the suit

9. Section 110: Right of Licensee Under Section 84 to Take Proceedings Against Infringement

Explanation:

  • A licensee under a compulsory license (granted under Section 84 for public interest) can sue for infringement if the patent holder fails to act after being notified.
  • This ensures the licensee can protect their rights.

Use Case:

  • Scenario: HealthCo, a compulsory licensee for a patented drug, finds another company selling the drug illegally. The patent holder refuses to sue.
  • Practice Question: What can HealthCo do under Section 110, and what conditions must they meet to file a lawsuit?

MCQs:

  1. Who can sue for infringement under Section 110? a) Any person using the patent b) A compulsory licensee under Section 84 c) A non-exclusive licensee d) The patent office Answer: b) A compulsory licensee under Section 84
  2. What must a compulsory licensee do before filing an infringement suit? a) Pay a fine to the court b) Notify the patent holder c) Surrender their license d) Publish a public notice Answer: b) Notify the patent holder

10. Section 111: Restriction on Power of Court to Grant Damages or Account of Profits for Infringement

Explanation:

  • The court may not award damages or profits if the defendant was unaware of the patent and had no reasonable grounds to know about it.
  • This protects innocent infringers from harsh penalties.

Use Case:

  • Scenario: NewCo sells a product not knowing it infringes a patent. The patent holder sues for damages. NewCo claims they had no knowledge of the patent.
  • Practice Question: Can NewCo avoid paying damages? What must they prove to the court?

MCQs:

  1. When can a court refuse to award damages for patent infringement? a) If the patent is invalid b) If the defendant was unaware of the patent c) If the plaintiff delays the lawsuit d) If the product is of low quality Answer: b) If the defendant was unaware of the patent
  2. What must a defendant prove to avoid damages under Section 111? a) They made no profit b) They had no reasonable grounds to know about the patent c) The patent holder was negligent d) The product was different Answer: b) They had no reasonable grounds to know about the patent

Additional Notes:

  • These sections collectively govern how patent infringement disputes are handled in India, balancing the rights of patent holders, licensees, and third parties.
  • Always refer to the Indian Patents Act, 1970, for precise legal text and consult a legal expert for real-world applications.
  • For practice, try analyzing real-world patent disputes or creating hypothetical scenarios to apply these sections.

 

 

 

 

 

 

Section 104: Jurisdiction

Notes:

  • Only the District Court (not lower courts) can try a suit for patent infringement.
  • If the defendant files a counter-claim for revoking the patent, the case moves to the High Court.

MCQ Example:
Q: Which court can try a suit for patent infringement in India?
A. Supreme Court
B. High Court
C. District Court
D. Family Court
Answer: C. District Court

Use Case:
A patent owner files a case of infringement in a district court. The accused claims the patent is invalid, so the suit shifts to the High Court for further proceedings.

Section 104A: Burden of Proof in Infringement Suits

Notes:

  • In “process patent” cases, the burden is on the defendant to prove that the process they use is different from the patented one, once the product is identical.
  • Useful where only the process is patented, not the product.

MCQ Example:
Q: Who must prove non-infringement in a process patent case?
A. Plaintiff
B. Defendant
C. Judge
D. Government
Answer: B. Defendant

Use Case:
Company A has a patent for making a drug. Company B is making and selling the same drug. Now, B must show its manufacturing process is genuinely different.

Section 105: Declaration as to Non-Infringement

Notes:

  • Anyone can approach the court for a declaration that their product/process does not infringe a certain patent.
  • This is useful to stop false allegations.

MCQ Example:
Q: What relief can a manufacturer seek under Section 105?
A. Damages
B. Declaration of non-infringement
C. Criminal penalty
D. Patent grant
Answer: B. Declaration of non-infringement

Use Case:
A manufacturer is accused of infringing a patent. To avoid legal threats, they ask the court for a declaration proving there’s no infringement.

Section 106: Relief for Groundless Threats

Notes:

  • If a person is threatened with legal action without real cause, he can sue for relief.
  • Court can grant injunctions and/or compensation for such groundless threats.

MCQ Example:
Q: If someone is threatened unfairly about patent infringement, what can they do?
A. File for revocation
B. Ignore the threat
C. File a suit for groundless threats
D. Apply for a new patent
Answer: C. File a suit for groundless threats

Use Case:
A competitor sends legal notices alleging infringement without real proof. The recipient can sue for “groundless threats” and get compensation or an injunction.

Section 107: Defences in Infringement Suits

Notes:

  • All grounds available for patent revocation can also be used as a defense in infringement suits.
  • For example, a defendant can argue the patent is not valid or the invention is not new.

MCQ Example:
Q: Which is a valid defense against a patent infringement suit?
A. Patent is not novel
B. Plaintiff is rich
C. Delay in reply
D. None of these
Answer: A. Patent is not novel

Use Case:
In a lawsuit, the accused argues that the patent itself should be revoked because it lacks novelty, offering a valid legal defense.

Section 107A: Acts Not Considered Infringement

Notes:

  • Certain acts, including use for research and submission of information (Bolar exemption), or parallel importation from authorized sellers, are not infringement.
  • Helps promote research and access to medicines.

MCQ Example:
Q: What is NOT considered patent infringement under Section 107A?
A. Commercial sale by unauthorized person
B. Use for research and submission to authorities
C. Making unauthorized copies for profit
D. Counterfeiting
Answer: B. Use for research and submission to authorities

Use Case:
A generic drug company makes and tests a patented medicine, only to submit proof to drug authorities for regulatory approval—this is allowed, not infringement.

Section 108: Reliefs in Suits for Infringement

Notes:

  • Court can issue injunctions to stop infringement, award damages, or order infringing goods to be seized/destroyed.
  • Both temporary and permanent injunctions may be granted.

MCQ Example:
Q: What relief can a patent holder get against an infringer?
A. Only imprisonment
B. Injunction and/or damages
C. Patent renewal
D. Patent transfer
Answer: B. Injunction and/or damages

Use Case:
A company copies a patented product—court issues an order to stop further production and orders payment of damages for losses.

Section 109: Right of Exclusive Licensee

Notes:

  • Exclusive licensee has the same right as the patent holder to start legal proceedings.
  • They can recover damages, profits, etc.

MCQ Example:
Q: Who else can file a suit for infringement besides the patent owner?
A. Licensee
B. Exclusive licensee
C. Both A and B
D. None
Answer: B. Exclusive licensee

Use Case:
An exclusive licensee of a patented technology files a suit against unauthorized users to claim damages.

Section 110: Right of Licensee under Compulsory License

Notes:

  • A licensee with a compulsory license (given by authorities) can also sue for infringement.

MCQ Example:
Q: Can a compulsory licensee sue for infringement?
A. Yes
B. No
Answer: A. Yes

Use Case:
A company with a compulsory license for a life-saving drug discovers another violating their rights, so they take legal action.

Section 111: Restrictions on Damages or Account of Profits

Notes:

  • No damages if the infringer was unaware of the patent's existence.
  • Courts may refuse damages if a patent renewal fee was not paid in time or if patent was substantially amended.

MCQ Example:
Q: When can a court deny damages in an infringement suit?
A. If the defendant proves he didn’t know about the patent
B. If the patent is shown on product
C. Always
D. Never
Answer: A. If the defendant proves he didn’t know about the patent

Use Case:
If a small manufacturer unintentionally infringes a patent and shows that he was unaware of its existence, the court may refuse to award damages.

 

 

 

 

 

Here’s a comprehensive study guide for Chapter XVIII – Suits Concerning Infringement of Patents (Sections 104–111) under the Indian Patents Act, 1970, in simple language, along with MCQs for practice and real-life use cases for each section.

✅ Exam Notes (Simplified)

Section 104 – Jurisdiction

  • Rule: Only a District Court or higher can hear patent infringement suits.
  • If the defendant files a counterclaim for revocation, the case moves to the High Court.

Use Case:
A company sues another for patent infringement in a lower court → Case dismissed because only District Court or above has jurisdiction.

Section 104A – Burden of Proof

  • Applies when the patent is for a process (not a product).
  • Court can ask the defendant to prove their process is different if:
    • The patent is for a new product, or
    • There’s a high chance the same process was used.
  • Patentee must first show the product is identical to the patented one.

Use Case:
Pharma company claims its patented process for making a drug was copied. Defendant must prove they used a different process.

Section 105 – Declaration of Non-Infringement

  • Anyone can approach the court to declare that their product/process does not infringe a patent.
  • Must first ask the patentee for a written acknowledgment.

Use Case:
A startup wants to launch a product similar to a patented one → asks court for a declaration of non-infringement.

Section 106 – Groundless Threats

  • If someone threatens legal action for infringement without basis, the threatened party can sue for:
    • Injunction (stop threats)
    • Damages

Use Case:
Company A sends warning letters to Company B without valid patent rights → B can sue A for groundless threats.

Section 107 – Defences in Infringement Suits

  • All grounds for revocation of patent (Section 64) can be used as a defense.
  • Example: Patent is invalid, not novel, or lacks inventive step.

Use Case:
Defendant argues the patent itself is invalid because it lacks novelty.

Section 107A – Acts Not Infringement

  • Bolar Exception: Making/using a patented invention for regulatory approval is allowed.
  • Importing patented products from authorized sellers abroad is allowed.

Use Case:
Generic drug maker tests a patented drug for approval before patent expiry → Not infringement.

Section 108 – Reliefs

  • Court can grant:
    • Injunctions (stop infringement)
    • Damages or account of profits
    • Seizure/destruction of infringing goods.

Use Case:
Court orders a company to stop selling infringing products and pay damages.

Section 109 – Exclusive Licensee Rights

  • Exclusive licensee can sue for infringement in their own name.

Use Case:
Company with exclusive license for a patented tech sues an infringer.

Section 110 – Licensee under Section 84

  • Compulsory license holder can sue if patentee doesn’t act within 2 months of being asked.

Use Case:
Generic company with compulsory license sues infringer after patentee ignores request.

Section 111 – Restriction on Damages

  • No damages if the infringer proves:
    • They didn’t know about the patent at the time of infringement.

Use Case:
Small manufacturer unknowingly infringes a patent → Court may deny damages.
