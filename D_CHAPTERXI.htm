Section C: Use Cases

Use Case 1: The "Two-Period" Design

●      Scenario: "Classic Furniture Co." registered its iconic chair design in 1998 under the 1911 Act. It secured the first 5-year term and paid the fee to extend it for the second 5-year term in 2003. The design was immensely popular.

●      Issue: A competitor started manufacturing identical chairs in 2008, arguing that the 15-year term under the 2000 Act does not apply to pre-2000 registrations.

●      Application of S.48(5): The copyright expired in 2008 (1998+5+5). The 2000 Act's 10+5 year term does not apply retrospectively to alter the expiration date of a right that was granted under the old law. The competitor's action in 2008 was not infringement. This illustrates the protection of vested rights under the savings clause.

Use Case 2: The Pending Application

●      Scenario: "TechGadgets Ltd." filed a design application for a mobile phone shape on April 1, 2001, under the 1911 Act. The application was pending examination on May 11, 2001.

●      Application of S.48(3): The application is governed by the 2000 Act. It will be examined for novelty and originality as per the 2000 Act's stricter standards. If registered, it will get a copyright for 10 years from the date of registration (not from the date of application), which is the term under the new Act. This shows the application of the new law to inchoate proceedings.

Use Case 3: The Halfway Infringement Suit

●      Scenario: In 2000, "SilkSaree Designs" sued a copycat for infringing its 1995-registered design. The suit was pending in the City Civil Court. The defendant files an application in 2001 arguing that the High Court now has exclusive jurisdiction under the 2000 Act.

●      Application of S.48(4): The suit will continue in the City Civil Court as if the 2000 Act was not passed. Jurisdiction is determined as per the law existing at the time of filing the suit. This prevents disruption of ongoing judicial proceedings.

Section D: Case Study Analysis

Case Study: M/s. Gopal Glass Works Ltd. vs. Assistant Controller of Patents & Designs & Ors.
(A key case interpreting Section 48's transitional provisions)

Facts:

  1. A design was registered under the 1911 Act on February 16, 1994.
  2. The initial 5-year term ended on February 15, 1999. The proprietor applied for extension for the second period of 5 years, which was granted. This extended the copyright until February 15, 2004.
  3. The 2000 Act came into force on May 11, 2001.
  4. In 2003, a third party filed a cancellation petition under the 2000 Act, alleging lack of novelty.
  5. The proprietor argued that the new Act's cancellation grounds (e.g., prior publication) could not be applied to a design registered under the old Act.

Legal Question: Does the 2000 Act, particularly its grounds for cancellation, apply to designs registered under the 1911 Act whose copyright was subsisting on the date of commencement of the new Act?

Analysis & Application of S.48:

●      The court had to reconcile Section 48(2) (things done under old Act continue) with Section 48(5) (term of expiration) and the overarching principle of repeal.

●      Key Holding: The court held that while the right itself (the copyright) and its term were saved and governed by the old Act's framework as per S.48(2) & (5), the procedure for its cancellation, once the new Act is in force, is governed by the new Act.

●      Reasoning: Section 48(2) saves the "registration" and the "right," but it does not fossilize the remedial and procedural machinery. The phrase "continue in force and have effect as if made... under the corresponding provisions of this Act" in S.48(2) indicates that the old registration is now to be treated as a registration under the new Act for all practical and procedural purposes, including defending its validity against new challenges.

●      Conclusion: The cancellation petition under the 2000 Act was maintainable. The design, though registered under the 1911 Act, had to withstand the validity tests of the 2000 Act for the remainder of its saved term.

Takeaway for Practitioners:
This case highlights the subtle distinction between substantive rights and procedural remedies in transitional law. A design owner from the 1911 era cannot claim complete immunity from the new law. Their right exists, but its shield (defense against cancellation) and sword (anti-piracy measures) may now be made of the new legal steel.

Answer Key to MCQs:

  1. b) January 14, 2007 (S.48(5) clearly states the term is the original 5 years + the extended 5 years, not the new 10+5 year term).
  2. c) The suit will continue in the District Court as if the 2000 Act had not been passed. (Direct application of S.48(4)).
  3. b) As if it was made under the corresponding provisions of the 2000 Act, subject to the new Act's conditions. (Literal interpretation of S.48(2)).
  4. b) The provisions of the 2000 Act will apply to it, including its examination and post-registration term. (Direct application of S.48(3)).
  5. b) That the repeal does not affect any right, privilege, obligation, or liability acquired or incurred under the repealed Act. (This is the essence of savings under the General Clauses Act, 1897).

Use Cases and Case Study

Use Case: Administrative Continuity

Scenario: A textile manufacturer received a "Certificate of Registration" for a fabric pattern in 1998 under the Designs Act, 1911. In 2002, a competitor copies the pattern. Application of Section 48(2): The manufacturer does not need to apply for a new certificate. Under Section 48(2), the 1998 certificate is treated as if it were issued under the Designs Act, 2000. The manufacturer can sue for piracy under Section 22 of the 2000 Act using their old certificate as valid evidence of ownership.

Case Study: Jurisdiction and Repeal (The "Pending Proceeding" Doctrine)

Context: This reflects the principle established in various IP litigations during transitional periods (similar to Sree Gajanana Motor Transport Co. Ltd. vs. Union of India). The Issue: Suppose a party filed a design cancellation petition in 1999 (under the 1911 Act) before a Controller. Simultaneously, an infringement suit was filed in a District Court. When the 2000 Act commenced, the question arose: should the District Court transfer the case because the 2000 Act has different provisions regarding High Court jurisdiction? Legal Analysis:

●      Court Proceedings: Per Section 48(4), the District Court retains jurisdiction. Even if the 2000 Act mandates certain matters go to the High Court, the "as if this Act has not been passed" clause protects the status quo of that specific litigation.

●      Controller Proceedings: Per Section 48(3), the procedure for the cancellation pending before the Controller would now be governed by the 2000 Act's rules, as it is an "application for registration... and proceedings consequent thereon." Outcome: This demonstrates the "Split-Track" transition: Courts stay in the past (1911 Act rules/jurisdiction) to avoid disrupting the judiciary, while the Patent Office (Controller) moves to the future (2000 Act rules) for administrative efficiency.

⚖️ Case Study: Interpretation of Savings

The Situation: An entity had a registration under the 1911 Act which was set to expire. They wanted to claim the 15-year total protection (10 years initial + 5 years extension) provided by the 2000 Act.

The Legal Conflict: The 1911 Act provided a different structure for terms of copyright. Section 48(5) specifically ensures that the "date of expiration" is pegged to the original registration periods.

The Ruling Logic: Courts and the Controller look to Section 48(2) to ensure continuity. If a certificate was issued under the old Act, it is treated as a certificate under the new Act. However, Section 48(5) acts as a "proviso" to ensure that the transition doesn't unfairly shorten or arbitrarily extend terms in a way that disrupts the public domain expectations.

Key Takeaway: Section 48 acts as a "bridge." It ensures that while the law evolves, people who acted under the old law are not left in a legal vacuum.

Use Case 2: Pending Application Processing

Scenario: ABC Inc. filed a design application on January 15, 2000. By May 11, 2000 (commencement of new Act), the application was still under examination.

Analysis:

●      Section 48(3) explicitly applies the 2000 Act to pending applications

●      Substantive examination will follow the 2000 Act's provisions

●      Procedural aspects will also follow the new Act

Answer: The application will be examined under the Designs Act, 2000, including all substantive and procedural requirements of the new Act.

Use Case 3: Pending Infringement Suit

Analysis:

●      Section 48(4) provides that pending proceedings continue in the same court

●      The non-obstante clause ensures continuity despite any contrary provision in the new Act

●      Substantive rights are determined by the law when cause of action arose

●      Procedural law of the new Act may apply

Answer: a) The case continues in Delhi High Court b) Substantive provisions of 1911 Act (for rights); procedural aspects may follow the new Act or court's inherent procedures

Use Case 4: Extension of Copyright Duration

Scenario: A design was registered on June 1, 1995, under the 1911 Act for 5 years. The registrant obtained an extension for a second 5-year period in 1999. The 2000 Act commences in May 2000.

Question: When will the copyright in this design expire?

Analysis:

●      Original registration: June 1, 1995 (5 years → expires May 31, 2000)

●      Extension granted: Second 5-year period (expires May 31, 2005)

●      Section 48(5) governs expiration dates

●      The provision states expiration occurs after the extended period

●      However, it's "subject to the provisions of this Act"

Answer: The copyright would expire on May 31, 2005 (after the extended period), unless the provisions of the 2000 Act modify this through extension mechanisms or transitional benefits.

Use Case 5: Rule Continuity

Scenario: The Controller issued a procedural rule under Section 30 of the 1911 Act regarding the format of design applications in April 2000. The 2000 Act commences in May 2000.

Analysis:

●      Section 48(2) provides for continuity of rules made under the old Act

●      The rule continues as if made under the corresponding provision of the 2000 Act

●      No re-issuance is required unless the rule conflicts with new Act provisions

●      The rule remains valid until superseded or amended

Answer: The rule continues to be valid and effective without re-issuance, having effect as if made under the 2000 Act.

Use Case 6: Mixed Timeline Application

●      Section 48(3) applies the 2000 Act to pending applications

●      Registration "in pursuance thereof" also under 2000 Act

Answer: a) 2000 Act governs examination b) 2000 Act governs opposition proceedings c) 2000 Act governs the registration and its terms

Use Case 7: No Extension Sought

Scenario: A design was registered in 1998 under the 1911 Act for 5 years (expiring 2003). The registrant never applied for extension. The 2000 Act came into force in 2000.

●      It mentions expiration after periods for which registration was made

●      The phrase "subject to the provisions of this Act" is key

●      Section 11 of the 2000 Act provides 15-year term

●      No explicit transitional provision automatically extends old registrations

Answer: Likely NO automatic extension to 15 years. The design would expire in 2003 (5 years from 1998) unless the registrant can apply for extension under any transitional provisions or the Controller's discretion under "subject to the provisions of this Act."

CASE STUDY

Case Study: Transition of Textile Design Rights

Background: Fashions Pvt. Ltd. (FPL) is a textile design company that registered multiple designs under the Designs Act, 1911. Their registration timeline is as follows:

●      Design A: Registered January 1, 1996 (5-year term)

●      Design B: Registered June 1, 1999 (5-year term)

●      Design C: Application filed March 1, 2000 (pending at commencement)

●      Design D: Application filed June 1, 2000 (after commencement of 2000 Act)

FPL obtained an extension for Design A in December 2000 for an additional 5 years under the transitional provisions.

Events:

  1. The Designs Act, 2000 commenced on May 11, 2000
  2. A competitor (CompCo) filed an infringement suit against FPL regarding Design A in April 2000 in the District Court
  3. FPL filed opposition to a similar design by CompCo (Design X) in July 2000
  4. FPL wants to know the protection period for all its designs

Questions:

Q1. What is the status of Design A's copyright protection period?

Analysis Points:

●      Original registration: January 1996 (5 years)

●      Extension obtained: December 2000 (5 more years)

●      Section 48(5) applicability

●      Impact of 15-year term under new Act

Answer: Design A was registered for 5 years (expires December 31, 2000). With the extension granted in December 2000, it receives an additional 5 years under Section 48(5), expiring December 31, 2005. The extension was "subject to the provisions of this Act," meaning while the 5+5 structure from the old Act applies, the overall framework is governed by the 2000 Act. FPL may need to explore if further extension to reach 15 years total is possible under transitional provisions.

Q2. Will the infringement suit filed in April 2000 continue, and under which Act's provisions?

Analysis Points:

●      Section 48(4) application

●      Court jurisdiction continuity

●      Applicable substantive law

Answer: Under Section 48(4), the suit continues in the same District Court as if the 2000 Act was not passed. The substantive rights (whether infringement occurred) will be determined under the Designs Act, 1911 since that was the law when the cause of action arose. The non-obstante clause in Section 48(4) ensures this continuity despite any provisions in the 2000 Act that might suggest otherwise.

Q3. Under which Act will Design C be examined and registered?

Analysis Points:

●      Section 48(3) application

●      Application pending at commencement

●      Examination standards

Answer: Design C, being an application pending at commencement (filed March 2000, before May 11, 2000), will be examined and registered entirely under the Designs Act, 2000 as per Section 48(3). This includes substantive criteria (novelty, originality), procedural requirements, opposition proceedings, and all consequent proceedings. The registration, if granted, will be under the 2000 Act's framework.

Q4. What is the protection period for Design B, assuming no extension was applied for before the new Act commenced?

Analysis Points:

●      Registration date: June 1999

●      Original 5-year term (expires May 2004)

●      Section 48(5) applicability

●      Possibility of extension under new Act

Answer: Design B was registered in June 1999 for 5 years, meaning it would expire in May 2004 under the old system. Under Section 48(5), the expiration date is "immediately after the period of five years for which it was registered," which would be May 2004. However, the clause "subject to the provisions of this Act" means FPL might be able to apply for extensions under the 2000 Act to reach the full 15-year term (until 2014), depending on how transitional provisions and extension mechanisms are interpreted and applied by the Controller.

Q5. If FPL's opposition to CompCo's Design X (filed July 2000) proceeds, which Act governs the opposition proceedings?

Analysis Points:

●      Opposition filed after commencement

●      Application date of Design X

●      Relevant provisions

Answer: Since the opposition was filed in July 2000 (after commencement of the 2000 Act on May 11, 2000), it is governed by the opposition provisions of the Designs Act, 2000. If Design X's application was pending before commencement, Section 48(3) would apply the 2000 Act to both the application and "proceedings consequent thereon," including the opposition. If Design X was applied for after commencement, it's entirely under the 2000 Act.

Q6. TRICKY: If FPL's Design A certificate issued under the 1911 Act had a clerical error, can they seek correction under the 2000 Act?

Analysis Points:

●      Section 48(2) savings clause

●      Certificate continuity provisions

●      Correction mechanisms

Answer: Yes. Under Section 48(2), the certificate issued under the 1911 Act continues to have effect "as if made, issued, given or done under the corresponding provisions of this Act." This means FPL can use the correction mechanisms available under the Designs Act, 2000 (such as Section 28 - Power of Controller to correct clerical errors) to seek correction of the clerical error. The certificate, though originally issued under the old Act, is treated as if issued under the new Act for all purposes, including corrections and amendments.

Q7. What strategic advice would you give FPL regarding maximizing protection for their pre-2000 designs?

Strategic Analysis:

●      Review all designs registered under old Act

●      Assess expiration dates under Section 48(5)

●      Explore extension possibilities

●      Consider re-registration if beneficial

●      Understand 15-year term benefits

Answer:

  1. Immediate Review: Assess all designs registered under 1911 Act for expiration dates
  2. Extension Applications: For designs approaching their 5-year mark, apply for extensions to avoid lapsing
  3. Transitional Benefits: Consult with IP counsel on interpreting "subject to the provisions of this Act" in Section 48(5) to potentially claim the full 15-year term
  4. Documentation: Ensure all certificates and registration documents are properly maintained as they continue under Section 48(2)
  5. New Applications: File any new designs directly under the 2000 Act to receive clear 15-year protection
  6. Monitoring: Track competitor activities and be prepared to enforce rights under whichever Act the registration falls under
  7. Legal Precedents: Stay updated on how courts interpret transitional provisions to maximize protection

KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR PATENT AGENT EXAM

  1. Repeal does not mean obliteration - Savings and transitional provisions preserve continuity
  2. General Clauses Act, 1897 works in addition to express savings clauses
  3. Pending matters (applications, proceedings, court cases) have specific transitional rules
  4. Non-obstante clauses (like in Section 48(4)) override other provisions
  5. "Subject to provisions of this Act" is a critical qualifying phrase that allows new Act to modify old arrangements
  6. Copyright duration for pre-2000 registrations requires careful calculation under Section 48(5)
  7. Administrative continuity is preserved - certificates, orders, notifications remain valid
  8. Court proceedings are specially protected - they continue unaffected in their original forums
