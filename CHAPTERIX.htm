Exam Notes on Chapter IX: Patents of Addition (Sections 54, 55, 56)

Section 54: Patents of Addition

  • Purpose: Protects improvements/modifications of an existing invention (main patent) without requiring a full patent.
  • Key Provisions:
    • Eligibility: The improvement must be new and non-obvious, but the inventive step threshold is lower than for a main patent.
    • Applicant: Must be the same as the main patent applicant or assignee.
    • Filing Timing:
      • Alongside the main application, or
      • After filing the main application but before its grant, or
      • After grant of the main patent if the improvement was developed post-grant.
    • Examination: The improvement must relate to the main invention and not be obvious.
    • No Separate Fees: No additional fees for filing or grant (except for printing).

Section 55: Term of Patents of Addition

  • Term: Same as the main patent (typically 20 years from filing). Expires with the main patent.
  • Conversion to Independent Patent:
    • If the main patent is revoked, the patent of addition may be converted into an independent patent.
    • Conditions:
      • The improvement must be patentable by itself (novel, non-obvious, industrially applicable).
      • The independent patent’s term is the remainder of the main patent’s term.
    • Procedure: Patentee must apply for conversion within 6 months of revocation.

Section 56: Validity of Patents of Addition

  • Dependence on Main Patent: A patent of addition is valid only if the main patent is valid.
  • Revocation of Main Patent:
    • If the main patent is revoked, the patent of addition automatically ceases to have effect.
    • Exception: If converted to an independent patent (under Section 55), it remains valid.
  • Infringement: Infringement of a patent of addition requires infringing both the main patent and the improvement.

Tricky MCQs

  1. Q: An inventor files a main patent for a "bicycle" and later files a patent of addition for an "improved gear system" after the main patent’s grant. The main patent is revoked for non-payment of renewal fees. What happens to the patent of addition?
    a) It remains valid as an independent patent.
    b) It is automatically revoked.
    c) It can be converted into an independent patent if the improvement is patentable.
    d) It expires immediately.
    Answer: c)
    Reason: Under Section 55, the patent of addition ceases to have effect upon revocation of the main patent but can be converted if the improvement is patentable.
  2. Q: Which requirement is NOT mandatory for a patent of addition?
    a) The improvement must be non-obvious.
    b) The applicant must be the same as the main patent applicant.
    c) The improvement must be filed before the grant of the main patent.
    d) The improvement must relate to the main invention.
    Answer: c)
    Reason: A patent of addition can be filed after the main patent’s grant if the improvement was developed post-grant (Section 54).
  3. Q: A patent of addition is granted for an "enhanced battery" (improvement) to a main patent for a "smartphone." The main patent is held invalid for lack of novelty. What is the status of the patent of addition?
    a) It becomes invalid automatically.
    b) It remains valid as it is an independent improvement.
    c) It can be enforced if the improvement is novel.
    d) It is valid only if the improvement was made after the main patent’s grant.
    Answer: a)
    Reason: Section 56 states a patent of addition is valid only if the main patent is valid. Revocation invalidates it unless converted (Section 55).
  4. Q: The term of a patent of addition is:
    a) 20 years from its filing date.
    b) The same as the main patent, expiring with it.
    c) 10 years from the main patent’s grant.
    d) Determined by the Controller.
    Answer: b)
    Reason: Section 55 explicitly ties the term of a patent of addition to the main patent.
  5. Q: A patentee files a patent of addition for a "modified nozzle" (improvement) to a main patent for a "sprinkler." The improvement was developed after the main patent’s grant. Can it be patented?
    a) No, as it was filed after grant.
    b) Yes, if it is non-obvious and relates to the main invention.
    c) Only if the main patent is still in force.
    d) No, patents of addition must be filed with the main application.
    Answer: b)
    Reason: Section 54 allows filing after grant if the improvement was developed post-grant.

Tricky Use Cases with Answers

Use Case 1: Post-Grant Improvement

  • Scenario: Inventor files a main patent for a "drill machine" (granted in 2020). In 2023, they invent an "auto-tightening chuck" (improvement) and file a patent of addition. The main patent is revoked in 2025 for prior art.
  • Question: Can the "auto-tightening chuck" be protected?
  • Answer: Yes, if the improvement is patentable by itself. The patentee can apply for conversion to an independent patent under Section 55. The term would be the remainder of the main patent’s term (until 2040).

Use Case 2: Obvious Improvement

  • Scenario: A patent of addition is filed for a "faster algorithm" (improvement) to a main patent for a "data processor." The algorithm is deemed obvious over the main patent.
  • Question: Can the patent of addition be granted?
  • Answer: No. Even for patents of addition, the improvement must be non-obvious (Section 54). Lower inventive step threshold ≠ no inventive step.

Use Case 3: Third-Party Infringement

  • Scenario: Company A holds a main patent for a "carburetor" and a patent of addition for an "eco-friendly valve." Company B manufactures only the valve.
  • Question: Is Company B infringing?
  • Answer: No. Infringement of a patent of addition requires infringing both the main patent and the improvement (Section 56). Since Company B only makes the valve, it does not infringe the main patent.

Tricky Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1: Revocation and Conversion

  • Situation: Main patent for a "lens" is revoked for lack of inventive step. The patent of addition for an "anti-glare coating" is still pending.
  • Question: Can the pending application proceed?
  • Answer: No. The Controller will refuse the patent of addition since the main patent is revoked (Section 56). The applicant can request to treat it as an independent application if the coating is patentable.

Scenario 2: Assignment After Filing

  • Situation: Inventor files a main patent for a "wheel." Before grant, they assign the main patent to Company X. Later, they invent an "improved rim" and file a patent of addition.
  • Question: Can the patent of addition be granted?
  • Answer: No. The applicant for the patent of addition must be the same as the main patent applicant (Section 54). Company X must file it.

Scenario 3: Term Extension

  • Situation: Main patent expires in 2030. Patent of addition for a "noise-reducing feature" is converted to an independent patent in 2028.
  • Question: What is the term of the independent patent?
  • Answer: 2 years (2030). The term is the remainder of the main patent’s term (Section 55), not 20 years from conversion.

Related Questions from Patent Agent Examination (1970)

  1. Q: "What is the effect of revocation of a main patent on the patent of addition?"
    Answer: The patent of addition ceases to have effect but may be converted to an independent patent if the improvement is patentable (Sections 55–56).
  2. Q: "Can a patent of addition be granted for an invention that is obvious over the main patent?"
    Answer: No. The improvement must be non-obvious, though the inventive step threshold is lower than for a main patent (Section 54).
  3. Q: "Is it necessary to file a patent of addition before the grant of the main patent?"
    Answer: No. It can be filed after grant if the improvement was developed post-grant (Section 54).
  4. Q: "What is the term of a patent of addition?"
    Answer: Same as the main patent; expires with it (Section 55).
  5. Q: "Who can apply for a patent of addition?"
    Answer: The applicant for the main patent or their assignee (Section 54).

Key Takeaway: Patents of addition are derivative rights tied to the main patent. Focus on their dependence, lower inventive step, and conversion provisions for exams. Always verify validity and infringement in the context of the main patent.

 

 

 

 

Exam Notes: Chapter IX - Patents of Addition

Core Concept:

A Patent of Addition (PoA) is a patent granted for an improvement in or modification of an invention disclosed in the main ("parent") patent. It is not an independent patent but is tied to the main patent's fate. The key philosophy is to encourage incremental innovation without the burden of multiple renewal fees.

Section 54: Patents of Addition

  • What it is: A patent granted for an improvement or modification of an invention for which a patent has already been applied for or granted (the "main invention").
  • Who can apply: The applicant for or the proprietor of the main patent.
  • Key Requirement: The invention disclosed in the PoA must be an improvement in or modification of the invention claimed in the main patent. It must be cognate (related) to the main invention.
  • No Separate Renewal Fee: The most significant advantage. A PoA does not require payment of renewal fees for its entire term. It remains in force as long as the main patent is in force.
  • Application Process: The application must explicitly state that it is for a patent of addition. The Controller has the power to make it a PoA even if not requested, if satisfied it qualifies.

Section 55: Term of Patents of Addition

  • Term: The term of a PoA is co-terminus with the main patent. It expires simultaneously with the main patent.
  • Exception - Section 55(1) Proviso: If the main patent is revoked, the Controller may order that the PoA become an independent patent. The term for this converted patent will be the remainder of the term of the main patent (i.e., 20 years from the date of filing of the main application), not a fresh 20-year term. The proprietor must then start paying renewal fees for the converted patent from the date of the order.

Section 56: Validity of Patents of Addition

  • Validity is Tied to Main Patent: A PoA cannot be invalidated solely on the ground that the invention claimed should have been the subject of an independent patent. Its validity is linked to the main patent.
  • Key Protection: This section protects the PoA from attacks based on non-obviousness/inventive step in isolation. The invention is judged as an improvement or modification of the main invention.
  • Revocation: However, a PoA can be revoked if the main patent is revoked, or on other standard grounds for revocation (e.g., lack of novelty, not an invention u/s 3, etc.).

Tricky Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

1. An application for a Patent of Addition can be filed by:
a) Any person who has invented an improvement to a patented invention.
b) Only the original inventor of the main invention.
c) The applicant for or the proprietor of the main patent.
d) Any registered patent agent on behalf of an interested party.

Answer: c) The applicant for or the proprietor of the main patent. (Section 54)

2. The term of a Patent of Addition is:
a) 20 years from its date of filing.
b) 10 years from the date of grant of the main patent.
c) Co-terminus with the term of the main patent.
d) The longer of 10 years from its grant or the life of the main patent.

Answer: c) Co-terminus with the term of the main patent. (Section 55)

3. The renewal fee for a Patent of Addition is:
a) The same as for an independent patent and must be paid annually.
b) Half the fee of an independent patent.
c) Not required to be paid as long as the main patent is in force.
d) Required only once, at the time of grant.

Answer: c) Not required to be paid as long as the main patent is in force. (Implied from Section 53(2) which exempts PoAs from renewal fees)

4. If a main patent is revoked by the Appellate Board, what is the fate of its associated Patent of Addition?
a) It automatically becomes an independent patent with a fresh 20-year term.
b) It is automatically revoked.
c) The Controller may, upon request, order it to become an independent patent for the remainder of the term of the main patent.
d) It remains in force for the full 20-year term from its filing date.

Answer: c) The Controller may, upon request, order it to become an independent patent for the remainder of the term of the main patent. (Section 55(1) Proviso)

5. A Patent of Addition can be invalidated on which of the following grounds?
a) Solely on the ground that the invention lacks an inventive step compared to prior art before the main patent's priority date.
b) Solely on the ground that it should have been filed as an independent patent.
c) If the main patent is revoked.
d) Both (b) and (c).

Answer: c) If the main patent is revoked. (Section 56 explicitly prevents invalidation solely on ground (b). It can be revoked if the main patent is, or on other standard grounds like lack of novelty).

Tricky Use Cases & Scenarios (with Answers)

Scenario 1: The "Slightly Related" Invention
Mr. X owns a main patent for a "Chair with a built-in fan." He later invents a "Novel ergonomic desk lamp." Believing it enhances the chair's functionality, he files it as a Patent of Addition to his chair patent. The Controller grants it. A competitor, Company Y, seeks to revoke the PoA for the lamp.

  • Q: Can Company Y successfully argue for revocation? On what grounds?
  • A: Yes, likely successfully. The key requirement is that the PoA must be an improvement or modification of the main invention. A desk lamp, even if used nearby, is not an improvement to the chair itself or its claimed invention (the chair with a fan). It is a separate, unrelated invention. The Controller might have erred in granting it. Y could argue it is not a "cognate" invention as required and should be revoked. Section 56 protects from claims it should have been independent, but not from claims that it is not an improvement/modification at all.

Scenario 2: The Premature Main Patent
An applicant files for a main patent for a "Chemical Compound A" on Jan 1, 2020. On June 1, 2020, before the main patent is granted, they file a PoA for "An improved process for synthesizing Compound A." The main patent is granted on Jan 1, 2022. The PoA is granted on July 1, 2022.

  • Q: When will the PoA expire?
  • A: The PoA will expire on Jan 1, 2040 (20 years from the filing date of the main patent: Jan 1, 2020). The term of a PoA is tied to the main patent's term, which is always 20 years from its filing date, not its grant date.

Scenario 3: The Revoked Main Patent
A main patent for "Device B" (filed Jan 1, 2015) has a PoA for "An attachment that makes Device B 50% more efficient" (filed Jan 1, 2018). In 2025, the main patent is revoked on the ground of obviousness. The proprietor of the PoA requests the Controller to convert it to an independent patent.

  • Q: If the Controller agrees, what will be the term of the now-independent patent for the attachment? Will renewal fees be due?
  • A: The term will be the remainder of the original main patent's term. The main patent would have expired on Jan 1, 2035. Therefore, the independent patent for the attachment will last until Jan 1, 2035 (approx. 10 years from 2025). The proprietor must start paying renewal fees from the date of the Controller's order (2025) onwards for the remaining term. They do not get a fresh 20-year term from 2018 or 2025.

Scenario 4: The Validity Challenge
A Patent of Addition is granted for an improved fuel injection system for a patented engine. An opponent challenges its validity, arguing: (i) The improvement was obvious to a person skilled in the art, and (ii) The invention is so significant it deserves its own independent patent status and should be revoked for not having one.

  • Q: Which of these arguments will hold, and why?
  • A:
    • Argument (i) CAN hold. A PoA must still meet all patentability criteria, including inventive step. It can be challenged and revoked on this ground. The assessment would consider if the improvement was non-obvious over the main invention and the prior art.
    • Argument (ii) WILL NOT hold. Section 56 explicitly states that a PoA cannot be revoked solely on the ground that the invention should have been the subject of an independent patent. This is the core protection offered to PoAs.

Chapter IX: Patents of Addition

Patents of Addition are granted for an invention that is a modification or improvement of an invention for which the applicant has already applied for or been granted a patent. The primary purpose is to allow a patentee to protect improvements to their invention without having to file a completely new, separate patent application and pay renewal fees for both.

Section 54. Patents of Addition

  • An applicant can apply for a patent of addition for an improvement or modification of an invention that they have already applied for or been granted a patent (called the main invention).
  • The date of filing for the patent of addition will be the date on which the application is filed, not the date of the main patent.
  • A patent of addition can't be granted before the main patent is sealed.
  • If the main application is converted into a patent of addition, it will be treated as if it was always an application for a patent of addition. This is particularly relevant if the main patent application is already published.
  • The grant of a patent of addition doesn't mean the invention is inventive or novel on its own; its validity is tied to the main patent.

Section 55. Term of Patents of Addition

  • A patent of addition expires on the same date as the main patent.
  • There are no renewal fees for a patent of addition. This is a key benefit.
  • If the main patent is revoked, a patent of addition can be made into an independent patent upon the request of the patentee and the payment of renewal fees. The new, independent patent will have the same expiry date as the original main patent would have had. This is a crucial exception.

Section 56. Validity of Patents of Addition

  • The validity of a patent of addition cannot be challenged on the ground that the invention ought to have been included in the main patent. In other words, you can't challenge it by saying it lacks inventive step over the main invention.
  • However, a patent of addition can still be revoked on other grounds, such as lack of novelty over the prior art (not the main invention), or lack of industrial applicability.
  • If the main patent is revoked, the court or Controller may order the patent of addition to be an independent patent.

Tricky MCQs

1. A patent of addition expires: a) 20 years from its own filing date. b) 20 years from the date of the main patent. c) On the same date as the main patent. d) 20 years from the date of its grant.

Answer: c) On the same date as the main patent. (Section 55)

2. A patent of addition can be granted for an invention that is: a) A new and independent invention by the same applicant. b) An improvement or modification of the main invention. c) A new invention by a different applicant. d) A modification of a main invention for which a patent has not been applied yet.

Answer: b) An improvement or modification of the main invention. (Section 54)

3. The validity of a patent of addition can be challenged on the ground that the invention: a) Was obvious in view of the main invention. b) Lacks novelty in view of prior art documents published before the filing date. c) Lacks inventive step over the main invention. d) Was not disclosed in the main patent application.

Answer: b) Lacks novelty in view of prior art documents published before the filing date. The validity cannot be challenged on the basis of a lack of inventive step over the main invention (Section 56).

Tricky Scenarios & Use Cases

Scenario 1: A company has a patent for a specific type of solar panel. After the patent is granted, they develop an improved coating for the panel that increases its efficiency by 15%.

  • Use Case: The company can file a patent of addition for the improved coating. This protects their improvement without the need for a separate, new patent and without paying separate renewal fees.
  • Why it's tricky: The coating, on its own, might not meet the inventive step criteria if the only prior art is the company's own solar panel patent. But Section 56 allows the patent of addition to stand, as its validity cannot be challenged on the ground that it lacks an inventive step over the main patent.

Scenario 2: A patent for a new drug delivery system is revoked on the grounds of lack of novelty. The patentee also holds a patent of addition for a minor modification to this system.

  • Use Case & Answer: When the main patent is revoked, the patent of addition does not automatically get revoked. Under Section 55, the patentee can request that the patent of addition be made an independent patent. The Controller or the court can grant this request, and the patent of addition will then require the payment of renewal fees to remain in force.
  • Why it's tricky: This scenario tests the candidate's knowledge of the exception clause under Section 55. The expiration date remains the same as the original main patent would have had, but it now becomes an independent, fee-paying patent.

CHAPTER IX - PATENTS OF ADDITION

Comprehensive Exam Notes for Patent Agent Examination

SECTION 54 - PATENTS OF ADDITION

Key Provisions:

Section 54(1) - Subject to the provisions of this section, where an application is made for a patent in respect of any improvement in or modification of an invention described or disclosed in the complete specification filed in pursuance of an earlier application for a patent in India, the applicant may, if he thinks fit, apply for the patent for such improvement or modification as a patent of addition.

Section 54(2) - Subject to the provisions of section 10, a patent of addition shall be granted for any improvement in or modification of an invention described or disclosed in the complete specification filed in pursuance of the earlier application referred to in sub-section (1) or in the complete specification relating to the main invention.

Critical Elements:

  1. Main Patent Requirement: Must have a main patent or pending application
  2. Improvement/Modification: Patent of addition must be for improvement or modification of main invention
  3. Same Applicant: Generally same applicant for both main and addition patents
  4. Optional Nature: Applicant may choose to file as patent of addition

SECTION 55 - TERM OF PATENTS OF ADDITION

Key Provisions:

Section 55(1) - A patent of addition shall, subject to the provisions of this Act, continue in force so long as the patent for the main invention remains in force, but no longer.

Section 55(2) - A patent of addition shall cease to have effect if the patent for the main invention is revoked under this Act.

Section 55(3) - The validity of a patent of addition shall not be questioned on the ground only that the invention claimed in the complete specification does not involve any inventive step having regard to any matter published before the priority date of the claim of that specification.

Critical Points:

  • Dependent Term: Addition patent term tied to main patent
  • Automatic Cessation: Dies with main patent
  • Lower Inventive Step Threshold: Different standard for obviousness

SECTION 56 - VALIDITY OF PATENTS OF ADDITION

Key Provisions:

Section 56(1) - Notwithstanding anything contained in this Act, a patent of addition shall not be deemed to be invalid on the ground only that the invention claimed therein was obvious having regard to what is disclosed in the specification of the main patent.

Section 56(2) - Where a patent of addition and the patent for the main invention are revoked, the court or the Controller, as the case may be, may direct that the patent of addition shall become an independent patent for the remainder of the term for which the main patent would have remained in force if it had not been revoked, and thereupon the patent shall continue in force as an independent patent accordingly.

Critical Elements:

  • Obviousness Exception: Cannot be invalid merely for being obvious over main patent
  • Independent Patent Option: May convert to independent patent under certain conditions

TRICKY MCQs

MCQ 1

A patent of addition filed in 2020 for a main patent granted in 2015 will expire: A) In 2040 (20 years from addition filing) B) In 2035 (20 years from main patent filing) C) When the main patent expires D) After 7 years from filing

Answer: C - Patent of addition continues only as long as main patent remains in force.

MCQ 2

Which statement about inventive step in patents of addition is INCORRECT? A) Lower threshold than regular patents B) Cannot be questioned solely on lack of inventive step C) No inventive step requirement at all D) Different from main patent requirements

Answer: C - There is still an inventive step requirement, but with lower threshold.

MCQ 3

If a main patent is revoked, the patent of addition: A) Automatically becomes independent B) Ceases to have effect C) Gets extended term D) Becomes prior art

Answer: B - Patent of addition ceases to have effect if main patent is revoked.

MCQ 4

A patent of addition can be filed: A) Only after main patent is granted B) Only before main patent is granted C) Both during pendency and after grant of main patent D) Only within 2 years of main patent

Answer: C - Can be filed during pendency or after grant of main patent.

MCQ 5

Under Section 56(2), conversion to independent patent: A) Is automatic upon main patent revocation B) Requires court or Controller direction C) Is not permitted under Patents Act D) Requires fresh application

Answer: B - Requires direction from court or Controller.

TRICKY USE CASES WITH ANSWERS

Use Case 1: Sequential Improvements

Scenario: Company A files Patent X for a basic engine design. Later files Patent Y as addition to X for fuel efficiency improvement. Still later, files Patent Z as addition to Y for emission control.

Question: Is Patent Z valid as addition to Patent Y?

Answer: NO. Patent of addition can only be filed for improvement of the MAIN patent (Patent X), not for improvement of another patent of addition. Patent Z should be filed as addition to Patent X or as independent patent.

Use Case 2: Different Applicants

Scenario: Individual A has main Patent P. Company B (which employed A) wants to file improvement as patent of addition.

Question: Can Company B file patent of addition?

Answer: Generally NO, unless there's proper assignment. Patent of addition typically requires same applicant as main patent, though assignment before filing may permit this.

Use Case 3: Priority Date Issues

Scenario: Main patent filed Jan 1, 2020. Addition patent filed Dec 1, 2020. Prior art exists from June 1, 2020 that makes addition obvious.

Question: Can addition patent be refused for obviousness?

Answer: YES. The addition can be refused for obviousness over prior art published before its priority date. Section 55(3) only protects against obviousness challenges based on matter published before the priority date, not all obviousness rejections.

TRICKY SCENARIOS WITH ANSWERS

Scenario 1: Maintenance Fee Strategy

Facts:

  • Main patent filed: 2015
  • Addition patent filed: 2018
  • Applicant stops paying renewal fees for main patent in 2020
  • Wants to maintain addition patent

Question: What happens to the addition patent?

Answer: Addition patent will lapse with the main patent. Applicant cannot maintain addition independently. Should have considered converting addition to independent patent before main patent lapsed.

Scenario 2: Invalidity Challenge

Facts:

  • Main patent for "Method of manufacturing steel"
  • Addition patent for "Method of manufacturing steel with reduced carbon emissions"
  • Challenger argues addition is obvious over main patent

Question: Is this a valid ground for invalidating addition patent?

Answer: NO. Under Section 56(1), patent of addition cannot be deemed invalid solely on ground that invention was obvious over main patent specification.

Scenario 3: Examination Complexity

Facts:

  • Main patent pending examination
  • Addition patent filed and examined first
  • Addition patent found allowable
  • Main patent subsequently refused

Question: What happens to addition patent?

Answer: Addition patent cannot be granted without valid main patent. If main patent is refused and not rectified, addition patent will also fail. Applicant should consider converting addition to independent application.

Scenario 4: Term Calculation Puzzle

Facts:

  • Main patent filed: March 1, 2010
  • Main patent granted: March 1, 2012
  • Addition patent filed: March 1, 2015
  • Addition patent granted: March 1, 2016

Question: When does addition patent expire?

Answer: Addition patent expires on March 1, 2030 (20 years from main patent filing date), regardless of when addition was filed or granted.

EXAMINATION TIPS

Key Points to Remember:

  1. Life Dependency: Addition patent's life completely depends on main patent
  2. Lower Bar: Different obviousness standard for addition patents
  3. Same Applicant Rule: Generally requires same applicant
  4. No Chain Additions: Cannot file addition to an addition
  5. Conversion Possibility: Can become independent under Section 56(2)

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  1. Confusing term calculation (always tied to main patent)

📘 CHAPTER IX: PATENTS OF ADDITION

Sections: 54, 55, 56

✅ Section 54: Patents of Addition

🔹 Key Provisions:

"Where an application is made for a patent in respect of an invention which is claimed as an improvement or modification of an invention claimed in an earlier application by the same applicant, then, if the Controller is satisfied that the invention so claimed is not such as to involve the invention of an inventive step, he may grant the patent as a patent of addition."

🔹 Explanation:

  • A Patent of Addition is granted for an improvement or modification of an invention already disclosed in an earlier application by the same applicant.
  • It cannot be granted independently — it is dependent on the main patent.
  • The improvement must not involve an inventive step (i.e., it may be obvious or minor).
  • However, if the improvement does involve an inventive step, it must be treated as a separate invention and not a patent of addition.
  • No separate renewal fee is required for a patent of addition; it is covered under the main patent.
  • The patent of addition cannot be exploited independently — it depends on the existence of the main patent.

🔹 Important Points:

  1. Same applicant – Must be the same as the main application.
  2. Earlier application – Can be a provisional or complete specification.
  3. Improvement or modification – Must relate to the same invention.
  4. No inventive step required – But must be new.
  5. Cannot be granted before the main patent – But can be filed earlier; granted only after the main patent is granted.
  6. No separate term – Term is linked to the main patent.

✅ Section 55: Term of Patents of Addition

🔹 Key Provision:

"A patent of addition shall expire at the same time as the patent for the main invention expires."

🔹 Explanation:

  • The term of a patent of addition is coterminous with the main patent.
  • Even if filed later, it expires with the main patent (typically 20 years from filing date of the main invention).
  • If the main patent is revoked, the patent of addition also becomes invalid.
  • Renewal fees for the main patent cover the patent of addition.

✅ Section 56: Validity of Patents of Addition

🔹 Key Provision:

"A patent of addition shall be void if the patent for the main invention is revoked on the ground of lack of novelty or of want of an inventive step or of not being useful, unless the Controller is satisfied that the invention claimed in the patent of addition is not lacking in novelty or inventive step or usefulness."

🔹 Explanation:

  • If the main patent is revoked due to:
    • Lack of novelty,
    • Lack of inventive step, or
    • Not useful (industrial applicability), then the patent of addition becomes void.
  • Exception: If the Controller is satisfied that the improvement itself has novelty, inventive step, and utility, it may be converted into an independent patent.
  • This is a safeguard for genuine improvements that are non-obvious even if the main invention fails.

Exam Notes on Chapter IX: Patents of Addition (The Patents Act, 1970)

Chapter IX of the Patents Act, 1970, deals with "Patents of Addition," which allow patentees to protect improvements or modifications to an existing patented invention (referred to as the "main invention") without filing a separate independent patent. This mechanism encourages incremental innovation by linking the additional patent to the main one, ensuring it doesn't extend beyond the main patent's life. The key sections are 54, 55, and 56. Below is a structured summary based on the exact provisions.

Section 54: Patents of Addition

  • Purpose: Allows granting a patent for an improvement or modification to a main invention.
  • Key Provisions:
    • Sub-section (1): An applicant can request a patent of addition if they apply for (or already hold) a patent for the main invention. The improvement/modification must be described or disclosed in the complete specification of the main invention.
    • Sub-section (2): If an improvement/modification is already patented independently, and the patentee owns both, the Controller can revoke the independent patent and convert it to a patent of addition with the same date as the revoked patent.
    • Sub-section (3): The filing date of the patent of addition application must be the same as or later than the main invention's filing date.
    • Sub-section (4): No grant of patent of addition before the main patent is granted.
  • Important Notes:
    • The improvement must not be a distinct invention but a genuine enhancement. The Controller has discretion but must follow due process.
    • No separate examination for inventive step vis-à-vis the main invention (linked to Section 56).
    • Form: Application via Form 1, with reference to the main patent/application.

Section 55: Term of Patents of Addition

  • Purpose: Aligns the lifespan of the patent of addition with the main patent.
  • Key Provisions:
    • Sub-section (1): Term equals the remaining term of the main patent; it ceases if the main patent expires or is revoked early.
      • Proviso: If the main patent is revoked, the patentee can request the court/Controller to convert the addition to an independent patent for the remainder of the original term.
    • Sub-section (2): No renewal fees for the addition while linked; fees apply only if it becomes independent, backdated to original grant dates.
  • Important Notes:
    • Protects against loss due to main patent revocation.
    • Renewal fees are waived to avoid double payment, but independence triggers standard fees.
    • Prescribed manner for request: Via petition under Rule 80.

Section 56: Validity of Patents of Addition

  • Purpose: Shields patents of addition from certain invalidity grounds.
  • Key Provisions:
    • Sub-section (1): Cannot refuse grant or revoke/invalidate solely for lack of inventive step based on publication/use of:
      • (a) The main invention's complete specification.
      • (b) Any prior addition to the main invention.
      • Also, cannot question validity on grounds that it should have been an independent patent.
    • Sub-section (2): For novelty assessment, the main invention's complete specification is considered prior art.
  • Important Notes:
    • Balances protection: Inventive step ignored relative to main, but novelty checked strictly, including against main spec.
    • Prevents opposition/revocation on "obvious over main" grounds.
    • Case law emphasis: Must show unity with main invention (e.g., not wholly distinct).

General Chapter Insights:

  • Definition: Section 2(1)(q) defines "patent of addition" as one granted under Section 54.
  • Advantages: No extra fees initially; extends protection without new term.
  • Limitations: Cannot survive main patent; must be owned by same patentee.
  • Amendments: Post-2005, aligned with TRIPS; no major changes since.
  • Exam Tip: Focus on interplay—e.g., revocation of main doesn't auto-kill addition if converted.

    • A. Lack of novelty.
    • B. Lack of inventive step over the main invention.
    • C. Fraudulent claim.
    • D. Non-disclosure of prior art.
    • Answer: B

🧩 Tricky Use Cases with Answers

Use Case 1:

Scenario: An inventor files a patent for a drone. Later, he develops a better battery system for the same drone. Can he file a patent of addition?

Answer: Yes, if the inventor is the same and the battery system is an improvement/modification of the original invention, he can file a patent of addition under Section 54.

Use Case 2:

Scenario: A company files a patent for a water purification system. A year later, a different department in the same company improves the filtration method. Can they file a patent of addition?

Answer: Only if the applicant remains the same (i.e., the company as a legal entity), the patent of addition can be filed. If the department is legally distinct, it may not qualify.

🧪 Tricky Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1:

Question: A patent of addition is granted for an improvement in a patented solar panel. The main patent is revoked due to lack of novelty. What happens to the patent of addition?

Answer: If the main patent is revoked, the patent of addition may also be affected unless it qualifies as an independent invention. The Controller may allow conversion to an independent patent.

Scenario 2:

Question: A patent of addition is challenged for lacking inventive step over the main patent. Is this a valid ground?

Answer: No. Section 56 explicitly states that lack of inventive step over the main invention is not a valid ground for revocation of a patent of addition.
