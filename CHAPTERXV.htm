Exam Notes: Chapter XV - Powers of Controller Generally (Sections 77-81)

Section 77: Controller to have certain powers of a civil court

  • Key Provisions:
    • Controller has powers of a civil court under CPC (Code of Civil Procedure, 1908) for:
      (a) Summoning/enforcing attendance of persons and examining them on oath.
      (b) Requiring discovery/production of documents.
      (c) Receiving evidence on affidavit.
      (d) Issuing commissions for examining witnesses/documents.
    • Controller is deemed a civil court for Sections 345 & 346 of CrPC (contempt of court).
  • Purpose: Ensures effective adjudication in patent disputes without transferring cases to civil courts.
  • Limitation: Powers are procedural; Controller cannot decide substantive civil rights beyond patent matters.

Section 78: Power of Controller to correct clerical errors, etc.

  • Key Provisions:
    • Controller may correct clerical errors or obvious mistakes in:
      (a) Patents,
      (b) Patent applications,
      (c) Documents filed in the Patent Office.
    • Conditions:
      • Correction without notice if error is patent (e.g., spelling, calculation errors).
      • Correction with notice/hearing if error is latent (e.g., technical ambiguity requiring evidence).
    • Scope: Errors must not alter the substance of the patent/document.
  • Trick: "Obvious mistake" excludes errors requiring interpretation or evidence.

Section 79: Evidence how to be given and powers of Controller in respect thereof

  • Key Provisions:
    • Evidence must be given by affidavit initially.
    • Controller may:
      (a) Require personal attendance for cross-examination.
      (b) Administer oaths/affirmations.
      (c) Compel production of documents relevant to proceedings.
    • Procedure: Affidavits must be sworn before a notary/authorized officer.
  • Limitation: Controller cannot compel disclosure of privileged documents (e.g., attorney-client communications).

Section 80: Exercise of discretionary powers by Controller

  • Key Provisions:
    • Controller must exercise discretionary powers judicially (not arbitrarily).
    • Mandatory requirement: Record reasons for decisions involving discretion.
  • Examples of Discretion:
    • Granting extensions of time (Section 81).
    • Allowing amendments to applications.
    • Deciding on patent restoration.
  • Principle: Decisions must be fair, reasoned, and non-discriminatory.

Section 81: Disposal by Controller of applications for extension of time

  • Key Provisions:
    • Controller may extend time for any act under the Act if:
      (a) Application is made in prescribed form with fee.
      (b) Delay was due to circumstances beyond applicant’s control.
    • Conditions:
      • Extension subject to terms (e.g., costs, conditions).
      • Refusal if delay is unjustified (e.g., negligence, avoidable error).
  • Limitation: No extension if delay prejudices third parties or public interest.

Tricky MCQs (with Answers)

  1. Section 77: The Controller’s power to summon witnesses under Section 77 is analogous to which court?
    a) Supreme Court
    b) Civil Court under CPC
    c) Criminal Court under CrPC
    d) High Court
    Answer: (b) Civil Court under CPC (powers are explicitly derived from CPC).
  2. Section 78: The Controller can correct a "clerical error" in a patent without notice if:
    a) The error affects the scope of claims.
    b) The error is obvious and requires no evidence.
    c) The error was made by the Patent Office.
    d) The patentee consents.
    Answer: (b) The error is obvious and requires no evidence (patent errors).
  3. Section 79: Evidence before the Controller must initially be:
    a) Oral testimony.
    b) By affidavit.
    c) By documentary proof only.
    d) Through expert witnesses.
    Answer: (b) By affidavit (Section 79 mandates affidavit as the primary form).
  4. Section 80: The Controller must record reasons when exercising:
    a) Statutory powers.
    b) Discretionary powers.
    c) Administrative powers.
    d) Ministerial powers.
    Answer: (b) Discretionary powers (Section 80 requires reasoned decisions for discretion).
  5. Section 81: An application for extension of time cannot be granted if:
    a) The delay was due to a natural disaster.
    b) The delay was caused by the applicant’s lawyer’s illness.
    c) The delay resulted from the applicant’s negligence.
    d) The application is filed with a fee.
    Answer: (c) The delay resulted from the applicant’s negligence (delays must be beyond control).

Tricky Use Cases (with Answers)

Use Case 1: Section 78 (Clerical Error Correction)

  • Scenario: A patent’s claim number "10" is mistakenly typed as "01". The Controller corrects it without notice. A licensee argues this altered the patent’s scope.
  • Question: Is the correction valid?
  • Answer: Yes. The error is purely clerical (typographical) and does not change the substance. No notice is required under Section 78.

Use Case 2: Section 79 (Evidence by Affidavit)

  • Scenario: In an opposition proceeding, an opponent files an affidavit. The patentee requests cross-examination of the deponent. The Controller denies this request.
  • Question: Is the Controller’s decision valid?
  • Answer: No. Section 79 allows the Controller to require personal attendance for cross-examination if the affidavit’s credibility is challenged. Denial without reason is arbitrary.

Use Case 3: Section 80 (Discretionary Powers)

  • Scenario: The Controller rejects a patent amendment without recording reasons. The applicant challenges this.
  • Question: Is the rejection valid?
  • Answer: No. Section 80 mandates recording reasons for discretionary decisions. Failure to do so renders the order invalid.

Use Case 4: Section 81 (Extension of Time)

  • Scenario: An applicant misses the deadline to respond to an examination report due to a server crash at their law firm. They seek an extension.
  • Question: Should the Controller grant the extension?
  • Answer: Yes. The server crash is beyond the applicant’s control. The Controller may grant extension under Section 81 with appropriate conditions (e.g., fee).

Use Case 5: Section 77 (Contempt Powers)

  • Scenario: A witness refuses to comply with the Controller’s summons. The Controller fines them for contempt.
  • Question: Can the Controller do this?
  • Answer: Yes. Under Section 77, the Controller is deemed a civil court for contempt (Sections 345–346 CrPC).

Tricky Scenarios (with Answers)

Scenario 1: Section 78 (Obvious Mistake vs. Substantive Error)

  • Situation: A patent’s abstract incorrectly describes a compound as "water-soluble" when it is "oil-soluble." The Controller corrects this without notice.
  • Trick: Is this correction permissible?
  • Answer: No. The error is not "obvious" as it requires technical interpretation. It affects the patent’s substance, necessitating notice/hearing under Section 78.

Scenario 2: Section 79 (Affidavit Evidence)

  • Situation: An applicant submits an affidavit from a foreign expert. The opponent demands the expert’s physical presence.
  • Trick: Can the Controller compel the expert’s attendance?
  • Answer: Yes, if the affidavit’s material facts are disputed. Section 79 allows the Controller to require personal attendance for cross-examination.

Scenario 3: Section 80 (Judicial Exercise of Discretion)

  • Situation: The Controller grants a time extension to one applicant but denies it to another in identical circumstances.
  • Trick: Is this permissible?
  • Answer: No. Section 80 requires judicial (non-arbitrary) exercise of discretion. Identical cases must be treated consistently; reasons must be recorded.

Scenario 4: Section 81 (Extension of Time for Filing Complete Specification)

  • Situation: An applicant delays filing a complete specification due to "financial constraints."
  • Trick: Can the Controller grant an extension?
  • Answer: No. Financial constraints are generally within the applicant’s control (e.g., loans, cost-cutting). Section 81 requires delays to be beyond control.

Scenario 5: Section 77 (Powers of Civil Court)

  • Situation: The Controller orders a third party to produce confidential documents in a patent dispute. The party refuses, citing trade secrets.
  • Trick: Can the Controller enforce production?
  • Answer: Yes, but with safeguards. Under Section 77, the Controller can compel production, but may issue protective orders (e.g., in-camera review) to protect confidentiality.

    Answer:

Summary: Chapter XV empowers the Controller with quasi-judicial authority to ensure efficient patent administration. Key themes include procedural fairness (Sections 77, 79, 80), error correction limits (Section 78), and strict conditions for time extensions (Section 81). Exam focus is on distinguishing patent vs. latent errors, discretionary reasoning, and contempt powers.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Chapter XV: Powers of Controller Generally - Exam Notes

This chapter is crucial as it defines the quasi-judicial powers of the Controller, making the role central to the patent prosecution and opposition process.

Section 77: Controller to have certain powers of a civil court

  • Gist: The Controller is vested with the powers of a civil court for specific purposes under the Act.
  • Key Powers:
    • Receiving evidence: Summoning and enforcing the attendance of any person and examining them on oath.
    • Requiring discovery & production: Of documents.
    • Issuing commissions: For the examination of witnesses or documents.
    • Awarding costs: The Controller can award costs (subject to the rules) in any proceedings before him.
  • Limitations:
    • These powers are not absolute; they are exercisable only for the purposes of the Act (e.g., for hearings, oppositions, etc.).
    • The Controller cannot execute his own orders like a civil court. For enforcement, one must approach a regular court.

Section 78: Power of Controller to correct clerical errors, etc.

  • Gist: Allows for the correction of errors in patents and applications.
  • What can be corrected? Any clerical error (typos, spelling mistakes) in:
    • Any patent.
    • Any specification or other document.
    • Any application for a patent.
  • Basis for Correction: The error must have arisen from an "inadvertence or accident" and not from an intent to deceive or a deliberate act.
  • Process: Correction can be made suo motu (on his own) by the Controller or upon a request from any person interested.

Section 79: Evidence how to be given and powers of Controller in respect thereof

  • Gist: Lays down the procedure for submitting evidence in proceedings before the Controller.
  • Key Provisions:
    • Evidence can be given by affidavit (sworn written statement).
    • However, the Controller has the power to require the personal attendance of the deponent (the person who made the affidavit) for cross-examination.
    • This section gives the Controller the discretion to manage how evidence is presented, ensuring a fair and efficient process.

Section 80: Exercise of discretionary powers by Controller

  • Gist: Mandates that the Controller must not act arbitrarily when using his discretionary powers.
  • Key Principle: The Controller must give "an opportunity of being heard" to any party that will be "adversely affected" by the exercise of his discretionary power.
  • Purpose: This is a fundamental principle of natural justice. It ensures fairness and prevents unilateral decisions that could harm a party's rights (e.g., refusing an extension, revoking a patent, etc.).

Section 81: Disposal by Controller of applications for extension of time

  • Gist: Governs how the Controller handles requests for extensions of time for any act under the Act or its rules.
  • Key Provisions:
    • The Controller has the power to extend time even if the requested time has already expired.
    • However, this power is subject to the provisions of the Act (e.g., timelines for convention applications under S. 135, restoration of lapsed patents under S. 60, etc., which have strict, non-extendable limits).
    • The extension must be applied for before the expiry of the prescribed period, or the Controller can allow it afterwards if he is satisfied that the failure to apply on time was unintentional.

Tricky Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

1. Under Section 77, the Controller has the power to:

A) Execute his own orders like a District Judge.

B) Summon witnesses and enforce their attendance for any matter.

C) Award costs in proceedings before him, subject to the rules.

D) Pass a decree for infringement of a patent.

Answer: C) Award costs in proceedings before him, subject to the rules. (A and D are powers of courts, not the Controller. B is incorrect as the power is only for the purposes of the Act, not for "any matter".)

2. A request for correction of a clerical error in a granted patent under Section 78 can be made by:

A) Only the patentee.

B) The patentee or an authorized agent.

C) The Controller suo motu or any person interested.

D) Only the Controller on his own motion.

Answer: C) The Controller suo motu or any person interested. (The section is broad and allows "any person interested" to bring the error to the Controller's attention.)

3. In an opposition proceeding, evidence is primarily submitted via an affidavit. The opponent's key expert witness has submitted a detailed affidavit. The applicant can:

A) Do nothing, as affidavit evidence is final.

B) Request the Controller to compel the opponent to submit more documents.

C) Request the Controller to require the personal attendance of the expert for cross-examination under Section 79.

D) File an immediate appeal against the affidavit evidence.

Answer: C) Request the Controller to require the personal attendance of the expert for cross-examination. (This is the specific power granted to the Controller under S. 79 to ensure a fair hearing.)

4. The Controller intends to refuse a request for an extension of time to file a Response to an Examination Report, which will lead to the abandonment of the application. He must:

A) Refuse it immediately as the time has lapsed.

B) Give a notice to the applicant, providing an opportunity of being heard, as per Section 80.

C) Consult the Central Government before refusing.

D) Award costs to the public exchequer.

Answer: B) Give a notice to the applicant, providing an opportunity of being heard. (The applicant is a party "adversely affected" by this discretionary decision, triggering the mandatory requirement of S. 80.)

5. An applicant failed to file the Request for Examination within the 48-month period. They applied for an extension of time under Section 81 two months after the deadline had passed, stating they were unaware of the deadline. The Controller:

A) Must grant the extension as the power is discretionary.

B) Can grant the extension only if satisfied the failure to apply on time was unintentional.

C) Has no power to extend this time limit as it is prescribed in the Act itself (S. 11B).

D) Can grant the extension but must impose a significant cost penalty.

Answer: C) Has no power to extend this time limit as it is prescribed in the Act itself (S. 11B). (S. 81's power is "subject to the provisions of this Act". Critical deadlines like the RFE period are statutory and absolute. The Controller has no jurisdiction to extend them.)

Tricky Use Cases & Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1: The "Accidental" Broadening
In a patent application for a "chemical compound A for treating cancer," the agent accidentally files a specification where a crucial paragraph defining the scope of "cancer" was copied twice, making the definition confusing and arguably broader. After grant, a competitor points this out. The patentee applies under S. 78 to delete the duplicated paragraph, claiming it was a "clerical error." The competitor opposes, arguing this is not a mere clerical error but an amendment that alters the scope. Should the Controller allow it?
Answer: This is a borderline case. A pure "clerical error" is a typo or transcription mistake. A duplication of a substantive paragraph that affects the technical scope is more than clerical; it's a substantive error. The Controller would likely refuse the correction under S. 78. The patentee's remedy would be to seek amendment under the more rigorous process of Section 57 (Amendment of patents), which involves advertising the amendment and dealing with opposition, as it affects the scope of the monopoly.

Scenario 2: The Absent Expert
In a pre-grant opposition, the opponent relies heavily on an affidavit from a renowned US-based expert to prove lack of inventive step. The applicant writes to the Controller, arguing that the expert's conclusions are flawed and requests the expert be produced for cross-examination. The opponent argues that cross-examination is unnecessary and would cause undue delay and expense. What should the Controller do?
Answer: The Controller has the discretion under S. 79 to order the personal attendance of the deponent. The key is the weight of the evidence. If the affidavit is pivotal to the opponent's case and its credibility is seriously challenged, the Controller should grant the applicant's request to ensure a fair hearing. Natural justice demands that a party be allowed to test evidence that is crucial to the case against them. The Controller can manage timelines by setting a strict deadline for the cross-examination.

Scenario 3: The Missed Deadline and The Hearing
An applicant files a response to an Examination Report 15 days after the 6-month deadline. The system automatically tags the application as "abandoned." The applicant immediately files a request for an extension of time under S. 81, explaining the delay was due to a change in in-house counsel. The Controller, reviewing the case, is initially inclined to refuse the extension. What is the correct procedural step?
Answer: Before making a final decision to refuse (which would permanently abandon the application), the Controller MUST, under Section 80, issue a hearing notice to the applicant. The applicant must be given an "opportunity of being heard" as they are "adversely affected" by this proposed exercise of discretionary power. The Controller can only decide after considering the applicant's arguments on the unintentional nature of the delay.

  1. "Discuss the powers of the Controller under the Patents Act, 1970. Can he be equated to a civil court? Explain with limitations." (This directly tests S. 77 and its boundaries).
  2. "Distinguish between the powers of the Controller to correct clerical errors under Section 78 and the power to amend patents under Section 57." (A very common and important question highlighting the different scopes of these sections).
  3. "An opponent in a post-grant opposition proceeding files evidence solely by way of affidavits. The patentee contends that the matter requires cross-examination of the deponents. Explain the relevant legal provisions and the procedure to be followed." (Directly tests the application of S. 79).
  4. "The principle of natural justice is embedded in the Patents Act, 1970. Explain with reference to a specific section under Chapter XV." (The answer is Section 80 – Exercise of discretionary powers by Controller).

The answer is generally no, as these are strict statutory timelines).

Final Exam Tip: Always remember that the Controller's powers, though wide, are not absolute. They are constrained by the purpose of the Act, specific statutory limits, and the overarching principles of natural justice (S. 80). When answering, always highlight this balance.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Chapter XV: Powers of Controller Generally

Section 77. Controller to have certain powers of a civil court

  • The Controller has the powers of a civil court under the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, specifically for certain matters.
  • What powers?
    • Summoning and enforcing the attendance of witnesses.
    • Examining witnesses on oath.
    • Compelling the discovery and production of documents.
    • Receiving evidence on affidavits.
    • Issuing commissions for the examination of witnesses or documents.
    • Awarding costs.

Section 78. Power of Controller to correct clerical errors, etc

  • The Controller has the power to correct clerical errors in any application, patent, or other document filed with or issued by the patent office.
  • This can be done either suo moto (on their own initiative) or upon a request in writing from any interested person.
  • The correction cannot be made if it would affect the substance of the document.
  • The correction request is subject to the provisions of Section 80 (discretionary powers).

Section 79. Evidence how to be given and powers of Controller in respect thereof

  • The Controller can take evidence viva voce (oral testimony) in any proceeding under the Act.
  • The Controller can also require that evidence be given by affidavit.
  • The Controller has the power to refuse to admit an affidavit as evidence if the deponent is not available for cross-examination.
  • This section relates to the procedural aspects of how evidence is presented to the Controller.

Section 80. Exercise of discretionary powers by Controller

  • Before exercising any discretionary power that is likely to affect the rights of any person adversely, the Controller must give that person an opportunity of being heard.
  • This is a fundamental principle of natural justice and ensures fairness.
  • The opportunity to be heard is a mandatory requirement.

Section 81. Disposal by Controller of applications for extension of time

  • The Controller can extend the time for doing any act under the Act.
  • The request for extension must be made before the expiry of the prescribed period.
  • The Controller can also extend the time after the prescribed period has expired if the delay was unintentional and there was no undue delay in making the request.
  • This section is subject to the provisions of Section 80.

Answer: b) Yes, the Controller can correct the error suo moto. Section 78 gives the Controller the power to correct clerical errors either on a written request or on their own initiative.

Q2. The Controller is about to refuse a patent application on a technical ground. Which section mandates that the Controller must provide the applicant an opportunity to be heard before making this final decision?

a) Section 77 b) Section 78 c) Section 79 d) Section 80

Answer: d) Section 80. This section deals with the exercise of discretionary powers and the principle of natural justice, requiring an opportunity to be heard when a decision may adversely affect someone's rights.

a) Section 77 b) Section 78 c) Section 79 d) Section 81

Answer: c) Section 79. This section gives the Controller the power to refuse to admit an affidavit if the deponent is not available for cross-examination.

Tricky Use Cases with Answers

Use Case 1: The Case of the Missing Annexure

An applicant, Z, files a patent application but forgets to include a crucial annexure referred to in the main document. The application is examined and a First Examination Report (FER) is issued. The Controller, upon reviewing the file, notices the missing annexure. What is the Controller's course of action under the discussed sections?

Answer: The Controller, under the general powers of the civil court granted by Section 77, can compel the production of the missing document. The Controller can also use the provisions of Section 78 to correct the application by incorporating the annexure, provided it is a clerical error and does not change the substance of the invention. However, since the Controller's decision to add the annexure might affect the application's priority date or the rights of others, the Controller must also follow the principles of Section 80 and give the applicant a chance to be heard before making any decision.

Since the request for an extension was made after the prescribed period, the Controller can still grant it if the delay was unintentional and there was no undue delay in making the request. Given the medical emergency, the delay is likely to be considered unintentional. The Controller, before granting the extension, must also consider the principles of Section 80 and ensure no other party's rights would be adversely affected without them being given an opportunity to be heard.

CHAPTER XV - POWERS OF CONTROLLER GENERALLY

Comprehensive Exam Notes for Patent Agent Examination

SECTION 77: CONTROLLER TO HAVE CERTAIN POWERS OF A CIVIL COURT

Key Provisions:

  • Civil Court Powers: The Controller is vested with specific powers similar to a civil court while trying a suit under the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908
  • Specific Powers Include:
    1. Summoning and enforcing attendance of witnesses
    2. Requiring discovery and production of documents
    3. Receiving evidence on affidavits
    4. Issuing commissions for examination of witnesses or documents
    5. Reviewing decisions made by subordinate officers
    6. Any other matter prescribed by rules

Important Notes:

  • Limited Jurisdiction: Powers are restricted to matters arising under the Patents Act
  • Procedural Compliance: Must follow principles of natural justice
  • Evidence Rules: Governed by Indian Evidence Act, 1872

SECTION 78: POWER OF CONTROLLER TO CORRECT CLERICAL ERRORS, ETC.

Key Provisions:

  • Suo Moto Corrections: Controller can correct clerical errors, mistakes in translation, or other obvious mistakes
  • Application-based Corrections: Interested parties can apply for corrections
  • Scope of Corrections:
    • Clerical errors in patents, applications, or documents
    • Mistakes in translation
    • Other obvious mistakes in patents or applications

Limitations:

  • No Substantive Changes: Cannot alter the scope or nature of the invention
  • Notice Requirement: Adequate notice to interested parties before making corrections
  • Opposition Rights: Provision for opposing proposed corrections

Practical Applications:

  • Correcting typographical errors in patent specifications
  • Rectifying wrong dates or reference numbers
  • Fixing translation mistakes in documents

SECTION 79: EVIDENCE HOW TO BE GIVEN AND POWERS OF CONTROLLER IN RESPECT THEREOF

Evidence Rules:

  • Statutory Declaration: Evidence by way of statutory declaration before any Magistrate or Notary Public
  • Affidavit Evidence: Controller may receive evidence by affidavit
  • Oral Examination: Power to examine witnesses orally
  • Documentary Evidence: Authority to require production of documents

Controller's Powers:

  1. Witness Examination: Can examine witnesses on oath
  2. Document Production: Can require production of books, papers, and documents
  3. Expert Evidence: Can seek expert opinions
  4. Commission Orders: Can issue commissions for examination of witnesses

Procedural Requirements:

  • Cross-Examination Rights: Parties have right to cross-examine witnesses
  • Relevancy Test: Evidence must be relevant to proceedings
  • Authentication: Documents must be properly authenticated

SECTION 80: EXERCISE OF DISCRETIONARY POWERS BY CONTROLLER

Discretionary Powers:

  • Hearing Rights: Before exercising discretionary powers adversely affecting any person, Controller must:
    1. Give opportunity of being heard
    2. Consider written representations
    3. Follow principles of natural justice

Key Principles:

  • Reasoned Decisions: All discretionary orders must be reasoned
  • Proportionality: Exercise of power must be proportionate to the issue
  • Fairness: Decisions must be fair and unbiased

Examples of Discretionary Powers:

  • Extending time limits
  • Allowing amendments to applications
  • Granting or refusing patents
  • Ordering re-examination

SECTION 81: DISPOSAL BY CONTROLLER OF APPLICATIONS FOR EXTENSION OF TIME

Time Extension Rules:

  • General Principle: Controller may extend prescribed time periods
  • Conditions for Extension:
    1. Sufficient cause shown by applicant
    2. Application made before or after expiry of time
    3. Payment of prescribed fees
    4. No undue delay

Limitations:

  • Statutory Restrictions: Some time limits cannot be extended
  • Public Interest: Extensions should not prejudice public interest
  • Reasonable Limits: Extensions must be reasonable

Fee Structure:

  • Different fees for different types of extensions
  • Higher fees for extensions sought after expiry
  • Additional fees for multiple extensions

🔹 Section 77: Controller to have certain powers of a civil court

Key Points:

  • The Controller has powers of a civil court under the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, while trying proceedings under the Act.
  • Powers include:
    • Summoning & enforcing attendance of witnesses
    • Requiring discovery & production of documents
    • Receiving evidence on affidavits
    • Issuing commissions for witness examination
    • Reviewing decisions
    • Dismissing applications for default or deciding ex parte
    • Setting aside orders
    • Any other matter prescribed

⚠️ Important: These powers are exercisable only for proceedings under the Patents Act, not general civil matters.

📌 Exam Tip: Controller ≠ Civil Court. Powers are borrowed only for statutory proceedings under the Act.

🔹 Section 78: Power of Controller to correct clerical errors, etc.

Key Points:

  • Controller may correct any clerical error, obvious mistake, or error arising from accidental slip or omission in:
    • Any document filed with/by the Patent Office
    • Any patent granted
    • Any entry in the Register

✅ Correction may be made on request or suo moto (on own motion).

⚠️ Limitation: Correction must not affect substance/rights — only clerical/obvious errors.

📌 Exam Tip: “Clerical error” ≠ substantive amendment. No new matter or change in scope allowed.

🔹 Section 79: Evidence how to be given and powers of Controller in respect thereof

Key Points:

  • Evidence may be given by affidavit.
  • Controller may:
    • Order any witness to be examined viva voce (orally)
    • Order discovery & production of documents
    • Impose reasonable conditions

⚠️ Affidavits are primary mode — oral evidence is exceptional/discretionary.

📌 Exam Tip: Affidavit = default mode. Oral evidence only if Controller orders for “just reasons”.

🔹 Section 80: Exercise of discretionary powers by Controller

Key Points:

  • Wherever Act confers discretionary power on Controller, he must:
    • Give opportunity of being heard (unless Act says otherwise)
    • Pass a reasoned order

✅ Applies to all discretionary powers — e.g., grant extension, refuse amendment, etc.

⚠️ Exception: If Act specifically says “without giving opportunity of hearing”, then not required.

📌 Exam Tip: Natural justice applies unless expressly excluded. Always look for “shall/may” + “opportunity of hearing” clause.

🔹 Section 81: Disposal by Controller of applications for extension of time

Key Points:

  • Controller may extend time for doing any act/procedure under the Act if application is filed BEFORE expiry of original time.

⛔ NO extension if:

  • Application filed AFTER expiry, unless:
    • Applicant shows sufficient reason (e.g., postal delay, natural calamity, hospitalization)
    • AND Controller is satisfied it’s just to allow

✅ Extension ≠ Right. It’s discretionary.

📌 Exam Tip: “Sufficient reason” is key for post-deadline extensions. Mere negligence or oversight = insufficient.

Exam Notes on Chapter XV: Powers of Controller Generally (Sections 77-81, Patents Act, 1970)

These sections outline the administrative and quasi-judicial powers vested in the Controller General of Patents, Designs, and Trade Marks to ensure efficient patent proceedings. The Controller acts as a quasi-judicial authority, balancing procedural fairness with expedition. Key themes include civil court-like powers, error correction, evidence handling, discretionary exercise, and time extensions. Notes are structured section-wise for quick revision.

Section 77: Controller to Have Certain Powers of a Civil Court

  • Core Provision: The Controller has limited powers akin to a civil court under the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (CPC), applicable only in proceedings under the Act and subject to rules.
  • Specific Powers (Sub-section 1):
    • Summon and enforce attendance of persons; examine on oath (1(a)).
    • Require discovery/production of documents (1(b)).
    • Receive evidence on affidavits (1(c)).
    • Issue commissions for examining witnesses/documents (1(d)).
    • Award costs (1(e)).
    • Review own decisions (application within prescribed time/manner) (1(f)).
    • Set aside ex-parte orders (application within prescribed time/manner) (1(g)).
    • Other prescribed matters (1(h)).
  • Enforcement: Costs awarded are executable as a civil court decree (Sub-section 2).
  • Key Notes: Powers are procedural, not substantive; no power to enforce judgments like a full court. Useful in oppositions, revocations. Limitation: No contempt powers explicitly.

Section 78: Power of Controller to Correct Clerical Errors, etc.

  • Scope: Allows correction of clerical/like errors in patents, specifications, applications, or register entries (without prejudice to Sections 44, 57, 59 on amendments).
  • Procedure:
    • On request (written, with fee) or suo motu (Sub-section 2).
    • Suo motu corrections require notice to affected parties (patentee/applicant/others) and opportunity to be heard (Sub-section 3).
    • If correction materially alters meaning/scope, publish notice; allow opposition within prescribed time; hear both sides before deciding (Sub-sections 4-5).
  • Key Notes: "Clerical error" is narrow (e.g., typos, not substantive changes). Distinct from amendments (which need Form 13). Opposition hearing mandatory only if published. No appeal on merits, but review possible under S.77.

Section 79: Evidence How to Be Given and Powers of Controller in Respect Thereof

  • Default Mode: Evidence by affidavit in proceedings before Controller, unless directed otherwise by rules.
  • Flexibility: Controller may:
    • Take oral evidence instead of/in addition to affidavits.
    • Allow cross-examination on affidavit contents if deemed right.
  • Key Notes: Promotes efficiency (affidavits reduce hearings) but ensures fairness (oral/cross-exam on discretion). Applies to all proceedings (e.g., oppositions). Controller decides "rightness" based on case complexity/credibility issues.

Section 80: Exercise of Discretionary Powers by Controller

  • Mandatory Hearing: Before exercising discretion adversely to an applicant (for patent/amendment), give opportunity to be heard if requested within prescribed time.
  • Condition: Request must be at least 10 days before time-limit expiry (Proviso).
  • Key Notes: "Adverse discretion" includes refusals, objections. Non-mandatory if no request. Protects natural justice; failure invites writ challenges. Applies only to applicants, not third parties.

Section 81: Disposal by Controller of Applications for Extension of Time

  • No Hearing/Notice Required: When extending time for any act (under Act/rules), no need to notify/hear opponents.
  • No Appeal: Order granting extension is final; no appeal lies.
  • Key Notes: Expedites process; contrasts with adversarial proceedings. Extensions via Form 4; fees apply. "Party interested in opposing" (e.g., opponents in revocation) get no say.

Overall Chapter Insights: Emphasizes Controller's role as efficient administrator (e.g., no appeals on extensions) while upholding fairness (hearings, notices). Cross-link: S.77 powers apply across; S.78 limits substantive changes. For exams, focus on procedural triggers (requests, notices, times).

Section 77: Controller to have certain powers of a civil court

  • The Controller has powers akin to a civil court when dealing with patent proceedings under the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908.
  • These powers include:
    • Summoning and enforcing attendance: Calling upon any person and examining them under oath.
    • Requiring document production: Demanding the discovery and presentation of documents.
    • Receiving evidence on affidavits: Accepting evidence presented through sworn written statements.
    • Issuing commissions: Ordering examinations of witnesses or documents.
    • Awarding costs: Determining and allocating expenses incurred during the proceedings.
    • Reviewing decisions: Reconsidering previous decisions upon application within the prescribed time and manner.
    • Setting aside ex-parte orders: Revoking orders passed without the presence of the other party, upon application within the prescribed time and manner.
  • Costs awarded: Any order for costs made by the Controller is enforceable as a decree of a civil court. 

Section 78: Power of Controller to correct clerical errors, etc.

  • The Controller can correct clerical errors in patents, specifications, documents filed, or entries in the register.
  • Correction Process: A correction can be made either:
    • Upon request: A written request with the prescribed fee from an interested person.
    • Suo motu: By the Controller without a request, after providing notice and an opportunity to be heard to the patentee or applicant and any other concerned parties.
  • Limitation: Section 78 corrections cannot be used to bypass procedural deadlines or reinstate applications deemed withdrawn due to missed deadlines. 

Section 79: Evidence how to be given and powers of Controller in respect thereof

  • The Controller can accept oral evidence in place of or in addition to evidence provided through affidavits.
  • The Controller may also allow cross-examination of parties on the contents of their affidavits. 

Section 80: Exercise of discretionary powers by Controller

  • The Controller's discretionary powers under the Act are to be exercised fairly and impartially.
  • The Controller must provide an opportunity to be heard to any person who may be adversely affected by the exercise of such power. 

Section 81: Disposal by Controller of applications for extension of time

  • The Controller is responsible for handling requests for extensions of time prescribed under the Act.
  • The Controller must decide whether to grant or refuse the extension, considering the reasons for the request and the impact on other parties.

Tricky Use Cases with Answers

Use Case 1: A patentee finds a minor spelling mistake in their patent certificate. How can this be corrected and under which section? 

Answer: The patentee can request the Controller to correct the clerical error under Section 78 of the Patents Act, 1970, by submitting a written request with the required fee. 

Use Case 2: In a pre-grant opposition, a party wishes to use an expert witness's oral testimony. Can the Controller permit this?

Answer: Yes, the Controller can accept oral evidence in addition to or instead of affidavits under Section 79 of the Patents Act, 1970. 

Tricky Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1: An application is deemed abandoned for not filing the FER response on time, including the extension period. The applicant blames a misunderstanding by their agent. Can the Controller reinstate the application? 

Answer: Under the Patent Amendment Rules, 2024, the Controller cannot condone delays in filing FER responses or extensions for them under Rule 137. The rules are now stricter on these deadlines for applications filed after March 15, 2024, although courts have sometimes reinstated applications due to agent error. 

Scenario 2: During examination, the examiner objects that claims are unclear. The applicant wants to amend the claims, which might slightly broaden the scope. Can the applicant request this, and what will the Controller consider? 

Answer: The applicant can request an amendment under Section 57. The Controller will check that the amendment does not introduce new matter or expand the claims beyond the initial disclosure. The Controller will likely consider if the amendment is supported by the original specification and clarifies the claims without broadening the invention beyond what was originally described.
