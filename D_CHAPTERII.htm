PART A: TRICKY MULTIPLE CHOICE QUESTIONS (MCQs)

Section 3: Controller and Other Officers

  1. Under Section 3(4), the Controller withdraws a design application pending before an Examiner and transfers it to another officer. The transferee officer:
    a) Must start the examination de novo.
    b) Must start from the stage it was transferred.
    c) Can proceed either de novo or from the stage of transfer, subject to special directions in the order.
    d) Has to send it back to the Controller for fresh directions.
    Answer: (c) The section explicitly gives the Controller this discretionary power.
  2. The authority to appoint Examiners and other officers under the Designs Act, 2000, lies with:
    a) The Controller-General of Patents, Designs, and Trademarks.
    b) The High Court.
    c) The Central Government.
    d) The Office of the Attorney General.
    Answer: (c) Section 3(2) vests this power in the Central Government.

Section 4: Prohibition of Registration of Certain Designs

  1. A design for a "mobile phone with a rectangular front screen with rounded corners" is filed for registration in 2025. This design is likely to be refused under Section 4 because:
    a) It comprises scandalous matter.
    b) It is not new or original.
    c) It is not significantly distinguishable from known designs.
    d) Both (b) and (c).
    Answer: (d) Given the ubiquitous nature of this design feature in smartphones globally, it would likely fail both the novelty/originality and significant distinguishability tests.
  2. Which of the following is NOT a ground for refusal of design registration under Section 4?
    a) The design was disclosed in a PhD thesis deposited in a German university library 6 months before the filing date in India.
    b) The design is a mere trade variant of an existing design for a similar article.
    c) The design is a mechanical device whose shape is solely dictated by the function it performs.
    d) The design contains artistic imagery that is deemed obscene by contemporary community standards.
    Answer: (c) While functionality is a ground for non-registration, it is covered under Section 2(d) ("design" definition) and not explicitly under Section 4. This is a classic trick question testing the distinction between definitions and prohibitions.

Section 5: Application for Registration of Designs

  1. The proviso to Section 5(1) mandates that before registration, the Controller must:
    a) Advertise the application for public opposition.
    b) Refer the application for examination as to its registrability.
    c) Seek a novelty search report from an international design registry.
    d) Obtain consent from all previous proprietors of similar designs.
    Answer: (b) The proviso introduces a mandatory examination step, which is a key feature of the 2000 Act.
  2. Under Section 5(5), an application is deemed abandoned if not completed in time due to "default or neglect on the part of the applicant." This implies:
    a) The time limit is absolute, with no possibility of extension under any circumstances.
    b) The applicant can appeal this abandonment directly to the High Court.
    c) If the delay was due to a genuine error by the Patent Office and not the applicant, the application should not be deemed abandoned.
    d) The Controller has no discretion in the matter once the time lapses.
    Answer: (c) The phrase "on the part of the applicant" is crucial. The deeming provision applies only if the fault lies with the applicant.

Section 6: Registration to be in Respect of Particular Article

  1. Proprietor 'A' registers a novel floral pattern design for "Ceramic Cups" (Class 07-01) in 2022. In 2023, 'A' applies to register the same design for "Ceramic Plates" (also Class 07-01). Under Section 6(3):
    a) The application must be refused as the design is no longer new.
    b) The application shall not be refused on grounds of lack of novelty only because it was previously registered.
    c) The registration will be granted, and it will extend the total copyright period by 10 years from 2023.
    d) The design must pass the test of significant distinguishability again for plates.
    Answer: (b) Section 6(3)(a) is a specific exception to Section 4(a) for subsequent registration of the same design in the same class. The proviso clarifies that the copyright period is not extended.
  2. Person 'X' files an application for a design (D1) for a "chair." During its pendency, 'X' acquires the rights to a previously registered design (D0) for a "stool" from 'Y'. D1 is essentially D0 with minor variations. As per Section 6(4):
    a) 'X's application for D1 will be rejected as it infringes 'Y's copyright.
    b) 'X's application will be treated as if 'X' was the proprietor of D0 at the time of filing D1's application.
    c) 'X' must now file a fresh application in 'X's own name.
    d) The application will proceed, but 'X' will have to pay royalties to 'Y'.
    Answer: (b) This subsection prevents rejection in scenarios where the applicant, during pendency, becomes entitled to the prior registered design.

Sections 7, 9 & 10: Publication, Certificate, and Register

  1. The Register of Designs maintained under Section 10 is:
    a) Conclusive proof of the validity of the registered design.
    b) Prima facie evidence of matters entered in it (like proprietorship).
    c) Not admissible in legal proceedings.
    d) Only for administrative use by the Patent Office.
    Answer: (b) Section 10(4) clearly states it is prima facie evidence.
  2. Under Section 7, after registration, the design is opened to public inspection. The primary legal implication of this is:
    a) It marks the start of the 10-year copyright term.
    b) It allows the public to inspect and challenge the validity of the design.
    c) It prevents the proprietor from keeping the design a trade secret.
    d) All of the above.
    Answer: (d) Publication is integral to the patent/design bargain: exclusive rights in return for public disclosure, which also enables validity challenges.

Section 8: Power of Controller to Make Orders Regarding Substitution of Application, etc.

  1. Two joint applicants, 'Alpha Ltd.' and 'Beta Inc.', apply for a design. Before registration, they have a dispute on how to proceed. Under Section 8(5):
    a) The application is automatically deemed withdrawn.
    b) The Controller has no power to intervene; they must resolve it in court.
    c) The Controller can give directions on proceeding with the application after hearing the parties.
    d) The application will proceed in the name of the first applicant only.
    Answer: (c) This section grants the Controller specific powers to resolve inter se disputes among joint applicants.
  2. A claimant seeks direction under Section 8(1) based on an assignment agreement. Which condition in Section 8(3) is NOT mandatory for the Controller to give such direction?
    a) The design is identified in the agreement by its application number.
    b) The agreement is stamped and notarized.
    c) The assignor provides an acknowledgement to the Controller relating the agreement to the application.
    d) The claimant's rights are established by a court decision.
    Answer: (b) Notarization is not a condition prescribed under the Act. The conditions are listed in (a), (c), and (d) of Section 8(3) as alternatives.

  1. Explain the concept of "New or Original" and "Significantly Distinguishable" under Section 4 of the Designs Act, 2000. How do these criteria differ? Illustrate with examples. (7 marks)

  1. What are the powers of the Controller under the Designs Act, 2000, concerning: (i) Withdrawal and transfer of cases, and (ii) Resolution of disputes among joint applicants? (5 marks)

PART C: USE CASES

  1. Startup Scenario: "Kalaa Innovations," a startup, creates a uniquely shaped, ergonomic bottle for athletes. They plan to launch in 6 months.

○      Action: File for design registration immediately under Section 5. Ensure the shape is novel and not merely functional. Request examination and aim for early registration and publication (Section 7) to secure copyright before market launch.

  1. M&A Scenario: "Classic Furniture Co." is acquiring "Moderne Designs Ltd." As part of due diligence, they need to verify Moderne's design portfolio.

○      Action: Search the Register of Designs (Section 10) for entries in Moderne's name. Request certified copies of registration certificates (Section 9) and verify any recorded assignments.

  1. Collaboration Gone Sour: Two independent designers, Riya and Sam, jointly created a jewellery design and filed an application. They now have a disagreement and Sam wants to withdraw the application.

○      Action: Riya can apply to the Controller under Section 8(5). The Controller will hear both and may direct the application to proceed in Riya's name alone, if justified.

  1. Portfolio Expansion: "Zentrix Appliances" has a registered design for its premium mixer-grinder jar. It now wants to use the same aesthetic design for its new line of food processor bowls and lids (same class of articles).

○      Action: File a fresh application under Section 6(3) for the new articles. The registration cannot be refused for lack of novelty solely due to the prior registration, ensuring brand consistency.

PART D: CASE STUDY ANALYSIS

Title: The Dilemma of "SwiftRide" Bicycle Frame

Facts:

  1. AeroCycle Inc., on 1st March 2023, files an application (App. No. 12345) for registration of a new, sleek design for a bicycle frame (Class 12-11).
  2. On 15th April 2023, Velocity Ltd. files an application (App. No. 23456) for a strikingly similar bicycle frame design.
  3. On 1st June 2023, AeroCycle's design is registered, and its particulars are published.
  4. On 15th June 2023, during the examination of Velocity's application, the Examiner cites AeroCycle's registered design and issues an objection under Section 4(c) (not significantly distinguishable).
  5. Velocity Ltd. argues that it had publicly displayed a prototype of its frame design at an international trade fair in Tokyo on 20th February 2023, i.e., before AeroCycle's filing date.

Questions for Analysis:

  1. What is the critical date for assessing the novelty of AeroCycle's design under Section 4(b)?
  2. Based on Velocity's claim of prior disclosure at the Tokyo fair, what ground can Velocity use to potentially cancel AeroCycle's registration? (Hint: It's not an opposition; post-registration, it's a petition for cancellation before the Controller on specific grounds).
  3. How does Section 7 (publication) become relevant in this scenario for Velocity Ltd.?
  4. If Velocity had acquired the business of AeroCycle (including the design application) in May 2023, what provision could have been invoked under Section 8 regarding the pending application of Velocity?

Suggested Analysis:

  1. The critical date is the filing date of the application (1st March 2023 for AeroCycle). Any disclosure before this date, anywhere in the world, can destroy novelty.
  2. Velocity can file a petition for cancellation of AeroCycle's registration under Section 19(1)(a), read with Section 4(b), claiming the design was not new/original as it was disclosed to the public prior to AeroCycle's filing date (at the Tokyo fair).
  3. Section 7: Only after publication (post-registration) could Velocity become aware of the conflicting registration to take action. Pre-grant opposition does not exist under the Designs Act.
  4. Section 8(1): Velocity (as claimant) could have requested the Controller to direct that its own pending application (No. 23456) proceed in its name based on the assignment from AeroCycle, potentially resolving the conflict. Alternatively, Section 6(4) might be analyzed if the designs are considered variants.

💼 Use Cases & Practical Scenarios

Use Case 1: The "Global Disclosure" Trap

Scenario: A furniture designer in Delhi creates a unique chair. Before filing an application in India, they post high-resolution 360-degree photos on a public Instagram account.

Legal Outcome: Under Section 4(b), the design cannot be registered because it was disclosed to the public via "publication in tangible form" (digital images) prior to the filing date.3

Use Case 2: Similar Article Extension

Scenario: A company registers a floral pattern for "Ceramic Plates" (Class 19). Six months later, they want to use the exact same pattern for "Ceramic Bowls" in the same class.

Legal Outcome: Under Section 6(3), the registration for bowls cannot be refused on the grounds of lack of novelty just because it was already registered for plates. However, the copyright for the "Bowls" design will expire at the same time as the "Plates" design.

⚖️ Case Study: Hello Kitty vs. Local Manufacturers

(Based on the principles of Section 4 & 5)

The Facts: A company attempts to register a design for a toy that is "significantly similar" to a well-known international character but with minor changes to the color of the clothing.

Legal Issue: Does this meet the criteria for registration under Section 4(c)?

Analysis:

●      Section 4(c) states a design cannot be registered if it is "not significantly distinguishable from known designs."

●      The court/Controller would look at the "visual appeal" and "totality of the design."

●      If the average consumer cannot distinguish the new design from the pre-existing one without a side-by-side microscopic comparison, the design lacks novelty and originality.

Case Study 1: Section 3

Case Study 2: Section 4

Scenario: ABC Ltd. designs a new smartphone case with a unique pattern. Before filing:

●      The pattern was displayed at their office reception (visible to visitors)

●      A prototype was given to a retailer under NDA

  2. Does Instagram posting affect registrability?

Case Study 3: Section 5

Case Study 4: Section 6

Scenario: Company P registers a floral design for "curtains" (textile class) on 1st Jan 2024. On 1st June 2024, Company P applies to register the same design for "bedsheets" (same textile class). On 1st Sep 2024, Company Q (competitor) opposes stating the design was already registered and published.

Questions:

  1. Can Company P's second application be refused on ground of prior publication?
  2. If registered, what will be the copyright period for bedsheets design?
  3. Would the answer differ if bedsheets were in a different class?
  4. What is the significance of "same class"?

Answers:

  1. No, Section 6(3)(b) protects subsequent registration in same class from being invalidated merely because design was applied to article in previous registration.
  2. The copyright period will not extend beyond that of the previous registration (1st Jan 2024) as per Section 6(3) proviso.
  3. Yes, if in different class, Section 6(3) protections wouldn't apply - it would be treated as potentially separate registration.
  4. "Same class" allows proprietor to extend design protection across articles in that class without penalty of prior publication.

Case Study 5: Section 8

Case Study 6: Section 10

Complex Scenario: TechDesign Ltd. creates an innovative laptop cover design on 1st Jan 2024. Events:
