I. EXAM NOTES: CHAPTER V - OPPOSITION PROCEEDINGS

Section 25: Opposition to the Patent

  • Core Concept: Allows third parties to challenge the grant of a patent before (Pre-grant) or after (Post-grant) it is sealed.
  • Pre-grant Opposition (S. 25(1)):
    • Who: Any person.
    • When: After publication of application (S. 11A) but before grant of patent.
    • Grounds: Wider than post-grant. Includes:
      1. Wrongful obtaining (S. 25(1)(a)) - akin to S. 26.
      2. Prior publication/public knowledge (S. 25(1)(b)).
      3. Obviousness (S. 25(1)(c)).
      4. Not invention within Act (S. 25(1)(d)).
      5. Insufficient description/utility (S. 25(1)(e)).
      6. Non-disclosure/wrong mention of source/geographical origin (S. 25(1)(f)).
      7. Convention application not filed within 12 months (S. 25(1)(g)).
      8. Complete specification not filed within 12 months of provisional (S. 25(1)(h)).
      9. Applicant not entitled to apply (S. 25(1)(i)).
      10. Nondisclosure of foreign applications/permissions (S. 25(1)(j)).
      11. Patent granted on application of person not entitled (S. 25(1)(k)).
    • Procedure: Representation in writing to Controller. No formal hearing required but Controller may if requested. Decision before grant.
  • Post-grant Opposition (S. 25(2)):
    • Who: Any person interested (Person aggrieved or having commercial interest).
    • When: Within 1 year from date of publication of grant (S. 25(2)).
    • Grounds: Narrower than pre-grant. Excludes:
      • Convention application delay (S. 25(1)(g)).
      • Complete spec delay (S. 25(1)(h)).
      • Applicant not entitled (S. 25(1)(i)) - unless it means the invention was derived from opponent/another.
      • Nondisclosure of foreign apps (S. 25(1)(j)).
      • Patent granted to wrong person (S. 25(1)(k)) - unless it means the invention was derived from opponent/another.
    • Procedure: Formal notice of opposition (Form 7). Evidence in support (Form 8). Evidence in reply (Form 9). Hearing (mandatory if requested). Controller's decision (can be appealed to IPAB).
  • Key Differences:
    • Timing: Pre-grant (before grant) vs. Post-grant (within 1 year after grant).
    • Who: Pre-grant (Any person) vs. Post-grant (Person interested).
    • Grounds: Pre-grant (11 grounds) vs. Post-grant (7 grounds, excluding procedural/eligibility grounds unless linked to derivation).
    • Procedure: Pre-grant (Less formal, representation) vs. Post-grant (Formal opposition procedure akin to pre-2005 Act).

Section 26: In cases of "obtaining" Controller may treat the patent as the patent of opponent

  • Core Concept: Addresses situations where a patent was granted to someone who obtained the invention (or rights to it) from the true inventor/assignor, often fraudulently or improperly.
  • "Obtaining": Implies acquisition of the invention/rights from the true owner (inventor or their successor in title) through deception, breach of confidence, or improper means. Not mere independent invention or lack of novelty.
  • Who Can Oppose? The true inventor, their legal representative, or the person from whom the invention was obtained (the "opponent").
  • Ground: Specific ground under both Pre-grant (S. 25(1)(a)) and Post-grant (S. 25(2)(a)) opposition.
  • Controller's Power (S. 26):
    • If the Controller is satisfied, on opposition, that the patent was obtained from the opponent (or someone under whom the opponent claims), the Controller may:
      1. Order the patent to be sealed to the opponent (or as the opponent may direct), OR
      2. Order the patent to be shared between the applicant (patentee) and the opponent.
    • Discretionary: "Controller may treat..." - Not automatic. Requires proof of "obtaining".
    • Alternative to Revocation: Offers a remedy other than revocation (which is the usual outcome for successful opposition on other grounds). Protects the true owner's rights.
  • Relationship to S. 64(1)(c): S. 64(1)(c) allows revocation if the patent was obtained wrongfully. S. 26 provides a specific alternative remedy (sealing to opponent/sharing) within the opposition framework when "obtaining" is proven.

Section 28: Mention of inventor as such in patent

  • Core Concept: Ensures the true inventor(s) are named in the patent document.
  • Requirement: The patent must mention the name(s) of the inventor(s) (S. 28(1)).
  • Application:
    • Applicant's Duty: Applicant must provide the inventor's name(s) to the Controller (S. 28(1)).
    • Controller's Duty: Controller must include the inventor's name(s) in the patent when sealed (S. 28(1)).
  • Consequence of Non-Compliance: Failure to mention the inventor does not invalidate the patent (S. 28(2)). The patent remains enforceable.
  • Remedy for Aggrieved Inventor: If an inventor believes they are wrongly omitted or someone is wrongly named, they can apply to the Controller (S. 28(3)). The Controller can:
    • Order the patentee to apply to the Controller to correct the register (patent document) by including the true inventor's name and removing any wrongly named person.
    • Advertise the application for correction.
    • Decide the application after hearing parties.
  • Key Points:
    • Naming is a formality with no impact on patent validity.
    • Provides a mechanism for the true inventor to get recognition/correct the record.
    • Distinct from ownership/entitlement (S. 6, S. 7). An inventor may not be the applicant/patentee (e.g., employee invention).

Section 27: [Omitted]

  • Status: This section was omitted by the Patents (Amendment) Act, 2002.
  • Original Content (Pre-2002): Dealt with the effect of amendment of complete specification during opposition. Its provisions were effectively relocated or superseded by other sections (like S. 57 - Amendment of application and specification) and the revised opposition procedures introduced in 2002/2005.
  • Exam Relevance: Know it is OMITTED. Do not attempt to answer questions based on its old content.

II. TRICKY MCQs

  1. Q: 'X' files a pre-grant opposition against a patent application published 18 months ago, arguing the applicant is not entitled to apply under S. 25(1)(i). The application is yet to be granted. Can this ground be used?
    • A) Yes, it's a valid pre-grant opposition ground.
    • B) No, this ground is only available for post-grant opposition.
    • C) No, this ground was removed by the 2005 Amendment.
    • D) Yes, but only if 'X' is a "person interested".
    • Answer: A (Trick: S. 25(1)(i) is a valid pre-grant ground. The trick is confusing it with post-grant grounds where it's generally excluded unless linked to derivation).
  2. Q: A patent is granted on 1st Jan 2024. On 2nd Jan 2025, 'Y' (a direct competitor) files a post-grant opposition arguing the invention lacks novelty. The patentee claims it's time-barred. Is the opposition valid?
    • A) Yes, novelty is a valid ground and it's within 1 year.
    • B) No, post-grant opposition must be filed within 6 months of grant.
    • C) No, 'Y' is not a "person interested" as a competitor.
    • D) Yes, but novelty is only a pre-grant ground.
    • Answer: A (Trick: The 1-year limit is absolute. Competitors are "persons interested". Novelty is a core ground for both pre and post-grant).
  3. Q: 'Z' proves during post-grant opposition that the patentee obtained the invention from 'Z' through breach of confidence. The Controller is satisfied. What is the most likely outcome?
    • A) The patent must be revoked.
    • B) The patent must be sealed to 'Z'.
    • C) The patent may be revoked or sealed/shared as per S. 26.
    • D) The patentee can avoid revocation by paying compensation to 'Z'.
    • Answer: C (Trick: S. 26 provides a discretionary alternative to revocation (sealing/sharing). Revocation (S. 64) is also possible, but S. 26 offers a specific remedy within opposition. "Must" in options A/B is incorrect as it's discretionary).
  4. Q: A patent is granted naming 'A' as the sole inventor. 'B', who collaborated with 'A' and contributed significantly to the inventive concept, is not named. 'B' applies under S. 28(3). What is the effect on the patent's validity?
    • A) The patent is automatically invalid.
    • B) The patent remains valid, but 'B' can get named as joint inventor.
    • C) The patent is voidable at 'B's option.
    • D) The patentee can be sued for damages by 'B'.
    • Answer: B (Trick: S. 28(2) explicitly states failure to name inventor does not invalidate the patent. S. 28(3) provides a correction mechanism).
  5. Q: During pre-grant opposition, an opponent argues the complete specification was filed 13 months after the provisional specification. The applicant claims this is not a ground for opposition. Who is correct?
    • A) Opponent is correct; it's ground S. 25(1)(h).
    • B) Applicant is correct; this is only a ground for pre-grant representation, not formal opposition.
    • C) Opponent is correct; it's ground S. 25(2)(h).
    • D) Applicant is correct; this ground was omitted in 2005.
    • Answer: A (Trick: S. 25(1)(h) is a specific pre-grant opposition ground. The trick is confusing it with post-grant grounds (which don't include this) or thinking it's only a representation ground).

III. TRICKY USE CASES WITH ANSWERS

  1. Use Case: Dr. C invents a new drug formulation. She discloses it confidentially to her PhD student, D, for research purposes. D, without C's knowledge or consent, files a patent application naming only himself as inventor. C discovers this after the application is published but before grant. She files a pre-grant opposition under S. 25(1)(a) (obtaining) and S. 25(1)(i) (applicant not entitled).
    • Question: What remedies can C seek? Can the patent be sealed to her?
    • Answer: C can seek revocation of the application (if opposition succeeds) or, under S. 26, request the Controller to treat the patent (if granted) as her patent or share it, if the Controller is satisfied D "obtained" the invention from her. The ground S. 25(1)(i) (applicant not entitled) also applies directly. Trick: S. 26 applies even pre-grant if the Controller is convinced "obtaining" occurred, potentially leading to sealing/sharing upon grant. C has multiple grounds.
  2. Use Case: Company E files a patent application. Employee F, who worked on the project but whose contribution was non-inventive (routine testing), is named as co-inventor. The patent is granted. G, a competitor, files post-grant opposition arguing F is not a true inventor, violating S. 28.
    • Question: Will this opposition succeed? What is the consequence?
    • Answer: The opposition on the ground of violation of S. 28 will fail. S. 28(2) explicitly states failure to mention the inventor does not invalidate the patent. The naming of a non-inventor (or omission of a true inventor) is a correctable defect under S. 28(3), not a ground for opposition or revocation. G cannot use this to invalidate the patent. Trick: S. 28 is a formal requirement; non-compliance doesn't affect validity. Opposition grounds under S. 25 do not include "wrong inventor named".
  3. Use Case: H invents a machine. He assigns his rights to Company I. Company I files a patent application naming H as inventor. The patent is granted. J, H's former business partner who claims H stole the idea from him during their partnership, files post-grant opposition under S. 25(2)(a) (obtaining) and S. 25(2)(k) (patent granted to person not entitled). J provides only circumstantial evidence of H's access to his ideas but no direct proof of derivation or theft.
    • Question: Can J succeed? What is the critical hurdle?
    • Answer: J faces a high hurdle. To succeed under S. 25(2)(a) (obtaining), J must prove the invention (or rights) were obtained from him (or someone under whom he claims). Mere access or suspicion is insufficient. S. 25(2)(k) is generally not a standalone post-grant ground unless it means the invention was derived from the opponent (J). J needs strong evidence of "obtaining" (e.g., confidential disclosure, misappropriation). Trick: "Obtaining" requires proof of acquisition from the opponent, not just that the applicant isn't the true inventor. S. 25(2)(k) is limited in post-grant.

IV. TRICKY SCENARIOS WITH ANSWERS

  1. Scenario: An opposition is filed after the patent is granted but before the 1-year deadline expires. The opponent uses the ground "the invention was anticipated by prior public use in India (S. 25(2)(b))". The patentee argues this ground is only available for pre-grant opposition.
    • Question: Is the patentee correct? Explain.
    • Answer: No. The patentee is incorrect. Anticipation by prior public use (S. 25(2)(b)) is a valid ground for post-grant opposition. The core novelty/anticipation grounds (S. 25(1)(b) & (c) for pre-grant; S. 25(2)(b) & (c) for post-grant) are available in both proceedings. The patentee is likely confusing the procedural/eligibility grounds (like S. 25(1)(g), (h), (i), (j), (k)) which are generally excluded from post-grant opposition. Trick: Distinguishing between core substantive grounds (novelty, obviousness, etc.) available in both vs. procedural/eligibility grounds restricted to pre-grant.
  2. Scenario: During post-grant opposition, the opponent proves that the patentee, K, obtained the invention from the opponent, L, through fraudulent misrepresentation. The Controller is fully convinced. The patentee K argues that since L is the true inventor, the patent should simply be revoked, not sealed to L, as revocation is the standard remedy.
    • Question: Can the Controller order the patent to be sealed to L? What is the legal basis?
    • Answer: Yes. The Controller can order the patent to be sealed to L (or shared) under Section 26. S. 26 provides a specific discretionary remedy within opposition proceedings when "obtaining" is proven. It offers an alternative to revocation (S. 64). The Controller is not bound to revoke; they can choose to protect the true owner's (L's) rights by sealing the patent to them. K's argument that revocation is the only remedy is incorrect. Trick: S. 26 is a powerful, often overlooked, alternative to revocation specifically for "obtaining" cases within opposition.
  3. Scenario: A patent is granted naming M and N as joint inventors. O, who worked in the same lab and performed routine experiments under M's direction but contributed no inventive concept, files an application under S. 28(3) claiming he should be named as a joint inventor.
    • Question: What is the likely outcome? What is the key legal principle?
    • Answer: The likely outcome is that O's application will fail. The key legal principle is that an "inventor" under the Patents Act is someone who has contributed to the conception of the invention (the inventive idea). Performing routine experiments, testing, or reducing the invention to practice under someone else's direction, without contributing to the core inventive concept, does not make one an inventor. The Controller will require proof of O's inventive contribution. Trick: Distinguishing between inventorship (conception) and contribution to development/experimentation. S. 28(3) requires proof of being a true inventor.

V. RELATED QUESTIONS FROM PATENT AGENT EXAM (1970 ACT ERA - RECONSTRUCTED)

(Note: Exact papers from 1970 are scarce. These are reconstructed based on the legal provisions in force then (primarily the 1970 Act before major amendments) and common exam patterns of that era, focusing on core principles that remain relevant.)

  1. Question (1970s Style): "State the grounds on which a patent may be opposed after the grant thereof under the provisions of the Indian Patents Act, 1970. Distinguish these grounds from those available for opposition before grant."
    • Focus: Testing knowledge of pre-2005 opposition grounds (which were only post-grant) vs. the later introduced pre-grant opposition. Under the 1970 Act (as initially enacted), opposition was only possible after grant (S. 25 of 1970 Act). The grounds were similar to the current S. 25(2) grounds (novelty, obviousness, not invention, insufficient, obtaining, non-patentable subject matter, false suggestion). The question would highlight the absence of a pre-grant opposition mechanism at that time.
    • Answer (1970s Context): Under the Patents Act, 1970, opposition is only permissible after the grant of the patent (Section 25). The grounds are: (i) that the invention, so far as claimed in any claim of the complete specification, was anticipated by prior publication or prior public knowledge/user in India; (ii) that the invention, so far as claimed, is obvious and does not involve any inventive step; (iii) that the subject of any claim is not an invention within the meaning of the Act; (iv) that the complete specification does not sufficiently describe the invention or the method by which it is to be performed; (v) that the applicant obtained the invention from the opponent or a person under whom the opponent claims; (vi) that the subject of any claim is not patentable under the Act. There is no provision for opposition before grant under the 1970 Act.
  2. Question (1970s Style): "In an opposition proceeding after grant, the opponent alleges that the patentee obtained the invention from him. Explain the powers of the Controller in such a case and the distinction between this remedy and revocation of the patent."
    • Focus: Testing understanding of S. 26 (then S. 26 of 1970 Act, similar to current S. 26) as an alternative to revocation.
    • Answer (1970s Context): If the Controller is satisfied, on opposition, that the patent was obtained from the opponent (or someone under whom the opponent claims), the Controller may (it is discretionary) either: (a) order that the patent shall be sealed to the opponent, or as the opponent may direct; or (b) order that the patent shall be shared between the applicant and the opponent (Section 26). This is a specific remedy distinct from revocation. Revocation (under Section 26 or Section 56 of the 1970 Act) would cancel the patent entirely. Section 26 allows the true owner (the opponent) to be recognized as the patentee or co-patentee, protecting their rights without destroying the patent monopoly. The Controller chooses the remedy based on the facts and justice of the case.
  3. Question (1970s Style): "A patent is granted naming 'A' as the sole inventor. 'B' claims he is the true inventor and that 'A' fraudulently filed the application. 'B' did not oppose the patent before grant. What recourse does 'B' have now? Can he have the patent revoked solely on the ground that he is the true inventor?"
    • Focus: Testing the distinction between inventorship (S. 28) and entitlement/obtaining (S. 25/S. 26/S. 64), and the time limits for opposition.
    • Answer (1970s Context): 'B's primary recourse is to apply to the Controller under Section 28(3) to have his name included as the inventor and 'A's name removed. However, this does not affect the validity of the patent (Section 28(2)). To challenge the patent's validity based on 'A' not being entitled (because 'B' is the true inventor and 'A' obtained it wrongfully), 'B' would have needed to file a post-grant opposition within the prescribed time limit (then 4 months from date of advertisement of acceptance, extendable by 1 month - S. 25(3) of 1970 Act). Since 'B' did not oppose within this time, he cannot now have the patent revoked solely on the ground that he is the true inventor and 'A' is not entitled. He might potentially seek revocation on other grounds (like novelty/obviousness) if they exist and are within the time limit for a revocation petition (then 1 year from grant - S. 26(1) of 1970 Act), or pursue civil remedies for breach of confidence/passing off, but the specific ground of wrongful obtaining/entitlement is time-barred for revocation via opposition/petition. Trick: Highlighting the time-bar for opposition/revocation petitions based on entitlement/obtaining and the limited remedy under S. 28(3) which doesn't invalidate the patent.

Key Takeaways for Exam:

  • Master the Differences: Pre-grant vs. Post-grant (Who, When, Grounds, Procedure).
  • Section 26 is Crucial: Understand "obtaining", who can use it, and the Controller's discretion to seal/share instead of revoke.
  • Section 28 is Formal: Naming inventor ≠ Validity. Know the correction mechanism (S. 28(3)).
  • Section 27 is Omitted: Don't get tricked.
  • Historical Context: For older questions, recall that pre-grant opposition didn't exist initially (only post-grant), and time limits/grounds differed slightly.
  • "Trick" Focus: Watch for confusion between pre/post grounds, the discretionary nature of S. 26, the non-viability of S. 28, and the precise meaning of "obtaining" vs. mere lack of entitlement.

 

 

 

 

 

 

Exam Notes: Chapter V - Opposition Proceedings

Section 25: Opposition to the patent

  • Two Types:
    1. Pre-grant Opposition (S25(1)): Anyone can file. There is no fee. Can be filed anytime after publication but before the grant of the patent.
    2. Post-grant Opposition (S25(2)): Only a "person interested" can file. Must be filed within 12 months from the date of publication of the grant. A fee is required.
  • Grounds for Opposition (Common to both, with slight variation):
    1. Wrongful obtaining (e.g., theft from the true inventor).
    2. Anticipation (prior publication, prior public knowledge/use, prior claiming in another patent).
    3. Obviousness/lack of inventive step.
    4. Not an invention (non-patentable subject matter under S.3 & S.4).
    5. Insufficient disclosure of the invention (specification not clear/complete).
    6. Non-disclosure of foreign application information or wrong mentioning of source/geographical origin of biological material.
  • Key Difference: Pre-grant is open to the public; Post-grant is a more formal inter partes (between parties) proceeding restricted to "persons interested".

Section 26: In cases of "obtaining" Controller may treat the patent as the patent of opponent

  • This is a specific remedy for a specific ground: Wrongful Obtaining.
  • If the opposition is filed on the ground that the applicant obtained the invention from the opponent (the true inventor), and the Controller finds this claim valid, he has the power to direct that the patent be granted to the opponent instead of revoking it.
  • The application is effectively diverted to the true inventor. This is an exception to the general rule where successful opposition leads to refusal/revocation.

Section 27: [Omitted]

  • This section was omitted by the 2005 amendment. It previously dealt with the opposition to the grant of a patent based on an application filed before the 1970 Act came into force. It is no longer relevant for current examinations.

Section 28: Mention of inventor as such in patent

  • Ensures that the true inventor is named in the patent, even if the application is filed by someone else (e.g., an assignee or employer).
  • The inventor can request to be mentioned in the patent if the applicant is not the inventor.
  • The applicant has a duty to inform the Controller of the name of the true inventor.
  • A failure to correctly name the inventor is not a ground for invalidating the patent, but it is a procedural requirement with potential penalties.

Exam Notes: Opposition Proceedings to Grant of Patents

This chapter outlines the process for challenging the grant of a patent. It provides mechanisms for interested parties to oppose a patent application, ensuring that the patent meets the criteria of patentability and has been properly filed.

Section 25: Opposition to the Patent

This section deals with the pre-grant and post-grant opposition of a patent.

  • Pre-Grant Opposition (Section 25(1)):
    • Any person can file a written opposition to the Controller of Patents after the patent application is published but before the patent is granted.
    • The opposition can be based on any of the grounds mentioned in Section 25(1)(a) to (h).
    • These grounds include wrongful obtaining of the invention, prior publication, prior public use, lack of inventive step, lack of novelty, non-patentable subject matter, and insufficient description.
    • The Controller provides an opportunity for the applicant to be heard and considers the opposition before deciding on the patent grant.
  • Post-Grant Opposition (Section 25(2)):
    • Any person interested can file an opposition to the Controller within 12 months from the date of the patent's grant.
    • The grounds for post-grant opposition are similar to pre-grant opposition, and are specified in Section 25(2)(a) to (k).
    • A post-grant opposition board is constituted to examine the matter, comprising three members. One member is the Chairman and all members are from the Patent Office.
    • The opposition board submits its report to the Controller, who then hears both parties and decides whether to maintain, amend, or revoke the patent.
  • Key difference: A pre-grant opposition can be filed by any person, whereas a post-grant opposition must be filed by a person interested. A "person interested" is someone engaged in or preparing to be engaged in the same field of invention.

Section 26: "Obtaining" and Treatment of the Patent

This section applies when a patent is challenged on the ground that the applicant wrongfully obtained the invention from the opponent.

  • If the Controller finds that the applicant did indeed obtain the invention from the opponent, they can, on the opponent's request, treat the patent as if it were granted to the opponent.
  • The Controller may also treat the patent as a joint patent if the invention was jointly conceived by the applicant and the opponent.
  • The Controller has the discretion to make any necessary changes to the patent application or grant, including altering the date of the patent.

Section 28: Mention of Inventor

  • This section allows the true inventor to be named in the patent.
  • An application to be mentioned as the inventor can be made by the inventor, the applicant, or the patentee.
  • The Controller, after hearing all concerned parties, can direct that the inventor's name be mentioned in the patent.
  • This is a right of the inventor and does not affect the ownership of the patent.

Scenario 1: The 'Person Interested' Trap

The opponent would have to show that a skilled person, faced with the problem the patent solves, would have been motivated to combine the features from documents A, B, and C to arrive at the patented invention. SECTION 25 - OPPOSITION TO THE PATENT

Key Legal Provisions

Section 25(1) - Pre-Grant Opposition:

  • Any person may oppose the grant of patent on grounds specified in Section 25(1)
  • Opposition must be filed after publication of application under Section 11A but before grant
  • Time limit: Usually within 6 months from publication date

Section 25(2) - Post-Grant Opposition:

  • Any person interested may oppose patent grant after it has been granted
  • Must be filed within 12 months from date of publication of grant
  • "Person interested" includes competitors, prior inventors, licensees

Grounds for Opposition under Section 25(1) - Pre-Grant:

(a) Wrongful obtaining: Invention obtained wrongfully from opponent or person under whom opponent claims (b) Prior publication: Invention published before priority date in any document (c) Prior knowledge/use: Invention publicly known or used in India before priority date (d) Obviousness: Invention obvious and lacks inventive step (e) Non-patentability: Subject matter not patentable under the Act (f) Insufficient description: Specification doesn't sufficiently describe invention (g) Non-disclosure of foreign applications: Applicant failed to disclose foreign filing information (h) Anticipation by prior claim: Invention anticipated by matter published in any specification filed in India

Grounds for Opposition under Section 25(2) - Post-Grant:

(a) to (h) - Same as pre-grant grounds (i) Prior secret use: Patentee or predecessor wrongfully obtained invention or has used it secretly before priority date (j) Prejudicial to public interest: Patent or manner of exploitation is mischievous to the state or generally prejudicial to public (k) No invention: Subject matter is not an invention within the meaning of this Act

Procedure and Timeline

Pre-Grant Opposition:

  • Filed with Assistant Controller
  • Counter-statement by applicant within 2 months
  • Evidence and hearing if required
  • Decision by Controller

Post-Grant Opposition:

  • Filed with Assistant Controller having jurisdiction
  • Notice to patentee within one month
  • Counter-statement by patentee within 2 months
  • Evidence phase and oral hearing
  • Final decision with detailed reasoning

SECTION 26 - TREATING PATENT AS OPPONENT'S PATENT

Legal Framework

When Controller finds that patent has been "wrongfully obtained" from opponent under Section 25(1)(a):

Powers of Controller:

  • Treat patent as if originally granted to opponent
  • Direct necessary amendments to register
  • Transfer all rights and interests to opponent
  • Make consequential orders

Conditions for Application:

  • Opponent must prove wrongful obtaining
  • Must be established during opposition proceedings
  • Clear evidence of original inventorship required

Effect of Transfer:

  • Patent deemed granted to opponent from original date
  • All intermediate transactions may become void
  • Priority and term remain unchanged
  • Opponent gets all rights as if original patentee

SECTION 28 - MENTION OF INVENTOR IN PATENT

Right to be Named

Fundamental Right:

  • Actual inventor has right to be mentioned as inventor in patent
  • Right exists regardless of who files application
  • Cannot be waived or assigned

Who Can Claim:

  • Actual inventor(s)
  • Legal representatives of deceased inventor
  • Joint inventors in case of joint invention

Procedure:

  • Application can be made at any time during patent term
  • Must be supported by evidence of inventorship
  • Controller may require verification/proof
  • After satisfaction, Controller orders amendment

Effect of Mentioning:

  • Does not affect patent ownership
  • Only recognition right, not ownership right
  • Important for inventor's moral rights
  • Cannot be used to claim monetary benefits

This section provides for pre-grant and post-grant opposition mechanisms.

A. Pre-Grant Opposition [Section 25(1)]

  • Can be filed by any person at any time after publication but before grant.
  • Filed under Rule 55 of the Patents Rules, 2003.
  • Must be filed in Form 7 along with evidence (Form 7A).
  • Grounds for opposition are listed under Section 25(1)(a) to (k):
    • (a) Non-patentable subject matter (Sec 3 & 4)
    • (b) Anticipation by prior knowledge or publication
    • (c) Public knowledge or use in India
    • (d) Invention obvious / lacks inventive step
    • (e) Insufficient disclosure
    • (f) Wrongful obtaining (e.g., derived from another)
    • (g) No new invention (if part of known art)
    • (h) Not useful
    • (i) Invention not disclosed clearly
    • (j) Post-dated claims (claims added after priority date)
    • (k) Concealment or wrong mention of source of biological material

📌 Key Points:

  • No formal hearing unless required.
  • Opponent does not become a party to prosecution.
  • Patent office considers representations while examining the application.
  • Cannot appeal pre-grant opposition decision; only writ petition possible.

B. Post-Grant Opposition [Section 25(2)]

  • Can be filed within 12 months from grant date.
  • Only by an interested person (not "any person").
  • Filed in Form 7 + fee + statement of grounds + evidence.
  • Heard by Opposition Board (Controller + 2 examiners).
  • Decision can be appealed to IPAB (now High Court) under Section 117A.

Grounds under Section 25(2)(a) to (l) — similar to (1), plus:

  • (l) Failure to disclose information regarding foreign applications (Section 8)

📌 Key Points:

  • Statutory proceeding.
  • Both parties heard.
  • Can result in revocation or amendment of patent.
  • Time-bound: must be filed within 12 months.

🔹 SECTION 26: IN CASES OF "OBTAINING", CONTROLLER MAY TREAT PATENT AS THAT OF OPPONENT

  • Applies only in post-grant opposition.
  • If the Controller finds that the invention was wrongfully obtained from the opponent (e.g., stolen idea, breach of confidence), then:
    • The patent may be transferred to the name of the opponent.
    • The original applicant is deemed to have no right to the patent.

📌 Conditions:

  • Must be proven that the invention was derived without consent.
  • Requires strong evidence (documents, emails, lab records, etc.).
  • Rarely applied; high burden of proof.

📌 Example: A researcher shares her unpublished work with a company scientist, who files a patent. She opposes and proves derivation → Controller may transfer patent to her.

🔹 SECTION 28: MENTION OF INVENTOR AS SUCH IN PATENT

  • Every patent must bear the name of the true and first inventor(s).
  • Inventor ≠ applicant (can be different, e.g., employer applies).
  • Failure to mention inventor does not invalidate the patent.
  • But the inventor has the right to be mentioned.
  • Request for correction can be made to Controller under Section 28(3) via Form 13.

📌 Key Points:

  • Omission or incorrect mention can be rectified.
  • Does not affect validity unless fraud involved.
  • Important for moral rights (especially in academic/employee inventions).

Chapter V of the Indian Patents Act, 1970 (as amended) deals with mechanisms to challenge the grant of patents through opposition, ensuring that only valid inventions are patented. It provides safeguards against invalid patents by allowing third parties to oppose applications or grants. Below are concise notes on the specified sections, based on the latest amended provisions (major amendments in 2002 and 2005 introduced pre-grant and post-grant oppositions).

Section 25: Opposition to the Patent

  • Overview: This section outlines pre-grant and post-grant opposition procedures. It allows challenges to patent applications or granted patents on specific grounds to prevent the grant or revoke invalid patents.
  • Pre-Grant Opposition (Sub-section 1):
    • Any person (not necessarily "interested") can file a written representation opposing the grant after the application is published under Section 11A but before the patent is granted.
    • Grounds: 11 specific grounds, including wrongful obtaining (clause a), prior publication (b), prior claiming (c), public knowledge/use in India (d), lack of inventive step (e), not an invention/not patentable (f), insufficient description (g), failure to disclose information under Section 8 (h), delay in convention application (i), wrong mention of biological material source (j), and anticipation by local/indigenous knowledge (k).
    • Procedure: The Controller must consider the opposition if the opponent requests a hearing. No time limit for filing is specified beyond "before grant," but in practice, it's after publication.
    • Explanation: For process patents, importation of product before priority date counts as public use unless for trial/experiment.
  • Post-Grant Opposition (Sub-section 2):
    • Any "person interested" (e.g., someone with commercial stake) can file notice of opposition within 1 year from the date of publication of grant.
    • Grounds: Same 11 grounds as pre-grant.
    • Procedure: Filed in prescribed manner (Form 7A, with evidence); Controller notifies patentee, hears both parties, and decides. Can lead to revocation, amendment, or dismissal.
  • Key Points: Opposition is not adversarial like court; Controller acts quasi-judicially. No appeal from pre-grant decision, but post-grant decisions appealable to IPAB (now High Court post-2021 Tribunal Reforms). Amendment: 2005 introduced post-grant within 1 year; pre-grant made "representation" to make it more accessible.
  • Importance for Exam: Focus on differences between pre/post-grant (who can file, timeline, grounds). Grounds must be memorized exactly; common trick questions on who is "any person" vs. "person interested."

Section 26: In Cases of "Obtaining" Controller May Treat the Patent as the Patent of Opponent

  • Overview: Addresses "wrongful obtaining" (ground under Section 25(1)(a) or 25(2)(a)), where the applicant stole the invention from the opponent.
  • Procedure:
    • If opposition succeeds on this ground, Controller can refuse the application (pre-grant) or revoke the patent (post-grant).
    • On opponent's request (prescribed manner), Controller may direct the application to proceed in opponent's name, with the original filing date.
    • If only part of invention was obtained, specification amended to exclude that part; opponent can file new application for the excluded part within prescribed time.
  • Key Points: Opponent must prove obtaining; not applicable if opponent is co-inventor. Request must be made before final decision. No amendment notes; section unchanged since 1970.
  • Importance for Exam: Link to Form 6 (request to proceed as applicant). Tricky on partial obtaining and new application timeline (3 months from decision).

Section 27: [Omitted]

  • Overview: This section was omitted by the Patents (Amendment) Act, 2002.
    • Original Provision (pre-2002): Allowed Controller to refuse patent without opposition if invention was obviously contrary to law or claimed in prior specification.
    • Reason for Omission: Redundant with expanded opposition grounds under Section 25 and examination under Section 12-13.
  • Key Points: No current applicability; exam questions may test if you know it's omitted and why (to streamline process).
  • Importance for Exam: Simple recall; tricks on confusing with old provisions or assuming it exists.

Section 28: Mention of Inventor as Such in Patent

  • Overview: Allows a person claiming to be the true inventor (or co-inventor) to request their name be mentioned as inventor in the patent, without affecting ownership.
  • Procedure:
    • Application (Form 8) to Controller within 6 months from grant (extendable).
    • If during pendency, can be requested before grant.
    • Controller hears applicant/patentee if opposed; can require proof.
    • If patentee agrees, simple endorsement; else, decision after hearing.
    • Not applicable if inventor already named or if claim is to ownership (that's under Section 26 or revocation).
  • Key Points: Does not change title/rights; purely honorary. Appealable to High Court. Amendment: Minor clarifications in rules.
  • Importance for Exam: Distinguish from assignment (Section 68) or wrongful obtaining (Section 26). Tricks on timeline and who can apply.

General Notes for Chapter V:

  • Rules: Governed by Patents Rules, 2003 (amended); e.g., Rule 55 for pre-grant, Rule 55A for post-grant.
  • Timeline: Pre-grant flexible; post-grant strict 1-year.
  • Evidence: Statements, affidavits; no cross-examination unless hearing.
  • Outcome: Refusal/revocation/amendment/dismissal.
  • Study Tip: Memorize grounds (a-k); compare with revocation grounds (Section 64, broader).

Tricky Use Cases with Answers

  1. Use Case: A pharmaceutical company files a patent for a drug process. A NGO discovers prior publication in a foreign journal but after Indian priority date. Can they file pre-grant opposition? Answer: Yes, under Section 25(1)(b)(ii), if the publication anticipates; proviso excludes if not anticipation per Section 29. Trick: Publication anywhere, but must be before priority; NGO is "any person."
  2. Use Case: Patent granted for a machine. A competitor finds the inventor stole part from their employee 6 months after grant. What action? Answer: File post-grant opposition under Section 25(2)(a) within 1 year; if successful, per Section 26, request to amend/exclude part or transfer. Trick: Partial obtaining allows split; timeline strict, "person interested" needed.
  3. Use Case: An individual claims to be co-inventor of a granted patent but was not named. Patentee opposes. Timeline? Answer: File under Section 28 within 6 months of grant; Controller hears both, decides. Trick: Extendable, but not ownership claim; if during pendency, no time limit.
  4. Use Case: Opposition on ground (d) for public use; product imported for sale before priority but labeled "experimental." Answer: Deemed public use per Explanation to Section 25(1)(d), unless proven solely for trial. Trick: Importation equates to use; burden on opponent to show commercial.
  5. Use Case: Pre-grant opposition dismissed; applicant amends claims voluntarily. Can same opponent re-oppose post-grant? Answer: Yes, if "person interested," on same/new grounds within 1 year of grant. Trick: Pre-grant not res judicata for post-grant; different stages.

Tricky Scenarios with Answers

  1. Scenario: Controller finds wrongful obtaining in pre-grant opposition but opponent doesn't request transfer. Outcome? Answer: Application refused; no automatic transfer per Section 26 (request mandatory). Trick: Opponent loses chance; must file new application separately.
  2. Scenario: Post-grant opposition filed 13 months after grant publication on ground (e) lack of inventive step, with new evidence. Answer: Invalid; strict 1-year limit under Section 25(2); use revocation under Section 64 instead (no time limit). Trick: Opposition vs. revocation; Section 64 broader but court/IPAB.
  3. Scenario: Inventor A assigns to B; C claims A obtained from him. Successful opposition. Answer: Per Section 26, Controller can direct proceed in C's name; B's assignment invalid for obtained part. Trick: Assignment doesn't protect against obtaining; chain of title broken.
  4. Scenario: Patent mentions wrong geographical origin of plant material (ground j). Opposition succeeds partially. Answer: Amendment to correct or revoke claim; not full revocation unless core. Trick: Links to Biodiversity Act; ground added 2002 for TK protection.
  5. Scenario: Under Section 28, request to add inventor after 7 months; good cause shown for delay. Answer: Allowed if extension granted; Controller discretion. Trick: 6-month base, but rules allow extension; hearing if opposed.

Section 25: Opposition to Patent

  • Two types: Pre-grant opposition (anyone before grant, after publication) and Post-grant opposition (any interested person within 1 year after grant publication).
  • Grounds include wrongful obtainment, anticipation by prior publication, incomplete disclosure, lack of inventive step, non-patentability, and insufficient description.
  • Controller considers evidence, forms Opposition Board (post-grant), and can maintain, amend, or revoke the patent.
  • Appeals are possible after Controller's decision.

Section 26: Patent Treatable as Opponent’s

  • If Controller finds invention was obtained from opponent, patent may be treated as opponent’s (full or part of invention), subject to opponent’s request and satisfaction of facts.

Section 28: Mention of Inventor

  • Controller ensures real inventor is mentioned in patent and register, based on requests or claims before grant.
  • Inventorship requests can be made by applicant or third party; objections to inventorship are reviewed with possible rectification by Controller.

1) Exam Notes — Chapter V (Sections 25–28)

Section 25 — Opposition to the patent

Two windows & who can file

  • Pre‑grant opposition (§25(1)): After publication and before grant; “any person” may file a written representation. Form: 7A (Patents Rules, Rule 55).ipindia+1
  • Post‑grant opposition (§25(2)): Within 12 months from the date of publication of grant; “any person interested” may file a notice of opposition. Form: 7 (Rule 55A).ipindia+1

Grounds (both pre‑ and post‑grant: mirror list a–k)

  • (a) Wrongful obtaining; (b) prior publication (with §29(2)/(3) exceptions); (c) prior claiming; (d) publicly known/used in India (import of product of claimed process counts, barring reasonable trial); (e) obviousness/no inventive step; (f) not an invention / not patentable; (g) insufficient disclosure; (h) §8 non‑disclosure/false info; (i) convention application not within 12 months; (j) no/false disclosure of source or geographical origin of biological material; (k) anticipated by traditional knowledge (anywhere).ipindia

Procedure (key exam points)

  • Pre‑grant (Rule 55): file Form 7A + statement + evidence + (optionally) hearing request; Controller considers only after RFE/Rx (Request for Examination) is filed; if prima facie case—notice to applicant; applicant has 2 months to file statement/evidence; speaking order ordinarily within 1 month of completion; no grant before 6 months from publication.ipindia
  • Post‑grant (§25(3)): Controller constitutes an Opposition Board (officers as determined) → Board examines & gives recommendations → after hearing both sides, Controller may maintain, amend, or revoke the patent (§25(4)); while deciding on grounds (d)/(e), Controller ignores personal documents/secret trials/secret use (§25(5)).ipindia

Essentials to memorize

  • Forms: Pre‑grant 7A | Post‑grant 7.ipindia
  • Time: Pre‑grant—any time post‑publication till grant; Post‑grant—within 12 months from publication of grant.ipindia
  • Who: “Any person” (pre‑grant) vs “Any person interested” (post‑grant).ipindia

Section 26 — “Obtaining” : Controller may treat patent as opponent’s

  • If in opposition the Controller finds the invention (as claimed) was obtained from the opponent (per §25 ground) and revokes on that ground, on the opponent’s request, Controller may direct the patent to stand in the opponent’s name (§26(1)(a)). If only part was obtained, he may order excision of that part (§26(1)(b)).ipindia
  • If the opponent had already filed his own application including the obtained matter, the Controller may treat that later application (for priority purposes for those claims) as filed on the earlier application’s date—while otherwise proceeding as a normal application (§26(2)).ipindia

Section 27 — [Omitted]

  • Earlier titled “Refusal to patent without opposition”, omitted by the Patents (Amendment) Act, 2005. Expect conceptual questions only.indiankanoon+1

Section 28 — Mention of inventor as such

  • On request/claim (by applicant alone, or applicant + inventor), Controller shall cause the inventor to be mentioned in the patent/specification/register if (a) the claimant is the inventor (or substantial part) and (b) the application is a direct consequence of his being inventor (§28(1)–(2)). Must be before grant (§28(4)).ipindia
  • No proprietary rights flow merely from being mentioned (§28 proviso). Disputes/questions of wrong mention can be rectified later—certificate & rectification (§28(7)).ipindia

Chapter V: Opposition proceedings to grant of patents

Section 25: Opposition to the patent

  • Section 25 covers both pre-grant and post-grant opposition to a patent application.
    • Pre-grant Opposition (Section 25(1)): Can be filed by any person after publication and before grant using Form 7A. Grounds include wrongful obtaining, prior publication/claiming, public knowledge or use in India, obviousness, non-patentable subject matter, insufficient disclosure, failure to disclose information (Section 8), convention application issues, geographical origin of biological material, and anticipation by traditional knowledge.
    • Post-grant Opposition (Section 25(2)): Can be filed by any person interested within 12 months of the grant's publication using Form 7. The grounds are the same as pre-grant opposition. An Opposition Board is formed to examine the case and make recommendations to the Controller, who then hears both parties and decides whether to maintain, amend, or revoke the patent.
  • Key Differences: Pre-grant can be filed by "any person" with no fee after publication but before grant, while post-grant is by a "person interested" within 12 months of grant publication, requires a fee, and involves an Opposition Board.

Section 26: In cases of "obtaining" Controller may treat the patent as the patent of opponent

  • If, during opposition under Section 25, the Controller finds the invention was wrongfully obtained from the opponent:
    • If the entire invention was obtained, the Controller can, on the opponent's request, amend the patent in the opponent's name.
    • If only a part was obtained, the Controller can order the exclusion of that part from the specification.
  • If the opponent had a pending application for the same invention before the Controller's order, it can be treated as filed on the same date as the original application for priority.

Section 27: [Omitted]

  • This section has been omitted from the Indian Patents Act, 1970.

Section 28: Mention of inventor as such in patent

  • This section allows the inventor to be named in the patent specification and register.
  • The Controller must be satisfied that the person is the inventor of the whole or a substantial part and that the application stems from their inventorship.
  • Requests can be made by the applicant or jointly by the applicant and inventor, but must be made before the patent is granted.
  • Mentioning an inventor does not alter any rights under the patent.
  • A person incorrectly mentioned can apply to the Controller for a certificate and rectification of records.

Tricky MCQs

Question 1

A patent has been granted to Applicant X. After six months from the date of publication of the grant, Opponent Y discovers that the invention claimed in the patent was publicly known and used in India before the priority date of Applicant X's claim. Opponent Y files a notice of opposition under Section 25(2) of the Patents Act, 1970. The Controller, after receiving the recommendations of the Opposition Board and hearing both parties, decides to revoke the patent. In this scenario, what is the legal recourse available to Applicant X?

A. Applicant X can appeal the Controller's decision to the Intellectual Property Appellate Board (IPAB).
B. Applicant X can file a fresh patent application for the same invention, provided they can prove the invention was not publicly known or used before the priority date.
C. Applicant X has no further legal recourse and the patent stands revoked.
D. Applicant X can file a writ petition under Article 226 of the Indian Constitution challenging the Controller's decision.

The correct option is A. Applicant X can appeal the Controller's decision to the Intellectual Property Appellate Board (IPAB).

Explanation

  • The Controller's decision in a post-grant opposition, including revocation, is appealable to the IPAB within 3 months.

Why other options are incorrect

  • B: Once revoked, the invention enters the public domain, preventing a fresh application for the same invention.
  • C: An appeal mechanism exists.
  • D: While possible, the primary remedy is an appeal to the IPAB.

Question 2

A post-grant opposition has been filed against a patent, alleging that a part of the invention claimed in the complete specification was wrongfully obtained from the opponent. The Controller finds this ground to be true. Which of the following is the most likely action the Controller will take under Section 26 of the Patents Act, 1970?

A. The Controller will revoke the entire patent.
B. The Controller will direct that the patent be amended in the name of the opponent.
C. The Controller will require that the specification be amended by the exclusion of the obtained part of the invention.
D. The Controller will grant a compulsory license to the opponent to use the obtained part of the invention.

The correct option is C. The Controller will require that the specification be amended by the exclusion of the obtained part of the invention.

Explanation

  • Section 26(1)(b) allows the Controller to order the exclusion of a part of the invention if it was wrongfully obtained.

Why other options are incorrect

  • A & B: These apply when the entire invention was obtained.
  • D: Compulsory licenses are granted under different circumstances, not typically for "obtaining".

Tricky use cases with answers

Use case 1: The forgotten inventor

Scenario: Inventor A develops a crucial component for a new medical device. They share the idea and initial prototypes with their colleague, Inventor B, who then takes the complete idea and files a patent application without including Inventor A as a co-inventor. The patent is subsequently granted. Inventor A, discovering this, wants to ensure their name is recognized.

Question: What actions can Inventor A take under the Patents Act, 1970?

Answer:

  • Opposition under Section 25(2): Inventor A can file a post-grant opposition within 12 months, citing wrongful obtaining.
  • Request under Section 28: While a request can be made before grant to be mentioned as an inventor, post-grant opposition is the more direct route here.
  • Revocation Petition: After the 12-month opposition period, Inventor A could pursue revocation in court based on wrongful obtaining.

Key Takeaway: Wrongful obtaining is a key ground in opposition for resolving inventorship disputes.

Use case 2: Prior publication in a non-patent document

Scenario: Applicant A files a patent application for a new filtering system for wastewater treatment. Prior to the priority date of Applicant A's claim, a detailed description of the same filtering system was published in a university research paper, which was publicly accessible online and in physical copies.

Question: Can this university research paper be a valid ground for opposing the patent under Section 25?

Answer: Yes, this can be a valid ground for opposition.

  • Ground of prior publication: Section 25 allows opposition if the invention was published before the priority date "in India or elsewhere, in any other document". The research paper's public accessibility fulfills this.

Key Takeaway: Prior art for opposition includes non-patent publications like research papers accessible globally before the priority date.

Tricky scenarios with answers

Scenario 1: Pre-grant opposition rejected, can still appeal?

Scenario: A pre-grant opposition was filed against a patent application. The Controller rejected the opposition, finding no merit in the arguments. The patent was subsequently granted.

Question: Can the opponent appeal the Controller's decision to reject the pre-grant opposition?

Answer: This area has some legal complexities.

  • Initially, it was thought that after a rejected pre-grant opposition and subsequent patent grant, the opponent's options were limited to post-grant opposition or revocation.
  • However, court rulings have clarified that appealing the rejection of a pre-grant opposition depends on the Controller's final decision: refusal of the application is appealable to the IPAB, but granting the patent after rejecting the opposition is appealable to the High Court.
  • A writ petition might be considered if no other remedy is available.

Key Takeaway: The appeal route after a rejected pre-grant opposition depends on whether the Controller ultimately refused or granted the patent.

Scenario 2: Omitting an inventor's name

Scenario: An invention was jointly developed by two individuals, X and Y. When the patent application was filed, only X's name was included as the inventor, while Y's name was accidentally omitted.

Question: What are Y's rights and options under the Patents Act, 1970?

Answer:

  • Inventor's Rights: Y has an "inchoate property" right as a true inventor, regardless of being named in the application.
  • Inclusion Request: Y can request to be included as a claimant (Sections 20(1), 20(3(c))) or inventor (Section 28(1)).
  • Separate Application: Y can file their own application, which could lead to proceedings addressing co-inventorship.
  • Request under Section 28(6): Y can apply to the Controller for a certificate stating X should not have been mentioned as the inventor, leading to potential rectification.

Key Takeaway: The Act provides mechanisms to correct the omission of an inventor's name and protect their rights.
