✅ Tricky MCQs

A. Tricky Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs) for Practice

Section 11: Copyright on Registration

  1. The initial period of copyright in a registered design is:
    a) 5 years from the date of application
    b) 10 years from the date of registration
    c) 15 years from the date of priority
    d) 10 years from the date of publication
    Correct: (b) The Act specifically states "ten years from the date of registration."
  2. The extension of copyright under Section 11(2) can be obtained:
    a) Automatically without any application
    b) Upon application and fee payment before the expiry of the initial 10 years
    c) Only after the design has lapsed, within a year
    d) For a further period of 10 years
    Correct: (b) Application must be made before expiration, and extension is for 5 years.

Sections 12-14: Restoration of Lapsed Designs

  1. If a design registration lapses due to non-payment of the extension fee, the proprietor can apply for restoration:
    a) Within 6 months from the date of lapse
    b) At any time, by paying a penalty
    c) Within one year from the date the design ceased to have effect
    d) Only if no third party has used the design in the interim
    Correct: (c) *Section 12(1) specifies the one-year window.*
  2. For restoration under Section 13, the Controller must be satisfied that:
    a) The failure to pay was unintentional and there was no undue delay in applying
    b) The design is commercially successful
    c) No third party has objected
    d) The design is a pioneer in its field
    Correct: (a) These are the key statutory conditions.
  3. After restoration of a lapsed design, what is the legal position regarding acts of infringement committed between the lapse and restoration dates?
    a) The proprietor can sue for damages retrospectively for that period.
    b) No suit or proceeding can be commenced for infringements during that period.
    c) Infringers must pay a compulsory license fee.
    d) Liability arises only if the infringer acted in bad faith.
    Correct: (b) Section 14(2) is a clear bar on legal action for infringements during the "lapse period."

Section 15: Marking & Requirements before Sale

  1. Failure to mark articles with the prescribed registration mark (e.g., 'Rd') has what consequence under Section 15(1)(b)?
    a) Immediate cancellation of the registration.
    b) Inability to recover penalty or damages for infringement, subject to two exceptions.
    c) The design copyright becomes invalid.
    d) It is a criminal offence.
    Correct: (b) The proprietor loses the right to recover damages unless they prove proper steps were taken for marking or the infringer had actual knowledge.
  2. Which of the following is NOT a consequence under Section 15?
    a) The Controller may erase the proprietor's name for not furnishing exact representations.
    b) The Central Government can modify marking requirements for specific industries.
    c) Selling unmarked articles is a criminal breach.
    d) Copyright ceases if the Controller erases the name for non-furnishing of specimens.
    Correct: (c) Selling unmarked articles is not a criminal offence; the penalty is loss of certain civil remedies.

Section 16: Effect of Disclosure on Copyright

  1. Which disclosure, prior to registration, does NOT invalidate the copyright of a design?
    a) Publication in a trade journal.
    b) Display at an international exhibition without protection.
    c) Disclosure in breach of good faith by a person other than the proprietor.
    d) Sale of the article to the public.
    Correct: (c) Section 16 provides exceptions, including disclosure in breach of good faith by a third party.

Section 17-18: Inspection & Information

  1. Who can inspect a registered design during its copyright term?
    a) Only the registered proprietor.
    b) Any person, upon payment of the prescribed fee and enabling identification.
    c) Only a person filing a cancellation petition.
    d) The design is kept secret until copyright expires.
    Correct: (b) Section 17 provides for public inspection upon fulfilling conditions.

Section 19: Cancellation of Registration

  1. Grounds for cancellation of a design's registration include all EXCEPT:
    a) The design is not new or original.
    b) The design is functional, not ornamental.
    c) The design was registered more than 10 years ago.
    d) The design was published in India prior to the date of registration.
    Correct: (c) Age of registration is not a ground. Grounds are listed in S.19(1); (c) corresponds to "not a design" under S.2(d) if it's only functional.
  2. An appeal against an order of the Controller on a cancellation petition lies to:
    a) The Supreme Court
    b) The High Court
    c) The Intellectual Property Appellate Board (IPAB)
    d) The Central Government
    Correct: (b) S.19(2) specifies appeal to the High Court.

Section 20: Designs to bind Government

  1. The provisions of which chapter of the Patents Act, 1970 apply to registered designs concerning the Government?
    a) Chapter XVI (Working of Patents)
    b) Chapter XVII (Use of Inventions for Government)
    c) Chapter XVIII (Suits Concerning Infringement)
    d) Chapter XIX (Appeals)
    Correct: (b) S.20 explicitly mentions Chapter XVII of the Patents Act.

C. Use Cases

  1. Use Case on Restoration & Intervening Rights:

○      Scenario: "Aarvi Designs" registered a design for a chair in 2015. They failed to pay the extension fee due in 2025, and the registration lapsed in January 2026. In June 2026, "Moderna Furnishings" started manufacturing and selling identical chairs. Aarvi realized the lapse and applied for restoration in November 2026, which was granted in December 2026.

○      Issue: Can Aarvi sue Moderna for infringement for sales made in July 2026?

○      Analysis: No. Under Section 14(2), no proceeding can be commenced for infringements committed between the date of lapse (Jan 2026) and the date of restoration (Dec 2026). Moderna's acts fall in this protected period.

  1. Use Case on Marking & Damages (S.15):

○      Scenario: "TechGadgets Ltd." has a registered design for a Bluetooth speaker but does not mark the products with the registration number (Rd. No.). A competitor, "CopyCat Inc.," starts selling a substantially similar speaker. TechGadgets sues for infringement.

○      Issue: Will TechGadgets be entitled to damages?

○      Analysis: Under S.15(1)(b), they will not be entitled to recover penalty or damages unless they can show either (i) they took all proper steps to ensure marking, or (ii) CopyCat knew or had received notice of the copyright before infringing. The burden of proof is on the proprietor.

  1. Use Case on Pre-registration Disclosure (S.16):

○      Scenario: A designer, Riya, showed her new textile design confidentially to a manufacturer, "MegaTextiles," under a Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA) to get a quote. MegaTextiles, in breach of the NDA, displayed the design at a trade fair. Riya immediately filed for design registration.

○      Issue: Will the publication at the trade fair invalidate Riya's subsequent registration?

○      Analysis: Likely not. Section 16 states that disclosure in breach of good faith by a person other than the proprietor does not invalidate copyright if registration is obtained subsequently. MegaTextiles' bad faith disclosure is protected.

D. Comprehensive Case Study

Title: The Curious Case of "Nova" Lighting

Facts:
Nova Designs Pvt. Ltd. registered a novel and ornamental design for a pendant light ("The Nebula") on 1st January 2020. They commercially launched the product in June 2020. Owing to an administrative error, they failed to apply for the extension of copyright before 1st January 2030. The copyright lapsed on 2nd January 2030.

  1. In March 2030, a competitor, "Lumen Copycats," launched an identical pendant light.
  2. On 1st December 2030, Nova Designs realized the lapse and immediately applied for restoration under Sections 12 & 13, stating the failure was unintentional. The Controller, satisfied, restored the design on 15th January 2031.
  3. During the period March 2030 to January 2021, Lumen earned significant profits.
  4. Nova had marked its original products with "Rd. No. XXXXXX" but its new batch produced in November 2030 (after lapse but before restoration) was inadvertently left unmarked.
  5. In February 2031, after restoration, a new player, "Spark Lights," began selling a similar light, arguing that Nova's design was published in an international catalog in December 2019 (before the registration date).

Questions for Analysis:

  1. Can Nova sue Lumen Copycats for infringement for their sales between March and December 2030? Why/Why not? (Refer: S.14)
  2. If Nova sues Spark Lights in March 2031 for infringement, what defense can Spark Lights raise, and what legal recourse is available to Nova Designs? (Refer: S.19)
  3. Assuming Spark Lights' defense fails, and they are found to have infringed in March 2031, would Nova's failure to mark the batch produced in November 2030 affect its claim for damages against Spark Lights? Analyze. (Refer: S.15)
  4. If, during the restoration process in December 2030, the Controller had imposed a condition that Lumen Copycats be allowed to continue selling for 6 months as a "person who had taken definite steps," what would be the legal basis? (Refer: S.14(1))

Answers (Guideline):

  1. No. Under S.14(2), no suit can be commenced for infringement committed between the date of lapse (2nd Jan 2030) and the date of restoration (15th Jan 2031).
  2. Spark Lights can file a cancellation petition under S.19(1)(b) on the ground that the design was published prior to its date of registration (1st Jan 2020). If successful, the registration will be cancelled. Nova would have to defend the novelty of its design.
  3. Potentially, Yes. Under S.15(1)(b), for the unmarked batch, Nova may not recover damages from Spark Lights unless Nova proves either (a) it took all proper steps to ensure marking (which it failed for this batch), or (b) Spark Lights had actual knowledge of the copyright before infringing in March 2031 (the restoration was in Jan 2021, so knowledge is likely provable).
  4. The basis is S.14(1), which allows the Controller to impose provisions for the protection/compensation of persons who began to avail themselves of the design during the lapse period. Granting a short grace period to Lumen is a typical such provision.

II. Answers & Explanations

  1. B – (Section 11: Original 10 years + 5 years extension = 15 years total).
  2. B – (Section 12: One year window for restoration).
  3. C – (Section 15 deals with recovery of damages; it is not a ground for cancellation of the registration itself).
  4. C – (Section 15(1)(b) specifically limits the recovery of penalty or damages).
  5. B – (Section 16 protects the proprietor if the disclosure was a breach of good faith by others).

Scenario: A furniture designer registered a chair design in 2014. In 2024, they forgot to pay the extension fee. Two months after the 10-year mark, they realized the error. V. Case Study: Cancellation of Design

Title: M/s. Steelbird Hi-Tech India Ltd. vs. S.P.S. Gambhir

The Facts: Steelbird registered a design for a helmet. The Respondent (Gambhir) challenged it under Section 19, claiming the design was not "new or original" because similar shapes existed in international catalogs before the Indian registration date.

Legal Issues:

  1. Does "prior publication" in a foreign country affect Indian registration?
  2. What constitutes a "new or original" design?

Court’s Observation (Applying Sections 11 & 19): The court held that under Section 19(1)(b), if a design is published in any other country prior to the date of registration in India, the registration is liable to be cancelled. The court emphasized that for a design to be protected, it must have "novelty"—a mere slight variation of an existing shape is not enough to maintain a valid copyright

PREVIOUS YEARS' EXAMINATION QUESTIONS

Question 1 (Long Answer - 10 Marks)

Discuss the provisions relating to restoration of lapsed designs under The Designs Act, 2000. What are the rights of the proprietor after restoration?

Model Answer: Under Section 12 of The Designs Act, 2000, when a design lapses due to non-payment of extension fee under Section 11(2), it can be restored. The key provisions include:

Eligibility: The proprietor, legal representative, or in case of joint ownership, one or more proprietors with Controller's leave can apply.

Time Limit: Application must be within one year from lapsing date.

Requirements: Verified statement explaining circumstances leading to non-payment, with additional evidence if required by Controller.

Procedure (Section 13): Controller must be satisfied that failure was unintentional with no undue delay. Payment of unpaid extension fee plus additional prescribed fee is required.

Rights After Restoration (Section 14): Rights are subject to protection of third parties who used the design during lapse period. No suit for piracy during lapse period is allowed, protecting innocent users.

Question 2 (Short Answer - 5 Marks)

What are the requirements before delivery on sales under Section 15 of The Designs Act, 2000?

Model Answer: Section 15 mandates two requirements:

  1. Furnishing Representations: If exact representations weren't furnished during application, proprietor must provide prescribed number to Controller. Failure results in name erasure and copyright cessation.
  2. Marking Articles: Each article must bear prescribed mark/words/figures denoting registration. Without marking, proprietor cannot recover penalty/damages unless:

○      Proper steps were taken to ensure marking, OR

○      Infringement occurred after infringer knew of copyright existence

Central Government can modify marking requirements for specific trade/industry classes upon representation showing expediency.

Question 3 (Short Answer - 5 Marks)

Explain the grounds for cancellation of registration under Section 19.

Model Answer: Under Section 19(1), any interested person may petition for cancellation on following grounds:

a) Previously registered in India b) Published in India or any country before registration date c) Not a new or original design d) Not registrable under the Act e) Not a "design" as defined in Section 2(d)

Appeal lies to High Court from Controller's order. Controller may also refer petition to High Court for decision.

Question 4 (Problem/Case-based - 10 Marks)

X registered a design on April 1, 2015. He failed to apply for extension and the design lapsed on April 1, 2025. On May 1, 2025, Y started manufacturing articles with the same design. On March 1, 2026, X applied for restoration which was granted on April 1, 2026. Advise X regarding his rights.

Model Answer: Analysis:

  1. Timely Application: X applied within one year of lapsing (May 1, 2025 to March 1, 2026), satisfying Section 12 requirements.
  2. Rights After Restoration: Under Section 14(1), X's rights are subject to protection of Y who began using during lapse period (May 1, 2025 to April 1, 2026).
  3. Y's Protection: Y may continue use with compensation/protection provisions as prescribed, having taken definite steps during lapse.
  4. No Suit for Lapse Period: Under Section 14(2), X cannot sue Y for piracy committed between April 1, 2025 (lapse) and April 1, 2026 (restoration).
  5. Future Rights: From April 1, 2026 onwards, X has full copyright protection against new infringers.

Advice: X cannot take action against Y for past use but can prevent future unauthorized use by others. Negotiate with Y for compensation/licensing arrangements.

CASE STUDIES

Case Study 1: Lapsed Design Restoration

Facts: ABC Ltd. registered a furniture design on January 15, 2014. Due to administrative oversight, they failed to apply for extension before January 15, 2024. The design lapsed. Meanwhile, XYZ Furniture began manufacturing similar furniture on March 1, 2024. ABC discovered this on June 1, 2024, and immediately applied for restoration, which was granted on August 1, 2024.

Questions:

  1. Was ABC's application timely?
  2. Can ABC sue XYZ for the period March-August 2024?
  3. What are ABC's rights after August 1, 2024?
  4. What protection does XYZ have?

Answers:

  1. Yes, timely: Application within one year of lapsing (Jan 15, 2024 - June 1, 2024 < 1 year).
  2. No suit possible: Section 14(2) bars suits for piracy during lapse period (Jan 15 - Aug 1, 2024).
  3. Full rights resume: From August 1, 2024, ABC has complete copyright protection subject to XYZ's vested rights.
  4. XYZ's protection: Under Section 14(1), XYZ may continue operations with compensation provisions as they began during lapse period in good faith.

Case Study 2: Marking Requirements

Facts: Designer Priya registered a textile design and started selling sarees without marking them as registered. After 6 months, she discovered that Fashion Hub was copying her design. Fashion Hub claims they didn't know about the registration.

Questions:

  1. Can Priya sue Fashion Hub?
  2. What defenses can Priya use despite not marking?
  3. What should Priya have done?

Answers:

  1. Limited rights: Under Section 15(1)(b), without marking, Priya cannot recover penalty/damages unless she proves exceptions.
  2. Available defenses:

○      She took all proper steps to ensure marking, OR

○      Fashion Hub knew or received notice of copyright before infringement

  1. Proper procedure:

○      Mark each article with prescribed mark showing registration

○      If marking impractical, apply to Central Government for exemption under Section 15(2)

○      Document all efforts at marking for future defense

Case Study 3: Confidential Disclosure

Facts: Designer Rohan showed his new smartphone case design to Manufacturer M under confidentiality agreement for production quote. M shared it with Competitor C who registered it first. Rohan later applied for registration.

Questions:

  1. Is Rohan's registration valid despite prior disclosure?
  2. What legal recourse does Rohan have?
  3. What is the relevance of Section 16?

Answers:

  1. Valid registration: Under Section 16, disclosure to M in circumstances contrary to good faith for M doesn't invalidate Rohan's subsequent registration. M's breach of confidence doesn't constitute sufficient "publication."
  2. Legal remedies:

○      Petition under Section 19 to cancel C's registration (ground: published by M in breach of faith)

○      Civil suit against M for breach of confidentiality

○      Claim priority based on original creation date

  1. Section 16 protection: Protects genuine designers from losing rights due to confidential disclosures or breaches of good faith by others.

Case Study 4: Joint Ownership Restoration

Facts: Three partners A, B, and C jointly registered a design. It lapsed due to non-payment. A wants to restore it but B is unreachable and C opposes restoration.

Questions:

  1. Can A apply alone?
  2. What is the procedure?
  3. What happens if restoration is granted?

Answers:

  1. Yes, with leave: Under Section 12(1), one or more joint proprietors can apply with Controller's leave without joining others.
  2. Procedure:

○      Apply for Controller's permission (leave)

○      File restoration application with verified statement

○      Explain circumstances and reasons for B's non-joinder and C's opposition

○      Pay prescribed fees

  1. Effect of restoration:

○      Design is restored for all proprietors

○      All three (A, B, C) regain ownership rights

○      Subject to Section 14 limitations for third party protection
