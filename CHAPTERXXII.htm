Exam Notes: Chapter XXII - International Arrangements (Sections 133-139)

Key Concepts:

  1. Convention Country (Section 133):
    • A country that is a member of the Paris Convention or WTO, or any country notified by the Central Government if it grants Indian citizens reciprocal privileges.
    • Reciprocity: The country must provide Indian citizens the same patent rights as its own citizens.
  2. Non-Reciprocal Countries (Section 134):
    • If a convention country denies reciprocity to Indian citizens, the Central Government may notify that Chapter XXII provisions do not apply to that country.
  3. Convention Applications (Section 135):
    • An application filed in India within 12 months of the first application in a convention country.
    • Priority Date: The date of the first application in the convention country.
  4. Special Provisions (Section 136):
    • Filing Deadline: Must be filed within 12 months of the priority date.
    • Delay Condonation: The Controller may condone delays up to 14 months if "sufficient cause" is shown.
    • Document Submission: A copy of the specification and documents must be filed within 6 months of the Indian filing date.
  5. Multiple Priorities (Section 137):
    • If claiming priority from multiple applications, the earliest priority date applies.
  6. Supplementary Provisions (Section 138):
    • Translation Requirement: Specifications in non-English languages must be translated to English.
    • Power of Attorney: Mandatory if filed by an agent.
  7. General Applicability (Section 139):
    • All provisions of the Patents Act apply to convention applications, except where inconsistent with Chapter XXII.

Exam Notes: Chapter XXII - International Arrangements (Sections 133-139)

This chapter deals with the procedural and substantive aspects of filing patent applications in India claiming priority from a first application filed in a Convention Country, as per the Paris Convention.

Core Concept: The Right of Priority

  • An applicant who first files a patent application in a "Convention country" can within 12 months file a corresponding application in India.
  • The Indian application will be treated as filed on the same date as the first application in the convention country. This crucial date is the "priority date".

Section 133: Convention Countries

  • What it does: Empowers the Central Government to declare any country a "Convention Country".
  • Key Point: The declaration is made via notification in the Official Gazette. It is a prerequisite for an applicant to claim priority from that country.

Section 134: Notification as to countries not providing for reciprocity

  • What it does: Provides a retaliatory measure.
  • Key Point: If a convention country does not grant rights akin to the Indian Act to Indian applicants, the Central Government can withdraw the convention status from that country via notification. Applicants from that country can no longer claim priority in India.

Section 135: Convention Applications

  • What it does: Defines a "convention application".
  • Key Points:
    1. It is an application for a patent filed under the Patents Act.
    2. It is filed by a person who is a citizen/national of, or is domiciled in, a convention country.
    3. It is filed within 12 months from the date of the first application in a convention country.
    4. The application must be accompanied by a priority document (a certified copy of the foreign application) and a verified English translation (if the original is not in English).

Section 136: Special provisions relating to convention application

  • What it does: Specifies the consequences of withdrawing or abandoning the foreign application.
  • Key Point: The withdrawal, abandonment, or refusal of the foreign priority application shall not be used to invalidate the Indian convention application. The Indian application has an independent existence once filed.

Section 137: Multiple priorities

  • What it does: Allows an applicant to combine disclosures from multiple priority applications into a single Indian application.
  • Key Points:
    1. Multiple Priorities: A single Indian application can claim priority from more than one previous foreign application.
    2. Division of Application: A single foreign application can form the basis of priority for more than one Indian application (e.g., by filing divisional applications in India).
    3. The 12-month period is always calculated from the earliest priority date.

Section 138: Supplementary provisions as to convention applications

  • What it does: Deals with the fate of a convention application if the priority application is filed in a non-convention country after the Indian filing.
  • Key Point: If an applicant first files in a non-convention country, and then files in a convention country, and then files in India claiming priority from the convention country application, the Indian application's priority date is the date of filing in the convention country, not the original non-convention country.

Section 139: Other provisions of Act to apply to convention applications

  • What it does: A standard clause stating that all other provisions of the Patents Act apply to convention applications as they do to ordinary applications, unless otherwise specified in this chapter.

Tricky Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

1. Under which section can the Central Government revoke the 'convention country' status of a member of the Paris Convention?
a) Section 133
b) Section 134
c) Section 7
d) Section 139
Answer: b) Section 134. Section 133 is for declaring a country as a convention country. Section 134 is the retaliatory provision for revocation of that status.

She then files another application in Country Y (also a convention country) for an improvement on 01-Apr-2023. a) 01-Jan-2023
b) 01-Apr-2023
c) The date of filing in India
d) A different date for each claim, based on the respective priority
Answer: a) 01-Jan-2023. As per Section 137(2), the priority date for the purpose of calculating the 12-month convention period is the date of the earliest application. The application must be filed in India by 01-Jan-2024.

3. The certified copy of the priority document, if not in English, must be accompanied by a verified English translation. This requirement is stipulated under:
a) Section 138
b) Section 8
c) Section 135
d) The Patent Rules
Answer: d) The Patent Rules. This is a crucial trick. While Section 135 mandates furnishing the priority document, the specific requirement for a verified translation is detailed in Rule 17(1)(b) of the Patent Rules, 2003.

4. If a priority application filed in a convention country is later deemed withdrawn, what is the status of the corresponding convention application filed in India?
a) It is automatically deemed withdrawn.
b) It proceeds unaffected, as if the withdrawal never happened.
c) The applicant must file a new ordinary application.
d) The Controller will refuse it.
Answer: b) It proceeds unaffected, as if the withdrawal never happened. This is the direct effect of Section 136. The Indian application is independent once filed.

Tricky Scenarios & Case Studies

Scenario 1: The Non-Convention Route
Mr. A, a Japanese national (Japan is a convention country), files his first application in India on March 1, 2023. He then files an application in Japan on October 1, 2023. Now, he files another application in India on December 1, 2023, and claims convention priority based on his Japanese application of October 1, 2023.

  • Question: Can he validly claim priority? What is the priority date for his December 1, 2023 Indian application?
  • Answer: No, he cannot validly claim priority. As per the Paris Convention and the spirit of the Act, the first application must be in a convention country. His first application was in India. Therefore, his application of December 1, 2023, cannot claim convention priority. It will be treated as a regular application with its filing date (Dec 1, 2023) as the priority date. The disclosure in his own Indian application filed on March 1, 2023, would likely constitute prior art against his December application.

Scenario 2: The 12-Month Deadline
An applicant files a first application in Germany on November 15, 2022. The 12-month period to file in India expires on November 15, 2023, which is a public holiday. The applicant files the convention application in India on November 16, 2023.

  • Question: Is the application valid? Can it claim the priority date of November 15, 2022?
  • Answer: Yes, the application is valid and the priority date can be claimed. As per Rule 80 of the Patent Rules, 2003, if the last day for doing any act (like filing a convention application) is a day when the Office is closed, the act may be done on the next succeeding day on which the Office is open.

Scenario 3: Multiple Priorities and Added Matter
An applicant files Application A in the US on Jan 10, 2023, disclosing a compound X. She files Application B in the US on July 10, 2023, disclosing a method of using compound X for treating a disease. On Dec 10, 2023, she files a single application in India with claims to "Compound X" and "A method of treating disease using Compound X".

  • Question: Can she claim priority for the entire application? How?
  • Answer: Yes, she can claim multiple priorities. She can claim priority for the claim to "Compound X" from Application A (Jan 10, 2023) and for the claim to the "method of treating..." from Application B (July 10, 2023). The 12-month deadline for filing in India is based on the earliest priority date, i.e., Jan 10, 2023. Filing on Dec 10, 2023, is within 12 months (just barely), so the application is valid.

Past Examination Trends & Related Questions

Questions from this chapter are very common and are often combined with questions on novelty and prior art. Be prepared for questions that mix these concepts.

  1. Direct Definition: "Define a convention application under the Patents Act, 1970." (Directly from Section 135).
  2. Consequences of Missing Deadline: "What is the consequence if a convention application is filed in India after the expiry of 12 months from the date of first filing in a convention country?" (Answer: The right to claim priority is lost. The application may still be filed, but it will be treated as a normal application with its Indian filing date as the priority date. The foreign application will then constitute prior art under Section 29(b)/29(a)).
  3. Relationship with Section 29 (Prior Art): This is critical. Examiners love to ask: "An application is filed in a convention country on Date D. The corresponding Indian convention application is filed after 12 months. Is the foreign application prior art against the Indian application?" (Answer: Yes. Under Section 29(b), a document filed abroad before the priority date of the Indian application is considered prior art if it is published on or after that priority date. Missing the deadline makes the foreign application a valid prior art document).
  4. Translation Requirements: As seen in the MCQ, know the difference between what the Act states and what the Rules require.
  5. Overall Validity: Questions often test the independence of the Indian application from the foreign one (Section 136).

Key Advice: Always remember the 12-month deadline. When solving any problem, the first step should be to calculate the last date for filing in India. This date is the cornerstone of most questions from this chapter.

 

 

 

Exam Notes: International Arrangements

This chapter deals with the special provisions for applications filed by persons from certain countries and the concept of priority.

Section 133: Convention Countries

  • A convention country is a country that has an arrangement with India for the mutual protection of inventions.
  • This arrangement can be made by an international treaty or convention.
  • The Central Government officially declares a country as a convention country by notification in the Official Gazette.

Section 134: Notification as to Countries Not Providing for Reciprocity

  • If a country doesn't grant reciprocal rights to Indian citizens regarding patent protection, the Central Government can notify this.
  • The effect of this notification is that citizens of that country cannot file a convention application in India.

Section 135: Convention Applications

  • A convention application is an application for a patent in India filed by a person (or their legal representative or assignee) who has already applied for a patent for the same invention in a convention country.
  • The key benefit is that the Indian application is treated as having been filed on the same date as the earliest application in the convention country, provided it's filed within 12 months of that date. This is known as the right of priority.
  • The application must be for the same invention, and the applicant must claim the priority date.

Section 136: Special Provisions Relating to Convention Applications

  • The Controller may require a certified copy of the convention application and the date on which it was filed in the convention country.
  • The invention claimed in the Indian application must not be broader in scope than the invention disclosed in the convention application.

Section 137: Multiple Priorities

  • An Indian patent application can claim priority from two or more prior applications for the same invention filed in one or more convention countries.
  • If multiple priorities are claimed, the 12-month period for filing in India starts from the date of the earliest of those prior applications.
  • If different claims in the Indian application are supported by different prior applications, each claim can have a different priority date corresponding to its supporting application.

Section 138: Supplementary Provisions as to Convention Applications

  • This section clarifies that a convention application cannot be refused or a patent invalidated solely because of publication or use of the invention in India after the priority date but before the date of filing the Indian application.
  • It also protects against a patent being revoked on the ground of prior publication or public use in India that occurred between the priority date and the date of filing.

Section 139: Other Provisions of Act to Apply to Convention Applications

  • Except where explicitly excluded, all other provisions of the Patents Act, 1970, also apply to convention applications.
  • This means that all general requirements for patentability (novelty, inventive step, industrial applicability) and all procedural rules (examination, opposition, etc.) still apply.

Tricky MCQs

  • Answer: (B) Convention Application.

  • Answer: (B) 12 months.

3. An application filed in India claims priority from two separate applications filed in a convention country on January 1, 2024, and April 1, 2024. The Indian application must be filed on or before: (A) April 1, 2025 (B) January 1, 2025 (C) January 1, 2026 (D) It depends on which application is a complete specification.

  • Answer: (B) January 1, 2025. The 12-month period starts from the earliest priority date (Section 137).

(B) After the priority date but before the Indian filing date. (C) After the Indian filing date. (D) None of the above.

  • Answer: (A) Before the priority date of the application in the convention country. The right of priority protects against prior use or publication only between the priority date and the Indian filing date (Section 138).

Tricky Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1: An inventor, Mr. A, a citizen of a convention country, files a patent application in his home country on March 1, 2024. He files a patent application for the same invention in India on February 15, 2025. Another inventor, Ms. B, independently invents the same technology and publicly demonstrates it in India on January 20, 2025. Can Mr. A's Indian patent be invalidated on the grounds of Ms. B's prior public use?

  • Answer: No. Mr. A's Indian application is a convention application and can claim a priority date of March 1, 2024. According to Section 138, any public use of the invention in India after the priority date but before the date of the Indian filing (i.e., between March 1, 2024, and February 15, 2025) is disregarded for the purposes of novelty and inventive step. Since Ms. B's public use occurred on January 20, 2025, it falls within this protected period.

Scenario 2: A company, Innovate Co., files a patent application in the USA (a convention country) on January 1, 2024, for "Method A." On July 1, 2024, they file another application in the USA for "Method B," which is an improvement on Method A. Innovate Co. then files a single patent application in India on December 1, 2024, claiming priority from both US applications. Can they do this? If so, what is the priority date for each claim?

  • Answer: Yes, they can do this. Section 137 allows an Indian application to claim multiple priorities. The claims in the Indian application relating to "Method A" will have a priority date of January 1, 2024. The claims relating to "Method B" will have a priority date of July 1, 2024. Each claim gets the priority date of the earliest application that supports it.

Tricky Use Cases with Answers

Use Case 1: Broadening the Scope

  • Use Case: An inventor files a convention application in India for "A method of producing a polymer using catalyst X." The original application in the convention country only disclosed the use of "catalyst X," but the Indian application claims the use of "any suitable catalyst."
  • Analysis & Answer: The Indian application's claim is broader in scope than the invention disclosed in the original convention country application. According to Section 136(3), the invention claimed in the Indian application must not be broader in scope than the invention disclosed in the convention application. The Controller would likely require the applicant to narrow the claim to "catalyst X" to maintain the priority date. If the applicant refuses, the claim might be allowed but would lose its priority date and would be examined based on its Indian filing date.

Use Case 2: Claiming Priority from a Non-Reciprocal Country

  • Use Case: An inventor from Country Z, which is not a convention country and has no reciprocal arrangements with India, files a patent application in his home country. He then files an application in India within 12 months, claiming priority from the Country Z application.
  • Analysis & Answer: The applicant cannot claim a priority date. A priority claim under Section 135 is only possible if the first application was filed in a convention country. If the Central Government has issued a notification under Section 134 for Country Z, the application will be treated as a regular Indian application, and the priority claim will not be granted.

Related Past Examination Questions

  • What is a convention country, and how is it declared under the Patents Act, 1970?
    • Answer: A convention country is one with which India has an arrangement for mutual protection of patents. The Central Government declares it a convention country by a notification in the Official Gazette (Section 133).
  • Explain the concept of "right of priority" in the context of convention applications. What are the conditions for claiming this right?
    • Answer: The right of priority allows an applicant to claim the filing date of their first application in a convention country as the effective filing date in India. The conditions are: the Indian application must be filed within 12 months of the first application, and both applications must be for the same invention (Section 135).
  • What are the provisions relating to multiple priorities under the Patents Act? Give an example.
    • Answer: The Act allows a single Indian patent application to claim priority from two or more prior applications from one or more convention countries. Each claim in the Indian application can have a different priority date corresponding to the earliest application that supports it (Section 137). Example: See Scenario 2 above.
  • How does a convention application differ from a regular patent application filed in India?
    • Answer: A convention application differs mainly in its effective filing date and protection against intervening acts. A regular application's effective date is its actual filing date in India, whereas a convention application can claim an earlier priority date from a foreign filing. This earlier date protects the invention from being invalidated by publications or public use that occurred between the priority date and the Indian filing date (Section 138).

TRICKY MCQs WITH DETAILED EXPLANATIONS

Question 1

A German company files a patent application in Germany on January 1, 2024. They file a corresponding application in India on January 15, 2025, claiming convention priority. Which statement is MOST accurate?

A) The application is valid as it's within 12 months B) The application is invalid as it exceeds 12 months C) The application is valid but loses convention priority D) The application needs special permission from Controller

Answer: B) The application is invalid as it exceeds 12 months

Explanation: Convention priority must be claimed within 12 months from the date of first filing. January 1, 2024 to January 15, 2025 is more than 12 months (exactly 12 months and 14 days), making the priority claim invalid.

Question 2

An Indian applicant files in the US on March 1st and in Japan on May 1st for related but different aspects of the same invention. On January 15th of the following year, they file in India claiming both priorities. Which is correct?

A) Only US priority is valid B) Only Japan priority is valid
C) Both priorities are valid for respective claims D) Neither priority is valid

Answer: C) Both priorities are valid for respective claims

Explanation: Under Section 137, multiple priorities are allowed. US filing (March 1) and Japan filing (May 1) can both be claimed if the Indian application is filed within 12 months of each (which it is - filed in January of next year). Different claims can have different priority dates.

Question 3

The Central Government withdraws convention status from Country X on June 1, 2024. An application filed in India on July 15, 2024, claiming priority from Country X dated February 1, 2024, is:

A) Valid with priority as the first filing predates withdrawal B) Invalid as Country X is no longer a convention country C) Valid but without priority benefits D) Subject to Controller's discretion

Answer: A) Valid with priority as the first filing predates withdrawal

Explanation: The first filing in Country X was made when it was still a convention country (February 2024 vs withdrawal in June 2024). Rights already accrued cannot be retrospectively taken away.

Question 4

A PCT application designating India is filed claiming priority from three different countries - US, Germany, and Japan - for different aspects. The application meets all PCT requirements. Under Indian law:

A) Only the earliest priority can be claimed B) All three priorities are automatically accepted C) Each priority must be individually verified under Section 137 D) PCT priorities don't need separate verification

Answer: C) Each priority must be individually verified under Section 137

Explanation: Even though it's a PCT application, Indian law under Section 137 requires verification of each priority claim. The mere fact that PCT office accepted them doesn't automatically make them valid under Indian law.

The Indian application is filed within the priority year but without the priority documents.

Exam Notes on Chapter XXII: International Arrangements (Patents Act, 1970)

Chapter XXII of the Patents Act, 1970, deals with international arrangements for patent protection, primarily under the Paris Convention for the Protection of Industrial Property, to which India is a signatory. It facilitates reciprocal rights for patent applicants from convention countries and outlines procedures for claiming priority in India based on foreign filings. Below is a section-wise summary, including key provisions, procedures, and implications. These notes are structured for quick revision, with tables for clarity where effective.

Section 133: Convention Countries

  • Key Provision: The Central Government may notify any country, group of countries, union, or inter-governmental organization (e.g., WTO members, Paris Convention signatories, PCT countries) as a "convention country" if it provides reciprocal patent privileges to Indian citizens similar to those granted to its own nationals.
  • Purpose: Ensures mutual benefits in patent filing and protection.
  • Implications: Notifications are published in the Official Gazette. A declaration may apply to all or only some provisions of the Act.
  • Procedure: No specific filing required; applicants rely on government notifications to claim convention status.

Section 134: Notification as to Countries Not Providing for Reciprocity

  • Key Provision: If a country does not accord the same patent rights to Indian citizens as to its own nationals, the Central Government notifies it in the Official Gazette. Nationals of such countries cannot apply for, own, assign, or license patents in India (solely or jointly).
  • Purpose: Protects Indian interests by enforcing reciprocity.
  • Implications: Bars ineligible applicants from patent-related activities.
  • Procedure: Government notification triggers restrictions; applicants must verify reciprocity before filing.

Section 135: Convention Applications

  • Key Provision: An applicant (or their legal representative/assignee) who filed a "basic application" in a convention country can file a convention application in India within 12 months, claiming the basic application's filing date as the priority date for claims based on disclosed matter.
  • Explanation: If multiple basic applications exist in different convention countries, the 12-month period starts from the earliest. Cognate or modified inventions can be combined into one application, but fees apply as if separate.
  • Purpose: Allows priority claiming to prevent others from patenting the invention in India during the interim.
  • Implications: Patent term is 20 years from the Indian filing date (not priority date).

Answer: No. The strict 12-month deadline under Section 135 is missed by one day, losing priority. The application proceeds as ordinary, potentially vulnerable to intervening prior art.
  2. Scenario: Company B has multiple priorities from filings in UK (June 2024) and France (July 2024). The Indian application claims both but omits a certified copy for the French filing despite Controller's request. Answer: Priority for the French claim may be invalidated under Section 137/138. Submit within 3 months or risk partial loss; translations required if non-English.
  3. Scenario: Applicant C from a non-reciprocal country (notified under Section 134) assigns rights to an Indian entity before filing in India. Answer: Still barred. Section 134 prohibits assignment or licensing by such nationals; the Indian entity must file independently without joint involvement.

Tricky Use Cases with Answers

These use cases focus on practical applications with twists.

  1. Use Case: A startup files a basic application in Australia (convention country) but includes minor developments in the Indian convention filing. The Controller objects to additions. Answer: Allowed if developments qualify under Section 136(2) as separately patentable (Section 6). Respond by justifying or amending claims; failure leads to rejection of additions.
  2. Use Case: An inventor claims multiple priorities but one basic application is in a language other than English, and translation is delayed beyond 3 months. Answer: Priority may be denied for that claim (Section 138). Request extension with fees; common in multinational filings where document procurement delays occur.
  3. Use Case: A convention application is filed timely, but the country is later de-notified for reciprocity issues under Section 134. Answer: The application remains valid if filed before de-notification, but future assignments/licenses may be affected. Monitor Gazette notifications.

📘 Exam Notes: Chapter XXII â€“ International Arrangements

Section 133: Convention Countries

  • Recognizes countries with reciprocal patent arrangements.
  • Includes WTO members and signatories to treaties like the Paris Convention.
  • Ensures Indian applicants get equivalent rights abroad and vice versa.

Section 134: Notification of Non-Reciprocity

  • Central Government can declare countries that do not offer reciprocal rights.
  • Patents from such countries may not be granted privileges in India.

Section 135: Convention Applications

  • Allows claiming priority from a patent filed in a convention country.
  • Must be filed in India within 12 months of the first filing abroad.

Section 136: Special Provisions for Convention Applications

  • Aligns Indian procedures with international treaties like the PCT.
  • Facilitates national phase entry for international applications.

Section 137: Multiple Priorities

  • Applicants can claim multiple priority dates from different convention applications.
  • Useful when improvements are filed in stages.

Section 138: Supplementary Provisions

  • Clarifies procedural aspects like filing requirements and timelines.
  • Ensures consistency with Indian law while honoring international commitments.

Section 139: Other Provisions Apply

  • Convention applications are treated like ordinary applications unless stated otherwise.
