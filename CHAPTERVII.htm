Exam Notes on Chapter VII: Provisions for Secrecy of Certain Inventions (Sections 35–42)

Section 35: Secrecy Directions for Defence-Related Inventions

  • Purpose: Protect inventions relevant to national security.
  • Procedure:
    • Controller may direct secrecy after consulting the Central Government if the invention is defence-relevant.
    • Direction applies to patent applications and related documents.
  • Duration: Initially 6 months (extendable).
  • Key Point: Secrecy starts before publication of the application.

Section 36: Periodic Review of Secrecy Directions

  • Mandatory Review: Controller must review secrecy directions at least once every 6 months.
  • Consultation: Review done with the Central Government.
  • Outcome: Secrecy revoked if no longer necessary for defence.

Section 37: Consequences of Secrecy Directions

  • Restrictions During Secrecy:
    • Application not open to public inspection.
    • No publication of application details.
    • No patent grant until secrecy revoked.
    • No foreign patent filing (without permission under Section 39).
  • Effect on Applicant: Rights suspended until secrecy lifted.

Section 38: Revocation of Secrecy & Time Extension

  • Revocation: Controller may revoke secrecy after consulting Central Government.
  • Time Extension:
    • Controller may extend deadlines missed during secrecy (e.g., filing complete specification).
    • Application processed as if secrecy never applied.
  • Key Point: Extension compensates for delays caused by secrecy.

Section 39: Restriction on Foreign Patent Applications by Residents

  • Prohibition: Indian residents cannot file foreign patents for the same invention without prior written permission from the Controller.
  • Applies Even If:
    • Secrecy direction revoked.
    • Indian application abandoned.
  • Exception: Permission may be granted with conditions.

Section 40: Penalty for Contravening Secrecy/Foreign Filing Rules

  • Offences:
    • Violating Section 35 (disclosing secret invention).
    • Violating Section 39 (foreign filing without permission).
  • Punishment:
    • Imprisonment up to 2 years or fine or both.
    • Up to 3 years if secrecy direction was in place.
  • Liability: Applies to applicants, agents, or any person aiding the violation.

Section 41: Finality of Orders

  • No Judicial Review: Orders by Controller/Central Government under Chapter VII are final and binding.
  • No Challenge: Cannot be questioned in any court (including High Court/Supreme Court).

Section 42: Disclosure to Government

  • Permitted Disclosures:
    • To Central/State Governments for official functions.
    • To persons authorized by the Central Government.
  • Savings Clause: These disclosures do not violate secrecy.

Tricky MCQs with Answers

  1. Q: The Controller can issue a secrecy direction under Section 35 only if:
    a) The applicant is a government employee.
    b) The invention is relevant for defence purposes.
    c) The application has been published.
    d) The complete specification is filed.
    Answer: b) The invention must be defence-relevant. Trick: Applicant’s employment status is irrelevant.
  2. Q: A secrecy direction under Section 35 must be reviewed:
    a) Annually.
    b) Every 6 months.
    c) Only if the applicant requests.
    d) After 1 year automatically.
    Answer: b) Mandatory review every 6 months per Section 36. Trick: Review is periodic, not applicant-driven.
  3. Q: During a secrecy direction (Section 37), the applicant:
    a) Can file a foreign patent without permission.
    b) Cannot disclose the invention to anyone.
    c) Must wait for secrecy revocation to proceed with the Indian patent.
    d) Can challenge the direction in court.
    Answer: c) Patent grant is suspended until secrecy revoked. Trick: Foreign filing requires permission (Section 39), and disclosure to government is allowed (Section 42).
  4. Q: Post-revocation of secrecy (Section 38), the Controller:
    a) Must extend all deadlines automatically.
    b) May extend deadlines missed during secrecy.
    c) Requires re-filing of the application.
    d) Publishes the application immediately.
    Answer: b) Extension is discretionary, not automatic. Trick: "May" implies discretion; "must" is incorrect.
  5. Q: An Indian resident files a patent in the US for an invention already filed in India. They did not obtain permission. This violates:
    a) Section 35 only.
    b) Section 39 only.
    c) Sections 35 and 39.
    d) No violation if secrecy was revoked.
    Answer: b) Violates Section 39 (foreign filing without permission). Trick: Section 35 applies only if secrecy direction exists; revocation doesn’t absolve prior violation.
  6. Q: Contravening Section 39 (foreign filing without permission) attracts:
    a) Imprisonment up to 1 year.
    b) Fine only.
    c) Imprisonment up to 2 years or fine or both.
    d) Imprisonment up to 3 years only if secrecy was in place.
    Answer: c) Base penalty is 2 years/fine/both (Section 40). Trick: 3 years applies only if secrecy direction was in place (Section 40).
  7. Q: An order by the Controller under Chapter VII:
    a) Can be appealed to the High Court.
    b) Can be challenged via writ petition.
    c) Is final and not justiciable.
    d) Requires Central Government approval.
    Answer: c) Section 41 prohibits judicial review. Trick: No appeal/writ is possible.
  8. Q: Disclosure of a secret invention to a State Government:
    a) Violates Section 35.
    b) Is permitted under Section 42.
    c) Requires Controller’s permission.
    d) Is allowed only if secrecy is revoked.
    Answer: b) Section 42 explicitly permits disclosure to governments. Trick: Secrecy does not restrict government disclosures.

Tricky Use Cases with Answers

Use Case 1: Foreign Filing During Secrecy

Scenario:
An inventor files a patent in India. The Controller issues a secrecy direction (Section 35). The inventor files a PCT application in Geneva without permission. Secrecy is revoked after 8 months.

Question: Is the inventor liable?
Answer: Yes.

  • Reason: Foreign filing without permission violates Section 39.
  • Penalty: Imprisonment up to 3 years (since secrecy was in place during filing) under Section 40.
  • Trick: Revocation does not validate prior violation; liability arises at the time of foreign filing.

Use Case 2: Disclosure to Foreign Company

Scenario:
An applicant discloses a secret invention (under Section 35) to a UK company for collaboration. The UK company files a patent in Europe.

Question: Who is liable?
Answer: The Indian applicant.

  • Reason: Disclosure violates Section 35 (unauthorized disclosure of secret invention).
  • Penalty: Imprisonment up to 3 years under Section 40.
  • Trick: Liability falls on the applicant, not the foreign company. Section 40 punishes "any person" contravening secrecy.

Use Case 3: Time Extension Post-Secrecy

Scenario:
A secrecy direction delayed filing of the complete specification by 10 months. Post-revocation, the applicant requests a 10-month extension. The Controller denies it.

Question: Is the denial valid?
Answer: No.

  • Reason: Section 38 requires the Controller to extend deadlines missed during secrecy to ensure fairness.
  • Trick: Extension is not discretionary if delay is directly caused by secrecy. Controller’s refusal violates Section 38.

Tricky Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1: Dual Filing Without Permission

Situation:
An Indian resident files a patent in India and simultaneously files in the US. No secrecy direction is issued.

Question: Is this a violation?
Answer: Yes.

  • Reason: Violates Section 39 (foreign filing without permission).
  • Key Point: Secrecy direction is irrelevant; Section 39 applies to all residents filing foreign patents for the same invention.
  • Penalty: Imprisonment up to 2 years under Section 40.

Scenario 2: Government Disclosure During Secrecy

Situation:
A government scientist discloses a secret invention to a State Ministry for policy advice.

Question: Is this permitted?
Answer: Yes.

  • Reason: Section 42 explicitly allows disclosure to Central/State Governments.
  • Trick: Secrecy (Section 35) does not restrict disclosures to government entities.

Scenario 3: Challenging Controller’s Order

Situation:
The Controller refuses to revoke a secrecy direction despite the Central Government’s recommendation.

Question: Can the applicant approach the High Court?
Answer: No.

  • Reason: Section 41 makes all orders under Chapter VII final and non-justiciable.
  • Trick: Even if the order is unreasonable, judicial review is barred.

Exam Notes: Chapter VII - Secrecy Provisions

This chapter deals with the state's power to control the dissemination of inventions critical to national security and to regulate foreign patenting by Indian residents.

Section

Key Provision

Crucial Points for Exams

S.35

Secrecy Directions (Defence)

- Controller must notify the Central Govt. if an invention is relevant for defence purposes.
- The Central Government is the authority that issues the secrecy direction to the Controller.
- The direction can be given even for a provisional specification (often a trick).
- Applies to "inventions", not just granted patents.

S.36

Periodic Review

- Secrecy directions must be reviewed every 6 months (not 12).
- The review is done by the Central Government.
- The applicant can request a review if they believe the direction is no longer necessary.

S.37

Consequences

- Application is kept secret; not published or granted while the direction is in force.
- The Central Govt. may use the invention (eminent domain) and will pay compensation to the applicant (amount is not specified in the act, can be referred to court).
- Applicant can still be granted a patent, but it will be subject to the secrecy direction.

S.38

Revocation & Extension

- Upon revocation of the direction, the Controller notifies the applicant.
- The applicant then has a prescribed period (e.g., 6 months) to comply with any requirements (e.g., putting the application in order for examination).
- The time between the direction and its revocation is excluded when calculating the term of the patent (to compensate the applicant for the delay).

S.39

Foreign Filing Permission

- No Indian resident can file a patent application outside India without written permission from the Controller.
- Applies unless an application for the same invention has been filed in India at least 6 weeks prior AND no secrecy direction has been received or is expected. (This is a major trick point).
- Applies to provisional specifications as well.

S.40

Liability for Contravention

- Contravention of S.35 (disobeying secrecy) or S.39 (illegal foreign filing) is punishable with imprisonment up to 2 years, or fine, or both.

S.41

Finality of Orders

- Orders of the Controller or Central Govt. under this chapter are final. No appeal is allowed against them. (This is absolute and a key exam point).

S.42

Savings for Govt. Disclosure

- Protects persons who have innocently obtained an invention and disclosed it to a Government department. They can use or publish it without infringement, subject to terms agreed with the govt.

Tricky Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

1. A secrecy direction under Section 35 can be issued by:
a) The Controller of Patents
b) The Central Government
c) The Appellate Board
d) The Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO)

Answer: b) The Central Government. (The Controller only recommends; the power lies with the Central Govt.)

2. An Indian resident files a complete specification in India on January 1. When is the earliest they can legally file for the same invention in the US without seeking permission from the Indian Controller?
a) January 15
b) February 12 (6 weeks later)
c) They must always seek permission
d) After the application is published in India

Answer: b) February 12. (S.39 allows foreign filing without permission if 6 weeks have passed since the Indian filing and no secrecy direction has been received.)

3. The periodicity for the review of a secrecy direction under Section 36 is:
a) Every 3 months
b) Every 6 months
c) Every year
d) Only upon request of the applicant

Answer: b) Every 6 months. (A common trick to use "12 months" as a distractor.)

4. What is the remedy available to an applicant against an order of the Controller imposing a secrecy direction?
a) Appeal to the Appellate Board
b) Appeal to the High Court
c) Writ Petition to the High Court under Article 226
d) No appeal or judicial review is permitted

Answer: d) No appeal or judicial review is permitted. (S.41 declares such orders as final. However, a writ petition under Article 226/32 of the Constitution for violation of fundamental rights might still be possible on limited grounds, but as per the strict letter of the Act, the answer is 'no appeal'.)

5. Under Section 42, if a person innocently discloses an invention to a government department without knowing it was obtained in confidence, they:
a) Are liable for infringement
b) Cannot use the invention commercially
c) Can use the invention subject to terms agreed with the government
d) Must immediately destroy all related information

Answer: c) Can use the invention subject to terms agreed with the government. (The section provides a "savings" or protection clause for such innocent parties.)

Tricky Scenarios & Use Cases (with Answers)

Scenario 1:
Dr. Sharma, an Indian citizen working in a Bangalore lab, develops a new high-strength alloy. He files a provisional application in India on March 1. Eager to get international protection, he files a PCT application designating the US on April 10 (i.e., before 6 weeks have passed). On April 20, the Indian Controller receives a secrecy direction from the Central Government regarding Dr. Sharma's Indian application. What are the legal implications for Dr. Sharma?

Answer:
Dr. Sharma has contravened Section 39. He filed outside India (the PCT application) before the 6-week period from his Indian filing had elapsed (which would have been around April 12). The subsequent issuance of a secrecy direction on April 20 reinforces that the invention was sensitive. He is liable under Section 40, which could lead to imprisonment up to 2 years, or a fine, or both. His foreign application is now also problematic.

Scenario 2:
The Central Government imposed a secrecy direction on Mr. Kapoor's patent application for a encrypted communication system in 2018. The direction was reviewed every 6 months and finally revoked in January 2023. The patent was granted in July 2023. What will be the term of Mr. Kapoor's patent?

Answer:
The term of a patent is 20 years from the date of filing. However, Section 38(2)(b) states that the period for which the secrecy direction was in force shall be excluded from calculating whether any prescribed time limit has expired. More importantly, the term of the patent itself is extended by the period for which the secrecy direction was in force (approx. 5 years in this case). Therefore, his patent's term will be 20 years from the date of filing plus approximately 5 years. The actual expiry date will be calculated and mentioned on the patent certificate.

Scenario 3:
An employee of a private company, Ms. Dixit, receives a technical document from an anonymous source. Unaware that it contains a confidential invention related to missile guidance from a defence lab, she shares it with a government department as part of a proposal. The true owner of the invention sues her for infringement. Can she claim any defence?

Answer:
Yes, Ms. Dixit can likely claim protection under Section 42. The section provides that any person who has innocently received an invention (in this case, anonymously) and subsequently discloses it to a government department, will not be liable for infringement. Her use/publication will be governed by any terms she may agree with the government. Her innocence is key to this defence.

If the amount of compensation cannot be agreed upon, it can be referred to the High Court for adjudication.

Chapter VII: Provisions for Secrecy of Certain Inventions

This chapter deals with inventions that are relevant for defense purposes and the regulations surrounding them to protect national security. The key sections are summarized below.

Section 35: Secrecy Directions for Defense-Related Inventions

This section empowers the Controller of Patents to issue a secrecy direction for an invention if it is considered relevant for defense purposes. This direction prevents the patent application from being published or granted.

  • Who issues it? The Controller of Patents.
  • What triggers it? A report from the Central Government that an invention is "relevant for defence purposes."
  • What happens? The Controller, upon receiving such a report, may prohibit the publication of information related to the invention and may even prohibit the grant of a patent. The applicant is notified of this direction.

Section 36: Periodic Review of Secrecy Directions

Secrecy directions are not permanent. The Central Government periodically reviews them to assess if they are still necessary.

  • Frequency: The Central Government reviews the directions at intervals of not more than six months.
  • Outcome: If the Central Government decides the secrecy is no longer necessary, it informs the Controller, who then revokes the secrecy direction. The patent application then proceeds normally.

Section 37: Consequences of Secrecy Directions

When a secrecy direction is in force, the patent application is held in abeyance.

  • Effect on Application: The application cannot be published, and no patent can be granted.
  • Compensation: If a patent would have been granted but for the secrecy direction, and the direction is not revoked within a specified period, the applicant may be entitled to compensation from the Central Government. The amount is determined by the High Court in the absence of an agreement.

Section 38: Revocation of Secrecy Directions and Extension of Time

This section details the procedure for revoking secrecy directions.

  • Revocation: When the Central Government reports that an invention is no longer relevant for defense, the Controller revokes the secrecy direction. The application then proceeds as if no such direction was ever issued.
  • Extension of Time: The time for doing anything required under the Act (e.g., filing a response) is extended for the period the secrecy direction was in effect.

Section 39: Residents Not to Apply for Patents Outside India Without Prior Permission

This is a critical section that restricts Indian residents from filing for a patent outside India first.

  • Who does it apply to? Any resident of India.
  • What is the restriction? They cannot apply for a patent for an invention made in India in a foreign country without first filing an application in India.
  • Exception: A grace period of six weeks from the date of filing in India is provided. After six weeks, if the Indian application is not a defense-related invention (under Section 35) or a secrecy direction has not been issued, a foreign application can be filed.

Section 40: Liability for Contravention of Section 35 or 39

This section specifies the penalties for violating the rules laid down in Sections 35 and 39.

  • Penalty: Any person who contravenes the secrecy directions under Section 35 or the foreign filing restriction under Section 39 is punishable with imprisonment up to two years, a fine, or both.
  • Loss of Patent Rights: A patent granted in contravention of these provisions is deemed to have been abandoned.

Section 41: Finality of Orders of Controller and Central Government

This section establishes that certain orders made under this chapter are final.

  • Finality: An order of the Controller under Section 35 and a report of the Central Government are not subject to appeal.

Section 42: Savings Respecting Disclosure to Government

This section provides an exception for disclosures made to the government.

  • Exception: The communication of an invention to the Central Government or to an authorized officer for the purpose of a report under Section 35 does not constitute a public disclosure that would prejudice the novelty of the invention.

Tricky Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

1. A secrecy direction under Section 35 can be issued by the Controller of Patents based on a report from: a) The Ministry of Defense b) The Central Government c) The Director General of Patent Office d) The Prime Minister's Office

Answer: b) The Central Government. Section 35 specifies that the report must come from the Central Government.

2. A resident of India invents a new type of military drone. He files a patent application in India on 1st January 2025. On what date can he apply for a patent for the same invention in the USA without the Controller's permission? a) 12th February 2025 b) 13th February 2025 c) 14th February 2025 d) 15th February 2025

Answer: b) 13th February 2025. This is six weeks after the filing date (1st January 2025). The day of filing (1st January) is not counted in the 6-week period. Six weeks = 42 days. The 42nd day after Jan 1 is Feb 12. He can apply on the day after the 42nd day, which is Feb 13.

3. If a patent is granted in a foreign country by a resident of India in contravention of Section 39, the Indian patent application for the same invention is considered: a) Abandoned b) Revoked c) Invalid d) Lapsed

Answer: a) Abandoned. Section 40 clearly states that such a patent shall be "deemed to have been abandoned."

Tricky Use Cases with Answers

Use Case 1: The Inventor and the Foreign Filing

A brilliant Indian scientist, Dr. Priya, invents a new technology for secure communication that has dual-use potential (civilian and military). She files a patent application for it in India. A week later, a foreign company offers her a lucrative deal to license the invention, contingent on her filing a patent in their country immediately. Dr. Priya, eager to close the deal, files the foreign patent application without waiting for the six-week period to expire. What are the consequences?

Answer: Dr. Priya has contravened Section 39. Under Section 40, her Indian patent application for the invention will be deemed to have been abandoned. Furthermore, she is liable for punishment with imprisonment up to two years, a fine, or both. The foreign patent application, even if successful, would not save her Indian rights.

Use Case 2: The Silent Secrecy Direction

An inventor, Mr. Sharma, files a patent application for a new propulsion system. The Central Government, suspecting its defense application, sends a report to the Controller. The Controller issues a secrecy direction but, due to an administrative error, the communication is not sent to Mr. Sharma. Unaware, Mr. Sharma files the application in another country. A few months later, the error is discovered, and he receives the secrecy direction notice. What is the legal standing of his foreign and Indian patent applications?

Answer: The foreign application is still in contravention of Section 39 because the Controller had issued the secrecy direction, even if the inventor wasn't aware. The lack of notification is a procedural lapse on the Controller's part. However, the legal liability under Section 40 is triggered by the contravention of the secrecy direction, regardless of notification. Therefore, the Indian patent is deemed to have been abandoned, and the inventor is liable for penalties. The fact that the inventor was not notified might be a point of defense, but the law's intent is to prevent foreign filings of defense-related inventions.

Tricky Scenarios with Answers

Scenario 1: The Compensation Clause

A secrecy direction is placed on a patent application for a new camouflage technology. The direction remains in force for five years before being revoked. The inventor had secured a licensing agreement with a private company, which would have generated significant revenue, but the deal fell through due to the secrecy direction. Can the inventor claim compensation from the Central Government?

Answer: Yes, the inventor can claim compensation. According to Section 37, if the secrecy direction remains in force and a patent would have been granted but for the direction, the applicant may be entitled to compensation from the Central Government. The amount of compensation would be determined by the High Court if an agreement cannot be reached. The loss of a licensing deal due to the secrecy direction would be a key element in determining the compensation amount.

Scenario 2: The Government's Final Say

An inventor files a patent application for a groundbreaking encryption algorithm. The Controller of Patents, based on a report from the Central Government, issues a secrecy direction. The inventor, a staunch believer in open-source technology, argues that the algorithm has no military application and is only for civilian use. He decides to appeal the Controller's decision. What is the outcome?

Answer: The appeal will fail. According to Section 41, an order of the Controller under Section 35 and a report of the Central Government are not subject to appeal. The Central Government's assessment that an invention is relevant for defense purposes is considered final and cannot be challenged in a court of law.

CHAPTER VII - PROVISIONS FOR SECRECY OF CERTAIN INVENTIONS

Comprehensive Study Notes for Patent Agent Examination

OVERVIEW

Chapter VII deals with inventions that may be prejudicial to national security or defense. It provides mechanisms for the government to impose secrecy directions on patent applications involving defense-related technologies.

Section 35: Secrecy Directions Relating to Inventions Relevant for Defence Purposes

Key Provisions:

  • Authority: Controller may give secrecy directions if invention appears prejudicial to defense of India
  • Consultation: Must consult Central Government before issuing directions
  • Scope: Applies to inventions that may be useful for or prejudicial to defense purposes
  • Effect: Prohibits publication of application and communication of information about invention

Important Points:

  • Direction can be given at any stage before grant of patent
  • Controller acts on own motion or on request from Central Government
  • No publication in Patent Office Journal while direction is in force
  • Applicant must be informed of secrecy direction

Section 36: Secrecy Directions to be Periodically Reviewed

Key Provisions:

  • Review Period: Directions reviewed every 12 months
  • Authority: Central Government conducts review
  • Continuation: Direction continues unless revoked during review
  • Notice: Applicant informed of each review and its outcome

Important Considerations:

  • Automatic review mechanism ensures directions aren't indefinite
  • Burden on government to justify continuation
  • Applicant has right to know status during each review

Section 37: Consequences of Secrecy Directions

Effects of Secrecy Direction:

  1. No Publication: Application not published in Patent Office Journal
  2. No Grant: Patent cannot be granted while direction in force
  3. Restricted Communication: Information about invention cannot be disclosed
  4. Limited Access: Only authorized persons can access application

Penalties for Violation:

  • Communication of information prohibited
  • Violation constitutes breach of secrecy
  • Subject to penalties under Section 40

Section 38: Revocation of Secrecy Directions and Extension of Time

Revocation Process:

  • Authority: Central Government can revoke directions
  • Effect: Normal patent process resumes after revocation
  • Publication: Application published after revocation (if still pending)

Time Extension Provisions:

  • Automatic Extension: Time periods extended automatically during secrecy period
  • Post-Revocation: Additional time may be granted after revocation
  • No Prejudice: Applicant not prejudiced by delays due to secrecy

Key Points:

  • Extensions apply to all time limits under the Act
  • Priority date remains unaffected
  • Applicant can take actions after revocation without penalty

Section 39: Residents Not to Apply for Patents Outside India Without Prior Permission

Core Requirements:

  • Permission Mandatory: Indian residents must get permission before foreign filing
  • Timing: Permission required before filing abroad (including PCT)
  • Authority: Controller grants permission
  • Scope: Applies to inventions made in India

Exceptions:

  • Applications first filed in India and cleared after 6 weeks
  • Applications where secrecy direction already considered and not imposed

Process:

  1. File application in India first OR get prior permission
  2. Wait 6 weeks for security clearance OR get express clearance
  3. Then file abroad with permission

Section 40: Liability for Contravention of Section 35 or Section 39

Penalties:

  • Imprisonment: Up to 2 years
  • Fine: As prescribed
  • Both: Imprisonment and fine may be imposed together

Covered Violations:

  1. Breach of secrecy directions (Section 35)
  2. Foreign filing without permission (Section 39)
  3. Communication of restricted information

Defenses:

  • Good Faith: Acts done in good faith may be excused
  • Authorization: Acts done with proper authorization
  • Ignorance: Genuine ignorance of secrecy direction (limited defense)

Section 41: Finality of Orders of Controller and Central Government

Key Points:

  • No Appeal: Orders under this chapter are final
  • No Court Review: Cannot be questioned in any court
  • Administrative Finality: Decisions are conclusive
  • Exception: Constitutional remedies may still be available

Implications:

  • Very limited judicial review
  • Emphasizes executive discretion in defense matters
  • Applicants have limited recourse

Section 42: Savings Respecting Disclosure to Government

Provisions:

  • Government Rights: Nothing prevents disclosure to government
  • Official Use: Government can use invention for defense purposes
  • Compensation: Provision for compensation to inventor/applicant
  • Crown Use: Similar to government use provisions in other sections

Important Aspects:

  • Government retains right to use invention
  • Inventor entitled to compensation
  • Use limited to defense/government purposes

EXAM NOTES ON CHAPTER VII: PROVISIONS FOR SECRECY OF CERTAIN INVENTIONS
Under the Patents Act, 1970 – Relevant for Patent Agent Examination

Overview of Chapter VII

Chapter VII of the Patents Act, 1970 deals with secrecy provisions for inventions that may have implications for national security or defence purposes. The objective is to prevent the unauthorized disclosure or patenting of sensitive technologies that could compromise India’s defence or strategic interests.

Section-wise Detailed Notes

Section 35: Secrecy directions relating to inventions relevant for defence purposes

  • The Controller may issue secrecy directions if he has reason to believe that the invention is useful for defence purposes.
  • Applies to:
    • Applications filed in India.
    • Applications filed outside India by Indian residents (if prior permission not taken under Section 39).
  • Once secrecy direction is issued:
    • The application shall not be published.
    • No patent shall be granted.
    • The applicant must not publish the invention.
  • The direction is issued after consultation with the Central Government.
  • The applicant is notified of the secrecy direction.

Tricky Point: Secrecy direction can be issued even after filing, but before grant.

Section 36: Secrecy directions to be periodically reviewed

  • Secrecy directions are not permanent.
  • The Central Government must review the secrecy direction at least once in two years.
  • Based on the review, the government may:
    • Continue the secrecy direction.
    • Revoke it, if no longer necessary for defence.
  • The applicant must be given an opportunity to be heard before revocation decision.

Tricky Point: Review is mandatory every 2 years, but revocation is discretionary.

Section 37: Consequences of secrecy directions

When a secrecy direction is in force:

  1. No publication of the application.
  2. No grant of patent.
  3. The applicant cannot disclose the invention to anyone (including foreign entities).
  4. No infringement proceedings can be initiated.
  5. However, the applicant is entitled to compensation if the government uses the invention.

Important: The secrecy direction does not terminate the application — it remains pending.

Section 38: Revocation of secrecy directions and extension of time

  • If secrecy direction is revoked, the application is:
    • Published.
    • Treated as if it had been published on the date it would have been but for the direction.
  • The applicant may be granted extension of time to meet procedural requirements (e.g., request for examination, filing response).
  • The extension is at the discretion of the Controller.

Tricky Point: Even after revocation, the application proceeds as a normal application — examination, opposition, etc., apply.

Section 39: Residents not to apply for patents outside India without prior permission

  • Applies to: Any resident of India (individual or entity).
  • Before filing a patent application outside India, the applicant must obtain prior permission from the Controller.
  • Exception: If the applicant has filed a corresponding application in India at least 6 weeks ago, and:
    • No secrecy direction has been issued.
    • The invention is not related to defence or atomic energy.
  • No permission required if:
    • Application is filed only in India.
    • PCT application is filed after 6 weeks of Indian filing and no secrecy direction issued.

Tricky Point: The 6-week rule is critical. Filing abroad before 6 weeks = violation, unless permission granted.

Section 40: Liability for contravention of section 35 or section 39

  • Contravention of Section 35 (secrecy direction):
    • Patent application shall be deemed abandoned.
  • Contravention of Section 39 (filing abroad without permission):
    • Application in India shall be deemed abandoned.
    • Also liable for penalty under Section 118 (fine up to ₹1 lakh).
  • No right to patent in India for such inventions.

Important: Abandonment is automatic — no need for formal order.

Section 41: Finality of orders of Controller and Central Government

  • Orders under Sections 35, 39, 38, etc., are final and not appealable.
  • However, judicial review is still possible under Article 226/32 of the Constitution.
  • No appeal to IPAB (now abolished) or High Court on merits, but procedural fairness can be challenged.

Tricky Point: "Finality" does not bar writ petitions on grounds of mala fides or violation of natural justice.

Section 42: Savings respecting disclosure to Government

  • Nothing in the Act prevents the disclosure of an invention to the Government or any authorized person.
  • Such disclosure shall not be deemed as publication or prior art.
  • Protects confidential communication with government agencies (e.g., DRDO, ISRO).

Use Case: Scientist at DRDO shares invention with Ministry — not public disclosure.

Exam Notes on Chapter VII: Provisions for Secrecy of Certain Inventions (Patents Act, 1970, as amended)

Chapter VII of the Patents Act, 1970 deals with maintaining secrecy for inventions that may impact national security, particularly those relevant to defence or atomic energy. These provisions balance innovation with national interests by allowing the government to impose restrictions on publication, communication, or foreign filing of such inventions. The chapter empowers the Controller of Patents and the Central Government to issue and review secrecy directions. Key themes include prohibition on disclosure, periodic review, consequences, revocation, foreign filing permissions, penalties, finality of orders, and savings for government disclosure. Below is a section-wise summary, including key points, procedures, and implications for patent applicants.

Section 35: Secrecy Directions Relating to Inventions Relevant for Defence Purposes

  • Key Provision: The Controller can issue directions to prohibit or restrict the publication or communication of information about an invention if it appears relevant for defence purposes (notified by the Central Government or otherwise).
  • Subsections:
    • (1) Directions can apply to applications filed before or after the Act's commencement, restricting communication to specified persons.
    • (2) The Controller must notify the Central Government, which assesses if publication prejudices India's defence. If not, directions are revoked.
    • (3) The Central Government can notify the Controller about relevant inventions even if no directions were issued, triggering the process before grant of patent.
  • Notes: Applies to inventions like weapons, military technology, or atomic energy. Applicants are notified, but the process is mandatory for national security. No appeal against directions during their force. Relevant rule: Rule 71 (application for permission under Section 39, but linked here for defence-related inventions).
  • Exam Tip: Focus on the role of the Controller vs. Central Government; directions are proactive and can be issued at any stage before grant.

Section 36: Secrecy Directions to Be Periodically Reviewed

  • Key Provision: Directions under Section 35 must be reviewed periodically to check if the invention remains relevant for defence.
  • Subsections:
    • (1) Review by Central Government at intervals not exceeding 6 months, or on reasonable applicant request, or as expedient. If no longer prejudicial (or declassified under Atomic Energy Act, 1962), directions are revoked.
    • (2) Results of review must be communicated to the applicant as prescribed.
  • Notes: Ensures directions are not indefinite; applicant can request review if they provide justification. If revoked, normal patent process resumes. Amendment in 2002 reduced review interval from 9/12 months to 6 months for efficiency.
  • Exam Tip: Remember the 6-month interval and linkage to Atomic Energy Act; questions often test timelines and revocation triggers.

Section 37: Consequences of Secrecy Directions

  • Key Provision: Outlines effects on patent applications while directions are in force.
  • Subsections:
    • (1) Controller cannot refuse the application; no appeal from Controller's orders. Application can proceed to grant stage but not published or granted (proviso).
    • (2) If specification is ready for grant during directions: (a) Government use invokes Sections 100-103 (as if patented); (b) Central Government may compensate applicant for hardship (solatium based on novelty, utility, etc.).
    • (3) No renewal fees during directions' force.
  • Notes: Protects applicant from loss (e.g., no fees, possible compensation) but delays grant/publication. Government can use invention without infringement liability.
  • Exam Tip: Link to Chapter XVII (Government use); tricky questions on appeal bars and compensation criteria.

Section 38: Revocation of Secrecy Directions and Extension of Time

  • Key Provision: Upon revocation of directions, Controller can extend timelines for any act under the Act.
  • Notes: Overrides other time limits (e.g., for filing complete specification or responses). Conditions may be imposed. Ensures fairness after delays due to secrecy.
  • Exam Tip: Emphasize discretionary power; often compared with general extension under Section 21 or Rule 138.

Section 39: Residents Not to Apply for Patents Outside India Without Prior Permission

  • Key Provision: Indian residents must obtain permission before filing abroad for inventions.
  • Subsections:
    • (1) No resident can file abroad unless: (a) Indian application filed at least 6 weeks prior; (b) No secrecy directions under Section 35, or revoked.
    • (2) Controller disposes application within prescribed period (21 days per Rule 71); for defence/atomic energy, needs Central Government consent.
    • (3) Exemption if first filing abroad by non-resident.
  • Notes: "Resident" means domiciled in India (not citizenship-based). Form 25 for permission. Aims to prevent leakage of sensitive tech. Amendment in 2002 clarified timelines.
  • Exam Tip: Key for international filings; questions on "resident" definition, 6-week rule, and penalties for non-compliance.

Section 40: Liability for Contravention of Section 35 or Section 39

  • Key Provision: Contravention leads to abandonment of Indian application or revocation of granted patent (without prejudice to Chapter XX penalties).
  • Notes: Civil consequence; criminal under Section 118 (imprisonment up to 2 years/fine). Automatic abandonment if secrecy breached or foreign filing without permission.
  • Exam Tip: Distinguish from general offences; links to Section 64(1)(q) for revocation grounds.

Section 41: Finality of Orders of Controller and Central Government

  • Key Provision: All orders on secrecy by Controller/Central Government are final; no court challenge.
  • Notes: Ensures swift, unchallenged decisions for national security. No judicial review.
  • Exam Tip: Absolute finality; contrast with appealable orders elsewhere (e.g., Section 117A).

Section 42: Savings Respecting Disclosure to Government

  • Key Provision: Controller can disclose application/specification to Central Government for examination under this Chapter or Government use (Chapter XVII).
  • Notes: Exception to non-disclosure; facilitates security checks without violating Act.
  • Exam Tip: Ties to broader government powers; often in questions on disclosure exceptions.

Overall Chapter Notes: Applies mainly to defence/atomic inventions. Process: Screening → Directions → Review → Revocation/Consequences. Penalties deter breaches. For exams, memorize timelines (6 weeks, 6 months, 21 days), forms (Form 25), and cross-links (Atomic Energy Act, Sections 100-103, 64).

Section-wise Notes

Section 35: Secrecy Directions for Defence

  • The Controller may issue secrecy directions for inventions relevant to defence/national security, preventing their publication or communication.
  • These directions can be issued at any stage after the patent application is filed.

Section 36: Review of Secrecy Directions

  • Directions imposed under Section 35 must be reviewed by the Central Government every six months to decide whether they should continue.

Section 37: Consequences of Secrecy Directions

  • If secrecy is imposed, the application is not published, nor is the patent granted until directions are revoked.
  • The applicant may be compensated if unable to use the invention.

Section 38: Revocation & Extension

  • Revocation of secrecy means normal processing resumes, and time lost due to secrecy is not counted against applicant for compliance deadlines.

Section 39: Foreign Filing License

  • Indian residents must obtain permission before applying for patents abroad for inventions relevant to defence.

Section 40: Liability for Contravention

  • Contravention of Section 35 or 39 attracts penalties such as refusal/grant of patent and further sanctions.

Section 41: Finality of Orders

  • Orders of Controller/Central Government are final in secrecy matters and cannot be challenged in court.

Section 42: Disclosure to Government

  • Disclosure to Government authorities is exempt from secrecy constraints.

Tricky Use Cases (With Answers)

  • Scenario 1: An Indian inventor applies for a patent in India for a cryptography device. The Controller suspects defence relevance.
    • Answer: The Controller can issue secrecy directions (Section 35). The application won’t be published or granted. Directions are reviewed every six months, and the inventor cannot file abroad without permission (Section 39).
  • Scenario 2: After secrecy is lifted, the applicant misses a compliance deadline due to the earlier delay.
    • Answer: Under Section 38, the lost time is exempted, so the applicant gets an extension for deadlines missed due to secrecy period.
  • Scenario 3: Resident of India files a patent in the US without obtaining prior permission for an invention subject to secrecy.
    • Answer: Contravenes Section 39. Liability under Section 40; patent could be refused; legal penalties may apply.

Tricky Scenarios (With Answers)

  • Scenario: The Central Government reviews and revokes secrecy directions after 8 months. When must IPO publish the application?
    • Answer: The application must be published as soon as possible after secrecy directions are lifted, regardless of the standard 18-month period.
  • Scenario: Application under secrecy is being licensed to a PSU for government use.
    • Answer: Section 42 allows disclosure to government departments for official use, despite secrecy directions.

 1. Exam Notes (Concise & Practical)

Section 35 – Secrecy Directions (Defence Purposes)

  • When issued?
    • If invention relates to defence or national security.
    • Controller can issue secrecy directions prohibiting publication or communication.
  • Process:
    • Controller → Notifies Central Govt.
    • Govt reviews → If no risk → Directions revoked.
  • Govt power: Can impose secrecy even if Controller hasn’t.

Section 36 – Periodic Review

  • Secrecy directions reviewed every 6 months or on applicant’s request.
  • If risk removed → Directions revoked.

Section 37 – Consequences

  • Application proceeds to examination but grant is withheld.
  • Applicant entitled to compensation if secrecy causes loss.

Section 38 – Revocation & Extension

  • When secrecy revoked → Time for filing/processing extended.

Section 39 – Foreign Filing Restrictions

  • Residents of India cannot file abroad without:
    • (a) Filing in India & waiting 6 weeks for clearance, OR
    • (b) Getting written permission from Controller.
  • Violation → Patent liable to revocation + penalty.

Section 40 – Liability

  • Contravention of Sec 35 or 39 → Patent revoked + penalties (Sec 118: up to 2 years imprisonment or fine).

Section 41 – Finality

  • Orders of Controller & Govt under this Chapter are final (no court challenge).

Section 42 – Disclosure to Govt

  • Controller can disclose application/specification to Govt even under secrecy.

✅ 2. Tricky MCQs

Q1. Secrecy directions under Section 35 can be issued: A) Only after publication
B) Before or after filing, but before grant
C) Only after grant
D) Only for foreign applicants

✔ Answer: B
Explanation: Directions apply any time before grant if invention relates to defence.

Q2. Under Section 39, an Indian resident can file abroad: A) Immediately after filing in India
B) After 6 weeks of filing in India unless secrecy directions issued
C) Only after patent grant in India
D) Only with Central Govt approval

✔ Answer: B
Trick: Many think Govt approval is always needed, but 6-week clearance suffices.

Q3. Which statement is FALSE? A) Secrecy directions are reviewed every 6 months.
B) Controller’s orders under Chapter VII can be appealed in High Court.
C) Govt can impose secrecy even if Controller hasn’t.
D) Compensation is payable for loss due to secrecy.

✔ Answer: B
Reason: Orders are final (Sec 41).

Case 1:

✔ Answer: Contravention of Sec 39 → Patent liable for revocation + penalty under Sec 118.

Case 2:

✔ Answer: Yes, applicant can request review anytime; otherwise, automatic review every 6 months.

✔ Answer: Controller revokes secrecy directions (Sec 38), extends time for compliance, and application proceeds to grant.
